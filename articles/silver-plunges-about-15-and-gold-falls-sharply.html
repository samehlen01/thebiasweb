Silver plunges about 15% and gold falls sharply

Silver plunged 15% and gold fell sharply in a historic precious-metals sell-off. In Asia trade on Monday, spot gold fell more than 9% to $4,403 an ounce, and silver slumped by 15% to less than $72 an ounce.

What happened

The BBC reported that spot gold suffered its sharpest one-day drop since 1983, falling by more than 9%, and that silver plunged about 27% on Friday.

CNBC put Friday's gold fall at nearly 10% and cited a Monday spot price near $4,616.79 per ounce; it described silver's Friday move as roughly a 30% nosedive, the worst single day since March 1980.

The Guardian said silver tumbled as much as 35% at intraday lows before trimming losses to close roughly 26% lower.

MarketWatch said the severity left silver on track for its worst single-day decline since 2011.

Markets beyond metals

The sell-off spread beyond precious metals: crude oil fell more than 5%, South Korea's Kospi plunged over 5%, Hong Kong's Hang Seng dropped about 3%, and Japan's Nikkei 225 was more than 1% lower.

Leverage and exchanges

Clearing houses moved quickly to limit risk: the CME Group raised margin requirements on COMEX gold futures to 8% from 6% and on COMEX 5,000-ounce silver futures to 15% from 11%. That tightening, together with forced selling, hit leveraged vehicles and miners; the ProShares Ultra Silver fund fell about 25% and the iShares Silver Trust ETF lost roughly 12.7%, while miners including Endeavour Silver and First Majestic showed double-digit losses in pre-market trade.

Rally before the rout

The drops followed a blistering run: in 2025 gold surged about 65% and silver roughly 150%, and by early 2026 silver had added about 37% while gold was up around 15.4% year-to-date.

Wall Street analysts were reported to expect the Federal Reserve to cut interest rates at least twice in 2026.

Both metals reached recent record intraday highs during the rally, with gold near $5,594.82 and silver around $121.64.

Physical demand in China remained firm ahead of Lunar New Year, with Shanghai prices at a premium and strong jewellery and bar buying.

Volatility and risk

The CBOE market volatility index (VIX) rose 9.6% to 19.1 points as traders absorbed the rout. U.S. stock futures and other risk assets were pressured after the metals sell-off.

Competing explanations

The sell-off coincided with President Donald Trump's announcement that he would nominate Kevin Warsh to be the next chair of the Federal Reserve.

Analysts at Deutsche Bank said the Warsh nomination looked like the clear catalyst for Friday's sell-off.

Tim Waterer of KCM said the nomination likely triggered the initial move but did not justify the full size of the downward move, pointing to forced liquidations and margin increases that cascaded through the market.

Ipek Ozkardeskaya at Swissquote argued Warsh is expected to favour balance-sheet reduction, a development she said could mark the end of the era of free money for markets and therefore be negative for asset prices.

Mark Matthews of Bank Julius Baer said precious metals collapsed because they had gone parabolic in the prior week and were vulnerable to a sharp correction.

Christopher Forbes of CMC Markets described the retreat as a classic correction after an extraordinary rally rather than a breakdown of the long-term bullish thesis.

José Torres of Interactive Brokers said the 'Buy America' trade had returned and that the domestic bid that pushed gold and silver toward nosebleed highs was now unraveling.

Anthony Scaramucci wrote that markets had decided Kevin Warsh was a Paul Volcker figure, implying a much more hawkish outlook.

Derren Nathan of Hargreaves Lansdown warned the FTSE 100 was set to open lower and that mining stocks would likely feel the pain after lenders increased margin calls.

Kathleen Brooks of XTB cautioned that if the sell-off continued, gold and silver risked eroding their year-to-date gains and that the approach of China's Lunar New Year could accelerate selling as traders reduce risk.

The selloff reflects an unwind of crowded positioning, not a shift in fundamentals... We think that fundamentals remain intact. Central banks continue to anchor demand, with roughly 800 tonnes of buying expected in 2026.
— Lale Akoner, eToro

Lale Akoner of eToro said the sell-off reflected an unwind of crowded positioning, not a shift in fundamentals, and she pointed to central-bank buying, roughly 800 tonnes expected in 2026, as supportive for longer-term prices.

Market consequences

In Europe the FTSE 100 opened lower and major mining names fell sharply; shares in Fresnillo and Endeavour Mining were both down by about 7% at the start of trading.

Silver-focused ETFs were dragged into the action: the ProShares Ultra Silver fund fell roughly 25% and the iShares Silver Trust ETF lost about 12.7%.

That pressure amplified losses for investors in those funds.

Lower raw metal prices were positive for jewellery makers; Pandora's shares jumped about 9% in early trading.

South African stock markets, which are sensitive to miners, recorded some of the biggest falls, at one point the steepest since March 2020.

More broadly, U.S. stock futures and other risk assets were pressured after the metals rout, adding to a volatile start to 2026.

Near-term scenarios

Christopher Forbes of CMC Markets suggested that renewed dollar weakness or confirmation that Warsh would be dovish could bring dip buyers back to the market.

Saxo's strategy team warned that the risk of second- and third-round selling remained elevated, especially if Shanghai, the main source of recent support, kept slipping and silver futures stayed limit-down.

Akoner cautioned that the outlook for silver is less clear than for gold and that further volatility remains likely.

Kathleen Brooks at XTB speculated that Chinese traders reducing risk ahead of Lunar New Year could accelerate the sell-off.

State of play

Reporting across outlets agrees that both gold and silver fell sharply last Friday and into Monday. In Asia trade on Monday, spot gold fell more than 9% to $4,403 an ounce and silver slumped by 15% to less than $72 an ounce. Clearing houses raised margin requirements, with the CME Group lifting margins on COMEX gold futures to 8% from 6% and on 5,000-ounce COMEX silver futures to 15% from 11%. Analysts disagree on the proximate cause: some attributed the initial spark to the Kevin Warsh Fed nomination, while others said it was a parabolic squeeze driven by crowded positioning, and some pointed to forced liquidations cascading through the market. It remains uncertain whether the episode will be a temporary correction that could draw dip buyers if the dollar weakens or a longer, policy-led recalibration. Market participants will now watch margin levels, China's physical demand into Lunar New Year and any fresh signals from policymakers.
