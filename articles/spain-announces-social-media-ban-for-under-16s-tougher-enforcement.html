Spain announces social media ban for under-16s, tougher enforcement

At the World Governments Summit in Dubai, Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez unveiled a package that would bar under-16s from major social platforms, create criminal penalties for executives whose platforms do not take down unlawful or hate-filled content, and criminalise deliberate algorithmic manipulation and the amplification of illegal material.

What Sánchez proposed

Sánchez framed the measures as a child protection intervention, saying the state must step in where platforms expose young people to harm. He proposed a system he described as a hate and polarization footprint to quantify how platforms fuel division and to underpin legal and financial penalties, and he said the government would work with public prosecutors to investigate and, if warranted, prosecute alleged infringements by Grok, TikTok and Instagram.

We will protect them from the digital Wild West.
— Pedro Sánchez, Prime Minister of Spain

On the summit stage, Sánchez described the online environment in stark terms, accusing some actors of creating and promoting disinformation and saying platform algorithms amplify that content in pursuit of profit. He said the package would make deliberate algorithmic manipulation and the amplification of illegal content a criminal offence.

Grok, investigations and raids

The announcement arrived while Grok, X's AI assistant, was already under scrutiny in Europe and the UK after regulators raised concerns it had been used to produce sexualised images of real people. French prosecutors' cyber-crime investigators reportedly raided X's Paris offices amid those inquiries.

Coalition and cross-border enforcement

Madrid said it is leading a Coalition of the Digitally Willing to coordinate stricter cross-border enforcement against major tech platforms. Coverage described the group's size differently: some reports called it a group of six European countries, while Sánchez framed it as Spain joining five other unnamed European nations.

Parliamentary maths and timing

Sánchez said he hoped to have the laws passed next week, but his left-wing coalition lacks a parliamentary majority, which the BBC said could make that timetable hard to meet.

Some outlets went further on timing, speculating a bill could be introduced as early as next week, a point that supporters and sceptics alike cited when assessing whether the window for rapid passage was realistic.

Political split at home

The domestic political response was mixed. Spain's main opposition, the conservative People's Party, appeared to support the measure and noted it had proposed similar restrictions in the past, while the far-right Vox party spoke out against the ban.

Industry reaction and campaign voices

Tech platforms and industry spokespeople pushed back quickly, arguing that a blunt, across-the-board prohibition on under-16s would be difficult to implement and could be ineffective in practice.

Those companies also warned policymakers that removing access entirely risks isolating vulnerable teenagers in need of support, an argument raised repeatedly in coverage of Australia's similar measures.

Meta made a formal call for constructive engagement with industry rather than blanket bans, urging incentives to lift safety, privacy and age-appropriate design across platforms.

Campaign figures were split: Daisy Greenwell of Smartphone Free Childhood described a ban as an understandable next step and one of the clearest policy responses available, while Jose Cesar Perales cautioned there is not unanimous agreement that social media harms adolescents.

Legal risks and international precedent

Australia became the first country last year to ban children under 16 from social media, and legal challengers there, most notably Reddit, have taken the case to the High Court.

Meta told reporters it removed 550,000 accounts it believed belonged to under-16s in Australia across its platforms, a figure the company used to argue that platforms are capable of broad enforcement when compelled by law.

Age verification and implementation problems

Practical questions about verifying age quickly rose in coverage. The BBC noted young people have sometimes bypassed checks by using a photo of an adult or other simple loopholes, a technical weakness that critics say weakens the promise of an immediate ban.

One enforcement model detailed in reporting would require platforms to implement age-verification systems or face heavy fines; CNBC said non-compliance could bring penalties of up to 49.5 million Australian dollars (about $32 million).

Platform liability and algorithm rules

A central thrust of the package is to change liability incentives: Sánchez proposed legal reforms aimed at making chief executives criminally liable if their platforms do not take down unlawful or hate-based content, an unprecedented move in scale and consequence if enacted.

Relatedly, the plan would criminalise deliberate algorithmic manipulation and the amplification of illegal content, a policy Sánchez said would target the profit motives that can drive polarising and disinformation-driven engagement.

X's response and Elon Musk's posts

X and its leadership pushed back. The company characterised the French inquiry into Grok as an attack on free speech, while Elon Musk responded directly on X with personal insults aimed at Sánchez.

Dirty Sanchez is a tyrant and traitor to the people of Spain,
— Elon Musk
Sánchez is the true fascist totalitarian.
— Elon Musk

Those posts amounted to a sharp personal attack and were reported as such across outlets, complicating a debate that also includes legal, technical and political dimensions rather than only rhetorical sparring.

International context and likely consequences

Analysts warned the Spanish package could have ripple effects: CNBC forecast that the announcement may encourage other governments to pursue similar measures, while legal challengers point to ongoing court fights as evidence that such laws can be stalled or overturned.

Timing, next steps and uncertainty

Sánchez said he hopes to have the measures considered quickly; some outlets have speculated a bill could be introduced as early as next week, but the combination of parliamentary arithmetic and the prospect of immediate legal challenges means the actual timetable is uncertain.

State of play

Reporting confirms Madrid has proposed banning children under 16 from social media. The package also includes plans to create new criminal liability for executives. It would also criminalise certain algorithmic practices. Officials said the government would work with prosecutors to investigate alleged platform breaches. Tech firms say bans would be impractical. They warn such measures could isolate vulnerable teenagers. European and national probes, including inquiries into Grok, mean platform behaviour may be investigated or prosecuted. It remains uncertain whether Sánchez's government can pass laws next week as he has said, and any enacted measures are likely to face immediate legal challenges and further international policy debate.
