Andy Burnham Announces VAT Cut on Household Electricity Bills

Andy Burnham announced a VAT cut on household electricity bills, with the charge due to fall from 5% to 0% on 1 October.

The same day Ofgem's new price cap on energy also comes in. Burnham cast it as the first major domestic policy of his premiership and said the move should give families breathing room, making the government look like a genuine cost-of-living administration.

The government says suppliers are expected to pass the relief through to customers, including people on fixed tariffs.

The measure applies to electricity only on dual-fuel bills. It covers England, Scotland and Wales, with Northern Ireland due to receive equivalent support.

Ministers also say the relief will reach small businesses, charities and residential care homes. Because the cut touches electricity use rather than gas, households that draw more power will save more in cash terms, especially homes with heat pumps or other high-use electric technology. That same design means dual-fuel households that mostly heat with gas will feel less of a change. In other words, the policy is broad in who it covers, but uneven in what it is worth from one home to the next, depending on how much electricity is used.

Funding dispute

The biggest row around the policy is not the tariff cut itself but how it is being paid for. The government says it will fund the change by cancelling the Digital ID programme and reprioritising spending, and John Healey argued the package is affordable and should help bring inflation down. Darren Jones said the Digital ID plan was unfunded and that ministers would have to explain the numbers at the Budget, while Kemi Badenoch said the explanations are falling apart. Claire Coutinho added that bills are still much higher than before and warned the funding story could point to more tax rises. The Office for Budget Responsibility's provisional £1.8bn ($2.41 billion) estimate for Digital ID over three years has also become part of the row, because the savings needed to replace it have not yet been pinned down. For critics, the issue is not whether households want relief; it is whether the fiscal arithmetic behind the promise has really been nailed down.

We are funding this through the cancellation of the Digital ID programme

UK government ministers say the change will be funded by cancelling the Digital ID programme and reprioritising spending.

That fight matters because the Treasury says the policy will cost around £850m ($1,138 million) this financial year. The Office for Budget Responsibility also said the Digital ID scheme had been provisionally forecast to cost £1.8bn ($2.41 billion) over three years, while the government said that cost had already been scored by the same watchdog. The disagreement is therefore less about the direction of the tax cut than about whether the offset is real, fully identified and large enough to match the bill ministers are creating. It is the kind of accounting dispute that can sound abstract, but in practice it determines how long the relief can last and whether the government needs to find more money later.

Burnham said the VAT cut is only the first major domestic policy of his premiership and that more measures are expected in the next few days. The government has also said longer-term support will be decided at the Budget, so the present move looks more like a bridge than a final settlement. That timetable matters because it tells households this is the first phase of a larger conversation, not the last word on electricity costs.

Officials expect the VAT change to shave around 0.10 percentage points off CPI inflation, and some reports put the effect on RPI at about 0.14 points. That is why John Healey described the move as affordable, while Martin Lewis called it a good totemic step that would have little impact in practice. The two comments point in different directions, but both reflect the same reality: the saving is real, yet it is small enough that households will still feel the rest of the energy bill.

The cut is also landing while energy forecasts remain unsettled. Cornwall Insight says the October price cap could rise by 2% if Middle East unrest keeps wholesale costs higher, analysts warned that the US-Iran conflict could keep oil, gas and UK bills elevated this winter, and one industry forecast puts the typical winter bill around £1,849 ($2,475). Those projections do not change the VAT plan, but they explain why a tax cut of this size can still feel modest against the wider market backdrop.

Supporters say it eases pressure

Burnham argued that the cut would give families breathing room and make the government look like a genuine cost-of-living administration, while John Healey said the policy was affordable and should help lower inflation. Ben Miskell also welcomed the move, saying more money in people's pockets should support local spending and the local economy. Ministers added that small businesses, charities and residential care homes are among the other groups expected to benefit, which they are presenting as evidence that the cut is not just a household giveaway but a broader boost to energy users. Supporters are, in effect, selling the measure as immediate help that can be felt quickly even if the headline saving looks small.

Supporters see it as a practical concession rather than a grand cure. On that reading, the value of the policy is partly psychological: even a modest drop can tell households the government is trying to do something visible about bills, and the inflation estimate gives ministers a second line of defence beyond the cash figure itself. Lower bills could support local spending and help local economies. That is also why Burnham and Healey have framed the move in cost-of-living terms instead of treating it as a narrow energy measure. They are trying to make the cut look like a first step, not the whole answer.

Critics question the scale

Martin Lewis said the cut is welcome as a symbolic step but will make little difference in real life. Justin Moy went further, calling the gain about 90p a week — roughly the price of a coffee each month — and a token gesture, and Scott Gallacher said the announcement risks turning into a political own goal. Their point is that a small tax tweak does not amount to the sort of help households want when energy bills remain high. In cash terms, they argue, the policy may be easier to announce than to feel.

Helen Miller said she was disappointed because the policy adds complexity and will be hard to unwind. Michelle Lawson took a more sympathetic line, saying any saving matters for vulnerable households even if the change is only a start, while Kirsty Blackman called the saving a drop in the ocean. Simon Francis of the End Fuel Poverty Coalition said the move is a positive signal but argued that government should go further on targeted support and market reform, and Chris O'Shea said any reduction is welcome but a social tariff would be more sustainable. That caution fits with evidence that energy debt remains a major problem and charities still want more targeted help.

Taken together, those reactions show a split between people who want universal relief and those who want support aimed only at the most exposed households. Helen Miller's concern about complexity, Simon Francis's call for more reform and Chris O'Shea's push for targeting all point in the same direction, while Michelle Lawson's view shows why the cut still has defenders even among people who think it is too small. It is a debate about fairness, but also about political design: a simple tax break is easier to understand, while a targeted system might deliver more value to fewer households.

Who benefits most

The policy's design also shapes who wins most. Households that use more electricity will save more in cash terms, especially homes with heat pumps or other high-use electric technology, while Adam Scorer of National Energy Action said the cut helps everyone but is less useful for low-income households that still heat with gas. The Institute for Fiscal Studies argued that the measure is not well targeted and may leave wealthier households better off in cash terms, even though lower-income families spend a larger share of income on electricity. Camilla Born said limiting the tax break to electricity rather than gas points towards clean power and electrification, which means the policy is being read not just as bill relief but as a sign of energy strategy too.

Nigel Farage took a different view, saying the policy does not tackle the real cost-of-living problem and only became possible because Brexit removed the EU VAT constraint. Richard Neudegg said the cut should still apply across tariffs and that switching to a fixed deal can preserve the tax saving, although market pressure may still push prices higher. This Is Money estimated that a household moving to a fixed tariff now could raise total annual savings to about £352 ($471.18), which shows how consumer advice around the cut is becoming part of the story.

That estimate should be treated as a scenario rather than a promise, because it depends on tariff choices and on how providers price the market once the cut is in place. Even so, it underlines a broader point about the announcement: the VAT change does not sit in isolation, but alongside wholesale energy trends, price-cap moves and household decisions about fixed deals. The practical question for families is therefore not only what the government has announced, but how the market responds once suppliers start billing at the lower tax rate.

What happens next

What is confirmed is that domestic electricity VAT will drop to zero on 1 October and suppliers are expected to pass it through, while officials think the cut will trim about 0.10 percentage points from CPI and cost roughly £850m ($1,138 million) this financial year. What remains disputed is whether the Digital ID cancellation really covers the bill, how well targeted the relief is, and how much it will help households that rely on gas rather than electricity. Low-income households that still heat with gas may benefit less from the cut. Burnham says more measures are expected in the next few days, and the government has pushed longer-term decisions to the Budget, where Nesta's Andrew Sissons argues wider reforms would be the right next step.
