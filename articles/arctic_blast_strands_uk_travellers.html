Overview

An Arctic blast has swept across the UK and parts of northern Europe this week, prompting fresh Met Office and local weather warnings. Heavy snow and strong winds forced rail operators in northern Scotland to suspend services and Network Rail to deploy snowploughs on key routes. Air travel was disrupted from Dublin to Aberdeen and Liverpool, with runways temporarily closed, flights diverted and hundreds of cancellations reported across continental hubs. Local ferry operators and island services also faced cancelled sailings, route changes and delayed freight runs. Authorities said at least six people had died in weather-related incidents across Europe, including five in France and one woman killed in Sarajevo, Bosnia. Authorities said power was being restored to thousands of households in Berlin after they had been without electricity for four days following a suspected far-left attack on high-voltage lines.

Airports and flights

Liverpool John Lennon Airport closed its runway on the morning of January 5, leading to dozens of cancellations and diversions. The runway later reopened but the airport warned that flights remained subject to delay or cancellation and urged travellers to check with their airline. A few planes were diverted to Manchester after Liverpool's closure, and Manchester recorded several cancellations that morning. Norwich Airport temporarily closed its airfield and cancelled flights to Amsterdam as Dutch conditions worsened. Aberdeen Airport suspended operations temporarily and some flights were diverted, with British Airways apologising for a service rerouted to Edinburgh. Amsterdam Schiphol Airport announced at least 700 flight cancellations because of snow and wind. More than 1,000 people spent the night at Schiphol, the airport said, and camp beds were set up and breakfast was offered to travellers forced to sleep there. Dutch airline KLM warned it was running out of de-icing fluid for its jets and said delays to supplies had made it difficult to replenish stock. France's civil aviation authority asked airlines to cut 40% of flights at Paris's Roissy-Charles de Gaulle and 25% at Orly. About 100 flights were cancelled at Paris's Charles de Gaulle airport and a further 40 at Orly, the transport minister said. A further 600 flights scheduled to take off from Schiphol on Wednesday were also cancelled, Dutch broadcaster NOS reported, and KLM grounded around 600 flights to and from its hub on Wednesday. Jet2 said it plans to operate all of its flights as close to schedule as possible but warned there may be some disruption should the weather or the forecast deteriorate, and advised customers to check in as scheduled and follow updates from staff in terminals. Birmingham Airport was forced to suspend use of its runway operations on Thursday night after 'heavy snow' made it unsafe to fly, while Heathrow cancelled dozens of flights due to severe weather. Birmingham Airport said at 5.20am on Friday it was completing safety checks after operations were suspended overnight because of heavy snow on the runway. At least 69 flights scheduled to operate to or from Heathrow airport on Friday have been cancelled. This is likely to have disrupted more than 9,000 passengers. East Midlands Airport said at 5am its runway was closed "due to heavy snowfall" and urged passengers to check with their airlines for the latest information. In a statement issued just after 6am, East Midlands Airport said its runway had reopened after being closed overnight due to heavy snow on the runway. Leeds Bradford Airport said: Delays of up to 90 minutes to all flights at Leeds Bradford Airport due to poor weather conditions. Passengers are advised to check the status of their flights before travelling to the airport. The 8.20am Jet2 flight from LBA to Agadir-Al Massira, in Morocco ended up setting off at 11.15am instead. The first supply of de-icing fluid has now arrived. We continue to replenish stock to ensure that the flights we operate can depart safely.

Railways and ploughs

Network Rail arranged snowplough locomotives and closed lines between Aberdeen, Dundee and Inverness to clear drifts and protect safety. ScotRail cancelled services to Thurso and warned that replacement buses could not run because roads were impassable. LNER advised customers not to travel between Edinburgh and Aberdeen, extending its 'do not travel' guidance into the afternoon. Transport for Wales confirmed trains between Machynlleth and Pwllheli were cancelled and National Rail cautioned that frozen points could cause delays into the afternoon. LNER warned that disruption to services in northern Scotland was expected to continue until the end of the day on Thursday 8 January. Network Rail will close the Hope Valley Line between Sheffield and Manchester from 6pm on Thursday. No rail replacement services will be operating during the closure "due to the severe weather conditions". Transport for Wales said that from 15:00 on Thursday 8 January a reduced timetable will be introduced, with 1 train per hour (tph) Carmarthen - Cardiff Central. Avanti West Coast is to reduce its timetable from 3pm. Instead of three trains an hour between both Birmingham and Manchester to London Euston, there will be two services, and only one an hour between Liverpool and London. Train operator Northern has also warned that the following routes will be closed between 6pm on Thursday and 10pm on Saturday: Buxton-Hazel Grove, New Mills Central-Sheffield and Guide Bridge-Glossop/Hadfield. Network Rail said it had arranged chainsaw teams to be on shift throughout the storm to respond quickly to fallen trees. West Midlands Railway has suspended all services and says none will resume 'until the afternoon'. DO NOT TRAVEL advice is in place on our Midlands routes until 13:00 today, Friday 9 January. All trains in Cornwall will be suspended from 6pm with no replacement buses - while services in Devon between Exeter, Okehampton and Barnstaple will also be axed.

Ferries and islands

NorthLink issued operational notices for the Northern Isles: one update said a northbound sailing to Lerwick could be delayed 'up to three hours', while a later bulletin put a similar arrival at 'up to two hours'. The operator also said a planned call at Kirkwall would be cancelled on one sailing and warned freight sailings were not scheduled to resume until Sunday. Inter-island ferry services in Shetland were suspended and some local bus runs were cancelled.

Local impact in Shetland

Shetland's emergency planners urged residents to avoid non-essential journeys and praised community resilience as crews worked to clear routes. NHS Shetland said reduced staffing was expected, that some appointments and planned theatre procedures would be postponed, and that health centres might offer telephone consultations. The islands' council confirmed schools would remain closed and said bin collections were paused while gritting and priority routes were tackled. Residents posted both criticism of road-clearance priorities and thanks for farmers who helped free vehicles. The SIC announced this morning every school in Shetland would be open, following several days in which all or some had been closed due to weather, resulting in some transport changes for pupils at the Anderson High School; Aith Junior High School; Lunnasting Primary School and Sound Primary School. Most schools will reopen tomorrow after two days of snow disruption. Only Mid Yell, Baltasound, Sandwick Junior High Schools and Nesting and North Roe Primary Schools will remain closed, the council said earlier this afternoon.

Warnings, health and safety advice

Forecasters warned of 10–40cm of snow on higher ground with drifting and temporary blizzard conditions in exposed areas, and yellow and amber alerts covered large parts of Scotland and northern England. The UK Health Security Agency kept an amber cold-health alert in place in England for the coming days. Police and transport officials urged people to plan ahead or avoid travel where possible. The AA reported a surge in callouts while the RAC advised drivers to de-ice, carry warm clothing and delay journeys if conditions are poor. Met Éireann issued a Status Yellow snow-and-rain warning for counties Cork, Kerry, Waterford and Wexford. The warning was due to kick in at noon tomorrow and remain in place until 8pm. The Met Office updated an amber wind warning for Cornwall and the Isles of Scilly to red between 4pm and 11pm on Thursday (8 January), with “dangerous stormy winds” forecast. The Met Office had warned peak winds of '100mph or more' were expected in exposed areas of Cornwall, and that 'violent gusts' could 'persist for two to three hours'...The warning said people should expect damage to buildings and homes, very large waves, flying debris resulting in 'danger to life', power cuts and transport disruption. More than 65,000 properties were left in the dark without power on Thursday evening, the National Grid said - most in southwest England. - Three lanes are closed on the M1 going north between J28 and J29, close to Heath, including the clip lane onto J29, with delays of over half an hour as of 7.30am. The AA said its teams had attended almost 13,000 breakdowns across the UK by 16:00 on Tuesday. News of its worst impact emerged when police announced a man had died after a tree fell onto his caravan in Cornwall. More than 200 schools were closed on Monday and more than 100 schools have already confirmed that they will be shut on Tuesday - a full list can be viewed here. The forecaster has issued a new Status Yellow - low temperature/ice warning for Leinster, Cavan, Monaghan, Munster, Galway and Roscommon, which is in place until 9am tomorrow.

Conflicting reports

Reports differ on NorthLink's delay: one update said 'up to three hours', while another said 'up to two hours'. Estimates for cancellations also vary: one international dispatch spoke of 'close to 1,000 flight cancellations' across northern Europe, while Dublin's airport said 'Airlines have already cancelled 8 flights' affecting Dublin services. Manchester coverage is inconsistent — a spokesman said the airport was 'operating as usual' while another update recorded 'a total of five flights' grounded that morning. LNER guidance ranged from an explicit 'do not travel' instruction to reports that disruption could continue 'until the end of the day on Tuesday'

Reactions and official statements

Manchester's airport reiterated it was 'operating as usual' while urging passengers to allow extra time and to check airline apps for updates. British Airways apologised for diverted services. easyJet said it 'does not tolerate disruptive behaviour onboard' after police attended a flight. Police and transport chiefs urged people to 'plan ahead' or avoid travel where possible, and Assistant Chief Constable Alan Waddell warned of 'hazardous' driving conditions. Aberdeen Airport thanked staff for 'working tirelessly' on snow clearing. Loganair offered free flight alterations to affected customers. The RAC reiterated advice to de-ice vehicles and pack warm supplies while the AA reported a spike in callouts. Health officials and the health secretary warned the cold snap would put extra pressure on hospitals and urged people to use A&E only for emergencies. Ministers in Scotland met police, local authorities, utilities companies and other bodies to discuss the response to the severe weather. Justice Secretary Angela Constance, who chaired the meeting, said difficult conditions were expected to remain this week. Aberdeenshire council also declared a 'major incident' as ongoing snow continued to cause travel disruptions and school closures. More than 250 schools are due to remain closed on Friday, including more than 150 in Aberdeenshire, dozens in the Highlands and Aberdeen, and a number in Moray. Cyngor Gwynedd's recycling centres are all shut and the council said its waste and recycling collections "will not be possible".

The UK will continue to experience a range of winter weather hazards through this week, with low temperatures as well as snow showers and the risk of ice for many.
— Matthew Lehnert, Met Office chief meteorologist

Practical advice repeated across outlets urged passengers to check with airlines and allow extra time, while rail customers were told to keep tickets and record journeys for potential compensation. Eurostar said services to the Netherlands would run only as far as Brussels and advised travellers to postpone journeys where possible. Eurostar said all the morning trains between London and Paris were delayed, some by over an hour, and an early-evening round-trip between the two cities had been cancelled. Transport for Wales said rail tickets for Thursday can be used for travel up to Sunday (January 11).

Ireland
Sources
RTE Ireland, Irish Times

RTE Ireland goes beyond Met Office bulletins by enumerating continental knock‑on effects: it reports roughly 380,000 households without power in France and says EDF took reactors 1 and 3 at Flamanville offline after a high‑voltage line failed; it also records an exceptional 213 km/h gust at Barfleur and lists cross‑border transport suspensions and Heathrow flight cancellations. Those operational and network impacts are more detailed than many UK summaries focused on warning levels.

Irish outlets (Irish Times) add local detail missing from UK roundups: Met Éireann’s yellow warnings were targeted at southern counties (Cork, Kerry, Waterford, Wexford), councils reported practical measures (Waterford said it had treated more than 500 km of roads) and welfare/transport mitigations were specified — precise timings and warming‑hub arrangements often absent from UK headlines.

Poland
Sources
TVN24 Poland

TVN24 emphasises municipal responses and policy trade‑offs: Warsaw has erected tents with hot drinks at major interchanges (fire services to staff them, water utilities supplying the tea), is deploying extra buses as warming spaces and stands ready to open more night‑shelter capacity for people experiencing homelessness. The mayor explicitly rejected portable coke‑heaters (koksowniki) to avoid worsening urban smog — a local public‑health choice not foregrounded in UK summaries.

On the meteorology side TVN24 highlights acute regional risk: forecasters warn of nights around −20 to −25°C in parts of eastern Poland (Lubelskie), substantial snow in southern/mountain areas (locally tens of centimetres) and a following Atlantic low that could bring a thaw and freezing‑rain (gołoledź) risk — a detailed, region‑by‑region timeline that supplements UK Met Office bulletins.

Germany
Sources
Tagesschau (ARD)

Tagesschau focuses on transport and regional variation: Deutsche Bahn suspended long‑distance services in northern Germany, several states cancelled in‑person school attendance and the German Weather Service (DWD) issued high‑level storm and snow warnings for large areas. The emphasis is on concrete disruption to rail, road and schools rather than only national warning colours.

Tagesschau also noted local nuance — some forecasts overestimated snowfall in Berlin/Brandenburg and there were early accidents linked to black ice (a bus slid in Baden‑Württemberg) — details that temper broad‑brush UK coverage.

Belgium
Sources
RTBF Belgium

RTBF gives granular local impact: it reports dozens to hundreds of emergency interventions (about 30 in Brussels, ~70 in Hainaut Occidental, ~100 in Brabant wallon), early school dismissals in Liège and provincial bans on trucks over 13 m on some routes — immediate municipal measures emphasised over national warning language.

A separate RTBF dispatch details winter‑maintenance scale in Brussels: more than 900,000 kg of salt and 250,000 L of brine spread, roughly 500 km of cycle lanes treated and four salters working day and night — an operational scale‑of‑response not reported in most UK headlines.

Netherlands
Sources
NOS Netherlands

NOS stresses north–south contrasts and model uncertainty for the Netherlands: most places had 5–10 cm of fresh snow, but model differences mean the north could see renewed heavy snow while the south gets rain; a sharp overnight freeze (around −10°C) is possible. Coverage focuses on local forecast splits and readiness rather than only national warning colours.

Norway
Sources
NRK Norway (State)

NRK highlights Arctic extremes at home: Karasjok recorded −41.4°C (the coldest there since 2017). NRK also carried yellow alerts for parts of Østlandet with 10–25 cm of snow forecast and reported that warnings were adjusted downward in amount while the alert status remained — an emphasis on both record lows and evolving local advisories.

Sweden
Sources
SVT Sweden (State)

SVT reports life in Tornedalen where temperatures approached −40°C (around −37°C): reporting focuses on how residents cope — heavy firewood consumption (one local cited up to ~1.5 m³), routine stove maintenance to prevent frozen pipes and reduced sleep to tend heating — a human‑scale angle largely absent from UK roundups.

Finland
Sources
Helsingin Sanomat, Yle Finland (State)

Helsingin Sanomat reports a Savukoski low of −41.5°C — the first sub‑40°C reading this winter — and warns the cold may deepen, with Lapland staying bitterly cold through the weekend. Yle complements that with wide photographic coverage that frames the extreme northern cold alongside storm impacts elsewhere in Europe.
