After 398 bids across the UK, the Department for Culture, Media and Sport announced a 15-town shortlist for the UK's first Town of Culture, including Strabane.

The scheme is meant to put smaller places in the spotlight and lift local pride. It is also intended to encourage investment in arts and culture.

Strabane reacts to shortlist

Strabane's place on the shortlist drew a mixed reaction. Ciara McCay said the nomination felt bittersweet because the Alley Theatre is closed, even though the town is being recognised in a positive way. Emer Maguire said the closure had upset audiences and that Strabane still needs sustained arts investment.

She said the venue is high-spec and that the town has produced many artists. Maguire said investment could lead to workshops, rehearsal rooms, small venues or festivals. It could also leave a lasting legacy by helping young people stay creative. Martin Gallan said Strabane has major historical and cultural significance and that bad press often hides its talent. He said the town has a lot of musical talent and deserves better recognition.

How the contest works

The competition is split into small, medium and large population brackets. Small towns have fewer than 20,000 residents, medium towns sit between 20,000 and 75,000, and large towns are above 75,000. It will run alongside UK City of Culture 2029.

Each shortlisted town will receive £60,000 ($80,320) to develop a fuller bid. An independent judging panel will choose one finalist from each category early next year, and the overall winner will receive £3 million ($4.02 million) while runners-up get £250,000 ($334,666).

Other towns respond

Other towns and councils responded with pride, disappointment and determination to keep building on their bids.

  • Bury: Councillor Charlotte Morris said culture sits at the heart of the town's identity and future, and that Bury is ready to build further on that strength. A Bury Council spokesperson said the town was disappointed by the outcome but still proud of the bid, which showcased heritage, creativity and a proud track record, and said it will keep looking for future opportunities.
  • Halifax: Calderdale Council said the town's cultural scene continues to thrive and that it will keep backing people, places and creativity. It also described the bid as ambitious. Halifax councillors said the town had presented a strong case, pointing to the advantages created by its Year of Culture.
  • Lymm: Lymm treated the result as a pause rather than an ending. Cllr Graham Gowland said the town was disappointed but would keep celebrating local culture and come back next year. The bid team said the work already done will support future cultural plans and help keep local culture going, adding that the application took passion and hard work.
  • Guildford: Guildford Borough Council said the town has extraordinary cultural wealth and wants that wealth connected to all communities through a lasting legacy. The council said its bid is built on heritage, creativity and innovation.
  • Ulverston: Ulverston Town Council said the bid had brought organisations together. It said it will keep supporting heritage, culture and creative communities. The council said the process had strengthened the town's cultural plans.

Leith split over shortlisting

A similar split emerged in Leith, where the shortlisting prompted debate over tourism, transport and the area's future.

Leith produced a sharply split reaction. Adrian Graham said the area has too many tourist attractions and that it should keep a mix of development and industry. He said tourism has become too dominant. Jharna Adwani said parking and commuting problems are already hurting customers and staff, and that the £60,000 ($80,320) bid-development funding would not ease those pressures.

By contrast, Hollie said Leith's new developments and community feel make it attractive, and that is why she moved there. She hoped the competition could bring more young people and more life. January Watson said Leith has talented creative people, art studios and music events, so the competition would mean a lot.

Wales hopes for a winner

The wider reaction then carried into Wales, where politicians framed the shortlist as a chance for a Welsh winner.

In Wales, Jo Stevens said Port Talbot and Pontypridd deserve celebration because of their rich heritage and that she wants to see a Welsh town crowned. She pointed to industrial heritage, actors and the Welsh national anthem as reasons for pride.

Stephen Kinnock said Port Talbot and the Afan Valley sit in the top 5%, and a £3 million ($4.02 million) prize could help renew industrial heritage, support creatives and nurture local communities. He said the funding could further develop and celebrate the Afan Valley.

The overall winner will be named in early 2027. The winning town is then set to host a season of cultural events in 2028.
