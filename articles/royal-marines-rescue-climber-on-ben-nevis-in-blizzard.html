Royal Marines rescue stranded climber on Ben Nevis in -20°C blizzard. Royal and Dutch marines taking part in Exercise Highland Warrior said they encountered a man near Ben Nevis's summit during a blizzard, improvised a stretcher and escorted him down, handing him to emergency services as a precaution; Police Scotland, in an official statement, offered a different account of the episode.

What rescuers say

Royal Marines Reserve members described finding the casualty curled up in the snow near the summit with ice crusted on his eyebrows, hat and gloves — signs of prolonged exposure to freezing conditions.

They reported that he had become separated from two companions and could not say where they were; he did not have crampons, slipped on an icy slope, and one walking pole was bent while the other appeared collapsed.

Using kit from their mountain training, the marines said they improvised a stretcher from a waterproof bivvy sack, ropes, a mat and a thick jacket so the casualty could be hauled down the slope.

According to the rescuers, they then moved him down to the snowline and on to the foot of the 1,345-metre mountain, where he was handed to emergency services as a precaution.

If we hadn’t seen him, he certainly would not have survived.
— Sgt Onno Lankhaar, section commander, Dutch marines

Sgt Onno Lankhaar was part of a Dutch contingent training with Royal Marine Reserve units in Scotland; Colour Sergeant McKenzie of RMR Scotland said the week's drills and preparation directly enabled the party to deal with the situation confidently and safely.

Conditions and gear

Colour Sergeant McKenzie described the wind as biting and relentless and said he had estimated gusts to be around 40 mph; combined with summit temperatures this put the wind chill in the region of -15C to -20C, making any exposed skin sting almost instantly.

Conflicting official accounts

Police Scotland said it was called at around 1.40pm on Friday, 23 January, 2026 to a report of concern for a man at the summit of Ben Nevis; in its statement it said a group of walkers assisted him down the mountain and that he did not require medical assistance.

That account differs materially from the marines' description: the Royal Marines Reserve and the Dutch Korps Mariniers described discovering, treating and carrying the casualty and said they handed him to emergency services as a precaution.

The Royal Dutch Navy added that the man appeared overtired and confused, had no compass or map and was suffering cramps in his legs so he could not walk down himself.

Defence sources and rescuers also said the casualty was later reunited with his fellow walkers at the foot of the mountain.

Why accounts may differ

One plausible reconciliation is that the various statements are describing different phases: the marines' account focuses on discovery high on the mountain and an early stretcher-based evacuation, while Police Scotland's statement and the reunion report describe later assistance lower on the slope.

Colour Sergeant McKenzie characterised the week's mountain training as decisive, saying the skills and drills practised during Exercise Highland Warrior directly enabled the party to act safely and confidently when it mattered.

State of play

Across the statements, sources agree a man was located close to Ben Nevis's summit in severe winter conditions and that he was taken down the mountain; the Royal Marines Reserve and Dutch Korps Mariniers say they discovered, treated and escorted him and handed him to emergency services as a precaution, while Police Scotland says walkers assisted him down and he did not require medical treatment, so the precise sequence of who carried the casualty and when a formal handover took place remains disputed between the agencies.
