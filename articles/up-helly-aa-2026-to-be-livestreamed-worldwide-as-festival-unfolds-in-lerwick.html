Up Helly Aa 2026 will be broadcast online: viewers worldwide can watch via uphellyaa.com, Promote Shetland’s YouTube and Facebook channels, and on the BBC.

How to watch

Promote Shetland will stream the torchlit procession live on uphellyaa.com and on its YouTube channel from 7pm. The same livestream will be available on Promote Shetland’s Facebook page from 7.25pm. The BBC listed a 19:30 start for its stream and The Scotsman reported pre-event coverage could be available from 6.45pm on YouTube.

Jarl, squad and procession

Lynden Nicolson is this year’s Guizer Jarl after an 18-year wait to lead the torch-lit procession. To earn the honour candidates must be elected to the Up Helly Aa Committee and serve on it for 16 years. That 18-year wait is said to be the longest in the event’s 145-year history and his appointment makes him the oldest serving Guizer Jarl. On the night, hundreds of torchbearers will follow the 58-year-old meter technician on a parade through Lerwick.

His Jarl Squad comprises 55 adults and 10 children, with the youngest member a six-year-old grandniece.

The festival culminates with the burning of a replica galley that the Jarl Squad built themselves.

Costumes and craft

The metal-and-leather suits weigh almost two stone and cost each squad member close to £3,000 to create. Nicolson said each breastplate uses 51 individual pieces of leather (3,315 for the squad), each suit requires 4,860 punched holes and about 9,720 hand stitches, and the project used roughly 10km of red thread in total. He added that the helmets drew inspiration from the Kurgan helmet in the film Highlander and described the design and production process.

Volunteers are responsible for building the galley boat and produced more than 1,000 torches for the event. At about 7.30pm a signal will be fired over the town hall to signal the lighting of more than 900 torches.

Voices from the festival

The livestream allows people who may never have the opportunity to visit Shetland in person to experience the atmosphere and community spirit that define the festival.
— Adam Civico, Head of content marketing, Promote Shetland

The Shetland Times forecast that the interest generated by the stream means Up Helly Aa could once again be watched by millions worldwide, shining a spotlight on Shetland.

Eighteen years is a long time for me, my family and my wife Paula, who has been brilliant through this,
— Lynden Nicolson, Guizer Jarl

Nicolson framed the role as being about community and family, saying it brings people together in the dark nights and gives them something to do. He also speculated that people would be surprised.

It shows you how happy it makes your community.
— Frankie Walterson, Junior Guizer Jarl

Frankie Walterson, the Junior Guizer Jarl, said the festival is just amazing and that it shows how happy it makes the community.

Disputed reports and travel disruption

Passengers said a Balmoral cruise call for Up Helly Aa was cancelled because of the weather. Some passengers also reported an outbreak of norovirus on board the Balmoral. By contrast a passenger told The Shetland Times those reports had been exaggerated, saying many unwell people were suffering seasickness, there was no quarantine and visitors still enjoyed excursions.

Access and safety

No members of the public or press are permitted into the galley-burning site at King George V playing fields.

History and scale

Up Helly Aa in its present form began taking shape around 1870 and later incorporated Viking elements such as the galley burning. It is Shetland’s largest fire festival, held on the last Tuesday of January to celebrate the islands' Norse heritage. More than 40 squads and hundreds of torchbearers join the evening procession through Lerwick.

State of play

Across reports, Up Helly Aa 2026 is confirmed as a torchlit procession through Lerwick led by Guizer Jarl Lynden Nicolson that will culminate in the ceremonial burning of a replica galley built by the Jarl Squad. Passengers reported a Balmoral cruise call was cancelled because of the weather and some passengers said a norovirus outbreak had occurred aboard, while another passenger told The Shetland Times those reports were exaggerated and blamed seasickness, leaving the precise facts unclear. The livestream is scheduled across multiple channels this evening and, as the Shetland Times forecast, the interest could mean millions of viewers worldwide while organisers and broadcasters provide live coverage as the festival unfolds.
