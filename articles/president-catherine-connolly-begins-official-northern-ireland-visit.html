President Catherine Connolly begins official Northern Ireland visit

President Catherine Connolly has arrived in Belfast to begin a three-day official visit to Northern Ireland. She arrived on Wednesday. She said the trip fulfils a commitment she made at her inauguration that Northern Ireland would be her first official visit as president.

Stormont reception

At Stormont Castle, Connolly was greeted by First Minister Michelle O'Neill and Deputy First Minister Emma Little-Pengelly; the three shook hands and posed for photographs. O'Neill said Connolly's arrival showed she was keeping her pledge and said she expected the two leaders would enjoy a constructive working relationship. Little-Pengelly called the meeting a positive engagement and underlined her support for building cooperative ties across the executive.

University speech and Bloody Sunday meeting

Connolly then spoke to students and business leaders at Ulster University in Belfast. She praised the progress that has followed the long peace process and said that justice for the Bloody Sunday victims remained unfulfilled.

After her speech, she held a private meeting with relatives of those killed and injured on Bloody Sunday in 1972, but outlets reported different attendance figures: the BBC said she met fifty relatives, while RTE said around thirty-five family members attended for roughly forty minutes. Tony Doherty and other family representatives told reporters they were pleased to meet the president and described the visit as a sign of political maturity.

Why the visit matters

Connolly made clear that choosing Northern Ireland as her first official destination was an explicit pledge from her inauguration. Her speeches mixed symbolic reference and local outreach: at Ulster University she acknowledged unresolved grievances connected to Bloody Sunday, while in Derry she highlighted the city's role from the civil rights era through the Good Friday Agreement as central to reconciliation.

Derry visits and community outreach

In Derry Connolly praised the city's transition from conflict to peace and expressly referenced the contribution of leaders such as John Hume, David Trimble and Martin McGuinness. She said the city remained indebted to those figures and noted John Hume's Nobel Peace Prize on display. She toured community venues including the Siege Museum, An Cultúrlann, the Museum of Free Derry, the Nerve Centre and Youth Action NI. On her final day she met young people at St Columb's Park House to hear about youth activism and issues such as climate change and foreign affairs. Young participants said she listened attentively and encouraged activism, and Youth Action NI suggested the engagement could help strengthen cross-community ties.

Confrontation and reactions

you're in our country
— Gregory Campbell, DUP MP

At the Guildhall in Derry the exchange was initiated by DUP MP Gregory Campbell. He interrupted Connolly's address. He later said the president apologised for not making proper references. Campbell insisted Northern Ireland would remain part of the United Kingdom, warned against attempts to rewrite the past and said he planned to attend a debate in Dublin that evening. He reiterated that Northern Ireland was not leaving the United Kingdom now or in the future. He said he believed the Derry remarks failed to reach across the divide and lacked balance, and he judged the visit a negative for relations between the Republic and the North on the day while saying future visits might improve ties. SDLP councillor Catherine McDaid described Connolly's speech as very gracious and called Campbell's interruption a bit embarrassing. Campbell also speculated the differing tones between the Belfast and Derry events might have been deliberate, accidental or an oversight by the Department of Foreign Affairs.

Responses across the political divide

Reactions recorded by outlets showed a split in emphasis among political figures. Gregory Campbell said Connolly's Derry speech did not attempt to reach across the divide and was not balanced, and he said the visit was a negative for relations between the Republic and the North on that day. By contrast, Catherine McDaid called the president's remarks very gracious and described Campbell's intervention as a bit embarrassing. Families and local activists, represented by voices such as Tony Doherty, welcomed the chance to meet the president and saw the visit as a sign of political maturity.

Political consequences and by-elections

Senior sources and the Taoiseach indicated that by-elections in Galway West and Dublin Central are likely in May, with Friday 22 May suggested as a possible polling day. TheJournal.ie reported that date has been earmarked and said the Dáil could have two new TDs before the summer recess.

State of play

Across outlets there is agreement that President Catherine Connolly arrived in Belfast to begin a three-day official visit and that she met Stormont leaders and spoke at Ulster University. Reporting diverges over attendance at a private meeting with Bloody Sunday relatives: the BBC reported fifty while RTE put the figure at about thirty-five. Family representatives said they were pleased and described the meeting as a sign of political maturity, while young participants said Connolly listened and encouraged activism; DUP MP Gregory Campbell has alleged the Derry speech failed to reach across the divide, said the visit harmed relations on the day and later said she apologised, and he has speculated whether the tonal differences were deliberate, accidental or an administrative oversight. Senior sources have signalled by-elections are likely in May, with 22 May suggested as a possible polling day, and local groups said Connolly's outreach to young people and community projects could strengthen cross-community ties in the months ahead.
