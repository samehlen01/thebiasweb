This article is about Australian Open players condemn broadcast of Coco Gauff outburst and call for privacy. Video of Coco Gauff angrily breaking a racket in a players' area after her Australian Open quarter-final was captured and shown to television audiences and circulated on social media. Gauff said she had tried to find somewhere away from cameras and urged conversations about player privacy. Tennis Australia said the cameras in warm-up and cool-down areas were intended to give fans a deeper connection and that it would work with players to find solutions. The footage followed Gauff's 6-1, 6-2 quarter-final loss, a match in which she committed 26 unforced errors.

Gauff made 26 unforced errors and the match lasted about 59 minutes.

The match statistics and the off-court footage together sharpened a debate about the limits of broadcast access.

Players' reactions

Several players publicly sided with Gauff on privacy. Iga Swiatek, offering analysis, said players are being observed 'like animals in the zoo' and called for some privacy. Novak Djokovic, also offering analysis, said he empathises with Gauff but that content is vital and he doubts cameras will be removed. Serena Williams defended Gauff's emotional reaction as passion. Jamie Murray said Gauff 'rightfully took it out on the racket at the end of that.' Laura Robson said 'Not that we advocate this' and suggested Gauff might have chucked the racket on the court to release tension. Amanda Anisimova said she went straight to the locker room after a loss to avoid being filmed and called the posting of private moments 'tough.' Jessica Pegula echoed Gauff's concerns and insisted more privacy is needed for players.

I went somewhere where I thought they wouldn't broadcast it, but obviously they did. Maybe some conversations can be had, because I feel like at this tournament the only private place we have is the locker room.
— Coco Gauff

Gauff told reporters she thought she had moved somewhere out of sight before smashing her racket and lamented that the act was captured and shown widely. The clip's circulation has prompted players to discuss whether some off-court zones should be camera-free.

Organizer response and context

Tennis Australia said: 'Striking the right balance between showcasing the personalities and skills of the players, while ensuring their comfort and privacy is a priority for the Australian Open.' The organisation added it would work with players to find solutions. When the Australian Open first brought cameras into player areas in 2019 Novak Djokovic described it as 'Big Brother'. Sky News reported that smashing rackets on court nearly always results in a fine from the tennis authorities.

I'm surprised that we have no cameras while we are taking a shower. That's probably the next step.
— Novak Djokovic

Novak Djokovic said he empathises with Gauff but that content is vital and he doubts cameras will be removed. He also joked that cameras might one day film players even in the shower, a speculative aside.

Multiple outlets said the broadcast of Gauff's off-court outburst is likely to spark broader conversations about player privacy at tournaments. Commentators argued cameras at Grand Slams may be scaled back in some areas but are unlikely to be removed entirely because behind-the-scenes content is valuable to fans and broadcasters. An increasing number of players are producing their own video content to control how they are shown to fans. The Daily Mail speculated it remains to be seen whether Gauff will pursue a broader campaign to secure more privacy for players on the ATP and WTA tours.

State of play

There is agreement that footage of Gauff's off-court outburst was filmed and widely disseminated. Players have alleged the filming invaded privacy and urged limits on where cameras operate. Organisers say they will work with players and that cameras exist to give fans a deeper connection. Commentators predict the episode will spark wider conversations about player privacy at tournaments. It remains disputed whether practical changes will follow beyond talks, and the next step is for those consultations to take place.
