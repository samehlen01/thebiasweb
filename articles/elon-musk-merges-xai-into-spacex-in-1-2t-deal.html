Elon Musk has merged his AI research arm xAI into SpaceX, bringing the privately held AI group and the launch-and-satellite company together under one corporate roof. SpaceX has acquired xAI. Bloomberg reported an all-stock transaction valuing the combined company at $1.25 trillion; Axios put the value at roughly $1.2 trillion. The move followed a Federal Communications Commission filing in which SpaceX sought permission to launch up to 1 million satellites described as "orbital data centers."

Deal mechanics and filings

SpaceX's Federal Communications Commission application describes the hardware as a distributed compute layer and seeks authority to put 1 million satellites into orbit that will operate as "orbital data centers." The filing, reported by Ars Technica and CNBC, would represent an order-of-magnitude addition to the company's space assets if regulators approved it.

To put the proposed buildout in context, SpaceX's Starlink system already operates more than 9,000 satellites in orbit and serves roughly 9 million customers, according to reporting; adding up to a million more would be a transformational expansion of on-orbit infrastructure.

SpaceX has acquired xAI to form the most ambitious, vertically-integrated innovation engine on (and off) Earth,
— Elon Musk

Musk framed the move as joining compute, data and launch capability within one organisation. The brief statement and accompanying filings and corporate records drew rapid questions from journalists and industry watchers about size, governance and national-security exposure.

Valuation, IPO and financing

Valuation estimates differ across outlets. Bloomberg's reporting put the combined private group at the higher end, while Axios reported a lower figure. SpaceX is still planning to do an IPO later this year, and Bloomberg linked the company to a mid-June initial public offering that could raise as much as $50bn. TechCrunch added that "It's unclear whether the merger will affect that timeline." PitchBook analyst Emily Zheng noted that "Consolidating these companies ahead of an IPO allows SpaceX to present a differentiated, capital-efficient growth narrative to public investors." Separately, Tesla announced it was investing about $2 billion into xAI last month.

xAI's platform assets

xAI acquired social media platform X in an all-stock deal last March, a move Musk said was intended to combine the companies’ data, models, compute, distribution and talent.

Government ties and regulatory questions

Both companies have major contracts with US government agencies such as NASA and the Department of Defense. Reporting notes SpaceX holds tens of billions of dollars worth of federal government contracts. SpaceX's Starshield unit also collaborates with military and intelligence agencies on specialised services.

Reporters and analysts warned that the agreement could draw scrutiny from regulators and investors over governance, valuation and conflicts of interest. Executives at SpaceX and xAI did not respond to requests for comment on whether the merger may require regulatory review, for example, by the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS). Several outlets also flagged that the deal could draw regulator and investor scrutiny and raise governance / conflict-of-interest questions.

xAI’s finances and shareholder impacts

Reporting says xAI has been burning cash at pace: according to The Information, xAI told investors it burned about $9.5 billion through the first nine months of 2025, and other coverage characterised the company as currently burning around $1 billion per month. Axios reported that xAI shareholders effectively get a lifeline through the deal. Tim Farrar of TMF Associates framed the story as primarily a liquidity rescue: For now, xAI has a much more pressing need: cash.

Technical ambitions and sceptics

Musk wrote, "In the long term, space-based AI is obviously the only way to scale." He also wrote, "My estimate is that within 2 to 3 years, the lowest cost way to generate AI compute will be in space." Microsoft president Brad Smith pushed back on the timescale, saying, "I’ll be surprised if people move from land to low-Earth-orbit," a remark several observers cited when expressing scepticism about the two-to-three-year window.

Content safety and platform risk

Separately, xAI’s Grok conversational tools and image features have raised moderation concerns. In recent weeks, the European Commission and UK watchdog Ofcom both launched investigations into X over concerns Grok was used to create sexualised images. xAI said it had imposed restrictions on Grok users to limit image editing.

Consequences and near-term outlook

Musk framed the longer-term upside as funding and enabling off-world bases, writing that "The capabilities we unlock by making space-based data centers a reality will fund and enable self-growing bases on the Moon, an entire civilization on Mars and ultimately expansion to the Universe." He also said the cadence of frequent, large Starship launches would be a "forcing function" to improve Starship performance.

At the same time, reporters emphasised that The agreement could draw scrutiny from regulators and investors over governance, valuation and conflicts of interest. TechCrunch reiterated that "It's unclear whether the merger will affect that timeline" for SpaceX's planned IPO, and a number of outlets noted the transaction could complicate the planned public offering.

State of play

Across reporting, some core facts are straightforward: SpaceX has acquired xAI, and the company filed with the FCC seeking up to 1 million satellites as proposed orbital data centers. Where accounts diverge is valuation: Bloomberg reported valuing the new company at $1.25 trillion while Axios said the deal values the combined company at roughly $1.2 trillion, and they also differ on how regulators and investors will respond. The agreement could draw scrutiny from regulators and investors over governance, valuation and conflicts of interest. Executives at SpaceX and xAI did not respond to requests for comment on whether the merger may require regulatory review, for example, by the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS). Reporting indicates xAI has been burning substantial cash, about $9.5 billion through the first nine months of 2025 and around $1 billion per month, which has made the acquisition an apparent lifeline to some shareholders. SpaceX is still planning to do an IPO later this year. It's unclear whether the merger will affect that timeline.
