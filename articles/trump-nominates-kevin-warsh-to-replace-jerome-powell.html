Trump nominates Kevin Warsh to replace Jerome Powell as Federal Reserve chair. The nomination must be confirmed by the Senate; if confirmed he would succeed Jerome Powell when Powell's term ends in May. The announcement arrived as the Department of Justice opened an inquiry into Jerome Powell over allegations tied to a major renovation, a development that several senators say will shape the confirmation timetable.

Who is Kevin Warsh?

Warsh is a familiar figure inside financial circles who previously served on the Federal Reserve's Board of Governors from 2006 to 2011. That span covered a politically charged era for U.S. monetary policy and places Warsh among a small group of officials who have held a seat on the Fed's governing board in the last two decades.

Observers note that during the financial crisis Warsh served as the Fed's principal ambassador to Wall Street, making use of relationships he had developed while working at Morgan Stanley. Those connections were frequently referenced by contemporaneous accounts of Fed outreach to markets during the worst days of the crisis.

In the years since leaving the Board, Warsh has held academic and private‑sector roles: he is a lecturer at the Stanford Graduate School of Business and has taken positions with firms including UPS, the e‑commerce company Coupang and the Duquesne Family Office; his wife is an heiress to the Estee Lauder fortune.

Accounts of Warsh's policy instincts are not uniform: he was historically described as a monetary‑policy hawk during his earlier Fed tenure, generally associated with a desire to restrain inflation, while other reporting says that in recent years his public positions have sounded more in sync with President Trump and the White House's calls for lower borrowing costs.

I think the Fed has done a very good job of blaming others for their mistakes. It's been very popular to blame the president because he's being so mean to them. Well, most of the Fed's mistakes are because of choices they've made.
— Kevin Warsh

In that public remark Warsh criticised the Fed for shifting blame and argued that the institution had weakened its case by trying to do too much rather than keeping inflation control at the center of its mission, an explicit signal of the approach he says he would prioritise.

Politics, the DOJ probe and the confirmation hurdle

The nomination comes amid heightened political friction: Senator Thom Tillis has said he will block any Fed nominees until the Justice Department resolves its probe into Jerome Powell and the Fed renovation. That stance ties the confirmation calendar to developments in the DOJ's inquiry.

The Department of Justice launched an investigation this month into Jerome Powell over alleged false statements about a reported $2.5 billion renovation of the Fed's headquarters. Powell's current term as chair is due to end in May, but some reports note he could nonetheless remain on the Fed's governing board as a governor until January 2028, a distinction that would limit how quickly a president could reshape the board.

President Donald Trump has repeatedly criticised Powell after policy disagreements, at times using derogatory language and even saying he would like to remove him. That public history is central to why some lawmakers and analysts worry about the optics of the nomination and the Fed's independence.

Senator Mark Warner said it is difficult to trust that any Chair of the Federal Reserve selected by this president will be able to act with the independence required of the position, a remark that captures a core political concern raised across the aisle.

Markets and the immediate reaction

Early trading showed the dollar strengthening by about 0.5% against a basket of currencies after reports that Warsh was expected to be the president's pick, while U.S. stocks opened with modest losses as investors digested the political and policy implications.

News wires recorded a roughly 4.8% drop in gold, citing a fall to $5,132 an ounce in early trades after the nomination was reported. Market commentators read those moves as an early signal that investors were not pricing in an immediate campaign of deep rate cuts.

How experts see the pick

Not all analysts reached the same conclusion about what Warsh will do in office. Renaissance Macro Research warned that Warsh's apparent dovish turn may be opportunistic and designed to appease the president rather than reflect a genuine ideological change.

By contrast, Stephen Brown of Capital Economics told market participants that Warsh looks like a relatively safe appointment who could allay fears the White House might install a full‑blown loyalist at the Fed, a view that seemed to calm some investor nerves on the first trading day after the reports.

Former central banker Mark Carney offered pointed praise for the selection, calling Warsh a strong choice to lead the Federal Reserve at a critical moment for the global economy.

David Wessel of the Brookings Institution said he expects Warsh may be prone to cut interest rates and noted that markets have been pricing in one or two rate reductions later this year; he added that the ultimate test of the new chair's independence will probably come in 2027 and 2028.

Nick Kennedy at Lloyds Banking Group said Warsh was his preferred candidate, pointing to a record that is on balance more hawkish on rates but — in Kennedy's view — academically aligned with the administration on shrinking the central bank's balance sheet.

Consequences and what to watch

Analysts warned of tangible consequences if investors conclude the Fed would no longer act independently under a Warsh chair: David Wessel said that perceived loss of independence could push up U.S. borrowing costs and that even a modest rise in yields would increase the interest bill for taxpayers.

The confirmation path will be watched closely: the nominee requires Senate approval, and multiple reports said the timetable for that approval may be complicated by Senator Tillis's declared holds tied to the Justice Department's inquiry into Powell.

Market moves after the nomination were interpreted by commentators as suggesting that investors did not immediately expect Warsh to act as a champion for deep and rapid rate cuts, an interpretation that will be tested if and when policy decisions near the Fed's next meetings shift from guidance to concrete action.

State of play

President Trump has nominated Kevin Warsh to be Fed chair, but the appointment still requires Senate confirmation. At the same time, the Department of Justice inquiry into Jerome Powell over alleged false statements about a $2.5 billion renovation and Senator Thom Tillis's announced holds mean the confirmation timetable is uncertain. Analysts remain divided about Warsh's likely policy trajectory: some described him as a relatively safe choice while others warned his recent dovish rhetoric may be opportunistic, and several commentators said the real test of his independence will probably arrive in 2027 and 2028.
