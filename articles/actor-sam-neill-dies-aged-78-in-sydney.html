Actor Sam Neill died in Sydney on Monday, 13 July 2026, aged 78. His family said he was surrounded by loved ones.

His screen career stretched from Sleeping Dogs and My Brilliant Career to The Piano, Jurassic Park and Peaky Blinders. The Piano and Jurassic Park brought him widespread international attention in 1993.

Early life and career

He was born in Northern Ireland, where his father was serving as an officer with the Royal Irish Fusiliers. The family relocated to New Zealand when he was seven, and he later changed his name from Nigel to Sam after finding that his new school already had several boys with his given name.

His early screen work included Sleeping Dogs, My Brilliant Career and the cult horror Possession. He said My Brilliant Career was one of the most important roles of his early career.

He later played a Belfast police chief in Peaky Blinders.

He was appointed an Officer of the Order of the British Empire in 1991 for services to acting, and later received a knighthood.

Illness, personal life and family

He had publicly discussed treatment for stage-three angioimmunoblastic T-cell lymphoma, a rare type of non-Hodgkin lymphoma and a form of blood cancer. He had been undergoing chemotherapy since March 2022, and the illness prompted a short break from acting. He later had CAR T-cell therapy. In April 2026 he said a scan showed no cancer in his body.

He established Two Paddocks in 1993 next to his friend Roger Donaldson, the director of Sleeping Dogs.

I wanted to produce a good pinot noir that would, at the very least, be enjoyed by my family and friends.
— Sam Neill

At Two Paddocks, he lived and wrote his memoir Did I Ever Tell You This? while undergoing treatment. The memoir gave him a reason to live, he said.

Family was a recurring theme in his comments, and he had spoken at length about the importance of family.

I’ve got a slightly unusual family; it’s more extended than most.
— Sam Neill

That extended family included four children, Tim, Elena, Maiko and Andrew, and eight grandchildren. His eldest son, Andrew, had been put up for adoption in 1969 before they found each other again after 25 years.

Legacy and tributes

His wider public work also included TS Queen Mary. In 2022 he became Commonwealth Patron of TS Queen Mary at the request of Robbie Coltrane. The Friends of TS Queen Mary said he brought boundless enthusiasm and unswerving commitment to the role, while Iain Sim, chairman of TS Queen Mary, said he needed little persuading to take on the patronage. He said Neill loved ships, the sea and Scotland, and that he had brought heart to the cause. The TS Queen Mary restoration will continue, with his support remembered.

After his death, tributes came from friends, co-stars, politicians and fans.

Among collaborators, Steven Spielberg said Neill was exceptionally collaborative and that he loved making the Jurassic films with him. Laura Dern remembered him as a beloved lifelong friend, loyal, protective and loving.

Richard E Grant described him as an officer and a gentleman who had helped him through a very difficult time. Colin Trevorrow remembered him as deeply soulful and beautiful, saying his calm and strength lifted the people around him.

In New Zealand, Karl Urban called him an inspiration and a national treasure. Toni Collette described him as a hero, a legend, a sweetheart and a great friend. Jacinda Ardern described him as thoughtful, curious, decent and principled and said he was willing to fight injustice. The Department of Conservation also praised him as a fierce and passionate champion for the environment.

Christopher Luxon said Neill was one of New Zealand's greats and helped take the country's stories to the world, adding that his work would be watched and loved long after all of us.

On screen, he is expected to appear posthumously in Godzilla x Kong: Supernova and The Last Resort in 2027.

Australia
Sources
The Guardian Australia, SBS Australia, 7News Australia
I saw Sam at the beginning of this year with his partner Heather and my and his friend Griz. We stayed the night at his beautiful winery and celebrated what seemed like impossible good fortune; that he was cancer-free and could now imagine new chapters.
— Jane Campion

Australian outlets report Campion stayed at his Two Paddocks winery early in 2026 and later visited Neill at St Vincent’s Hospital in Sydney, bringing coffee, food and a watercolour set while he sketched. They emphasise his Central Otago ties—he lived in Earnscleugh valley, was patron of the community-run Central Cinema and of a local hospital, and supported community fundraisers—and say his death left a profound sense of loss in nearby towns. Coverage adds that after his recovery Neill campaigned for broader Australian access to CAR T‑cell therapy, advocated for an Aboriginal-flag–inspired Event Horizon spacesuit design and said he was bitterly disappointed by the 2023 Voice referendum. Australian reports also note a Silver Logie nomination for The Twelve (S3), which he hoped to attend on 16 August 2026, and identify The Fox (in which he voices a sweary magpie) among posthumous projects.

Western Europe
Sources
De Morgen (Belgium), La Vanguardia, SVT Sweden (State)
I am sick. Possibly I am dying.
— Sam Neill

Belgian and Spanish outlets add personal details absent from the UK synthesis: De Morgen recounts his farm animals named for co‑stars (a cow called Helena Bonham Carter and sheep named Jeff Goldblum and Laura Dern) and reports he leaves three ex‑wives (Lisa Harrow, Noriko Watanabe and Laura Tingle), four children and eight grandchildren; La Vanguardia reproduces blunt lines from his memoir and notes he had nearly a million Instagram followers and a private farm life in Sydney's Surry Hills.

De Morgen also reports Neill refused a knighthood in 2009 but accepted one in 2023 because he felt the end of his life was approaching. Swedish state TV supplies a Jurassic Park casting anecdote that Spielberg initially wanted Harrison Ford for Alan Grant, and quotes Ford as replying, "This isn't for me, mate."

Canada's Globe and Mail highlights several small‑screen roles less emphasised in UK summaries—Thomas Jefferson in the CBS miniseries Sally Hemings: An American Tragedy; Oklahoma sheriff John Bell Tyson in Apple TV+'s Invasion; and a 2024 role opposite Annette Bening in Apples Never Fall—and notes his habit of posting images of farm animals named after celebrity friends. The piece quotes Neill saying the difficult parts of cancer treatment made him more grateful for each day.
