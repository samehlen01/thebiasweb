Suicide bombing at Shiite mosque near Islamabad kills at least 31 worshippers, according to officials at the scene. A city official said the toll was 31 dead and 169 injured, while a separate police source put the wounded at more than 130; both said the blast came during Friday prayers. The explosion struck the Khadija al-Kubra / Imam Bargah Qasr-e-Khadijatul Kubra mosque in the Tarlai area on the outskirts of Islamabad as worshippers assembled for the main weekly congregational service. Images and witnesses later showed bloodied bodies and a chaotic scene inside and around the mosque.

Hospitals in Islamabad declared an emergency as they began to receive the wounded, and health authorities issued urgent appeals for blood donations. Staff at city hospitals were put on alert as medics received large numbers of casualties, and officials warned the figures were provisional while emergency teams treated the injured. No group immediately claimed responsibility for the blast, and initial official statements concentrated on rescue and medical response rather than attribution.

How the attack unfolded

Security sources and eyewitnesses said the attacker was stopped at the gate, fired at guards and then detonated explosives, according to accounts passed to news agencies; the explosion ripped through the mosque while it was full for Friday prayers and sent worshippers scrambling. Those who were there described chaos and a rapid emergency response, and multiple outlets relayed the same basic sequence in their early reporting.

The attacker was stopped at the gate and detonated himself.
— Security source, cited by AFP

The security-source account aligns with eyewitness remarks that shots were fired at the entrance, suggesting the bomber attempted to breach the mosque perimeter and detonated at the entry point rather than walking unchallenged into the main hall.

Scene, images and immediate response

Photographs and video from the site showed blood on the mosque floor and people trying to tend to the wounded, and reporters described a chaotic response as bystanders and emergency teams worked at the scene. Journalists also criticised mainstream outlets for a delay in carrying the full story while images and accounts circulated on social media.

Casualties and medical response

Doctors and emergency staff said they were treating blast and shrapnel wounds and that some patients were in serious condition; officials warned the death toll could rise. Health authorities urged the public to donate blood and to refrain from crowding hospital entrances so medics could work effectively.

31
Reported fatalities
City official reported 31 dead and 169 injured; a police source said 31 were killed and more than 130 were wounded.

Those contrasting injury totals, 169 in some reports and more than 130 in others, underline how early tallies can diverge in the immediate aftermath of mass-casualty incidents while hospitals and police reconcile lists and confirm identities.

Claims and counterclaims

Defence Minister Khawaja Asif publicly accused India and Afghanistan of being behind the attack and said the attacker had travelled to and from Afghanistan; his comments were issued as an allegation by a senior Pakistani official rather than as a verified finding.

Islamic Republic of Afghanistan condemns such attacks that violate the sanctity of sacred rituals and mosques and target worshippers and innocent people.
— Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Islamic Republic of Afghanistan

Afghanistan's foreign ministry condemned the strike, and the Taliban government has repeatedly denied Pakistan's accusations that Afghan-based actors were responsible, setting up an immediate diplomatic dispute between the two capitals.

Official reactions inside Pakistan

President Asif Ali Zardari condemned the bombing, calling the targeting of innocent civilians a 'crime against humanity'. Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif expressed deep grief, ordered a full investigation and said those responsible must be identified and punished. Deputy Prime Minister and Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar described the attack as a heinous crime and vowed to bring those responsible to justice. Interior Minister Mohsin Naqvi urged authorities to ensure the best possible medical care for the wounded.

Government leaders also instructed that all possible medical assistance be provided to the injured and vowed that those responsible would be identified and brought to justice as investigators collected evidence at the site and hospitals.

Journalist Nadir Gurmani of Dawn TV criticised mainstream media outlets for initially failing to carry the story while it circulated on social media, an observation echoed by other commentators about the speed of digital versus legacy reporting in the first hours.

Investigators and early lines of inquiry

Authorities have ordered a full investigation and said they were following multiple leads. No group immediately claimed responsibility. Analysts and some media reports suggested suspicion would likely fall on militant groups such as the Pakistani Taliban (TTP) or an Islamic State affiliate, though that remains speculative until forensic work or a claim of responsibility confirms a perpetrator.

Pakistan's defence minister suggested investigators check whether the suspect crossed into and back from Afghanistan. That line of inquiry has been disputed by Afghan officials, creating a tense cross-border diplomatic backdrop to the probe. Some reports also said the attacker may have travelled to and from Afghanistan.

State of play

Authorities agree that a blast at the Khadija al-Kubra / Imam Bargah Qasr-e-Khadijatul Kubra mosque in Tarlai killed at least 31 people during Friday prayers and wounded dozens, though outlets quoted different injury totals. Defence Minister Khawaja Asif has alleged links to India and Afghanistan and suggested the attacker travelled to and from Afghanistan, while Afghanistan's foreign ministry condemned the assault and the Taliban administration has repeatedly denied Pakistan's accusations. No group has yet claimed responsibility, and analysts say suspicion may fall on groups such as the Pakistani Taliban or Islamic State affiliates but that remains speculative; leaders have ordered a full investigation, instructed that medical assistance be provided to the injured and vowed to identify and punish those responsible as investigators work to corroborate evidence.

Pakistan
Sources
ARY News Pakistan, Express Tribune Pakistan

Pakistani outlets published new investigative details: ARY News cited government sources saying two brothers of the suicide bomber and four other individuals have been detained in connection with the blast.

The Express Tribune reported investigators have identified the attacker as Yasir Khan, a Peshawar resident who allegedly spent about five months in Afghanistan, received militant training there and may have links to Islamic State Khorasan Province; those links are described as preliminary while inquiries continue.

Express Tribune also noted immediate political fallout: Jamiat Ulema-e-Islam chief Maulana Fazlur Rehman said his party would support the Tehreek-e-Tahaffuz-e-Ain Pakistan’s Feb 8 wheel-jam and shutter-down protest and would align its programmes with that schedule.

The bombing at an Islamabad mosque earlier today is condemnable and India condoles the loss of life it has caused. It is unfortunate that, instead of seriously addressing the problems plaguing its social fabric, Pakistan should choose to delude itself by blaming others for its home-grown ills. India rejects any and every such allegation, which is as baseless as it is pointless.

India's foreign ministry condemned the attack but explicitly rejected Pakistani public allegations of external involvement, calling such claims baseless and pointless.
