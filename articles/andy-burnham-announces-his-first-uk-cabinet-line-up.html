Andy Burnham became prime minister after Sir Keir Starmer resigned and started naming his first government, with BBC Politics saying he entered office backed by 379 of Labour’s 403 MPs.

Burnham’s opening message was that the new administration would shift power away from Whitehall, widen public control over water and energy, build more council houses and keep the main income tax and National Insurance rates unchanged. The combination put the cost of living, housing and the reach of the state at the centre of his opening pitch.

In his first speech, Burnham called the change a circuit breaker moment and said his ten-year vision would be published in autumn. He also said his first instruction would be to end long-term rough sleeping and that a separate ten-year plan would follow later this year. Those remarks made rough sleeping and the longer vision look like the first markers of the new premiership.

Electricity VAT cut

Burnham's first fiscal move was to remove VAT from household electricity bills, taking the rate from 5% to 0% from 1 October. He said the cut would be funded by scrapping the digital ID scheme. That made the electricity pledge an immediate test of how the new government says it will pay for visible relief.

Darren Jones later said the Digital ID programme was unfunded. He added that ministers would have to explain how the government’s new policies would be paid for at the budget. That leaves the financing question hanging over one of Burnham’s quickest promises.

Cabinet and key appointments

John Healey was appointed chancellor. Ed Miliband became foreign secretary. Shabana Mahmood stayed home secretary. Wes Streeting took over defence from Dan Jarvis.

Yvette Cooper was appointed health secretary and is expected to lead Burnham’s national care plans. The move also puts her in charge of Burnham’s £18 billion ($24.1 billion) plan for a national care service. Louise Haigh became first secretary of state and chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster, running the Cabinet Office. Angela Rayner returned as housing secretary after leaving government over a stamp-duty issue. Lucy Powell took education while Bridget Phillipson moved to women and equalities. Jonathan Reynolds was given the new Business, Innovation, Science and Trade department. Miatta Fahnbulleh became energy secretary. Douglas Alexander stayed on as Scotland secretary. Kanishka Narayan joined the cabinet as AI minister. The Guardian also speculated that Wes Streeting’s influence on defence may be limited because John Healey in the Treasury will help decide the defence settlement.

Foreign policy reaction

Miliband said he would keep close ties with the EU and the United States, maintain Britain’s commitments to Nato and the United Nations, and fight for Britain’s interests and values of democracy, freedom and the rule of law. He also described the appointment as a great honour and privilege. White House sources were reported to be deeply concerned about his appointment. The Guardian speculated that his soft-left, net-zero positioning could spook financial markets.

Boris Johnson called Miliband an appalling choice and said his Syria stance had helped pave the way for Vladimir Putin’s aggression. Nile Gardiner described the appointment as disastrous and high-risk because of Trump criticism and net-zero politics. He also said Miliband’s record makes him a point of friction with Washington. Gardiner predicted the White House and Trump allies would remain deeply concerned and that tensions with Donald Trump were likely to rise. Kevin Hollinrake said the special relationship would be harder to manage. He argued that the Chagos deal should be scrapped.

Health, social care and business

Sir Ciarán Devane said the NHS needed clarity and continuity while it was already undergoing a major reorganisation. He warned that further changes could slow progress and distract from better care. He said the service cannot afford to keep shifting while it is trying to deliver. Sarah Woolnough said social-care reform should be at the top of Cooper’s in-tray. She argued that Burnham wants social care fixed on an NHS principle and that the review already under way means Cooper has to move quickly. Layla Moran argued that kicking social care into the long grass would cost the economy, carers and families. She said patients cannot be discharged properly without the right care, which is why treatment delays and A&E waits get longer when social care is sidelined.

Chris O'Shea said the electricity VAT cut was encouraging but argued that a targeted social tariff would be more sustainable. He said support should be focused on households that need it most. He also forecast that the cut should flow through to household bills. The Russell Group welcomed Jonathan Reynolds’ new Business, Innovation, Science and Trade department. It said the regional innovation focus should help put research, innovation and regional growth at the centre of national ambition. The PIE News also predicted that the new cabinet will be at pains to show it is cracking down on migration, reflecting rising anti-immigration sentiment.

Disputes and next tests

Burnham has installed a first cabinet after Sir Keir Starmer resigned and paired it with promises on devolution, public ownership and housebuilding. He has also said his first instruction would be to end long-term rough sleeping. In his first speech, Burnham called the change a circuit breaker moment. He says he will publish a fuller ten-year vision in autumn. Burnham said the electricity VAT cut is fully funded and would be funded by scrapping the digital ID scheme. Darren Jones said the digital ID programme is unfunded. He said the government would have to explain how new policies would be paid for at the budget. Miliband has said it is a great honour and privilege to serve as Foreign Secretary. White House sources were reported to be deeply concerned about his appointment.
