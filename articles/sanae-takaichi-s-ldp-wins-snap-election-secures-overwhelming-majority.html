Sanae Takaichi's LDP wins snap election, secures overwhelming majority

Sanae Takaichi's Liberal Democratic Party won a commanding majority in a snap lower house election held on February 8, 2026. Takaichi, Japan's first female prime minister, dissolved the lower house on Jan. 23 to call the election.

Exit polls and early counts

Public broadcaster NHK's exit polls projected the LDP would win between 274 and 328 of the lower house's 465 seats. NHK's exit poll also suggested the LDP together with its coalition partner, the Japan Innovation Party, could secure as many as 366 of the 465 seats. As counting progressed, The New York Times reported the LDP had won at least 289 seats as of late on election night.

There is a clear difference in scope between NHK's exit projections and the early tallies reported as counting advanced. NHK's numbers were an exit-poll estimate issued at the close of voting, while later tallies reported by outlets such as The New York Times reflected votes already counted. NHK's estimates therefore describe an expected range, and the running count provided by other outlets captured the unfolding reality as ballots were tallied.

Vote-day disruption and turnout

Heavy snowfall on Sunday disrupted travel and voting in several regions, forcing closures of transport links and likely depressing turnout. Japan's transport ministry said 37 train lines and 58 ferry routes were closed and 54 flights cancelled as of Sunday morning. Japan's Ministry of Internal Affairs reported turnout stood at 26.01 percent as of two hours before polls closed.

Pledges that shaped the campaign

A central thread of the campaign was direct relief for households and an activist fiscal stance. Takaichi pledged to suspend the eight percent sales or consumption tax on food. Her administration put forward a record $783 billion (£576 billion) budget for the next fiscal year and had earlier introduced a $135 billion (£99.3 billion) stimulus package to help households with rising living costs. Japan's Finance Ministry estimates that suspending certain consumption taxes could cost more than $30 billion (£22.1 billion) annually.

Markets and investors are concerned about how Takaichi will fund her tax-cut and spending promises and have reacted with worries about bonds, the yen and fiscal sustainability.

Reactions: markets, business and critics

Some established business groups welcomed a return to political steadiness. Yoshinobu Tsutsui, the head of Keidanren, welcomed the result as restoring political stability.

Analysts say a decisive LDP majority gives Takaichi the power to push through legislation, including large budgets and defence spending, with limited parliamentary opposition. With an absolute majority, she will likely be able to pass large budgets and expand defence and immigration measures with limited opposition. Financial markets may react by lifting equities while the yen and government bonds come under pressure as yields rise.

Essentially, she can push through any legislation she wants, whether it’s the record budget that was recently approved or defence spending,
— Mark, analyst (to Al Jazeera)

The comment to Al Jazeera underscored a common analytical line that control of the lower house would make parliamentary roadblocks less likely and give the cabinet latitude on both spending and security initiatives.

Domestic debate: rights and 'Sanamania'

Critics argue that Takaichi's tougher immigration, anti-espionage and related measures risk undermining civil rights. At the same time, coverage from several outlets identified a strong personal popularity for Takaichi, especially among younger voters, a phenomenon described in some reporting as 'Sanamania'.

Foreign policy and China

Observers and analysts said Beijing would not welcome Takaichi's victory and tensions with China were a prominent issue. The result could further escalate tensions with China and prompt additional diplomatic or economic backlash from Beijing.

Speculation and market risks

Analysts speculated that a strong mandate could give Takaichi an opportunity to move Japan away from its postwar pacifist posture and bolster military capabilities. Some commentators warned the combination of big tax cuts and heavy spending could trigger a bond-market crisis similar to the UK's 2022 episode. State-directed industrial initiatives and M&A activity could accelerate as the government seeks to create larger national champions.

What happens next

Analysts and parliamentary watchers expect the LDP to move quickly to draft and approve its record budget and any supplementary spending measures, using its numerical advantage to limit parliamentary obstruction. Financial market commentators said equities may rise while the yen and bond markets come under pressure as investors price in higher borrowing or fiscal strain. Suspending the consumption tax on food would reduce revenue and increase fiscal pressure on an already high-debt government. That point is underscored by the Finance Ministry's estimate that such a suspension could cost more than $30 billion (£22.1 billion) annually.

State of play

Reporting generally agrees that the snap lower house election was held on February 8, 2026. NHK exit polls projected a dominant showing for the LDP. Later tallies showed the party with at least 289 seats as counts advanced. Markets and many analysts warned the combination of big spending and tax-cut promises poses fiscal and market risks. Keidanren's leadership welcomed a return to political stability. Critics warned that tougher security and immigration drives could threaten civil liberties. What remains contested is the final, certified seat distribution and how quickly the government will reconcile fiscal promises with long-term debt pressures, while commentators and officials watch both market signals and possible diplomatic consequences from Beijing in the days ahead.

Japan
Sources
NHK World (State)
The Liberal Democratic Party is now virtually certain to reach 310 seats on its own — two‑thirds of the total.
— NHK World (live bulletin)

NHK's running count placed the LDP on 310 seats on its own — a single‑party two‑thirds majority in the 465‑seat lower house — a threshold that would allow the governing bloc to override the Upper House and open a formal pathway toward constitutional amendment. NHK also ran a live vote‑count programme featuring Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi (the broadcaster published the full Q&A) and posted constituency declarations such as LDP wins in Iwate's 3rd and Tokyo's 27th districts.

East Asia
Sources
Liberty Times (Taiwan), Newtalk (Taiwan)
We will not issue new government bonds and will secure funding while speeding up discussion of a two‑year zero rate on food consumption tax.
— Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi (post‑vote interview)

Taiwanese coverage relayed Takaichi's immediate policy commitments: no cabinet reshuffle for now, an expedited debate on a two‑year zero rate for the food consumption tax as a short‑term measure, and a longer‑term refundable tax‑credit scheme she said would take two to three years to implement.

Liberty Times and Newtalk published reactions from Taiwan's representative to Japan, Li Yi‑yang, who congratulated Takaichi and flagged a new 'Growth Strategy Headquarters' to steer investment into 17 strategic sectors — including AI, semiconductors, energy, aerospace and defence — framing the result as an opening for deeper industrial cooperation.

Local reporting also recorded party‑level consequences: the Democratic Progressive Party issued a formal congratulation; several Taiwanese outlets noted the LDP's single‑party seat total exceeded 300 (its highest since 1955); early counts showed Takaichi's strong personal result in Nara 2 (about 125,805 votes, ~85.2%), and Newtalk confirmed veteran centrist Katsuya Okada lost Mie's 3rd district — a defeat that, because he was not on the proportional list, cost him his Diet seat.

The prime minister called a snap election... and boy, has she had a big victory today.
