Two Powerful Earthquakes Hit Venezuela, Triggering Rescue and Political Dispute

Two major earthquakes ripped through Venezuela, striking 39 seconds apart on June 24. The first quake measured 7.2 near San Felipe and the second 7.5 near Yumare in north-central Venezuela, just after 6 p.m.

Later reports described the quakes as Venezuela's deadliest in more than two centuries, saying they flattened entire districts in the coastal state of La Guaira.

Casualty toll climbs

Early estimates put the death toll at nearly 600, with more than 4,000 injured.

Later reports said at least 920 were dead and more than 3,300 injured.

Government figures then said nearly 1,500 people were dead and tens of thousands were missing.

Another report said nearly 2,000 people were dead and thousands were missing.

By Saturday, the official count had reached 2,954 dead and 16,592 injured.

Later, Jorge Rodríguez said 4,333 people had died and 16,740 were injured.

International rescue crews arrive

The United Nations said hundreds of rescue workers from at least 16 countries had arrived in Venezuela.

Brian Richards and Gavin Brown joined a 68-member UK rescue team. They said reinforced concrete made rescues harder.

Deportees and hotel allegations

Venezuelan officials said dozens of recent U.S. deportees were being held at Hotel Santuario La Llanada in La Guaira when the earthquakes struck.

Survivors and Javier Salcedo alleged that the hotel doors were locked.

Survivors said the migrants inside were left to die as the building failed around them.

Victims and rescues

The disaster also killed the wife and two children of Argentine soccer player Lucas Trejo. The Venezuelan Football Federation announced the death of 18-year-old Yimvert Berroterán.

Multinational rescue teams pulled Hernán Alberto Gil Flores, a security guard, out alive after he had spent more than a week trapped under rubble. Firefighters wheeled him out on a gurney.

Rubén Darío Sillie said his daughter could still be alive in an air bubble. He also said she might be in a cavity or gap and that miracles do happen.

The response turns political

Delcy Rodríguez said she activated immediately after the earthquakes. She asked King Charles to release about 30 tons of Venezuelan gold frozen under UK sanctions.

We did not wait one day, two days or three days. We activated immediately.
— Delcy Rodríguez

In La Guaira, critics said the government's response was delayed and bungled. Critics also said civilians were left to dig through rubble themselves while official help lagged.

Separately, Marco Rubio said damage to Caracas' airport delayed aid crews and made recovery harder.

Residents in La Guaira said public anger and disapproval could fuel unrest and pressure for change. Carlos Genatios said the revolution was a lie and argued that better planning could have reduced the losses.

Francisco González said God was punishing the politicians. Milagri Rodríguez Guanire said the quakes were the straw that broke the camel's back.

International aid and recovery

$300m
UN relief appeal
(£224 million) The UN also estimated direct physical damage at about $37 billion (£27.6 billion) and said 1.3 million people needed aid.

Aid groups warned that displaced people could face infections and more deaths, especially if sanitation and shelter remained stretched.

The U.S. Southern Command said American forces were surging aid by air and ship for relief operations. Los Angeles County sent 73 firefighters, six canine teams and 80,000 pounds ($107,093) of equipment.

Behind that deployment, Greg Short said the three-member support team in Pacoima was on duty 24 hours a day throughout the rescue mission, helping families and firefighters back home.

What the quakes exposed

Residents and engineers said the buildings were poor-quality death traps, pointing to soft soil, flimsy construction, weak oversight and non-ductile concrete. Eduardo Núñez Castellanos said soft ground, tall concrete structures and weak building standards made Venezuela vulnerable.

The problem is a lack of control in building standards.
— Eduardo Núñez Castellanos

The quakes did not create every collapse from scratch. They punished weaknesses that had been left in place.

Seismic lessons

The U.S. Geological Survey said its calculations gave a 40% chance of 10,000 to 100,000 fatalities. Michael Schmitz said that range made as many as 50,000 deaths plausible.

Live Science's Julián García Mayordomo said single-fault hazard models break down because neighboring faults share stress and can trigger one another. He said the Venezuelan sequence was a reminder that one shock can set off another and that the shaking can last longer when fault systems behave like a connected network.

Researchers said a major event could recur in 100 years or tomorrow because recurrence intervals are only statistical averages.

Lucy Jones separately urged California to retrofit buildings and prepare neighborhood plans.

Where things stand

The official death toll rose to 4,490, with 16,740 injured and 17,907 homeless. The final toll remains disputed, along with how much of the damage came from the shaking itself versus long-standing weaknesses in building standards. The emergency is moving from immediate search toward shelter, repair and reconstruction.
