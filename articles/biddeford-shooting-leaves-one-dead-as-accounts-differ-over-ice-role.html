The shooting in Biddeford, Maine, left one person dead Monday morning. Biddeford police said the incident involved ICE personnel at Pool and Hill streets and that the area was being secured. How the encounter unfolded, along with the victim's age and legal status, remains disputed.

Gov. Janet Mills said she had been briefed and that Maine State Police were cooperating with state and federal officials. House Speaker Ryan Fecteau said a person was killed, ICE was involved, and state police, the Department of Public Safety and the FBI were gathering details.

The FBI said it responded to the scene, and ICE and DHS did not immediately comment.

What remains disputed

Witnesses and video described a white or unmarked vehicle, shots fired, bullet holes and a man bleeding after the shooting. One witness said the driver tried to stop.

Immigrant-rights groups described a 26-year-old Colombian man who was authorized to work in the United States and had a Social Security number.

Secondhand official accounts also differed on the victim's identity and legal status. Sen. Angus King said he had been told the victim was in his early 20s, had a final deportation order, was targeted for arrest and had used his vehicle as a weapon.

Accounts of how the encounter unfolded also differed. One eyewitness said the motorist was killed shortly after 7 a.m. after ignoring officers' orders and accelerating at them, while another said he heard shots around 7:30 a.m. and saw a large white vehicle slam into a smaller one before the two vehicles collided again. Separate Ring camera footage from a nearby home captured the sound of about five rapid shots.

Reactions and protests

In Maine, Rep. Chellie Pingree said she was deeply disturbed and angry, and that she wanted answers about why ICE was in Maine. She also raised the possibility that the person shot could have been legally in the United States. Biddeford Mayor Liam LaFountain said he was shaken and called for a full, thorough and transparent investigation. Maine officials and politicians also called for an FBI investigation and a transparent review of what happened.

The broader immigration debate also drew a contrasting response from the White House. A White House official said the administration is fulfilling its promise to deport criminal illegal aliens.

Advocacy groups said they were in touch with the family and described the shooting as a source of grief for the community. Troy Jackson said immigrant communities are under attack by ICE and argued that the agency has gone too far.

Mufalo Chitam said the community was grieving and furious and that the death should not be treated as routine or inevitable. Paige Loud argued that empowering armed agents with sweeping authority and too little transparency and accountability creates serious problems.

Biddeford Saco for Racial Justice and local protesters organized a noon protest after the shooting. A community rally followed in Biddeford. The group said it expected more demonstrations.

Broader scrutiny

The shooting has added scrutiny of ICE's use of force, body-camera practices and the Trump administration's immigration crackdown. It came six days after an ICE agent fatally shot Lorenzo Salgado Araujo during a traffic stop in Houston, and Reuters said that earlier killing had already sparked protests there.

Western Europe
Sources
Euronews, ABC Spain, eldiario.es (Spain), El País, Aftonbladet Sweden

Western European coverage framed the Biddeford shooting as part of a wider US enforcement surge: reports said the case is being jointly investigated by the Department of Homeland Security's Office of Inspector General and the FBI, and that an officer has been suspended. They also placed the incident among recent lethal enforcement actions and reported that Homeland Security later told a senator the person killed was not the operation's target.

10,000+
Arrests in five days
End of June, nationwide enforcement

Outlets cited internal figures showing ICE arrests in Maine more than quadrupled to about 70 a day in early July; another count recorded 546 detentions in Maine through 11 March 2026, of whom roughly 45% had criminal records (compared with 69% in a similar earlier period).

This development makes this tragedy even more disturbing and infuriating, and it underscores the reckless and haphazard manner in which immigration enforcement operations are being conducted in Maine and across the country. This has to end.
— Janet Mills, Governor of Maine

The reporting linked those developments to calls from other US officials to pause non‑urgent vehicle stops and to reports that ICE temporarily suspended most vehicle traffic stops after two recent fatal, traffic‑related enforcement incidents.

North America
Sources
CBC Canada
I watched a wife fall to her knees looking at her husband's dead body on the ground. I watched a little girl crying with a little pink backpack on because she's never going to see her father again.
— Mary Hayes, neighbour

Canadian coverage highlighted security‑camera footage that shows a white vehicle circling an intersection before a law‑enforcement SUV blocked its path and officers dragged out a limp body; it also reported the Colombian embassy is in contact with U.S. authorities to formally confirm the individual's identity and that several hundred demonstrators later gathered in Biddeford, with a smaller pro‑ICE/pro‑Trump group across the street.

Latin America
Sources
La Nación Argentina

Argentine reporting supplied local demographic and enforcement detail not emphasised elsewhere.

  • Biddeford: about 3.5% foreign‑born; Maine: roughly 28,400 non‑citizens (≈2% of 1.4 million residents).
  • Operation 'Catch of the Day' detained about 200 people, but only 11 had criminal convictions.
  • The Maine Immigrant Rights Coalition reported a 35% increase in calls to its hotlines after the enforcement surge.

Those figures were presented to underline that the enforcement sweep affected a relatively small immigrant population, produced few criminal‑conviction cases and drove spikes in community demand for assistance.
