New UK petrol station rule comes into force nationwide

The Fuel Finder scheme has gone live nationwide. From 2 February, petrol stations must report fuel price changes within 30 minutes. The price data will be made freely available to third-party apps and mapping services.

The Fuel Finder scheme is now live, with registration open to petrol stations. From 2 February, petrol stations will be required to report their fuel prices within 30 minutes of a change.
— Martin McCluskey, Department for Energy Security and Net Zero

Fuels Industry UK has told members participation is compulsory for all petrol filling stations in the United Kingdom, whether independently operated or part of a larger group. The new rules apply to around 8,300 forecourts, which must submit price updates.

How the scheme will work

30 minutes
Maximum reporting delay for price changes
Applies from 2 February across registered forecourts.

Registration is open to forecourt operators, and the Fuel Finder website will publish the feed openly so developers, satnav makers and price comparison apps can access it. The Competition and Markets Authority said live price data could appear in satnavs and map apps, which would end the need to drive round to check prices on the forecourt.

We need to reignite competition among fuel retailers and that means two things. It needs to be easier for drivers to compare up to date prices so retailers have to compete harder for their business.
— Sarah Cardell, Competition and Markets Authority

Cardell set out the CMA's aim as making up-to-date comparisons simple so retailers must fight harder for customers, and the authority has estimated motorists could save as much as £4.50 a tank within a five-minute driving radius if local price gaps are exploited.

Real-time prices will drive up competition and is set to see working families save around £40 a year at the pump.
— Martin McCluskey, Minister for Energy Consumers

Ministers have pointed to roughly £40 a year in household savings, while the CMA's estimate focuses on local per-tank differences; both figures are presented as estimates rather than guaranteed outcomes.

Industry and motoring group reactions

Simon Williams of the RAC said he hoped the scheme would produce more competitive pump prices in 2026. Matt Crole-Rees of Confused.com said greater transparency around petrol and diesel prices was a positive step that will help drivers avoid overpaying. Howard Cox of FairFuel UK criticised the planned duty increases and attacked the government's stance on drivers.

RAC Fuel Watch data show Asda among the cheaper supermarket options, with petrol at 128.9p per litre and diesel at 137.7p per litre. Drivers refuelling at supermarket-operated forecourts saved around 3p per litre in December, roughly £1.50 on a 55-litre family car. RAC figures published at different times put the recent UK average petrol price at 131.91p per litre, while a 2025 average was reported at 135.69p for petrol and 142.96p for diesel.

At the same time, the government has confirmed it will end the fuel duty freeze and scrap the 5p-per-litre discount, with petrol duty rising by 1p on September 1, 2026, another 2p on December 1, 2026 and a final 2p on March 1, 2027, a change ministers say will add roughly £3 to a 60-litre fill-up.

What happens next

Reporting across outlets agrees that the Fuel Finder scheme is live, that stations will have to upload price updates within 30 minutes, and that those feeds will be published for third-party apps and mapping services. Fuels Industry UK and officials say participation is compulsory and the new rules will apply to roughly 8,300 forecourts. Ministers and the Competition and Markets Authority have put out differing estimates of possible savings, with about £40 a year from ministers and up to £4.50 per tank from the CMA. Critics point to the government's planned duty rises from late 2026, and the pace of app and satnav integration remains speculative; the rules start on 2 February and further work with mapping and comparison services is expected next.
