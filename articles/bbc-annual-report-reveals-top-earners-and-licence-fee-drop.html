The BBC's annual report points to another fall in active TV licences, adding to pressure over funding reform.

23.3 million
Active TV licences
2025/26 — down by about 540,000 from a year earlier

Bérangère Michel forecast that the decline in licence-fee payers will accelerate, saying most of the drop comes from people no longer consuming licensable content. The BBC is planning up to 2,000 job losses and about £500m ($669 million) in savings over three years.

The BBC also said 81% of UK adults use its services every week and 94% every month, making it the country's most used media brand. Fewer than 80% of households pay the licence fee.

The BBC's pay list covers on-air and off-air staff who receive more than £178,000 ($238,282) from licence-fee revenue, excluding BBC Studios and independent production company payments. The corporation is required to publish that threshold. Scott Mills was named as the corporation's highest-paid presenter, Greg James second and Stephen Nolan third.

  1. Scott Mills's BBC pay — £745,000 ($997,304) to £749,999 ($1 million) — for the year to March 2026
  2. Greg James's BBC pay — £440,000 ($589,012) to £444,999 ($595,704) — for the year to March 2026
  3. Stephen Nolan's BBC pay — £425,000 ($568,932) to £429,999 ($575,624) — for the year to March 2026

Separately, the BBC said it sacked Scott Mills in March after new information emerged about a police investigation into allegations. Mills's lawyers said he co-operated fully and wanted privacy because the inquiry had been closed for seven years.

Mills also defended his salary and said few people resent it, describing himself as a working-class guy from Northern Ireland who is proud to earn well.

Gary Lineker and Zoe Ball no longer appeared on the list after leaving the BBC, while four women made this year's top 10, up from three last year.

Rhodri Talfan Davies said there had been remarkable progress on salaries, pointing to a 50% reduction in pay above £500,000 ($669,331) and a £20 million ($26.8 million) fall in on-air presenter costs. He said the downward trend would continue.

Controversies

The annual report cited Panorama, Gaza, Glastonbury and Bafta controversies. Samir Shah said mistakes like these damage confidence, trust and accountability, which is why the corporation must apologise and be transparent.

The report separately said a workplace review was followed by 53 bullying and harassment cases and three sexual harassment cases.

Regional performance

In Northern Ireland, the BBC said online and iPlayer use reached record levels even as radio and television use fell. It said the corporation remained the most used media brand there, reaching 84% of adults weekly, and that it collected about £101 million ($135 million) from licence-fee payers while employing 635 people in Northern Ireland during 2025/26.

Current finances and funding pressure

The annual report says the corporation cannot sustain its public service mission without funding reform. Its financial outlook deteriorated in the second half of 2025, and declining licence-fee sales and changing audience habits are widening the gap between income and costs.

The BBC says the planned savings will affect what it makes and how it delivers it. Delivering the planned savings and job losses will be critical to long-term financial sustainability as the current charter period draws to a close in December 2027.

Samir Shah described the BBC as a fundamental public good. He said it brings unique benefits to society, the economy and democracy.

The BBC itself said a subscription model would turn a public service into a consumer product. It said advertising would lead to a different BBC.

Matt Brittin said “The BBC faces a moment of real jeopardy”. He said the licence fee model ties the BBC to the past, and the warning lands as active licences continue to fall.

Katie Razzall called the annual report “A call to arms for a new funding model”, reflecting the pressure created by falling licence-fee take-up.

Possible funding models

BBC funding may be expanded to streaming services by 2027. The BBC could replace the flat licence fee with a household levy or sliding payment scale. It is unclear whether ministers would prefer a streaming-based approach over advertising.

Lisa Nandy warned that extra charges would hit consumers and businesses and could deter investment in the UK.

John O'Connell of the TaxPayers' Alliance called streaming charges an outrageous expansion of an unpopular tax, and a source with knowledge of the negotiations said it is pretty desperate to make everyone pay for the BBC.

The dispute leaves the BBC's central problem unchanged: active licences are falling, and the forecast of faster licence-fee decline keeps pressure for reform in place.
