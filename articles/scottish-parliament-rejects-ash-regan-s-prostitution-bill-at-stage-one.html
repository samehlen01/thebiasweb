The Scottish Parliament rejected Ash Regan's prostitution bill at Stage 1, with MSPs voting 64-54 against its general principles.

64-54
Stage 1 vote

The result ends this version of Regan's 'Unbuyable' bill for the current session and prevents it progressing to later stages.

What the bill proposed

Regan's bill would have criminalised the purchase of sex, repealed soliciting offences and quashed historic convictions; it would have guaranteed support for people seeking to exit prostitution and imposed penalties on buyers, including fines of up to £10,000 or, in some cases, up to 12 months in prison.

Support, rebellion and party lines

The Conservatives and Scottish Labour supported the Bill at Stage 1, while the Scottish Greens and Liberal Democrats opposed it. Six SNP backbenchers broke the party whip to vote for the measures.

  • Stephanie Callaghan, Annabelle Ewing and Kenneth Gibson
  • Ruth Maguire, Michelle Thomson and Elena Whitham

Michelle Thomson, who backed the Bill, accused the criminal justice committee report and the government response of bias and said the committee had not properly scrutinised contested evidence.

Who missed the vote

The Daily Record said Kate Forbes was one of ten MSPs who did not vote on the Bill. The Herald reported eleven MSPs did not vote, including Deputy First Minister Kate Forbes, Scottish Labour deputy leader Jackie Baillie, and Labour MSPs Pam Duncan-Glancy, Paul Sweeney and Mercedes Villalba. A source told the Daily Record Forbes had a family issue.

Police Scotland costings

Police Scotland told the criminal justice committee that, under the most complex scenario, dealing with incidents arising from the proposals could cost up to around £321m, and that it had provided category-by-category estimates.

£321m
Maximum estimated policing cost

The written evidence included Category A, initial call and attendance to ensure safeguarding, with a range of about £166,440 to £400,672; and Category B, local investigation, with a range of about £9,746,595 to £23,863,708.

Inspector Paul Thomson set out those scenarios in a letter to the criminal justice committee and was described in reporting as representing a force that supports the Bill.

Reactions and arguments

Ash Regan said the Nordic-style approach in her Bill was the only way to reduce harm and that criminalising buyers recognises prostitution as exploitation rather than treating people who are sold sex as offenders.

Today, Parliament chose cowardice over action - despite overwhelming evidence, survivor testimony, and support from police, prosecutors and international experts. Inaction is not neutral. It is a decision, and it has consequences.
— Ash Regan

Regan also warned that those who voted against the Bill would have 'a stain' on their parliamentary voting record.

Regan told the chamber she believed her approach had won the argument decisively in the parliamentary debate.

The Scottish Government agrees with this clear principle in the bill and its overall aim of supporting women. However, having very carefully considered the Committee’s Report and reflected on what we have heard during evidence sessions, the Scottish Government has concluded that whilst we support the fundamental principle of criminalising the purchase of sex, we still retain significant concerns with the provisions as drafted in the bill.
— Siobhian Brown, Victims and Community Safety Minister

Brown and ministers said they supported the underlying principle but regrettably could not back the bill as drafted because of significant issues. They also said there was insufficient time in the parliamentary session to address those concerns.

The Scottish Government said it would establish an independent commission to consider options for legislation in the next parliament. It pledged extra money for exit services, including a further £65,000 in addition to the £400,000 outlined in the draft 2026 to 27 Budget.

Maggie Chapman of the Greens said the Bill would only create more exploitation by driving the industry even further underground and that the state should support, not penalise, consenting sex workers.

Alex Cole-Hamilton warned that evidence from other countries suggested criminalising the purchase of sex can increase marginalisation and barriers to accessing support services, and he argued for decriminalisation and safer conditions.

Labour MSP Pauline McNeill said prostitution involves an 'uneven power dynamic' where women are overwhelmingly exploited and men cause harm, and that it is not a true choice for many women.

Douglas Ross said the government 'bears a grudge' and suggested its opposition was politically motivated. MSP Graham Simpson said ministers were acting to prevent Ash Regan having any success.

Opponents and campaign groups also warned of consequences. Scotland For Decrim said that in Northern Ireland violence against sex workers rose by 225% and in Ireland by 92% after similar legal changes, and argued the Bill would increase violence against women.

Some sex workers said they felt heard by MSPs who opposed the measure; one, Amelia, said she was relieved and vowed to continue campaigning and to return to the parliament.

Some opposition politicians repeated the claim that ministers were acting for political reasons rather than policy. Regan described the government's proposal for another commission as 'paralysis', saying survivors needed protection, justice and leadership rather than another review process.

State of play

MSPs rejected Ash Regan's Bill by a 64-54 Stage 1 vote. The Scottish Government said it will establish an independent commission and provide additional funding for exit services. Supporters argue criminalising buyers is the only realistic way to reduce exploitation. Critics, including the Greens, say it will push the trade underground. Maggie Chapman of the Greens said the Bill would create more exploitation by driving the industry further underground. Liberal Democrat leader Alex Cole-Hamilton warned that evidence from other countries suggests criminalising the purchase of sex can increase marginalisation and barriers to accessing support services. Campaigners pointed to spikes in violence abroad after similar laws. Key disputes that remain are enforcement costs and likely safety outcomes, and officials have begun work on the commission while ministers prepare the extra funding pledged.
