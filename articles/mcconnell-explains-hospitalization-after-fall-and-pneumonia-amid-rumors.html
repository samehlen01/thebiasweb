Mitch McConnell's office gave his first public explanation of the hospitalization amid online speculation about his condition. The statement said he fell about four weeks ago, was briefly unconscious before being taken to the hospital, and developed a mild case of pneumonia.

The statement said doctors found no broken bones, concussion, heart attack, stroke, tumors or hemorrhages. The Office of the Attending Physician said the 84-year-old seven-term senator had experienced several falls this year, which were attributed to his post-polio condition. He is still in rehabilitation rather than back on the Senate floor.

Recovery and retirement

That recovery period also framed his explanation for retiring at the end of his term, and McConnell said part of the decision was being honest about the demands of Senate work.

I still have unfinished business to complete on your behalf.
— Mitch McConnell

That same perspective also shaped another comment he has made about aging: he has said people of his generation often hesitate to talk openly about the vulnerability that comes with growing older.

As much as it frustrates me, this process takes time. And on the advice of my doctors, I won't be able to return to the Senate floor to vote quite yet.
— Mitch McConnell

The delay underscored how slowly he is returning to work.

Rumors and photo dispute

McConnell's office tried to calm speculation by releasing a photo showing him with Elaine Chao and a Washington Post copy. BBC's Jake Horton said there was no evidence the rehab photo had been manipulated with AI, and the image was meant to show he was alive and recovering.

The photo came amid reports and online claims that McConnell had died or was incapacitated. Some CNBC and Fox News reporting cited emergency dispatch audio and suggested a heart attack. Laura Loomer also said a White House source told her he was brain dead and not coming back.

Loomer dismissed it as AI-generated, and the claim spread through other conspiracy theorists online. The Independent's Mackenzie Lockhart called the allegation conspiracy-minded misinformation. Isaac Saul called it complete nonsense and noted that there was no widely shared 2023 version of the image. The Republicans Against Trump group also said the photo was real.

Pressure and Senate impact

In an unusual step last week, Kentucky Gov. Andy Beshear, a Democrat, issued a public letter. He asked McConnell to update the public in a transparent manner and said the senator should be frank about his health and fitness to serve.

Separately, Rep. Ritchie Torres is drafting the Duty of Transparency Act, which would require lawmakers to file a disclosure statement with the House or Senate ethics committee after three weeks of missed votes and spell out whether a member is working remotely, how long the absence is expected to last, and a contact point.

Axios said such bills face strong headwinds on Capitol Hill.

Fox News said McConnell's absence and Senator Lindsey Graham's death left the chamber short of two crucial votes.
