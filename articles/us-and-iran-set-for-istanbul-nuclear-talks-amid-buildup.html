Multiple sources said White House envoy Steve Witkoff is expected to meet Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi in Istanbul on Friday to discuss a possible nuclear deal. The talks come amid reports of a U.S. deployment of ships and warplanes to the region. Turkish, Egyptian and Qatari diplomatic efforts have been cited as the catalysts for the outreach.

What leaders are saying

President Masoud Pezeshkian said he had instructed his foreign minister to pursue "fair and equitable" negotiations if a non-threatening environment can be secured.

I have instructed my minister of foreign affairs, provided that a suitable environment exists, one free from threats and unreasonable expectations, to pursue fair and equitable negotiations, guided by the principles of dignity, prudence, and expediency.
— Masoud Pezeshkian, President of Iran

Pezeshkian's remarks emphasised dignity and national interest as conditions for talks. Abbas Araghchi said there is currently no plan to meet or talk with U.S. officials, and that Turkey and other countries are working on a framework for potential talks. He added that diplomacy is incompatible with pressure, intimidation and force.

How the Istanbul session is being framed

Multiple U.S. and regional sources said the planned session would include White House envoy Steve Witkoff and Iran's Abbas Araghchi. The foreign ministers of Turkey, Qatar and Egypt are also expected to attend. Other reported participants include Oman, the UAE, Saudi Arabia and Pakistan.

A U.S. official said the summit in Istanbul aims to assemble a package to prevent war, and that the Trump administration hopes Iran will come ready to make the needed compromises. A separate planning source cautioned that a Friday meeting was the 'best case scenario' and that nothing is final until it happens.

What would be on the table (and what would not)

Tehran has said it will not negotiate over its ballistic missile programme, a declared red line. That limits the scope negotiators can credibly cover, and it is not yet clear how Witkoff and Araghchi would attempt to bridge the gap between U.S. demands and Iran's stated position. U.S. officials, by contrast, have publicly downplayed the prospect of a diplomatic solution and say Tehran has not shown real willingness to accept U.S. terms.

Military posture, warnings and the risk of escalation

Coverage and official statements described a U.S. build-up in the region that some outlets called a 'massive armada', naming the aircraft carrier USS Abraham Lincoln, guided-missile destroyers and dozens of warplanes as part of the deployment. President Donald Trump said that reaching an agreement would be 'great' if it could be done and warned of consequences if not, and he also referenced large ships heading toward Iran.

Reporting tied the carrier and its air wing to the broader U.S. deployment described above. Iranian commanders warned that any U.S. attack would trigger a wider regional conflict and that any hostile act would be met with a 'decisive, crushing and regret-inducing' response.

Market and political pressures

Brent fell 4.96% to $65.88 a barrel as of 3.22 a.m. ET; U.S. WTI was about 5% lower at $61.76 per barrel.

Analysts said the drop followed reports of back-channel contact between Washington and Tehran, which raised hopes tensions could ease. Andy Lipow said the slide followed reports that the two sides had been communicating through intermediaries, boosting hopes of de-escalation. Marko Papic said President Trump is sensitive to oil spikes of $70 to $80 a barrel ahead of the midterms, which could act as a brake on escalation. Market commentary also noted additional Venezuelan crude entering the market and OPEC+'s decision to hold output as factors helping to cap prices.

Diplomatic manoeuvres and external backchannels

The planned meeting was reported as the product of mediation efforts by Turkey, Egypt and Qatar over several days. Ali Larijani wrote on X that the formation of a framework for negotiations is progressing, a post carried in coverage as a signal of internal Iranian movement toward a negotiating structure.

U.S. officials present a mixed posture: one senior official described the summit as an attempt to put together a package deal, while others in Washington have downplayed the immediacy of a diplomatic solution. Regional ministers and former diplomats have urged direct talks to prevent a further confrontation, with UAE adviser Anwar Gargash explicitly calling for direct Iranian-American negotiations.

Speculation and alternative scenarios

Unnamed officials quoted in different outlets advanced competing scenarios about how far Iran might go. Some anonymous sources told the New York Times that Tehran could be willing to suspend or shut down parts of its nuclear programme. Other anonymous sources, cited via Reuters reporting, suggested Ali Larijani had told Vladimir Putin that Iran might agree to send enriched uranium to Russia as it did under the 2015 agreement.

Risks and warnings

An attack combined with demonstrations by angry people could lead to a collapse (of the ruling system). That is the main concern among the top officials and that is what our enemies want,
— Current and former Iranian officials (quoted by Reuters)

Reuters reporting, citing current and former Iranian officials, said Tehran is increasingly worried that even a limited U.S. strike could reignite protests and potentially threaten the ruling system. At the same time, Britain announced wide-ranging sanctions on Iranian officials and a state security body for their role in recent crackdowns on protesters.

State of play

Multiple sources report a planned meeting in Istanbul while Iranian officials publicly say there is no set meeting, a contrast that leaves the initiative uncertain. The reporting also documents a significant U.S. military deployment in the region, even as U.S. officials describe the summit as an attempt to assemble a package to avert war and some U.S. voices privately downplay the odds of a diplomatic breakthrough. Markets and analysts have reacted, with oil prices sliding on hopes of de-escalation, and speculation continues over whether negotiators can bridge the gulf on missiles and proxies. Planning sources said the hoped-for Friday session remains the next concrete step, but they cautioned that nothing is final.
