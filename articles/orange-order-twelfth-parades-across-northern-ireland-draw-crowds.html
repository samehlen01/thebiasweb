Preview and expectations

In the build-up, Harold Henning said large numbers of Orange family members would be on the streets over the weekend. The PSNI said it would run one of its biggest policing operations of the year to facilitate the parades. Weather forecasts pointed to warm and sunny conditions.

BBC Northern Ireland's preview figures suggested that Maguiresbridge would host 20 districts, 90 lodges and around 65 bands, while Rathfriland was expected to draw about 15,000 spectators and participants.

Rossnowlagh was held on Saturday and Scarva was moved to Tuesday.

Tens of thousands of people from across Northern Ireland took part in the Orange Order's annual July 12 parades on Monday, July 13, after July 12 fell on a Sunday. The main Twelfth parades were spread across 18 locations to mark the 336th anniversary of the Battle of the Boyne. More than 1,000 lodges and around 600 marching bands were involved.

Crowds and route lengths

County Armagh was the biggest gathering, while Belfast had the longest route.

In Belfast, 77 bands marched a six-mile route, or about 10km to the field. In County Armagh, BBC Northern Ireland called Newtownhamilton the largest Orange gathering in the world and put attendance at upwards of 20,000 spectators.

Regional round-up

  • Maguiresbridge hosted the County Fermanagh Twelfth at noon. The parade moved along Belfast Road, Gardiners Road, Main Street and Lisnagole Road, reached the field around 2pm before platform proceedings began at 2.15pm. Temperatures reached 24C, and no major incidents were reported.
  • Larne brought 70 lodges and about 40 bands to Sandy Bay, where Boyne Defenders LOL 1297 led its 125th anniversary parade.
  • Derry saw hundreds of Orange Order and band members march from the Waterside and the Fountain through the city centre and back across the Craigavon Bridge.
  • Lurgan District lodges marched through the town before heading to Newtownhamilton.
  • Cookstown saw more than 40 bands leave the assembly field just after noon.
  • Limavady was estimated at about 2,000 Orangemen and women from 50 lodges with 35 bands.

William Loane said the day created heavy parking and traffic pressure and made it the busiest day in a decade. From there, attention shifted to the weekend's bonfires, where firefighters, police and political leaders all came under scrutiny.

Bonfires and public-order issues

Bonfires were lit throughout Northern Ireland, with the final ones lit at midnight on Sunday into Monday morning. A replica of a mosque was placed on a bonfire in Moygashel, and a picture of Jeffrey Donaldson and his wife Eleanor was put on the Sandy Row pyre before it was lit. Most bonfires passed without incident, but several were criticised for their height, their location or the symbols they carried. Emergency callouts added to the pressures over the Twelfth weekend.

We received 303 emergency calls between the hours of 6pm, July 11 and 2am, July 12.
— Area Commander Dermot Rooney

The fire service said crews dealt with 151 operational incidents in that window, 54 of them bonfire-related. A hostile crowd forced firefighters to pull back from a Cookstown bonfire. In Greenisland, two homes were destroyed and two more damaged.

The PSNI said the Ardoyne outward parade passed off without incident while police were present. In East Belfast, a 42-year-old woman was arrested after items were thrown at a parade on Beersbridge Road, and she remained in custody. A man also died in hospital after falling from a loyalist bonfire in east Belfast on Friday night.

Supporters and critics

Harold Henning said the parades express identity, culture and heritage and attract visitors. He also said Northern Ireland should be respected as part of the UK and have the same rights as Great Britain.

Rev. Mervyn Gibson said unionism has to speak beyond its traditional base, including ethnic minorities, soft nationalists and Alliance voters.

Rt. Wor. Bro Derek Reaney tied the Twelfth resolution to Protestant reformers winning Bible access and said it reaffirmed loyalty and civil liberty.

Michelle O'Neill said the burning of effigies, flags and symbols was not normal and was rooted in sectarianism and racism.

Colm Gildernew said the unregulated bonfire model had failed and that action was needed.

Harold Henning pushed back, insisting that the Orange Institution was not offensive and would continue despite criticism.

Local reactions

David Mahon described Rossnowlagh as magical, with a carnival atmosphere and a strong family tradition.

In Hillsborough, David Irvine said the parade brought visitors and boosted the local economy, with crowds and higher business.

Flora Workman said the day was good for the village but bonfires should not be so high or so close to houses.

Taken together, the weekend mixed big crowds, policing pressure and local celebration with continuing debate over bonfires and their symbolism.
