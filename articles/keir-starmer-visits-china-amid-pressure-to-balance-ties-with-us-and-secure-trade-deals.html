Keir Starmer visits China amid pressure to balance ties with the US and secure trade deals. He will travel to China on Tuesday for the first prime ministerial visit in eight years, and dozens of UK business leaders are expected to travel with him, Downing Street said.

What Starmer says

Starmer portrayed the trip as a way to open 'significant opportunities' for British companies. He insisted the UK would not be forced to choose between China and the US and said it would maintain close ties with the US on business, security and defence.

I'm often invited to simply choose between countries. I don't do that.
— Sir Keir Starmer

The prime minister's language underlined the government's public line that engagement with Beijing can be pursued while keeping security cooperation with Washington intact.

Cybersecurity row

The Foreign Office disclosed it had been the target of a sustained cyber-attack suspected to be the work of a Chinese group known as Storm 1849; US intelligence sources said the operation, codenamed Salt Typhoon, was ongoing. US sources said the campaign included reported attempts to target the mobile phones of close aides to Boris Johnson, Liz Truss and Rishi Sunak between 2021 and 2024. It remains unclear whether those reported hacking attempts were successful in obtaining sensitive information. A spokesman for the Chinese embassy said Beijing 'firmly oppose[s] the practice of politicising cybersecurity issues or accusing other countries without evidence'.

Chinese embassy and MI5 concerns

Ministers approved plans for a vast new Chinese embassy in London days before the visit. MI5 chief Ken McCallum said it was not possible to 'wholly eliminate' the risks posed by the proposed facility.

Chagos controversy

The government postponed a House of Lords debate on the Diego Garcia/Chagos Bill after Tory peers tabled an amendment calling for a pause 'in the light of the changing geopolitical circumstances'. Former US president Donald Trump branded the deal an 'act of great stupidity'. The UK has said it would pay Mauritius an average of £101m a year for 99 years; Sir Keir Starmer said this amounted to a net cost of £3.4bn after adjusting for factors including inflation.

Policy Exchange warned the treaty would 'badly prejudice US-UK defence interests' and said Mauritius might accept payments from our adversaries and otherwise act in ways that would seriously prejudice the security of Diego Garcia. Foreign Office minister Stephen Doughty defended the deal as protecting the joint UK-US base on Diego Garcia and accused peers of attempting to 'sabotage' the process.

Reactions and opinions

Ian Williams in the Daily Mail argued that Mr Starmer was 'kowtowing' to China and described the visit as a reckless, 'humiliating spectacle'. Peter Frankopan in The Guardian warned that Britain's economic reliance on China and embedded supply chains give Beijing leverage that constrains UK policy and said Mr Starmer needed clarity of purpose. A No 10 source said the government was pursuing a 'hard-headed, grown-up' approach that 'puts British families first'. Lord Patten urged Mr Starmer to raise the Jimmy Lai case and said not doing so would be unacceptable.

Security officials and commentators warned of an extensive Chinese cyber-espionage campaign codenamed 'Salt Typhoon' and said China poses an increasing cyber-risk to the UK. Housing Secretary Steve Reed suggested Donald Trump's criticism of the Chagos deal was a negotiation tactic and said he suspected Mr Trump might U-turn rather than pursue the point. Some commentators speculated the trip could leave Mr Starmer politically exposed at home and risk accelerating leadership plotting while he is abroad. Ian Williams suggested Beijing may have conditioned confirmation of the visit on approving the new embassy. Jason Groves wrote that Labour's 'China audit' was finished last year but not published in full, apparently to avoid inflaming tensions with Beijing.

Consequences and next steps

Officials say the visit could bring significant opportunities for British companies. Observers warned it could also provoke criticism or punitive measures from the United States. Conservative peers said they believe they can bog down the Chagos legislation so it does not pass before the end of the parliamentary session in May. Conservative critics warned the treaty could, unless updated, breach the 1966 UK-US agreement and complicate defence arrangements. MI5 warned that approval of the new Chinese embassy increases security risks that cannot be wholly eliminated.

State of play

Reporting agrees that Mr Starmer is travelling to China this week and that ministers approved plans for a much larger Chinese embassy days earlier. The Foreign Office and US intelligence have said Britain was targeted by a sustained cyber-espionage campaign, but Beijing's embassy rejects those allegations and it remains unclear whether any reported intrusions yielded sensitive material. Opinion remains split: tabloid columnists accused the prime minister of kowtowing, No 10 describes a hard-headed, family-first approach, and security experts warn of rising cyber-risk. Next steps to watch include Mr Starmer's arrival and talks in China and the parliamentary manoeuvres over the Chagos settlement, where Conservative peers say they can delay the bill before Parliament rises in May.
