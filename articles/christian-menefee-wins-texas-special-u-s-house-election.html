Christian Menefee wins Texas special U.S. House election

Democrat Christian Menefee wins Texas special election for US House seat. The victory narrows the Republicans' already-slim majority in the U.S. House. The seat, which covers a heavily Democratic area of Houston, had been vacant for nearly a year. Menefee prevailed in a runoff against Amanda Edwards. He will fill the remainder of Sylvester Turner's term.

Results and immediate reactions

Menefee is the Harris County attorney and ran in the special contest as the Democratic choice. He framed the result as a mandate to oppose the Republican agenda and to press investigations into wrongdoing he associated with that agenda. At his victory event he also outlined policy priorities that included a push for universal health insurance and an aggressive stance on immigration enforcement leadership.

The results here tonight are a mandate for me to work as hard as I can to oppose your agenda, to fight back against where you're taking this country and to investigate your crimes,
— Christian Menefee, Harris County attorney

Menefee's comments were echoed by national Democrats who cast the outcome as a sign that their message is finding traction with voters in contested places. Amanda Edwards, who ran in the same runoff, said her campaign was always about creating opportunity in communities rather than simply seizing a seat.

Scheduling, weather and turnout

The special election's timing drew controversy: Gov. Greg Abbott did not schedule the first round of voting until November. Abbott said he left that interval because Houston officials needed roughly six months to prepare to run a special election. Democrats sharply disputed that explanation and alleged the delay was intended to give Republicans a short-term cushion in the U.S. House.

Winter weather added to voter confusion and operations: local officials canceled two days of advance voting and a civil rights group took the issue to court and won a two-day extension of early voting into Thursday. Even after the extension, some voters reported waits of around 20 to 30 minutes but said they would remain in line to cast a ballot.

What the calendar looks like next

Menefee and Amanda Edwards are already listed on the March 3 Democratic primary ballot for a newly drawn 18th congressional district. That primary will determine the nominee for the full term that begins when the next Congress convenes in 2027.

A state Senate upset in Fort Worth

Separately, Democrat Taylor Rehmet captured the special election for Texas state Senate District 9, flipping a reliably Republican seat. With all results reported, Rehmet drew roughly 54,267 votes (about 57 percent) to Leigh Wambsganss's 40,598 votes (about 43 percent). The district had been strongly carried by President Trump in 2024, and the special contest came after Sen. Kelly Hancock resigned last year to become acting Texas comptroller.

This win goes to everyday working people.
— Taylor Rehmet, union president in Fort Worth

Rehmet ran as a union president from Fort Worth and drew support from national groups as well as local voters. VoteVets, a veterans advocacy group, reported spending about $500,000 on advertising backing Rehmet, and the race drew attention from both national Democrats and staunch Trump supporters on the right.

Former President Donald Trump posted on his social platform urging voters to support Leigh Wambsganss and praising her as an 'incredible supporter' of his Make America Great Again movement. Despite those appeals and outside spending on both sides, Democrats framed Rehmet's win as evidence of a broader pattern of Democratic overperformance in special contests this cycle.

Partisan interpretations and immediate political readings

Democrats hailed the results as a warning sign to Republicans, with DNC Chair Ken Martin saying the outcome showed the party's agenda was hurting working families and that no Republican seat should be considered safe. The DNC's communications team also said the national party was deploying volunteers and resources to help the Texas coordinated campaign in advance of key tests. Gov. Greg Abbott, representing the Texas Republican view, said he was not alarmed and stressed that the results come down to how well each side mobilizes voters.

Some party strategists offered a speculative reading that the special-election outcomes reflect a motivated electorate pushing back against Republican candidates under President Trump's second term. That interpretation is being advanced by Democrats and by outside analysts who study isolated races, although Republicans attribute the results to specific turnout dynamics in these districts.

Political consequences and broader context

Menefee's win trims the GOP edge in the U.S. House and therefore modestly changes the arithmetic for close votes in Washington. Rehmet's victory adds to a series of Democratic successes in special contests and has been cited by party officials as proof that voters are reacting negatively to the national Republican agenda. At the state level, however, the single-seat flip does not erase Republicans' comfortable majority in the Texas Senate.

What happens next

The immediate calendar is straightforward: Menefee and Amanda Edwards will appear on the March 3 Democratic primary ballot for the newly drawn 18th congressional district. Rehmet will serve the remainder of Kelly Hancock's state Senate term and is expected to face Wambsganss again in November for the full four-year term beginning in 2027.

State of play

Reporting across outlets confirms that Christian Menefee won the special U.S. House election. It also confirms that Taylor Rehmet won the Texas state Senate runoff. Menefee will serve out the remainder of Sylvester Turner's term and Rehmet will serve the remainder of Kelly Hancock's unexpired term; both seats are set to be contested again on the regular calendar this year. The scheduling question that drew partisan dispute remains unresolved in practice: Gov. Abbott said he allowed time for local election officials to prepare, while Democrats alleged the long delay was meant to provide Republicans with a short-term cushion, and both parties now turn toward March 3 and the November general election as their next tests.
