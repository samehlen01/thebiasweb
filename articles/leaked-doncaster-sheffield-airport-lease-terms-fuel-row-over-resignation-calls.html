Leaked Doncaster Sheffield Airport lease terms spark row as Mayor Ros Jones resists calls to resign. The leaked document was first reported by the Yorkshire Post and later verified by the Local Democracy Reporting Service, and it shows Doncaster Council signed a 125-year lease for the Doncaster Sheffield Airport site in March 2024. The lease appears to allow site owner Peel to terminate the lease if planning permission for nearby developments is not granted, and Mayor Ros Jones has said she will not resign.

Lease terms and targets

The leaked lease requires a base rent of £5m a year and a turnover rent set at 20% of gross turnover in excess of the annual base rent, with gross turnover defined to include grants and subsidies. It is unclear whether the gross turnover clause applies to public grants already approved towards reopening costs.

£5m a year
Annual base rent required by the lease
Turnover rent: 20% of gross turnover above the base level

Those headline figures were among lease terms highlighted in reporting.

Break clauses and forecasts

The lease sets out dates of break clauses tied to passenger and freight targets: one million passengers and 13,000 tonnes of freight by March 2031, and 2.3 million passengers and 25,000 tonnes by March 2036, with further five-year checks thereafter. When the airport was operated under private ownership by Peel, Companies House filings show in the years prior to Covid it was regularly handling around 1.2 million passengers per year, with 1.24 million the highest number recorded in the 2017/18 financial year. An earlier official forecast cited in reporting projected a 'high case' of 1.1m passengers in 2031 and 1.9m in 2036, leaving the 2036 break-clause target roughly 400,000 passengers above that projection. Advisers warned a risk could crystallise in 2036 with the landowner having the option to terminate the lease unilaterally, rendering the MCA's investment abortive. Speculatively, Peel has committed 'in principle' to reducing the 2036 passenger targets to 'achievable levels', reporting suggested.

Funding, approvals and political tensions

The South Yorkshire Mayoral Combined Authority gave conditional approval of £159.52m in September 2025 and said satisfactory completion of conditions remains required before funding is released. Release of SYMCA funding is conditional and will not proceed until required lease-related conditions are satisfactorily completed. The Department for Transport said it is backing the reopening and cited wider transport investment including £1.5bn for South Yorkshire and £11m for the new Rotherham Gateway station. The council was unable to secure a private operator, and the mayor's office said the 'complex lease structure' had restricted private investor appetite, delaying a funding decision until September 2025 and asking for renegotiation of the lease. Councillors were also asked to approve a £57m loan for reopening DSA in November 2025.

Local planning and governance

Doncaster planning committee approved outline planning permission for applications of 1,200 homes and 1,400 homes in November 2025, and planning members said they were not aware of the lease termination clause when deciding. Both Debbie Hogg and Christian Foster attended the planning committee meetings and will be members of Fly Doncaster Ltd’s board of directors; Grant Thornton previously raised 'conflict of interest' concerns about Ms Hogg and the council chief executive Damian Allen. Mayor Ros Jones claimed the details for the termination clauses had been public 'for many years'. The March 2024 decision document on the council website to accept the lease makes no reference to planning permission-linked termination clauses and instead refers generally to Section 8 (Option to Determine) and break clauses. A Doncaster Council spokesperson said the authority does not comment on apparently leaked documents and that clauses in leases are advised upon by legal representatives and follow due diligence, and that planning matters are governed by a quasi-judicial committee.

I will not resign and I do not believe I misled the public.
— Ros Jones, Mayor of Doncaster

Ros Jones said she did not believe she had misled the public and that she was working with the council's monitoring officer on a way to share the commercially sensitive lease with elected members while keeping it confidential.

Political responses

Reform UK Doncaster said the Mayor is withholding information and threatened to suspend support for reopening unless full disclosure and scrutiny are provided; Councillor Craig Ward asked whether the omission of the planning-linked termination clauses has damaged public confidence; and Cllr Oliver Bloor said his party 'will not sign up to blank cheques' and demanded a full list of commitments. The Yorkshire Post editorial argued that nothing short of complete transparency is required for the DSA arrangements and described the 20% turnover clause and break clauses as a source of grave concern, while a Yorkshire Post opinion piece said Zack Polanski’s opposition was misguided and urged pragmatism on aviation alongside public transport improvements. Zack Polanski described the £193m-plus public reopening plan as 'outrageous' and said the money should be spent on public transport, while South Yorkshire Mayor Oliver Coppard defended the reopening as building a 'world leading sustainable aviation hub' and said the region should be at the forefront of sustainable aviation.

Environmental concerns

SYMCA's climate impact report found that, while new aircraft technology reduces fuel consumption per passenger over time, the growth in traffic outstrips aircraft decarbonisation and emissions will increase over time.
