Google upgrades AI Overviews to Gemini 3 and rolls out $8 AI Plus subscription in the US. Google says it has made Gemini 3 the default model for AI Overviews globally, and it introduced a lower-cost Google AI Plus plan in the United States priced at $7.99 per month.

What Google announced

On mobile, Google says users can tap a 'See More' tab at the bottom of an AI Overview and enter a conversation in AI Mode to ask follow-up questions. Testing by Ars Technica found a lightweight Gemini 3 Flash variant produced substantially better results on knowledge-based benchmarks than the 2.5 branch.

However, Mashable and Ars Technica have documented that AI Overviews has a reputation for making basic factual mistakes.

People come to Search for an incredibly wide range of questions — sometimes to find information quickly, like a sports score or the weather, where a simple result is all you need. But for complex questions or tasks where you need to explore a topic deeply, you should be able to seamlessly tap into a powerful conversational AI experience.
— Robby Stein, VP of Product, Google Search

Auto Browse: agentic browsing in Chrome

Auto Browse is an agentic feature powered by Gemini 3 that Google says can interact with any web page and can click, input text, and scroll through the page to carry out user-requested actions. The company said Auto Browse is rolling out in preview and is initially limited to paying AI Pro and AI Ultra subscribers on desktop in the U.S.

Privacy, safety and logging

Google says Auto Browse does not run locally: page content from an Auto Browse tab is streamed to a cloud-based Gemini model, and that page activity is logged temporarily to the user's Google Account (and to Gemini Apps Activity if Keep Activity is enabled). The company also said it has implemented a range of security and safety rules to prevent misuse of Auto Browse.

Privacy researchers warned that streaming and temporary logging of page content could raise privacy and security concerns that deter users. Security analysts said Auto Browse raises security and privacy concerns and may be vulnerable to prompt injection attacks. Wired described the prompt-injection risk as speculative and flagged it as a possible failure mode for agentic browsing. Multiple outlets noted that agentic browsing tools have often been sluggish or unreliable enough to require human supervision.

Limits and practical behaviour

Google says Auto Browse will not complete sensitive actions on its own — it will advance to a purchase screen but stop short of finalizing purchases and request user intervention. Chrome will open new tabs as needed, mark Auto Browse activity with an AI icon, and allow multiple background tasks that will notify the user when input is required.

AI Plus rollout and country count discrepancy

Google said the AI Plus plan is now available in markets where its paid AI tiers are sold; in the United States it is priced at $7.99 per month and includes access to Gemini 3 Pro, Nano Banana Pro, Flow, NotebookLM, 200GB of storage and the ability to share plan benefits with up to five other family members.

TechCrunch reported the expansion brings the lower-cost plan to 35 new countries and territories, while Engadget wrote that the plan is making its way to 34 more countries.

TechCrunch suggested the roughly $8 price could attract a more casual set of users and compete directly with OpenAI’s ChatGPT Go, a shift that analysts say could accelerate consumer adoption of Google’s AI tools.

Other product updates and education

Google said Gemini now offers full-length JEE mock exams with vetted content from Indian education firms, providing immediate feedback and customized study plans.

Maps now supports hands-free Gemini interactions while walking and cycling, and Google said the update is available worldwide on iOS and is rolling out on Android.

Within Chrome, Gemini now defaults to a split-screen Sidepanel and integrates Nano Banana for image editing from the Sidepanel.

Separately, Yahoo announced a beta answer engine called Yahoo Scout that runs on Anthropic’s Claude and is integrated across Yahoo products.

Industry reaction and outlook

Analysts told Wired that Gemini's improved performance is putting pressure on competitors and may position Google as a leading AI platform.

Ars Technica argued that pushing AI Mode deeper into Search risks further shifting users away from traditional blue links into Google’s own conversational surface. News outlets have argued that AI Overviews and AI Mode providing answers directly risks reducing referral traffic to publishers. Mashable and Ars Technica also continue to report that AI Overviews can produce basic factual errors.

Commentators speculated that Gemini could be used by Apple to power features in Siri and perhaps support future smart-glasses features. One analyst told Wired they expect smart glasses to ship by mid-year, though commercial success will depend on style as well as software. Wired and others forecast that Auto Browse will likely roll out more broadly beyond the initial preview, and Ars Technica suggested Google may eventually make Auto Browse available to free users after the preview, probably with strict usage limits.

State of play

Google has confirmed the Gemini 3 upgrade for AI Overviews and set the U.S. AI Plus price, and it says Auto Browse is appearing as a desktop preview for paying AI Pro and AI Ultra subscribers. Security analysts and some outlets have alleged privacy and accuracy risks, warning of prompt-injection vulnerabilities and noting recurring factual errors in AI Overviews, while TechCrunch and Engadget reported different counts for the AI Plus expansion. Google says it has implemented safety rules, and observers expect the preview to widen, so independent testing, publisher monitoring and security reviews will help determine whether the features scale or encounter pushback.
