Colombian president Gustavo Petro met President Trump at the White House on Feb. 3, 2026, in a closed-door session. Reporters were kept out of the Oval Office, and there was no press access. Both presidents publicly characterised the encounter as productive.

Background

The visit followed months of escalating tensions and angry public exchanges between Washington and Bogotá. The State Department revoked Petro's visa on Sept. 27, 2025. Washington also removed Colombia's certification on drug control and halted aid.

Petro had also escalated his rhetoric toward U.S. authorities, at times using incendiary language against U.S. immigration enforcement and accusing American agencies of fascist practices. Petro and other Colombian officials said the U.S. decertification and punitive measures were politically motivated.

Tensions first flared in January 2025 when Petro initially refused to allow U.S. deportation flights carrying Colombian nationals to land. In November Petro ordered a temporary pause of intelligence sharing with U.S. security agencies. Still, Washington granted Petro a temporary visa so he could travel to the United States for the meeting.

What the meeting covered

Officials said the two-hour conversation touched on energy, the situation in Venezuela and bilateral efforts to combat drug trafficking. Both sides also agreed to restart dialogue on contentious issues, including counternarcotics, migration and trade.

In the run-up to the visit Bogotá extradited Andrés Felipe Marín Silva, known as 'Pipe Tuluá', to the United States. The leaders exchanged gifts: Petro brought Colombian coffee and a gown for the First Lady, and Mr. Trump presented a framed portrait and a book; Petro was photographed wearing a MAGA cap.

Mr. Trump's past comment that a military operation 'sounded good' has been cited by analysts as evidence he is willing to contemplate forceful options in the region, a line of commentary that shadowed the visit. Observers said such statements complicated the atmosphere around bilateral talks.

Security concerns on the border

Tensions surged after a U.S. operation in Caracas that resulted in Nicolás Maduro being captured, an episode that heightened regional alarm. In the wake of that upheaval Petro ordered 30,000 Colombian soldiers to the Venezuelan border, his government said.

Insight Crime estimates that 1,200 of the group's approximately 6,000 members operate in Venezuela, a presence analysts say deepens cross-border security risks. Security analysts warned the ELN could pose a threat to U.S. oil firms moving into Venezuela and that some proceeds frequently flowed through elements of the Venezuelan armed forces.

Despite the political rupture, cooperation on security matters has continued in practice, with militaries and law-enforcement agencies from both countries maintaining channels. Bogotá has announced it will soon restart aerial spraying of coca crops with glyphosate.

Analysts' assessments

Brett Bruen, speaking to the BBC, suggested Petro might offer concessions at the White House to avoid punitive measures from Mr. Trump. Michael Shifter argued the meeting could be a 'win-win' for Petro: a successful visit would blunt opposition claims about the left's ties to Washington, while a failure would not necessarily cost him his base. Melissa Ford Maldonado said U.S. officials expect Colombia to demonstrate unmistakable resolve against cartels and will not tolerate ambiguity on narco-terrorism. Kevin Whitaker urged that a more forceful military approach aimed at undercutting illegal armed groups could be attractive and potentially effective. Julio Londoño Paredes warned that both leaders were unpredictable, a dynamic that made the outcome uncertain. Gimena Sánchez cautioned that Petro can be easily provoked and may speak without restraint if triggered, a trait analysts saw as a risk for sensitive diplomacy.

Political and policy consequences

Analysts warned the meeting's outcome could affect the May presidential election in Colombia and bolster the chances of the candidate Petro supports, Senator Iván Cepeda. Some commentators cautioned that economic punishment by the United States, including tariffs, could inflict pain on Colombia's economy and damage the left's electoral prospects. U.S. and Colombian officials and several analysts also forecast that an easing of tensions may restore some elements of counternarcotics cooperation and intelligence sharing.

Speculation and next steps

Some observers have speculated Mr. Trump may consider or is signalling openness to a U.S. military operation in Colombia, a reading tied to his earlier remarks and cited by commentators in the run-up to the visit. Others have speculated that a public dressing-down of Petro at the White House could paradoxically boost his domestic popularity. Alejandro Gaviria suggested the president may be using high-profile diplomacy to bolster his international stature and legacy, an interpretation offered as moderate-certainty speculation rather than confirmed intent.

State of play

Multiple sources confirm Petro travelled to the White House for a closed-door meeting on Feb. 3, 2026. Both sides agreed to restart dialogue on counternarcotics and migration. Petro and Colombian officials continue to allege the U.S. decertification and punitive steps were politically motivated. U.S. authorities say the measures reflected Colombia's demonstrable failure to meet drug-control obligations. It is uncertain whether rhetoric about military options will move beyond talk; some analysts point to Mr. Trump's earlier comments as reason for concern, while both governments say the immediate next step is to translate the agreed dialogue into concrete cooperation.
