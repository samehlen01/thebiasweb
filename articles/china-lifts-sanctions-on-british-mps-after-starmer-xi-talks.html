China lifts sanctions on British MPs and peers after Starmer-Xi talks. Prime Minister Sir Keir Starmer said that, following his meeting with President Xi Jinping, Chinese officials had indicated the restrictions imposed on some UK parliamentarians had been lifted and that Beijing would be prepared to receive visits by sitting MPs and peers.

What happened

The measures at the centre of the announcement date back to 2021 and were explicitly personal. Those named by Beijing were banned from entering China, Hong Kong and Macau; any property they held in mainland China was frozen, and Chinese citizens and institutions were prohibited from doing business with them.

Those 2021 measures were targeted at nine people identified by Beijing in 2021 and were widely treated at the time as both symbolic penalties and practical barriers to routine legislative exchanges between the two countries.

The UK itself in 2021 imposed sanctions on four Chinese officials and a state-run organisation involved in human rights abuses in Xinjiang province, including against the Uighur Muslim minority, a separate set of measures that the government has maintained.

Starmer said he had raised the case of imprisoned British-Chinese dual national Jimmy Lai and the treatment of Uyghurs during his discussions with President Xi, and noted Mr Lai has been held in a high-security prison for five years and was found guilty of national security offences in December.

On trade and travel, the prime minister said an agreement to halve import taxes on British whisky was due to come into force on Monday next week, while a start date for visa-free travel for British citizens to China had not been agreed.

Chinese position

The two sides agreed in principle to resume normal exchanges between the legislatures of the two countries. China welcomes British parliamentarians who have the willingness to visit China more and experience the real China.
— Chinese foreign ministry

In its official formulation Beijing presented the move as an in‑principle decision to resume normal exchanges between the legislatures and explicitly encouraged British parliamentarians who wished to visit.

Those close to discussions in London said there had been no exchange of concessions in return for the thaw and underlined that the UK had not lifted any sanctions it imposed on Chinese individuals and organisations.

Reporting differences

Accounts diverge over who has been cleared: some outlets said China had lifted sanctions from six serving British MPs and peers, while The Sun reported a removal of punitive restrictions on four Tory MPs.

Sir Keir's statements referred specifically to sitting parliamentarians, which left open whether former MPs such as Tim Loughton remain affected and whether non‑parliamentary individuals including Geoffrey Nice KC and Dr Jo Smith Finley are covered.

Parliamentary access and protocol

I'm the guardian of MPs. I'm there to protect them from sanctions. It would be wrong to let Xi into their workplace while these sanctions are in place.
— Lindsay Hoyle, Commons Speaker

The Speaker said he had learned of the announcement through media reports, that he would reflect on its implications and that he would ensure MPs were updated when the House returns next week on any changes to access to the parliamentary estate.

Political reactions

The response in Westminster split between those who said engagement had worked and those who saw a political cost. Sir Keir argued the result vindicated his emphasis on engagement and said it supported his diplomatic approach. Critics were emphatic: Conservative MP Tom Tugendhat said the prime minister had surrendered leverage and described the outcome as capitulatory, while Geoffrey Nice KC warned the arrangement was extraordinary and damaging to Britain's international standing. Liberal Democrat Wera Hobhouse cautioned that a reset which ignored secret bans would not truly be a reset.

This is a British citizen. It's the job of the British government to stand up and defend him, so before we sign anything let's free people like him.
— Nigel Farage, Reform UK leader

Farage urged the government to reject trade deals with China until Jimmy Lai is freed, framing the matter as a question of defending a British national over striking commercial arrangements.

Abroad, former president Donald Trump warned that closer approaches to Beijing were risky, describing such outreach as highly risky for leaders who cosy up to China — a critique that added an international dimension to domestic scepticism.

Speculation and consequences

Some commentators suggested a practical consequence: removing the bans on MPs could remove a major diplomatic obstacle to President Xi accepting an invitation to the United Kingdom for the 2027 G20 summit. The Speaker said he would reflect on the announcement and update MPs about access arrangements when Parliament reconvenes. Conservative minister Kemi Badenoch cautioned that she would not be surprised if sanctions were reinstated after a possible visit, a comment she presented as speculative.

State of play

Taken together, reporting establishes that Mr Starmer said Beijing had indicated some measures affecting serving MPs and peers no longer applied and that Chinese officials had signalled a willingness to resume parliamentary exchanges; the UK government, however, has emphasised it has not removed sanctions on Chinese officials. Outlets differ over the tally — some reporting named six serving MPs and peers while others reported four Tory MPs — and it remains uncertain whether former MPs and non‑parliamentary campaigners are covered. Observers say the change could remove a major diplomatic obstacle to President Xi attending the UK G20 in 2027, yet ministers and parliamentary authorities have warned the easing might be reversed and will take time to consider next steps.
