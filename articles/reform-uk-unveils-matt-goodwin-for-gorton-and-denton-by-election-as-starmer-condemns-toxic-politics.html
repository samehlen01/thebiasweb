Reform UK unveils Matt Goodwin as by-election candidate as Starmer attacks his 'toxic' politics. The Gorton and Denton by-election is now set for 26 February after Andrew Gwynne stood down for health reasons, and the contest has already prompted strategic warnings from senior Labour figures and campaigning claims from Reform.

Candidates and campaign

Goodwin is a GB News presenter and a former professor of politics at the University of Kent. He has framed the contest as a test of Sir Keir's leadership, but he has also sparked controversy after social-media remarks that compared a suspect born in the UK to the 7/7 bombers.

This by-election is a referendum on Keir Starmer.
— Matt Goodwin, Reform UK candidate

Reform leader Nigel Farage echoed that message, calling the contest a referendum on Sir Keir and using it to criticise Labour's record on immigration and the cost of living.

Labour response and internal debate

There's only one party to stop Reform and that's the Labour party.
— Keir Starmer, Prime Minister

Labour's National Executive Committee said it denied Andy Burnham permission to stand to avoid what it called an unnecessary mayoral election and related costs, and around 50 Labour MPs wrote to Sir Keir urging the NEC to reconsider, calling the block a 'real gift' to Reform UK; Sir Keir, however, denied Burnham had been warned in advance.

Observers speculated the NEC move may have been motivated by fears Burnham could pose a leadership threat to Sir Keir, and Nigel Farage said his party's chances were 'considerably better' after Burnham was blocked.

Campaign warnings and early mistakes

Reform's early campaigning was criticised after chief whip Lee Anderson appeared to post pictures from the wrong constituency, an error opponents said undermined the party's organisational pitch.

Campaign criticism and opinions

Senior Labour figures and allied politicians criticised Goodwin's selection as a symbol of division; Lucy Powell said he represented politics that would drive a wedge between Manchester communities, Lisa Smart accused him of chasing headlines rather than addressing the cost of living, and Zack Polanski said Reform had parachuted in an extremist figure whom local voters would reject.

The Liberal Democrats described his nationality comments as racist rhetoric and a disgrace; Hope Not Hate described Goodwin as one of the most influential radical right figures in the country; Anna Turley criticised Suella Braverman's defection to Reform; and Richard Littlejohn wrote that Sir Keir's praise of troops rang hollow given his record on veterans.

Electoral math and next steps

At the 2024 general election Labour won the seat with a 13,000 majority and Reform UK came second with about 5,000 votes; after Suella Braverman's defection Reform now has eight MPs, raising the stakes for the Gorton and Denton contest.

Where things stand

Matt Goodwin's selection and the 26 February by-election date are confirmed by party statements, and Andrew Gwynne's decision to step down for health reasons created the vacancy; Labour's NEC said it blocked Andy Burnham to avoid an unnecessary mayoral election while Sir Keir denied Burnham had been warned in advance. Observers have speculated the move could reflect leadership concerns and Nigel Farage said Reform's chances were 'considerably better', while Green Party officials say they have a real chance and around 50 Labour MPs urged the NEC to reconsider. Views remain sharply divided between those who warn of divisive, 'toxic' politics and those who see a plausible opening for Reform; the next formal step is ballots being cast on 26 February.
