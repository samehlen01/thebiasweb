Walt Disney Co. named parks chief Josh D'Amaro its new chief executive, succeeding Bob Iger. Josh D'Amaro will take the role on March 18, 2026, and Bob Iger will remain a board member and senior adviser until his retirement at the end of the year.

Who is Josh D'Amaro?

Josh D'Amaro joined Disney in 1998 and rose from park-level operations into senior company leadership, eventually leading the division that runs parks and resorts, a background the board cited in choosing him.

As chairman of Disney Experiences, he oversaw the business that operates the parks and resorts. One source said his remit includes 12 theme parks and 54 resorts globally.

Other outlets described the role differently, saying it covered 12 theme parks and 57 resort hotels across six global destinations and cited a global team of 185,000 cast members.

The Experiences business has been cited as generating about $36 billion in revenue in the last fiscal year and is commonly linked in reports to roughly 185,000 employees worldwide.

Other reports framed D'Amaro's remit as encompassing the whole company, citing a workforce of 230,000, a figure that refers to Disney as a whole rather than just the Experiences unit.

As much as I feel honored, it’s also a burden,
— Josh D'Amaro

He made the remark to The Orlando Business Journal in 2020, shortly after becoming the new president of Walt Disney World.

Board decision and creative reshuffle

Disney said the board voted unanimously to select D'Amaro, choosing him over three other internal candidates, Dana Walden, Alan Bergman and Jimmy Pitaro. The announcement also named Dana Walden the company’s president and chief creative officer, effective March 18; she will report to D'Amaro and becomes the first woman to serve as president at the 102-year-old company.

We won't have the same drama we had last time, that I can assure you.
— James Gorman, Disney board chair

Gorman framed the reshuffle as designed to broaden creative oversight, saying the board wanted to expand creativity across the studios and streaming services while D'Amaro focuses on operations.

TD Cowen's Doug Creutz said D'Amaro 'lacks experience on the creative side of the business' and that Walden's promotion should address that shortfall.

Pay and contract terms

$38.5 million
Approximate first-year compensation package
Includes a $2.5m base salary, at least $26.3m in Disney shares annually and a $9.7m one-time award.

Company filings list a $2.5m base salary, annual equity awards worth at least $26.3m and a one-time award of $9.7m for D'Amaro this year, elements that together explain the roughly $38.5m first-year package.

Market response and analyst views

Markets reacted immediately: shares dipped 1% in early trading after the announcement.

Analysts were cautious. Paolo Pescatore warned that 'Disney can ill afford another messy handover' and urged the company not to lose sight of its content capabilities. TD Cowen's Doug Creutz wrote that D'Amaro's most immediate priorities will be managing the Parks business through what continues to be a bumpy economic environment and that he will also be tasked with maintaining creative momentum in the studios and on Disney+, a framing that underscores the twin operational and creative challenges facing the new CEO.

Speculation and strategic implications

Commentators offered several speculative takes. The BBC suggested the board's pick signals a tilt toward the parks business as the most reliable source of profits while Disney works through streaming challenges. Creutz speculated that D'Amaro may lean on Dana Walden for creative leadership because he lacks experience on the creative side of the business. NPR's business coverage added a separate speculative angle, suggesting the new CEO will work with licensing arrangements such as a reported deal with OpenAI's Sora.

Discrepancies in reporting

Outlets differed on the scope of D'Amaro's remit. Some, including the BBC, used a 54-resort description, while others, including The Independent, reported 57 resort hotels and emphasised a global cast of 185,000. Some stories attached the 185,000 headcount and the roughly $36 billion revenue figure specifically to the Experiences division; others used 230,000 to describe Disney's total workforce. That distinction changes how large the parks operation appears relative to the corporation as a whole.

Context: succession history and political headwinds

D'Amaro's promotion closes a long-running succession saga at Disney: the company named Bob Chapek CEO in 2020 and later removed him in 2022 when Bob Iger returned to the top job. The handover comes at a moment of heightened political scrutiny for media companies, and Disney has been publicly criticised by some Republicans, including Florida Governor Ron DeSantis, over cultural and content decisions.

State of play

Across reporting there is agreement on the essentials: Walt Disney Co. has named Josh D'Amaro as chief executive with an effective start date of March 18, 2026, and Bob Iger will remain on the board and as a senior adviser through the end of the year. Company filings and the board statement set out D'Amaro's compensation and Dana Walden's new creative remit, while analysts warned the company 'can ill afford another messy handover' and flagged questions about D'Amaro's experience on the creative side; commentators have speculated the appointment tilts the company toward parks as a source of reliable profits. What remains disputed are some of the specific counts and whether numerical descriptions refer to the Experiences division or to Disney as a whole, and the immediate next step is the formal handover on March 18, after which scrutiny will focus on execution in parks and creative output across studios and streaming.
