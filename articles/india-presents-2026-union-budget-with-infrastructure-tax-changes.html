India presents 2026 Union Budget with infrastructure spending push and new tax proposals, a package laid out in Parliament on Sunday by Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman that sets total central-government expenditure at an estimated $583bn. The plan earmarks 12.2 trillion rupees of capital expenditure for infrastructure in the coming fiscal year, roughly a 9% increase on the prior year. The government raised the Securities Transaction Tax on futures and options trading, a change that provoked an immediate market sell-off. It also proposed a tax holiday through 2047 for foreign cloud companies that build data centres in India and supply cloud services worldwide.

The finance ministry put infrastructure centre stage, setting the capital spending target at 12.2tn rupees and describing it as part of an effort to accelerate transport and logistics upgrades. Reporting on last year's base differs slightly by outlet: Al Jazeera's comparison recorded last year's figure at 11.2tn rupees, while the BBC's tally gave last year as about 11.1tn rupees — a narrow reporting variation on the prior-year baseline.

Sitharaman told Parliament the government will push for stronger domestic manufacturing in seven strategic sectors — pharmaceuticals, semiconductors, rare-earth magnets, chemicals, capital goods, textiles and sports goods — while also stepping up investment in niche technologies such as artificial intelligence. She announced dedicated corridors for rare-earth minerals in four states, naming Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Andhra Pradesh and Odisha. The budget launched a second semiconductor mission with an outlay of $436m to support equipment and materials and to encourage full-stack chip design. The plan also seeks to attract cloud infrastructure investment through the multi-decade tax holiday for foreign cloud companies.

Fiscal numbers and strategy

Officials said the government intends to shrink the federal government's debt load only slightly in the coming year, aiming to ease the debt-to-GDP ratio to about 55.6% from around 56.1% while trimming the fiscal deficit to an estimated 4.3% of GDP. The ministry signalled a structural shift: from a rigid annual deficit target toward managing the overall debt-to-GDP ratio, and it set a medium-term aim of bringing that ratio down to about 50% (+/−1%) by 2030-31 to create room for higher capital spending.

Budget documents showed a sizeable reallocation to security and borrowings: defence outlays were increased by more than 20%, and the government plans to issue a record volume of bonds in the coming fiscal year — a move Bloomberg characterised as risking added pressure on local debt markets.

Markets and investor reaction

Market moves were immediate. In the special Sunday trading session held for the budget, the Nifty 50 fell about 1.7% soon after Sitharaman began speaking and closed roughly 1.96% lower as investors digested the announcements. Commentators and traders linked the fall in part to the decision to raise taxes on equity derivatives, a move aimed at curbing speculative activity but one that hit derivatives liquidity and valuations in the short term.

Coming on top of last year's hike, this is likely to raise impact costs for traders, hedgers and arbitrageurs. This could cool derivative activity and lead to a reduction in volumes,
— Shripal Shah, managing director and CEO, Kotak Securities

Shripal Shah's warning echoed traders' instincts that further increases to the Securities Transaction Tax could raise impact costs for market participants and damp derivatives volumes, an assessment that coincided with the sharp market plunge following the STT hike.

That market reaction came against a backdrop of sustained foreign selling: foreign investors have disposed of a record amount of Indian equities, roughly $22bn since January last year, and those outflows have contributed to a weakening rupee, which has moved to all-time lows in recent trading.

Analysts and industry response

Assessments of the budget split along predictable lines. Some market commentators described the fiscal package as cautious and restrained rather than dramatic: Aishvarya Dadheech told Reuters the budget was not a big positive nor a big negative. By contrast, professional services firms such as PwC India said the Union Budget holds opportunities to reinforce financial stability and to help businesses prepare for AI adoption, while also flagging implementation challenges around talent, governance, infrastructure and trust.

Coverage also included speculative scenarios. Some reporting suggested economic expansion may slow next year if steep US tariffs on Indian exporters bite, a downside risk cited in coverage. Observers also interpreted the long tax-holiday proposal for cloud companies as intended to attract further data-centre investment into India, with commentators pointing to large recent commitments such as a roughly $15bn Google announcement last year.

Implications for industry and next steps

Taken together, the targeted missions and sector incentives amount to a state-led attempt to deepen domestic supply chains: the $436m semiconductor mission and focus on seven strategic sectors are meant to shore up local capability in higher-value manufacturing and design. Yet policy-makers will have to balance that industrial push against financing needs, and analysts warned that the planned record bond issuance could add pressure to local debt markets and complicate borrowing costs.

State of play

Across reporting, it is clear that Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman presented the 2026–27 Union Budget in Parliament and set headline capital spending at 12.2 trillion rupees while estimating total expenditure at about $583bn. Commentators diverge: some framed the package as restrained rather than transformative, while consultancies such as PwC India emphasised opportunities to shore up financial stability and to help firms adopt AI. What remains contested is how markets and the government’s financing plans will play out — whether higher STT and record bond issuance will translate into sustained debt-market stress or chiefly cool speculative activity — and attention will now focus on bond auctions, capital-flow data and the next policy pronouncements.
