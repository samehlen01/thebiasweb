US and Iran Exchange Strikes as Hormuz Standoff Escalates

In separate statements, U.S. Central Command said American forces struck Iranian targets near the Strait of Hormuz after attacks on commercial ships. It also said an Iranian attack damaged a commercial ship and left a civilian crew member missing.

That standoff quickly turned into a direct dispute over control of the strait. The IRGC said the passage would be closed until further notice and that no vessel would be permitted to transit the strait, while CENTCOM said the Strait of Hormuz remains open and that Iran does not control it.

One U.S. official told Axios that Trump approved the strike plan while he was in Turkey for the NATO summit.

Strike totals and sea drones

The strikes were meant to degrade Iran's ability to keep attacking commercial shipping. CENTCOM described the operation in separate statements, and the counts varied. It said it hit over 80 targets in one account. In another description, CENTCOM said roughly 140 Iranian military targets were struck with precision munitions launched by land- and sea-based fighter aircraft, drones and naval vessels, and the strike lists included air-defense systems, coastal radar sites, missile and drone capabilities, naval infrastructure and more than 60 IRGC small boats in and near the strait.

CENTCOM also said the latest round used one-way attack sea drones against an Iranian port for the first time. Three Corsair unmanned surface vessels struck a ship maintenance facility and a submarine at Bandar Abbas Naval Base, which CENTCOM called the headquarters of the Iranian Navy.

Hormuz standoff and reactions

Even as officials disputed the strait's status, traffic through the waterway had already slowed sharply. The Independent said little traffic was moving through the strait and that Kpler estimated only six vessels crossed on Sunday.

In an NBC interview, Donald Trump rejected Tehran's closure claim, saying the strait was open:

It's open. We bombed the hell out of them last night,
— Donald Trump, U.S. president

Trump warned that if Tehran strikes commercial ships again, the response will get much worse.

Iran's military and diplomatic leaders responded with threats and accusations. The IRGC said its retaliation targeted Gulf states and U.S.-linked facilities in the region. Iran's Foreign Ministry accused Washington of breaching the interim U.S.-Iran agreement, aimed at reopening the strait, and of committing war crimes. The ministry said Washington had openly violated nearly all parts of the agreement and attacked civilian infrastructure.

Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf said U.S. bullying and broken promises were no longer cost-free and that the Strait of Hormuz would only open under Iranian arrangements, not under American threats. Ebrahim Rezaei said Iranians would deliver a hard slap and would not fear America.

Shipping, markets and diplomatic fallout

Oil prices rose, putting inflation worries back on the table after some easing in June. The Independent said a local naval confrontation can quickly be reflected in energy bills and broader economic sentiment far outside the Gulf. It also said the dispute over the strait is both a geopolitical story and an economic one at the same time.

The Treasury Department revoked sanctions waivers that had allowed Iran to sell oil, removing that channel for oil sales.

French and British officials were studying Omani proposals that may allow navigational fees in the strait. The proposals were still under review.

The escalating attacks cast doubt on the future of the interim U.S.-Iran agreement aimed at reopening the strait.

Iran
Sources
IranWire, Tehran Times, Mehr News Iran

Iranian outlets and IRGC statements added several concrete claims not emphasised elsewhere: provincial authorities said a U.S. strike hit the RMD Hendijan drainage pumping station on the Mahshahr–Hendijan axis, killing Shaker Mohseni and injuring another worker; Khuzestan IRGC public relations named three members of Iran’s armed forces killed in strikes on Abadan county.

  • Iran's integrated air‑defence network said it intercepted and destroyed a hostile US drone over Bandar Abbas.
  • The IRGC claimed coordinated missile and drone strikes against long‑range air‑surveillance and maritime‑detection radars in Oman and said it struck US‑linked radar and Fifth Fleet systems in Bahrain and Kuwait, including Patriot and other air‑defence equipment.
  • The IRGC said it hit two supertankers that switched off navigation and tried an unauthorised route through the Strait of Hormuz.

Hormozgan governorate separately said no civilian casualties or damage to residential or commercial infrastructure had been reported so far from attacks on Bandar Abbas and Qeshm; the detailed damage and casualty counts above are reported as IRGC or provincial statements.

South Asia
Sources
News18 India, India Today, ARY News Pakistan, Express Tribune Pakistan

Indian outlets reported two new angles: Moscow reportedly flew an elite Tu‑214PU airborne command post into Tehran, raising questions about external involvement; India also published Tehran's claim that more than 200 non‑Iranian vessels coordinated with a newly created Persian Gulf Strait Authority and that most received transit authorisation and insurance.

I don't think anybody should be able to charge a fee for the Strait. I don't like the concept of a fee for use of Strait of Hormuz.
— Donald Trump, US President

Indian reporting framed that remark as a walk‑back of an earlier transit‑fee idea; Pakistani outlets reported a Cyprus‑flagged container ship attack left one Indian sailor missing, said Oman rescued 23 crew members and summoned Iran's ambassador, and New Delhi lodged protests over attacks that killed Indian seafarers.

Middle East
Sources
Al Jazeera (Qatar), AL-Monitor

Qatari reporting published the draft 'Hormuz management' bill in some detail: the draft would bar vessels flying US or Israeli flags, require permits and cargo disclosure, impose fees and could block passage or confiscate up to 20% of cargo value, with revenues directed to military and reconstruction budgets.

AL‑Monitor warned the dispute could widen: Tehran appears capable of using Houthi allies to threaten Bab al‑Mandeb, creating a second pressure point on Red Sea shipping.

Western Europe
Sources
BFM TV, ZDF Heute

French coverage flagged a separate maritime incident off Oman — a Norwegian tanker was hit by an unidentified device but its crew were reported safe — and France's defence minister said Paris wants a ceasefire and freedom of navigation and is 'ready to clear mines in the Strait of Hormuz.'

German reporting highlighted a new U.S. remark: President Trump suggested the deeply buried 'Pickaxe' site near Natanz 'was a possible target' for a 'big, fat' attack and said such an attack could happen 'relatively soon'.

Latin America
Sources
Folha Brazil

Brazilian reporting relayed detailed IRGC claims that strikes hit US‑linked sites across Bahrain, Kuwait and Oman — including alleged destruction of fuel tanks and Patriot air‑defence systems at Ali Al‑Salem and an FPS radar at Ahmed Al‑Jaber — and said depots at Jordan’s Prince Hassan Air Base were struck; these are presented as IRGC assertions.

Eastern Europe
Sources
Kommersant

Russian reporting emphasised President Trump's remark that he knows the location of Iran's military leadership and that additional US strikes would continue 'today and tomorrow' and be 'very strong', underlining Washington's stated intent to press on.

Oceania
Sources
SBS Australia

Australian outlets, citing Iranian state media, reported local strikes on southern islands: at least 10 projectiles hit Qeshm Island and a strike on Farur Island killed a telecommunications worker and wounded two others — casualty details that add local colour to the wider strike narrative.

East Asia
Sources
South China Morning Post

Hong Kong coverage reported a separate maritime incident near Oman: the Norwegian chemical tanker Stolt Magnesium suffered an explosion from an unidentified external device, which triggered an engine‑room fire but left the crew 'safe and accounted for.'

Southeast Asia
Sources
Channel News Asia, Straits Times Singapore

Singapore outlets emphasised market and humanitarian angles: US consumer inflation cooled to 3.5% in June even as oil prices rose on renewed hostilities, and the UN human‑rights chief warned that any disruption to the Strait of Hormuz would have severe socio‑economic and humanitarian consequences for food, medicines and other essentials.

North America
Sources
Globe and Mail

Canadian reporting picked up a headline US proposal: the president said the US would be 'reimbursed' by 20 per cent of the value of cargo to help cover costs of providing safety and security in the strait — a line that fed discussion of a possible renewed blockade or transit fees.
