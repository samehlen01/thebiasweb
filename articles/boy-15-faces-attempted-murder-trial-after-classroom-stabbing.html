For jurors, the case comes down to intent. A 15-year-old boy denies attempted murder, grievous bodily harm with intent and unlawful wounding, while admitting that he possessed a knife on school premises. The hearing before Judge Paul Thomas KC is expected to last about three weeks.

Prosecution says it was planned

The prosecution alleges a planned attack. The jury heard the boy visited Williams's classroom twice that afternoon and had not waited outside for her return. Christopher Rees KC told the court the boy followed Vicki Williams into a classroom, shut the door and produced a large kitchen knife. He said the prosecution case was that the teenager intended to kill her and would have kept attacking if she had not managed to grab the knife and fend him off.

Williams describes the attack

Williams said the attacker's expression gave her the impression of pure hatred or pure rage. She later told paramedics, 'I feel like I'm in a nightmare... I will wake up in a minute.'

I thought I was dying... I thought I was going to bleed out.
— Vicki Williams

Medical evidence

The court heard that Williams suffered injuries to her head, hands and back. Dr David Rouse said the head wound was consistent with a stabbing motion, the cuts on her hands were defensive wounds and the knife was massive, more like a restaurant knife, and distorted in a way that fitted how it had been used. He could not rule out the possibility that the injuries were caused during a scuffle.

The Crown says the injuries point to a planned attack, while the defence says the same wounds fit a sudden scuffle in tight quarters.

The defence says it was a struggle

Matthew Roberts KC argued that Williams spotted the knife and tried to take it away, and that the injuries were caused accidentally in the struggle that followed.

In his own evidence, the defendant said he had taken the knife to school to impress friends and to show off. He said he wanted to see what they could get away with. He denied wanting to hurt the teacher and said he had no ill feeling toward her.

Police interviews and aftermath

In police interviews he answered no comment. The defendant later said he had been in shock and that his memory did not begin to return until a couple of weeks afterwards.

He then went to his grandmother's house, where she said he came crying, with his head in his hands, and described him as a really quiet boy.

Something went in my head, nan
— — the boy, as recalled by his grandmother

Police later arrived at the house and arrested him there.

What jurors must weigh

Jurors will have to decide intent and how to read the expert evidence. The court also heard there was nothing on his phone to suggest he had been researching an attack, and that he had no previous convictions, warnings or reprimands.
