FBI searches Fulton County elections office for 2020 ballots. Federal agents executed a search warrant at a Fulton County elections office on Wednesday as part of an investigation into the county's 2020 presidential ballots, officials said. Photographs from the scene showed election boxes being loaded into trucks at the Fulton County Election Hub, and local lawmakers said roughly 700 boxes were taken.

Warrant and legal basis

A county-posted copy of the search warrant directed agents to seize all physical ballots, voter rolls, tabulator tapes and ballot images from the 2020 election, and it points investigators to possible violations of two federal criminal laws: one that criminalizes intimidation or fraud by election officials and another that requires federal election records to be retained for 22 months. The warrant was signed by U.S. Magistrate Judge Catherine Salinas.

State Sen. Josh McLaurin told reporters the FBI sought '700 boxes of ballots' and witnesses said those boxes were loaded into trucks during the operation.

At the scene

The FBI agents are here to get the 2020 ballots,
— Che' Alexander, Fulton County Court Clerk

Fulton County Commissioner Dana Barrett said both the FBI and Clerk Che' Alexander were filming the inventorying of the boxes to preserve chain of custody.

Robb Pitts, chairman of the Fulton County Board of Commissioners, said he can no longer certify that those ballots are secure.

I don't know where they are. I don't know who has them. I don't know what they're doing with them. We can no longer be held responsible for those ballots and other data that was seized yesterday.
— Robb Pitts, Chairman, Fulton County Board of Commissioners

Legal fight and defense

The Justice Department filed a civil lawsuit last month asking a judge to force Clerk Che' Alexander to hand over the county's 2020 election ballots and other voting records, and Alexander asked the court to dismiss that action, arguing the Justice Department had not offered a legitimate reason and that some records remained under seal. Kash Patel defended the search as legally timely, said a judge had found probable cause, and an FBI spokesperson described the action as a 'court-authorized law enforcement activity.'

Political reactions and national-security concerns

Several lawmakers pushed back. Senator Jon Ossoff said he suspects the raid is 'a continuation of this sore loser's crusade,' and Representative Nikema Williams said raids and renewed investigations based on the lie that President Trump won lack a factual basis and only serve to intimidate voters and undermine the integrity of our upcoming elections.

David Becker, an elections analyst, said the search is unlikely to yield successful prosecutions and 'may be more about poisoning the well for 2026,' and a New York Times analysis suggested the move could signal a willingness to use law enforcement in election matters as the 2026 midterms approach.

Senator Mark Warner said Director Gabbard's presence at the scene was deeply concerning, arguing that if a legitimate foreign-intelligence nexus existed it should have been reported to congressional intelligence committees, and otherwise the attendance reflected poor judgment; by contrast a senior administration official told CBS News that 'Director Gabbard has a pivotal role in election security' and that her participation reflected that responsibility. An unnamed official speculated the appearance may have been an attempt to make herself relevant.

Kevin Carroll, a former CIA officer, said ODNI involvement in a domestic law-enforcement action is inappropriate and could make the director a fact witness in any suppression hearing or trial related to the evidence seized by the bureau. Lawmakers also asked whether President Trump knew about the search in advance or played any role in it, a question Representative Nikema Williams pressed to Justice Department and administration officials.

Where things stand

A magistrate judge signed the warrant and the Justice Department had already filed a civil suit seeking the county's 2020 ballots, while images and witness accounts show agents loading boxes into trucks and county leaders say they can no longer account for or certify the security of the seized materials; defenders stress that judicial authorization and the FBI's description of the action as court-authorized indicate the operation was lawful and timely, while critics say it is politically motivated and risks intimidating voters as the legal fight and any related inquiries move forward.
