Danone has issued a recall of several batches of its Aptamil and Cow & Gate infant formulas after tests suggested the possible presence of the toxin cereulide. The UK Health Security Agency and partner agencies have received 36 clinical reports of infants developing symptoms consistent with cereulide toxin poisoning.

What was recalled and why

Danone says the recall applies to multiple identified batches of Aptamil and Cow & Gate formula distributed in the United Kingdom and into export markets. The Food Standards Agency reported that laboratory testing located the cereulide toxin in a particular ingredient, arachidonic acid oil (ARA oil). The FSA said it is working with the UK Health Security Agency, local authorities and international food safety authorities to trace affected products and coordinate a broader, global response.

BBC reporting identified one affected Danone pack as an 800g can carrying the expiry code EXP 31-10-2026. Danone said laboratory tests found low levels of cereulide in some batches; those packs have been replaced and products currently for sale are safe to use. The company said it was also withdrawing certain exported batches while investigations continue.

Clinical reports and symptoms

Public health agencies reported 36 clinical accounts of infants whose symptoms fitted toxin exposure. Other coverage described the total more generally as 'more than 30' cases. The BBC said it understood none of the infants were described as gravely ill. Reported symptoms have mainly included rapid-onset vomiting and diarrhoea that usually resolve within 24 hours. Officials cautioned that, in rare cases, ingestion can lead to more serious outcomes including liver or kidney injury, muscle breakdown and multi-organ failure.

The affected batches are unlikely to still be on shop shelves but may be in your cupboard.
— Katie Pettifer, Food Standards Agency

Ms Pettifer warned recalled packs may remain in homes. The Food Standards Agency urged anyone with affected products to stop using them, switch to a replacement and contact their GP or NHS 111 if a baby has already consumed a recalled formula.

Supply-chain findings and manufacturer responses

Investigators have traced the contamination in formulas made by Danone and Nestle to a shared third-party ingredient supplier in China, according to official accounts. Both manufacturers have told regulators they are no longer sourcing from the implicated supplier. Danone said it had replaced the affected batches and was withdrawing certain exported shipments as part of the response. Nestle issued a statement telling parents and caregivers that the safety of its products and the well-being of infants is its top priority.

In Ireland the Food Safety Authority of Ireland said its expanded recall followed a recommendation from the European Food Safety Authority, which published an acute reference dose for cereulide in infant formula.

How the contaminant may have entered products

The Food Standards Agency identified arachidonic acid oil as the ingredient in which cereulide was detected. Experts and some reporting have highlighted that ARA oil is often produced using microbial fermentation, and they have warned that if that process is not done properly it can allow bacteria that produce the cereulide toxin to proliferate.

Officials including Katie Pettifer said that, at the levels found so far, the risk of significant harm to infants is low. They reiterated that consuming contaminated formula can cause symptoms such as vomiting and diarrhoea, which usually resolve within 24 hours. They also noted that a very small number of cases have been associated with more severe medical problems.

Regulatory and industry posture

The Food Standards Agency said it is coordinating with UKHSA, local authorities and international partners to continue tracing products and sharing laboratory results. Gauri Godbole of the UKHSA said the pattern of reports was not unexpected given the widespread availability of the affected products prior to the recall and subsequent testing. Both Danone and Nestle confirmed they are no longer using the implicated supplier. Danone said it never compromises on food safety. In a statement, Nestle said the safety of its products and the well-being of infants is its top priority.

Regulators said further sampling and testing will continue and that they are working with partner agencies to trace and remove suspect packs where identified.

Low levels of cereulide were identified in some specific batches and we have replaced them with new products. All products available for purchase are safe to use.
— Danone

Danone's statement that low levels were found and replaced underlines the company's position that it acted on laboratory results. Regulators say trace-back and further testing remain under way as part of the investigative response.

International ripple effects and next steps

Irish authorities expanded their recall after the European Food Safety Authority published a recommended acute reference dose for cereulide. Agencies are sharing data and test results as they withdraw linked supplies. Both companies have said they are no longer using the implicated supplier and that investigations are ongoing. Regulators have said further updates and test results will be shared as enquiries continue.

State-of-play

Danone recalled certain batches of Aptamil and Cow & Gate following tests that suggested traces of cereulide. The Food Standards Agency identified the affected ingredient as arachidonic acid oil. Authorities are coordinating internationally to trace and withdraw affected products. The FSA said it is working with UKHSA, local authorities and international food safety authorities to trace products and respond to the global recall. Companies involved, including Danone and Nestle, say they are no longer using the implicated supplier and have issued public assurances about product safety. Danone also said it replaced the specific low-level batches it identified. It remains under investigation whether a failure in a microbial fermentation process used to make some ARA oils could explain the contamination; experts have described that possibility as speculative, and agencies said further sampling and test results will be published as enquiries continue.
