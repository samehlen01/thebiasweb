What happened

Unredacted planning documents and press reports say China is seeking permission to build a large new embassy — widely described as a 'mega‑embassy' or 'super‑embassy' — at Royal Mint Court in central London. The reports include diagrams showing a concealed triangular chamber in the basement, located within metres of fibre‑optic cables that link the City of London and Canary Wharf. Journalists who saw the plans reported the triangular space as roughly 40 metres across and 2–3 metres deep. Downing Street and ministers have said the application is being treated as a quasi‑judicial planning matter, and officials say the housing secretary will decide on or before 20 January.

What the plans show

Press accounts and copies of the drawings describe a complex network of concealed basement spaces beneath the proposed embassy. Journalists and campaigners have reported roughly 208 rooms in the redacted and unredacted material. Some filings reportedly refer to 'two suites of anonymous unlabelled basement rooms and a tunnel', with those annotations redacted in public documents. The unredacted plans, sources say, list at least two air‑extraction systems in the triangular chamber — a detail security commentators associate with cooling for server‑grade equipment — and the site sits between the City and Canary Wharf with fibre‑routes running along Mansell Street.

Who is warning and why

Concerns have come from across Westminster and from academic and security commentators. MPs and campaign groups say the concentration of facilities on a single site near sensitive infrastructure could be used for intimidation of dissidents and for espionage against financial and communications systems.

  • Alicia Kearns said the plans showed 208 secret rooms and warned they could be 'a launchpad for economic warfare'.
  • Sarah Champion (Labour MP) helped lead a letter saying the site's risks were 'significant and unresolved'.
  • Professor Anthony Glees said it would be 'absolutely mad' to allow the development and warned it could be an intelligence hub for Europe.
  • Professor Alan Woodward called the proximity of the cables a 'red flag' and an 'enormous temptation'.
  • Dame Priti Patel said the revelations are 'yet another reason to reject China's sinister plan' for a 'spy hub' embassy.

Conservative commentators and some opposition MPs have used strong language, accusing the prime minister of being 'too weak' to stand up to China and urging ministers to refuse the scheme on national‑security grounds.

This is a quasi-judicial process and an independent planning decision will be made by the Secretary of State for Housing, Communities and Local Government in due course.
— Prime Minister's official spokesman

Downing Street has repeatedly said the planning process must be respected, and some officials and briefings have argued that consolidating diplomatic sites would have security advantages while the application is examined.

Disputed points and reporting differences

Reporting differs on whether UK security services formally objected. One account says security services 'scrutinised' the plans and did not object after review, while MPs and campaigners counter that multiple agencies remain concerned and the issues are 'significant and unresolved'.

There is also a scope difference in how outlets describe the location: some reports cite a 22,000‑square‑foot embassy footprint, while others note that China bought a 215,280 sq ft site in 2018 for £255 million — the two figures describe different measures (building footprint versus total site area).

Claims limited to particular sources

A number of more specific allegations appear only in some outlets' accounts: one report quotes diplomatic sources saying the complex would include a 'spy campus' with on‑site accommodation for more than 200 intelligence officers, and earlier Mail reporting described 'spy dungeons' — two suites of basement rooms and a tunnel — in redacted material. Telecoms firms have said they have protective arrangements in place.

Legal challenge and next steps

Residents who live at Royal Mint Court say they will consider legal action if the housing secretary approves the project. The final decision rests with the Communities Secretary (or Housing Secretary) and, according to multiple reports, is expected to be issued on or before 20 January. Some outlets have linked the timing of any approval to a planned prime ministerial visit to China later this month.

State of play

Journalists and planning documents agree that China has applied to concentrate its London diplomatic presence at Royal Mint Court and that unredacted drawings include subterranean spaces close to critical cables. MPs and campaigners allege the proposal could be used for espionage and to intimidate dissidents, citing recent cases and redacted material. It remains contested whether security agencies formally objected — some reporting says services scrutinised the plan and did not object, while MPs say concerns are 'significant and unresolved' — and the housing secretary is expected to issue a decision on or before 20 January after which residents have said they would consider legal action.
