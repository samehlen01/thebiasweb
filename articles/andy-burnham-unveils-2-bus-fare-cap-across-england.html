Andy Burnham has announced that most single bus fares in England will be capped at £2 ($2.67) from January 2027. The scheme will apply on participating buses outside London and last throughout 2027. The cap on buses outside London is currently £3 ($4.01) until the end of March 2027. An earlier England-wide £2 ($2.67) cap ran from January 2023 to December 2024 under the Conservatives. As mayor of Greater Manchester, Burnham had already imposed a £2 ($2.67) limit there.

The announcement sets up a wider test of Burnham's local model.

Why it matters

The government said the previous £2 ($2.67) cap was followed by an estimated 30 million more journeys in 10 months. It also said earlier fare caps were linked to higher patronage, which helped investment in bus networks.

Officials said cheaper fares should improve access to work, training, education, childcare, healthcare, leisure and independence. The cap should particularly help passengers in rural and coastal areas and young people entering work.

Lydia Horbury said buses are a vital public service, but cheaper fares only matter if services are reliable and accessible.

Campaign for Better Transport said rural areas still need better routes and services, warning that lower fares alone will not help if routes stay poor or sparse.

Funding plan and criticism

The scheme is expected to cost more than £500m ($669 million) and be backed by £454m ($607 million), including about £400m ($535 million) from climate finance loans. The rest would come from £54m ($72.2 million) in savings in the Department for Energy Security and Net Zero budget.

Mel Stride said the government had not explained where the money will come from.

Richard Holden said Labour's pledges will probably need higher borrowing or taxes.

Transport reaction

ActionAid UK said the plan would throw global south countries under the bus and push them deeper into debt. Burnham said good, affordable transport links are essential and people should not be priced out. He argued lower fares will ease cost-of-living pressures and put money in people's pockets.

Kim McGuinness said a £2 ($2.67) bus fare would make a massive difference in the North East, where she said the cost of living is the biggest challenge and buses remain central to daily travel.

Paul Nowak called the cap a good start, but he said richer measures would still be needed, including a bank-profit tax and a social tariff.

Luke Marion said the cap was welcome because higher patronage has helped investment, but he still wants a long-term strategy rather than another stop-start policy.

Graham Vidler said the national scheme needs a sustainable footing and fair reimbursement for operators.

A Green Party spokesperson said temporary fare cuts are welcome, but they should be paired with free travel under 22 and more routes and services.
