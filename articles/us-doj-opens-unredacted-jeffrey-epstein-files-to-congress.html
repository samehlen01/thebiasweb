US Justice Department lets Congress see unredacted Jeffrey Epstein files. Assistant Attorney General Patrick Davis told lawmakers in a written notice that unredacted copies of the more than three million pages the department has already made public will be available for members of both houses to review in a Department of Justice reading room beginning Feb. 9; the department said computers will be provided from 9 a.m. to 6 p.m. Monday through Friday, members must give at least 24 hours' notice and electronic devices are prohibited but note-taking is allowed. The Justice Department also says it holds more than six million Epstein-related records in total, meaning millions of pages are still not accessible online.

What the files include

The earlier January 30 release assembled by the department delivered roughly three million documents, along with about 2,000 videos and about 180,000 images, according to media summaries of the dump. Journalists who mined the material flagged repeated references to victims; Mashable, for example, counted roughly 6,500 occurrences of a shorthand phrase used in the files to refer to alleged victims. At the same time Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche said roughly 200,000 pages were withheld or redacted on grounds including deliberative-process privilege, the work-product doctrine and attorney-client protection.

withheld or redacted files covered by various privileges, including deliberative process privilege, the work-product doctrine, and attorney-client privilege.
— Todd Blanche, Deputy Attorney General

Blanche's phrasing was used by the department to explain why some records were redacted, but Democrats and other critics have said those restrictions look like an attempt to skirt the Epstein Files Transparency Act rather than narrow protections for victims' privacy. Even with members able to view unredacted material in person, critics note the department acknowledges it still holds a much larger archive that is not publicly posted, so full transparency may not be achieved immediately.

Who appears in the files

Major technology figures appear in the records: Elon Musk, Mark Zuckerberg, Reid Hoffman and Bill Gates are listed in email threads and invitation logs. News outlets have emphasised that appearing in the documents is not, by itself, evidence of criminal conduct; Mashable explicitly said none of the people named in those files have been accused of participating in Epstein's abuse or trafficking of minors.

The material contains invitations and exchanges involving Elon Musk; according to reporting, Musk has said he 'declined repeated invitations' and that he never visited Epstein's private island or flew on his private plane.

Reid Hoffman acknowledged that he visited Epstein's island for a fundraising visit and has said he deeply regrets interacting with Epstein after the earlier conviction. Epstein also made donations to academic institutions: for example, Epstein contributed $850,000 to MIT between 2002 and 2017.

European connections and fallout

Emails in the files show French lawyer Matthieu de Boisséson was in contact with Jeffrey Epstein and with Jean-Luc Brunel; in one message from April 2015 he suggested Epstein meet with Brunel 'to discuss the present situation, and perhaps explore the conditions of a way out.' Other exchanges indicate Epstein offered to reimburse de Boisséson's travel expenses.

Photographs released with the records show de Boisséson pictured with a redacted-faced woman and another image of him being served vodka from a bottle shaped like a rifle. Littleton Chambers responded by suspending his associate membership while it conducts a full investigation, and de Boisséson has denied any wrongdoing. The files also underscore allegations surrounding Jean-Luc Brunel, who founded MC2 Model Management with Epstein's finances and has faced misconduct accusations for decades.

Disputed surveillance footage and timeline

One of the most scrutinised items in the records is Metropolitan Correctional Center surveillance from Aug. 9, 2019, which investigators logged in different ways. FBI reviewers recorded seeing an orange-colored shape moving up the L Tier stairs toward Epstein's cell at about 10:39 p.m., a detail the FBI noted could 'possibly be an inmate'; the Office of Inspector General wrote that the same clip looked like an officer carrying orange linen or bedding and logged an 'unidentified [corrections officer]' on the stairs. Investigators and analysts have speculated that further review of that footage could shift the timeline of who entered the tier that night.

A flash of orange looks to be going up the L Tier stairs — could possibly be an inmate escorted up to that Tier.
— FBI investigators

Official reports continue to record that Epstein died by suicide some time before his body was discovered at about 6:30 a.m. on Aug. 10, 2019, though investigators wrote that no precise official time of death was determined. Investigative notes also document problems with corrections staff that night: officers reported gaps in their memories and failures to complete required counts and wellness checks, and one officer, Thomas, told investigators he discovered Epstein shortly after 6:30 a.m. and that he 'ripped' Epstein down from the hanging position.

Misinformation and public reaction

A social-media poll run on X by Representative Thomas Massie captured the degree to which the release rekindled conspiracy talk: about 147,000 people responded, of whom 46% answered that Epstein was 'still alive', 33% said he had been murdered, and just 3% chose suicide.

Some online claims were demonstrably false: Epic Games said the Fortnite account linked online to Epstein was another player using the name, not evidence Epstein himself was active in the game. Media observers say the files' release has already generated a surge of conspiracy theories and analysts expect false narratives and other misinformation will continue to spread as new material attracts attention.

Politics, oversight and next steps

Representative Jamie Raskin formally asked for a review of the 'complete unredacted Epstein files', pressing that redactions be limited to narrow protections such as victims' personally identifiable information. Representatives Ro Khanna and Thomas Massie denounced the redaction approach and said they were prepared to move on impeachment or contempt if outstanding material was not produced, and Khanna characterized congressional access as a 'hard-won victory' that shows 'when Congress pushes back, Congress can prevail.' The Justice Department responded that the in-person reviews demonstrate its commitment to maximum transparency and reflect good-faith processing of a very large number of records, and lawmakers say they will use the reviews to prepare oversight and questioning of Justice Department officials in forthcoming hearings.

Observers warn that the releases could produce investigations, resignations and serious reputational damage for public figures; analysts speculate that once unredacted pages are examined in full, additional names or leads could surface and spur further probes. Financial entries in the files list small and large transactions alike: one record shows Epstein donated $50,000 to a Branson-linked charity at Richard Branson's suggestion, which Branson has said his foundation and companies did not accept; another message from July 2010 refers to Sheffield Forgemasters and Aston Martin in the context of business outreach, against a backdrop in which the government had announced an £80 million loan to Forgemasters earlier that year. Reporting has already connected the disclosures to U.K. fallout: the former duke who visited Forgemasters in 2009 was later stripped of royal titles by the King in November 2025 amid continuing controversy about links with Epstein. The records also show Epstein was an early investor in the cryptocurrency company Coinbase in 2014.

How reporting and processing are being judged

Journalists and columnists have leveled sharply different assessments of how the materials were handled and of what their publication will mean. Jason Leopold of Bloomberg said the dump exposed a frenzied, chaotic processing effort inside the FBI; some analysts argue the exposure will permanently stain reputations and potentially cost political careers, while other outlets and commentators emphasised that being mentioned in the files is not proof of criminal activity. Critics of the access rules and processing have also argued that limiting review to congressional members, banning electronic devices and requiring in-person inspection shows the Justice Department is tightly controlling dissemination of sensitive material.

State of play

Across reporting, officials and media say the Justice Department has granted controlled, in-person access to unredacted copies of the publicly released Epstein files. Assistant Attorney General Patrick Davis wrote that the department is making those unredacted copies available for review by both houses. Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche said roughly 200,000 pages were withheld or redacted on grounds including deliberative-process privilege, the work-product doctrine and attorney-client protection. Democrats say that judgment risks skirting the Epstein Files Transparency Act. Lawmakers say they will use on-site inspections to prepare oversight and questioning of Justice Department officials.
