This article covers Trump says federal government will 'de-escalate' Minnesota after Alex Pretti shooting. President Donald Trump told Fox News that 'we're going to de-escalate a little bit' in Minnesota after the fatal shooting of Alex Pretti.

Trump's statement and White House moves

In a follow-up account the president said he had put Tom Homan in charge of the Minnesota operation and that Homan had met with the governor and the mayor. He added that he would be watching the case and wanted a 'very honorable and honest investigation' into Alex Pretti's death. Mr. Trump also said Pretti 'shouldn't have been carrying a gun' and that he 'had two fully loaded magazines.'

we're going to de-escalate a little bit.
— President Donald Trump

Karoline Leavitt, the White House press secretary, said 'Nobody in the White House, including President Trump, wants to see people getting hurt or killed in America's streets' and called it the president's 'hope and wish and demand for the resistance and chaos to end today.'

Dispute over the shooting

Customs and Border Protection told Congress that during a struggle two officers fired at Alex Pretti, that 'a Border Patrol agent yelled, "He's got a gun!" multiple times,' and that the agents discharged their CBP-issued weapons, killing Mr Pretti. But video footage and eyewitness accounts do not show Pretti holding or pointing a firearm and some clips appear to show he had a phone in his hand when he was shot.

Legal experts widely told outlets that because Minnesota law legally permits carrying firearms, including at protests, Pretti would have been within his rights to carry and he held a concealed-carry permit, the experts said.

Federal presence and operational changes

2,000+
Federal officers deployed in Minnesota
More than 2,000 federal officers have been brought into Minnesota in recent weeks to carry out the operation.

Despite the pledge, the Associated Press reported that immigration agents remained active across the Twin Cities. Walz's office said Mr. Trump agreed to 'look into reducing the number of federal agents in Minnesota' and Mayor Jacob Frey said the president 'agreed the present situation can't continue' and to begin withdrawing officers as soon as tomorrow. DHS removed Border Patrol official Gregory Bovino from the Minnesota mission.

Legal and investigatory steps

A federal judge has blocked the Department of Homeland Security from destroying or altering evidence, and court orders are in place as the question of what happened is litigated. Minnesota Attorney General Keith Ellison said Operation Metro Surge should end immediately and that officials must show results. Reporting by the Poynter Institute suggested the administration's internal probe may be limited in scope, and separate filings indicate a judge is considering whether Operation Metro Surge is illegal.

Political reactions and opinions

Several Republican officials reacted critically. Senator Lisa Murkowski wrote that 'ICE agents do not have carte blanche in carrying out their duties.' Senator Thom Tillis said Kristi Noem 'has proven to be way out of her depth' and that her leadership 'should be disqualifying.' Representative Max Miller said, 'There are serious unanswered questions about federal use of force in Minnesota. Transparency and accountability are essential.' Senator Ted Cruz criticised the administration's tone and warned against celebrating incidents.

Local Trump supporter Jay Cielinski defended federal agents, saying 'They're just doing their job, which is right.'

Democratic leaders called for Noem to be removed. Senate Democratic Leader Chuck Schumer called Kristi Noem 'a liar,' 'vicious' and 'incompetent' and said she must be fired. House Democratic Leader Hakeem Jeffries said 'Kristi Noem should be fired immediately or we will commence impeachment proceedings in the House.' Senator Tammy Baldwin said she supported impeaching Noem if she is not fired. Senator John Fetterman said 'Americans have died' and called on Mr. Trump to fire Noem.

First Lady Melania Trump urged unity and peaceful protest: 'I'm against the violence. So, if — please, if you protest, protest in peace.' Victor Wembanyama said 'Every day I wake up and see the news, and I'm horrified' and called it 'crazy' to make murder of civilians seem acceptable.

Political consequences and forecasts

Analysts said the Minneapolis backlash creates a political dilemma for Republicans who must decide how to distance themselves from controversy while defending the administration's immigration record. Senate Democrats vowed to block a spending package if it includes additional DHS funding, raising the prospect of a government shutdown. Calls for Kristi Noem's removal could lead to her being fired or to impeachment proceedings. Some outlets reported allies warn that Mr. Trump's pledge to de-escalate risks alienating MAGA loyalists and could imperil the administration's mass deportation effort.

Speculations and outlook

Some media reports speculated that Tom Homan's arrival may calm tensions and represent a leadership shakeup in Minnesota enforcement operations. Reporting also suggested that DHS's internal investigation into Pretti's death may be limited in scope. A federal judge is reportedly considering whether Operation Metro Surge is illegal.

State of play

Courts have already intervened: a federal judge has blocked DHS from destroying or altering evidence, and state leaders say the president agreed to look into reducing or withdrawing federal agents. Federal authorities and public footage offer sharply different accounts of Pretti's death — Customs and Border Protection told Congress agents fired after someone shouted 'He's got a gun!', while video and eyewitnesses do not show Pretti with a weapon. With Senate Democrats threatening to block DHS funding and judges weighing the legality of Operation Metro Surge, further legal and political developments are expected in the coming days.
