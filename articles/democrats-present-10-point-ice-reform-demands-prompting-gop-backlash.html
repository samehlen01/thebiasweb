Schumer and Jeffries sent a letter to Senate Majority Leader John Thune and House Speaker Mike Johnson listing demands for ICE reforms. Congress has until Feb. 13 to produce a bipartisan plan funding the Department of Homeland Security for the remainder of the fiscal year, creating a very short window for lawmakers to try to reach an agreement.

The demands

In their letter, the pair outlined specific rules they said would rein in ICE operations: Prohibit ICE and immigration enforcement agents from wearing face coverings; require federal immigration officers to wear body-worn cameras when interacting with the public; and bar DHS officers from entering private property without a judicial warrant. Republicans responded that unmasking could expose officers to harassment, doxxing and threats.

Those camera proposals intersect with existing appropriations and agency action: the homeland security spending bill had already directed $20 million to outfit immigration enforcement agents with body-worn cameras, and Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem ordered body-worn cameras to be issued to every DHS officer on the ground in Minneapolis, saying the policy would expand nationwide as funding becomes available. Supporters and some law-enforcement analysts say cameras can help exonerate officers in disputed encounters, while critics note cameras also raise thorny questions about when recordings must begin and the rules governing release.

Political reaction: GOP pushback

Republican responses were immediate and sharp. House Republicans including Rep. August Pfluger described parts of the demand list, particularly the unmasking request, as 'ridiculous' or 'insane' and called the package a non-starter, suggesting Democrats were not negotiating in good faith. Senate Republicans amplified that position, publicly accusing Democrats of wanting to defund or abolish ICE and of pushing 'open borders' rhetoric.

Federal immigration agents cannot continue to cause chaos in our cities while using taxpayer money that should be used to make life more affordable for working families,
— Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer and House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries

Schumer and Jeffries wrote that federal immigration agents 'cannot continue to cause chaos' and that Congress must act to rein in ICE. They framed the demands as common-sense reforms and warned they could withhold votes on DHS funding until there is accountability. The pair also said the administration could show good faith by fully ramping down the surge in Minnesota and by removing Secretary of Homeland Security Kristi Noem from her position.

Democratic split

There is a split among Democrats: some progressives have called to abolish ICE while other Democrats seek targeted reforms.

Rules, memos and competing accounts

Investigative reporting has flagged a key tension. The Associated Press reported an internal ICE memo that authorizes ICE officers to use force to enter a residence based solely on a narrower administrative warrant to arrest someone with a final order of removal. Federal regulations, by contrast, require immigration officers to identify themselves 'as soon as it is practical and safe to do so,' and ICE officials insist those rules are being followed.

enter private property without a judicial warrant.
— Schumer and Jeffries

Legal analysts warned that requiring a judicial warrant would reshape enforcement by curbing the use of administrative warrants and reducing roving patrols. Advocates say the change would impose legal guardrails on immigration operations. Former officials and other analysts also flagged the practical complications of cameras, arguing that body-worn cameras can help exonerate officers but raise complex questions about when footage must be activated and when it should be released.

Funding and feasibility

Budget and appropriations realities complicate the political calculus. Commentators pointed to last year's omnibus, lauded by supporters as the 'One Big, Beautiful Bill,' as having put substantial resources into immigration work; ICE is already pre-funded to the tune of $75 billion, a fact many analysts say makes immediate defunding of ICE unlikely.

That financial cushion and the compressed schedule sharpened doubts about whether the talks can yield a durable deal. Senate Majority Leader John Thune called it an 'impossibility' to negotiate and pass a package that is so complicated in two weeks, while Senate Democrats said they were uncertain whether the White House or Senate Republicans were leading the talks and have asked for clarity. Independent analysts warned that failure to agree on who is leading negotiations could make a deal dead from the start.

Against that backdrop, Republican leaders signaled they might prefer procedural fixes to a long policy fight: some said they would rather put in place another short-term funding patch than accept a wide-ranging package of reforms, and party officials warned they may respond to the demands by using another short-term funding patch instead of accepting the Democrats' list.

Democratic leaders pushed back, saying they were prepared to withhold votes to enforce accountability. Schumer and Jeffries warned they could withhold votes and precipitate a DHS funding lapse if their demands were ignored; analysts say such a lapse would impact FEMA and TSA while immigration funding could remain intact.

Opponents of the unmasking demand emphasize officer safety. Republicans argued that removing face coverings from ICE agents could expose them to harassment, doxxing and threats, while reform advocates insisted that transparency, backed by body-worn cameras, could help exonerate officers in some cases. Analysts cautioned that body-worn cameras could help exonerate officers but require rules about when footage is activated and released.

What happens next

Across reporting, it is established that Schumer and Jeffries sent a formal list of ICE-focused demands to congressional leaders and that Congress has until Feb. 13 to produce a bipartisan plan funding the Department of Homeland Security for the remainder of the fiscal year; the package asks, among other measures, to prohibit ICE and immigration enforcement agents from wearing face coverings, to require federal immigration officers to wear body-worn cameras when interacting with the public, and to bar DHS officers from entering private property without a judicial warrant, and the homeland security spending bill has already directed $20 million to outfit immigration enforcement agents with body-worn cameras while Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem has ordered body-worn cameras to be issued to every DHS officer on the ground in Minneapolis. Democrats framed these as common-sense reforms and warned they could withhold votes on DHS funding; Republicans called the list unreasonable and accused Democrats of seeking to defund or abolish ICE; reporting that an internal ICE memo authorizes officers to use force to enter a residence based on a narrower administrative warrant sits in tension with federal regulations that require officers to identify themselves 'as soon as it is practical and safe to do so' and ICE officials' insistence those rules are being followed, and whether negotiators can bridge those divides before Feb. 13 is uncertain, with leaders like John Thune calling the schedule an 'impossibility' and some Republicans weighing another short-term funding patch.
