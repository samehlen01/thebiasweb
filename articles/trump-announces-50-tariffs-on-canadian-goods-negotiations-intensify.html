President Donald Trump said his administration would impose 50% tariffs on many Canadian imports on Monday. The White House said the duties would begin in 30 days.

The order came under Section 338 of the Tariff Act of 1930, a rarely used provision that put the dispute onto a much more confrontational track. Trump also signed three proclamations under Section 338 to launch the tariffs.

Tariff rate

The White House said the tariffs would apply to a wide mix of goods, including wine, hockey sticks and cement. It also said energy, potash, fish and critical minerals would be excluded. Officials further said the duties would reach goods covered by USMCA. That meant the package crossed from symbolic pressure into a broad and practical trade move.

A senior administration official accused Canada of retaliating against earlier U.S. tariffs and of discriminating against American autos, alcohol and dairy. The same side said Canada had kept a 25% counter-tariff on selected U.S. imports and vehicles. The White House used those claims to argue that the latest step was justified.

That 30-day delay gives both governments a narrow window to test whether the threat can be softened before it becomes a customs problem. Carney and Trump said they would intensify trade negotiations, but a senior administration official said the two sides had only substantive discussions and were not yet in formal negotiations, adding that the first objective is to get a comprehensive agreement. The countdown and the talks are now running at the same time.

Trump defended the move as a way to level the playing field and support U.S. manufacturing and jobs. He also complained that Canada had been very, very tough on the United States and that previous presidents had done nothing about it. In his telling, the tariffs were leverage rather than a one-off penalty.

Ottawa and business groups push back

Canada stands ready to engage intensively to address outstanding issues with the U.S. to the mutual benefit of our citizens.
— Mark Carney

Carney said the tariffs were a direct violation of USMCA. He also said Canada had already made proposals and was ready to engage intensively. Trump and Carney agreed to intensify trade negotiations. A senior administration official, however, said the two governments had substantive discussions but were not yet in formal negotiations.

Candace Laing called the move regrettable and said the 30-day window should be used to make meaningful progress in formal talks. Jamieson Greer expected to see some action soon. That combination of urgency and caution suggested that the grace period would be judged by results, not by rhetoric.

Canadian backlash spreads

Nearly every Canadian province except Alberta and Saskatchewan had already restricted U.S. alcohol. Ontario Premier Doug Ford urged a tariff-for-tariff response, while British Columbia Premier David Eby said U.S. alcohol would not be going back on shelves in his province. Alberta Premier Danielle Smith said the new duties would hurt workers in both countries.

The restrictions already meant U.S. drinks were harder to find in much of Canada. British Columbia's warning showed the response was moving into everyday purchasing, not just political messaging. For American producers, provincial shelf access can matter almost as much as a tariff notice.

The Conservative Party called the tariffs an unacceptable and unjustified attack and said Canadians were not a punching bag. Chris Swonger, speaking for the U.S. spirits sector, urged a negotiated solution that would restore market access and avoid more harm to hospitality businesses. Those reactions showed that opposition to the tariffs was coming from both politics and industry.

Wildfire politics shadow the trade fight

We are holding Canada responsible for the fact that they are not properly maintaining their Forests, and Brush therein
— President Donald Trump

Trump had already threatened wildfire-related tariffs and blamed Canada for smoke and forest maintenance. He also told reporters the two men talked about potential action in response to the wildfires, but later said the new tariffs were separate from that dispute. BBC Verify and wildfire experts countered that the wildfire picture was more complicated. They said it would be wrong to suggest Canada alone caused or could have prevented the fires. Their broader point was that weather, climate and fire conditions do not stop at a border.

That dispute has not stayed in the realm of rhetoric. John James, John Moolenaar, Jack Bergman and Lisa McClain warned that the U.S. could consider direct involvement in wildfire protection if Canada did not act, saying they were done accepting apologies in place of action. A senior administration official also said Trump may still seek additional wildfire-related tariffs after asking for options tied to Canadian smoke and poor U.S. air quality.

Analysts warn of fallout

Economists said higher charges on imported goods could push consumer prices higher. NPR described the move as the kind that could unleash a new wave of economic chaos and further fray relations between the two countries. Scott Lincicome called Section 338 the nuclear option, arguing that it could inject massive uncertainty into the global economy. In that reading, the announcement was not just a tariff hike but a gamble on how much strain the system could absorb.

The Guardian said the tariffs could carry serious political and economic risks for Trump ahead of the midterms. Representative Suzan DelBene warned that the new taxes would raise prices for American families and likely lead to retaliation. Those warnings pointed to a second set of costs that could show up well beyond the Canada file.

The U.S. Trade Representative's office said the duties cover roughly $20 billion (£14.9 billion) in annual Canadian imports. Even with exclusions for energy, potash, fish and critical minerals, the package still reaches wine, hockey sticks, cement and other goods that fall under USMCA. That breadth helps explain why importers, retailers and governments are trying to work out the damage before the duties begin.
