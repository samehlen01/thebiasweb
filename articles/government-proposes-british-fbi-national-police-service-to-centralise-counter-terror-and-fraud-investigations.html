Government proposes 'British FBI' unit to take over counter-terror and fraud investigations. The Home Secretary said the plan would create a National Police Service (NPS) intended to free up local forces so they can focus on everyday crime rather than large-scale probes. Ministers presented the package as a redesign of policing in England and Wales rather than primarily a cost-saving measure.

What the proposals would centralise

Under the proposals the NPS would bring together the National Crime Agency, Counter Terror Policing, regional organised crime units, police helicopters and National Road Policing under one organisation. It would be led by a national police commissioner who would become the most senior police chief in the country. The service would formally cover England and Wales while retaining the ability to operate across the wider UK and to set national standards and training. The Home Office also said the NPS would centralise procurement, buying technology for all forces, including a nationwide roll-out of facial recognition. The government said facial recognition has reportedly led to 1,700 arrests in the past two years.

The government has cited those arrests to justify wider deployment of the technology. Campaigners, by contrast, have warned the tools pose risks of bias and intrusion into privacy.

Support and endorsements

Graeme Biggar said the overall policing system is out of date and that some threats now require national and international responses. Neil Basu, the former head of Counter Terrorism Policing, said a single national system would be far more capable of dealing with major crime, organised crime and terrorism. The Met, Counter Terror Policing and the National Police Chiefs' Council said they support a joint police service but warned the transformation must be delivered with care to maintain strong local connections.

Criticism and warnings

Shadow home secretary Chris Philp warned the changes must not come at the expense of community policing and cautioned against top-down reorganisation. The Police Federation said fewer forces will not automatically deliver more or better policing for local communities. The Association of Police and Crime Commissioners said creating larger regional forces would be expensive, slow to implement and risk separating police from their communities. Tijs Broeke of the City of London Police Authority warned that folding fraud into a larger organisation risks losing specialist expertise and that victims and businesses would feel the difference. Matt Cane of the Metropolitan Police Federation said officers would have questions and cautioned that licensing could become bureaucratic and burdensome. Campaign groups raised worries that facial recognition technology can be biased and threatens privacy.

Costs, structure and disputed figures

The Home Office estimated that centralising procurement through the NPS could save about £350 million by the next election. The Home Secretary and ministers, however, said the reforms are intended as a redesign of policing rather than a cost-cutting exercise. Ministers also announced plans to abolish police and crime commissioners in 2028. They said abolishing commissioners would save at least £100 million and help fund neighbourhood policing.

Ms Mahmood also announced a new national forensics service intended to clear a backlog of roughly 20,000 digital devices awaiting examination.

Local pushback and specific concerns

Officers in the City of London said they remained confident they would retain national responsibility for fraud under the restructuring plans. Tijs Broeke of the City of London Police Authority warned losing that specialist role could leave victims and businesses worse off.

A Yorkshire Labour source speculated that the region's four forces could be merged into a single super force, although Home Office sources pushed back on that possibility.

State of play

What is clear across reporting is that ministers intend the National Police Service to centralise national investigative capabilities and procurement, and that the new body would be led by a national police commissioner. The government and some security figures say the change will free local forces to focus on neighbourhood crime and could produce savings, while the Home Secretary insists the reforms are about redesign rather than saving money and critics argue centralisation risks undermining local policing and specialist fraud work. Next steps include further design work and consultations, and ministers have proposed abolishing police and crime commissioners in 2028 as part of the broader reform.
