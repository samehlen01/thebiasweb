Reeves announces pubs support package and temporary business rates relief after backlash. Treasury minister Dan Tomlinson said the package would cut business rates for pubs and live-music venues in England by 15% in 2026/27, freeze bills in real terms for the two years that follow and be worth £1,650 for the average pub next year.

What the package includes

Alongside the rates change, ministers said venues would be allowed to stay open to boost takings during the men's World Cup, with licences extended up to 2am during the later stages, and that the government is launching a review into how pubs are valued ahead of the 2029 revaluation. Industry body UKHospitality warned that without further support the average pub's business rates bill would increase by 76% over the next three years. Some reports suggested the targeted package could total about £300 million over the next three years.

Ministers presented the measures as a rapid response to lobbying and looming bill increases, pointing to an existing £4.3bn fund to help businesses as reliefs are phased out.

Reactions and divisions

Reaction split quickly. Emma McClarkin of the British Beer and Pub Association (analysis) said the package would help to stave off immediate financial pressure and give landlords a breathing space. By contrast, Chris Tulloch of Blind Tiger Inns (other) warned the measures were not a rescue and that they would only slow the rate of decline. Shadow Chancellor Sir Mel Stride (other) said the move was a temporary fix that would merely delay pain for some firms, and landlord Phil Cutter (other) argued the 15% discount would be insufficient for many.

It's almost like the government are saying to pubs 'we were going to shoot you and now we're not'
— Chris Tulloch, Blind Tiger Inns

Tulloch's phrasing crystallised frustration among smaller operators and campaigners who said the package felt like a slowdown rather than a lifeline.

Other voices registered different frustrations. Michael Kill of the Night Time Industries Association (analysis) said the support would be minimal compared with the wider tax system and the cumulative impact of recent budgets. Andy Lennox, a landlord and campaign organiser (other), welcomed the concessions but said sending a junior minister showed the government did not take the sector seriously. Campaign for Real Ale (Camra) (analysis) described the announcement as short-sighted and said it did not deliver permanently lower rates, while James Daunt of Waterstones (analysis) said he thought ministers were trying to do sensible things for high streets.

Campaigns and counting disagreement

Campaign organisers and proprietors also disagreed on the size of their protest: one report put the number of pubs banning Labour politicians at around 1,000, while an organiser said the ban covered more than 1,500 pubs and would remain in place until support was extended to the whole hospitality sector.

Wider hospitality and warnings

Hoteliers said they had been left out. Kate Nicholls, chair of UKHospitality (analysis), said restaurants and hotels face severe challenges from successive Budgets. Industry data suggested hotels face average business rates increases of about 115% over the next three years, and more than 130 accommodation businesses, including Hilton, Butlin's and Travelodge, wrote to the Chancellor asking for support to be extended across the sector. The signatories warned that failure to provide help would lead to tough decisions regarding employment levels and future investment, and said such price hikes would exacerbate existing cost-of-living pressures for the British public and potentially deter domestic holidaymakers; separate industry analysis suggested six hospitality venues a day could close this year unless further help is provided.

Ministers said they would review how pubs and hotels are valued ahead of the next revaluation, and Scotland's finance secretary Shona Robison said she had written to the Chancellor seeking urgent detail and would pass on any Barnett consequentials to support Scottish businesses. The Treasury also highlighted the existing £4.3bn fund that is intended to help firms as transitional reliefs are phased away.

Speculation and next steps

Commentators, including Nils Pratley (speculation), suggested that if warnings of closures and job losses materialise the Chancellor may have to make further changes to business rates in the autumn budget. Labour MP Stella Creasy (speculation) warned that excluding cafes and soft-play centres could lead to toddlers being taken to pubs for daytime socialising.

State of play

Across reporting, ministers have announced a targeted package for pubs and live-music venues — a 15% cut in business rates in 2026/27 with a real-terms freeze afterwards and an average minister-stated benefit of about £1,650 to the average pub next year — and pledged valuation reviews and possible Barnett consequentials. Industry bodies and some landlords described the measure as a short-term buffer, while other actors called it an inadequate temporary fix and warned of closures; campaigners disagree on whether around 1,000 or more than 1,500 pubs have staged bans. Commentators have speculated that further intervention could be needed ahead of the autumn budget, and ministers are expected to set out more detail on valuations and support in the coming weeks as excluded parts of hospitality press for broader help.
