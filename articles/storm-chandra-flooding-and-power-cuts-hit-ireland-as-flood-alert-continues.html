Storm Chandra flooding and power cuts hit Ireland as flood alert continues. NIE Networks (official statement) said there are approximately 3800 customers currently off supply with a peak of 10,000 customers off supply at 9am and it continues to operate under the Amber weather warning which will be in place to 9pm tonight. ESB Networks (official statement) said about 2,500 homes, farms and businesses remained without power on Tuesday night. Met Éireann (forecast) warned the worst is not over and that flood risks will persist for the rest of the week. It has issued a status yellow rainfall alert covering seven counties in the east and south-east, including Wexford and Wicklow.

There are approximately 3800 customers currently off supply with a peak of 10,000 customers off supply at 9am. Local incident centres have been escalated in the impacted areas and we are currently responding to any faults on the network. As we carry out repairs there is a chance that customers could experience temporary outages as the team isolate areas of the network to make them safe to restore.
— NIE Networks spokesperson

NIE Networks (official statement) said local incident centres had been escalated in the impacted areas and that as repairs are carried out there could be temporary outages. NIE Networks (speculation) said there may be further outages this evening due to increasing wind speeds.

Flood-hit towns and services

Official data from the Office of Public Works (official finding) shows the Slaney at Enniscorthy almost equalled its record high water level of almost 4.7 metres. Enniscorthy was effectively cut off by flood waters as the town suffered its 16th bout of flooding in the past 25 years. Around 20 businesses and 30 to 40 houses and apartments were affected in and around Enniscorthy, Wexford County Council (official statement) said. Irish Rail (official statement) suspended Rosslare services due to flooding on the line through the town, with bus transfers put in place.

Uisce Éireann (official statement) issued a boil-water notice for 8,000 homes and businesses in Co Tipperary. About 20 flights were affected at Dublin Airport, Irish Times (other) reported, with further disruption possible later in the week.

Wicklow County Council (official statement) said the Pavilion in Aughrim is operating as a day hub and rest centre, with an initial 20 beds and up to 60 beds available if required for overnight use. The Department of Social Protection (official statement) has announced the Emergency Response Payment is available to people whose homes were directly affected by the storm. The Department of Enterprise (official statement) has confirmed it will activate the Business and Community Flooding Support Scheme and appointed the Irish Red Cross as administrator.

Government response and next steps

Taoiseach Micheál Martin (official statement) said the Department of Social Protection will assist households affected by flooding and provide immediate needs payments and other supports where required. Tánaiste Simon Harris (official statement) said flood relief schemes will be treated as 'critical infrastructure' under new emergency laws. Transport Minister Darragh O'Brien (analysis) said work on flood mitigation and adaptation will be ongoing and may never be complete.

Numbers and weather data

40mm
Rain in 24 hours at Phoenix Park
The Phoenix Park got 40mm of rain in 24 hours up to 9pm Tuesday night, which is two-thirds its average rainfall for the full month of January.

Met Éireann rainfall data and OPW water-level gauges show that some of the areas worst hit received almost half their average January rainfall in one 24-hour period. Met Éireann (forecast) warned the worst is not over and that flood risks will persist for the rest of the week. Linda Hughes / Met Éireann (speculation) said there is going to be an elevated risk of flooding in the south-east from Wednesday into Thursday and that any further rainfall on already saturated ground could cause further issues. Matthew Martin / Met Éireann (speculation) said there will be further outbreaks of rain and no real let up in that wet weather over the next five to seven days. Keith Lambkin, head of climate services at Met Éireann (official statement), said rainfall in Ireland has increased by seven per cent because of climate change.

Responses, criticism and debate

Housing Minister James Browne (accusation) criticised Met Éireann and other state bodies for what he said looked like 'guarding' information and said agencies should not withhold warnings. Professor Mary Bourke (analysis) said communities are being left to shoulder flood-relief burdens and that information was not joined up ahead of the floods. Keith Leonard (official statement), director of the National Directorate for Fire and Emergency Management, said Met Éireann had informed county councils that there was a 'high and elevated risk for flooding' across the east region.

Transport Minister Darragh O'Brien (analysis) said he did not accept that it would take another 10 years to implement a national flood-warning system and argued that data exists to inform faster deployment. Keith Lamkin (official statement) of Met Éireann said building a fully functioning national flood-warning system is a long-term project that can take many years and that, typically in other jurisdictions, it can take up to 20 years.

SIPTU (analysis) said Storm Chandra exposed gaps in treatment and pay for frontline emergency workers and renewed calls for a binding national protocol and fair compensation. Dublin Labour councillor Lettie McCarthy (analysis) said it was 'unforgivable' to award planning permission for properties in areas uniquely vulnerable to flooding.

Economic and social consequences

Ed Murphy, president of Wexford Chamber of Commerce (estimate), warned that businesses in Enniscorthy could be closed for months.

State of play

Across official reporting there is agreement that Storm Chandra brought very heavy rain and caused widespread disruption: NIE Networks reports and other utility data showed thousands without power, and the Office of Public Works recorded near-record river levels in places such as Enniscorthy. The Government has activated supports and payments for affected households, the Taoiseach said. But critics, including Housing Minister James Browne (accusation) and Professor Mary Bourke (analysis), said information had not been properly joined up, while Keith Leonard (official statement) said local authorities had been alerted to a 'high and elevated risk for flooding'; Transport Minister Darragh O'Brien (analysis) and Met Éireann officials offered different assessments of how long a national flood-warning system would take to build. Forecasters (speculation) warn further outbreaks of rain are likely over the next week and local business leaders have warned that closures could last for months as recovery and support roll out.
