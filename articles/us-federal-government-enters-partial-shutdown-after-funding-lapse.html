US federal government enters partial shutdown after funding deadline lapses, with the lapse taking effect at about 12:01 am Eastern on Saturday. The Senate cleared a stopgap spending package late Friday that separated funding for the Department of Homeland Security and passed the broader bill by a 71-29 margin, but limited DHS funding to a two-week window.

What happened

Senators approved five appropriations measures intended to fund most agencies through September while treating DHS as a separate, short-term item.

The Senate vote on the package was 71-29, and five Republican senators — Rand Paul, Ted Cruz, Mike Lee, Ron Johnson and Rick Scott — were recorded as opposing the measure.

The revised package now goes to the House, which is on recess until Monday, and Speaker Mike Johnson is expected to bring the changes up under a suspension of the rules that would require a two-thirds majority to pass.

Why Democrats pushed for changes

Senate Democrats said they would use the two-week DHS window to press for operational reforms at immigration agencies, including banning masks during encounters, requiring body cameras to be switched on, ending roving patrols and setting enforceable accountability and use-of-force rules.

Reporting and Democratic lawmakers linked the funding fight to outrage over the recent fatal shootings in Minneapolis — the deaths of Alex Pretti and Renee Good during immigration operations — which Democrats say crystallised urgency for change.

If our colleagues are not willing to enact real change, real strong change, they should not expect Democratic votes,
— Sen. Chuck Schumer, Senate Democratic leader

Schumer framed the demand as a response to what he described as widespread public calls for change and warned Democrats would withhold votes unless lawmakers enacted meaningful reforms.

Republican response and procedural moves

Several Republican senators said they did not see a need for additional policy changes to ICE and emphasised that the agency must continue its work.

We're not going to keep ICE from doing their job,
— Sen. Markwayne Mullin, R-Okla.

That stance, voiced by Sen. Markwayne Mullin and echoed by other Republicans, reflected a broader GOP argument that further restrictions on immigration-enforcement tactics are unnecessary.

The path for a Senate vote was cleared when Sen. Lindsey Graham lifted his procedural hold after securing commitments from leaders to bring up measures he sought, enabling the chamber to proceed to passage.

House conservatives and the SAVE Act

A bloc of House conservatives, led by Rep. Anna Paulina Luna, pushed to attach the SAVE Act to the compromise and warned that a small number of Republicans was enough to block or halt floor business to force changes.

I know for a fact that if the SAVE Act is a standalone vote in the Senate, just like every other good piece of legislation, it's going to die,
— Rep. Anna Paulina Luna

Luna warned that a relatively small group of Republicans could sink the procedural rule and thereby extend the partial shutdown if their policy demands were not met.

White House guidance and presidential signal

The White House and the Office of Management and Budget issued instructions to agencies to prepare shutdown plans and told employees to report to work to undertake 'orderly shutdown' activities.

President Donald Trump publicly backed the Senate compromise and urged lawmakers of both parties to cast a bipartisan 'YES' vote to avoid a prolonged lapse of funding.

Operational and economic risks

Estimates suggested roughly three-quarters of federal operations could be touched by the lapse, meaning many programmes and services would shift to reduced activity or orderly shutdown modes.

Observers cautioned that if the funding gap stretched beyond a few days, tens of thousands of federal workers could be placed on unpaid leave or required to continue working without pay.

MarketWatch reported the IRS could run out of funding during the lapse, which risked interruptions to tax-refund processing just after the agency had begun handling 2025 filings.

Reporting contrasted the situation with last autumn's shutdown, which stretched 43 days and was estimated to have cost the U.S. economy roughly $11 billion.

Media takes and partisan framing

Some outlets, including TheJournal.ie, argued the White House's willingness to split DHS funding from the broader budget package signalled a recognition that deportation strategy needed to be recalibrated after the political backlash over the Minneapolis deaths.

At the same time, House Republicans such as Rep. William Timmons argued Democrats should bear responsibility if a shutdown continued and urged political accountability.

Timing and prospects

Observers suggested there was a reasonable chance the impasse could be resolved early next week, but they warned that partisan division meant a quick resolution was not guaranteed.

Where things stand

The partial shutdown took effect at about 12:01 am Eastern on Saturday. Senators voted to fund most agencies through September under five appropriations measures. They carved DHS funding out and approved only a two-week extension for that department. The House returns Monday and Speaker Mike Johnson is expected to bring the changes up under a suspension of the rules, but a small group of House conservatives could still halt proceedings to press for the SAVE Act and Democrats say they will withhold support unless agreed enforcement reforms are adopted, so whether the lapse ends quickly or stretches into next week remains uncertain.
