Jesy Nelson has revealed her newborn twins Ocean and Story were diagnosed with Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA) Type 1. They were born prematurely in May at 31 weeks after a pregnancy complicated by twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome (TTTS). Nelson has told producers and reporters she and her former fiancé Zion Foster have separated and are concentrating on co-parenting their children while they navigate urgent medical treatment.

How the diagnosis unfolded

Nelson has set out the sequence in a new film and in interviews: the pregnancy was complicated by twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome (TTTS), she underwent an operation to treat the condition, and the babies were born early. After urgent postnatal tests, clinicians identified a genetic disorder and began a specialist treatment programme for the newborns.

I am, from the test results, 95 per cent sure that they've got SMA type one
— Doctor

Medical reporting describes SMA Type 1 as a severe and rare genetic condition that causes progressive muscle weakness. Without timely treatment it can lead to death within about two years. Specialists say that if SMA1 is treated pre-symptomatically, at or near birth, the disease can be largely prevented and many children may develop with minimal or no symptoms.

Treatment, monitoring and early screening

Nelson says the twins have already had an infusion and now attend hospital check-ups twice a week because of side effects, and she has described them as doing as well as they can. She has used her profile to join calls for SMA to be added to the newborn heel-prick test and met Health Secretary Wes Streeting to press the case.

of babies would be screened for SMA from next year
Department of Health said the trial will build evidence to support a national screening programme

The Department of Health has confirmed plans for a large-scale trial that it says will screen around two-thirds of babies from next year to build the evidence base for any national programme. The department added that Streeting would push for the timetable to be brought forward and for the scheme to be expanded to all babies if the trial supports that step.

Campaigners and Nelson say the calculus is different. They point to the low per-test cost (tests cost around £1 each) and argue that if newborn screening had been in place earlier some of the twins' disability might have been avoided. Activists who support adding SMA to the heel-prick test estimate the change could prevent dozens of severe cases each year; one figure cited is that it could help avoid 33 babies a year left needing a wheelchair for life.

Personal and public reaction

When the girls got their diagnosis, I felt like my whole world just fell apart
— Jesy Nelson

Nelson says she felt 'so grateful' the cameras were there and wants the series to raise as much awareness as possible about SMA. She has also publicly said she supports banning social media for under-16s in the UK, and she has indicated the door is open to a possible Little Mix reunion, remarks that mix personal grief with campaigning and broader commentary.

Different perspectives on screening

The policy argument now centres on timing and scale. The department frames the staged trial as a way to gather robust evidence before any national expansion, while campaigners and Nelson say the low per-test cost and the possibility of preventing long-term disability justify moving faster to universal screening.

State of play

Medical teams have confirmed Ocean and Story have SMA Type 1 and are receiving specialist treatment with twice-weekly monitoring, and Nelson and Foster say they remain focused on co-parenting as that care continues. The Department of Health will run a trial screening about two-thirds of newborns from next year to build the evidence base for any national programme, while campaigners and the family press for immediate screening because early treatment can change outcomes and tests are inexpensive.
