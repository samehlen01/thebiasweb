Apple unveils second-generation AirTag with improved range and louder speaker, introducing a refreshed item tracker that the company says brings an expanded finding range and a louder speaker. Apple says Precision Finding can guide users to lost items from up to 50 percent farther than the prior model, and that the new internal design makes the speaker 50 percent louder and audible up to 2x farther.

Apple introduced the new AirTag, a powerful accessory that helps users keep track of and find the items that matter most with Apple’s Find My app — now with an expanded finding range and a louder speaker.
— Apple

Apple said the new AirTag is powered by its second-generation Ultra Wideband chip, a detail the company highlighted as central to the range gains. CNET's analysis, however, identified a different compatibility footprint, saying the chip matches the one in iPhone 15 and later models (but not the iPhone 16E) and in Apple Watch Series 9 and later and Ultra 2 and later.

Precision and sound improvements

Apple says Precision Finding now uses haptic, visual and audio cues to guide users from farther away — up to 50 percent farther than the previous model — while an updated internal design makes the chime 50 percent louder and audible up to 2x farther. The company added that an upgraded Bluetooth chip expands the range at which items can be located, and that the new AirTag keeps the same physical form and remains compatible with existing AirTag accessories, though it still lacks a built‑in keyring hole.

Because my watch is almost always on my wrist, it's the most convenient way to search for things like this. So being able to use Precision Finding without having to grab my phone is going to be much more helpful and convenient.
— Jeff Carlson, CNET

Apple said Precision Finding will be available on the Apple Watch Series 9 and later and the Apple Watch Ultra 2 and later, and that wrist‑based searching will be a new option for AirTag owners; Jeff Carlson at CNET said this is likely to make searching from the wrist more convenient.

Pricing and availability

Apple kept the same retail prices: one AirTag for $29 and a four‑pack for $99. The company said the new AirTag is available to order on apple.com and in the Apple Store app now, and that it will be in Apple Store locations and at Apple Authorized Resellers later this week. Apple also released iOS 26.2.1, iPadOS 26.2.1 and watchOS 26.2.1 to add support for AirTag (2nd Generation). CNET's Jeff Carlson speculated that retailers were not yet stocking the second‑generation AirTags but expected them to show up soon.

Airline partnerships and baggage tracking

Apple said the new AirTag integrates with Share Item Location and that it has partnered directly with more than 50 airlines to accept Share Item Location links. Wired reported that the feature is available with 36 airlines now and is expected to expand to 50, though Apple did not give a timeline. SITA told carriers that using Share Item Location has reduced baggage delays by 26 percent and reduced incidences of "truly lost" luggage by 90 percent.

Privacy, materials and battery

Apple said AirTag does not physically store location data or history on the device, and that end‑to‑end encryption protects all communication with the Find My network; Bluetooth identifiers also rotate frequently to guard against unwanted tracking. Apple warned that Precision Finding is not available in countries and regions where Ultra Wideband technology is restricted. On environmental claims, Apple said the enclosure uses 85 percent recycled plastic, magnets use 100 percent recycled rare earth elements, and Apple‑designed PCBs use 100 percent recycled gold plating, with the paper packaging 100 percent fiber‑based. The company still cites battery life as "more than a year," and CNET noted a slight weight change from 11 grams to 11.8 grams between generations.

Reception and analysis

Commentary split between calling the new AirTag a cautious refresh and praising practical points. Ars Technica argued the product adds accuracy, volume and range but not major new features; Mashable said the best part is that the price did not change; Engadget wrote that AirTag remains a favorite Bluetooth tracker for iPhones and that the convenience of Find My almost makes up for the absence of a keyring hole. Apple and some outlets framed the upgrades as practical improvements that, paired with enhanced Precision Finding and a new chime, make it easier for users to find their important items.

State of play

Reporting agrees that Apple has launched a second‑generation AirTag with improved finding range and a louder speaker and that the device is being sold at the same $29/$99 price and supported by recent iOS, iPadOS and watchOS updates. Apple has officially said the tag uses a second‑generation Ultra Wideband chip and that it has partnered with more than 50 airlines, while CNET and Wired published different device‑compatibility and airline‑coverage figures, leaving those points disputed; one reviewer speculated retailers will stock the new AirTags soon. Apple presented privacy protections and recycled‑material claims as confirmed features, and analysts broadly described the release as incremental rather than transformative.
