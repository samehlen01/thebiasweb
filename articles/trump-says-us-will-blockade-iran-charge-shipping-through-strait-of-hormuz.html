Donald Trump said the United States was reinstating a naval blockade on Iran. The U.S. Navy said the measure would begin on July 14 at 4 p.m. ET and cover Iranian ports and coastline. Vessels without authorization could be intercepted, diverted or captured.

Trump also said the announcement included a separate 20% charge on cargo moving through the Strait of Hormuz.

The two sides offered opposing official accounts of current access. U.S. Central Command said lawful traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remains open and a southern route near Oman is available. Iran said passage was closed and not currently possible until stability returned.

20%
Trump's proposed 20% charge on cargo moving through the Strait of Hormuz
He said the charge would apply to cargo moving through the Strait of Hormuz, describing it as payment for protection.

Trump cast the United States as the guardian of the route, saying the charge was payment for protection and that the United States should be reimbursed for providing security in the strait.

Legal, commercial and diplomatic pushback

The criticism split into legal, commercial and diplomatic strands.

There is no legal basis through which to introduce mandatory tolls simply to transit through a strait
— International Maritime Organization

Calum Miller MP called the charge economic extortion, and James Kraska said tolls on Hormuz would breach international law.

David Goldwyn said the charge looked like bluster because the United States has not shown it can safely escort ships. Herbjorn Hansson said the 20% rate is unrealistic, and an unnamed shipping official asked whether the charge would make sailing safer or offer any guarantee worth the money.

Abbas Araghchi said whoever provides safe passage should be compensated, and Iran would be fair. By contrast, Kaja Kallas said freedom of navigation has to be respected and the strait should stay open.

Shipping traffic and market reaction

8
Commercial vessels crossing on Sunday
Kpler and MarineTraffic said that was down from 21 on Saturday and 14 on Friday, and the pre-conflict average was 138 ships a day.

The access dispute is already showing up in the traffic numbers. Kpler and MarineTraffic said traffic through the strait has already fallen sharply, to far below normal levels. A separate report from The Guardian said Sunday traffic was six, the fewest in five weeks. CNBC and Reuters reported that oil prices rose and stock indexes fell after Trump's announcement.

The figures suggest ship owners are already adjusting to risk before any formal blockade takes effect. The Guardian and CNBC said uncertainty could escalate tensions and distort shipping economics.

Regional fallout and possible motives

U.S. and Iranian forces have traded strikes across the Gulf region. BBC and Associated Press said the renewed fighting is further undermining the ceasefire and peace talks.

BBC and The Guardian offered different interpretations of Trump's move. BBC said it could be an effort to restart negotiations and bring more countries into the process. The Guardian said the plan may become politically complicated at home because practical enforcement is unclear and higher petrol prices are politically sensitive.

What remains unresolved

The main unresolved questions are whether traffic through the Strait of Hormuz is in fact open, whether any fee is legally defensible, and how the blockade and cargo charge would be enforced. U.S. officials said lawful traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remains open. Iran said it has the right to manage traffic through the strait and potentially charge fees. The legal basis for the charge remains disputed.

Based on highly productive conversations with Middle East leadership, I have decided to replace the 20% United States reimbursement fee with trade and investment deals that the various Gulf states will be making into the United States.

Express Tribune reported that Trump told reporters he would replace the proposed 20% reimbursement fee with trade and investment deals after talks with leaders from Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Bahrain and Kuwait — a factual variant from outlets describing an upcoming mandatory toll.

News18 quoted the UAE Ministry of Defence saying Iranian cruise missiles struck two Emirati tankers, the Mombasa and Al Bahiyah, in Omani waters; one Indian crew member was killed and eight others injured (six Indians, two Ukrainians). The report warned a 20% surcharge would raise landed costs for Indian refiners and could feed into domestic fuel prices.

Express Tribune also said an Iranian lawmaker submitted a bill to regulate transit through the Strait of Hormuz and that Oman reaffirmed neutral, transparent cooperation to restore freedom of navigation.
