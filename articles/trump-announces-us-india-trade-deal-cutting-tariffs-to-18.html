Trump announces US-India trade deal cutting tariffs to 18%

President Donald Trump said the United States would lower its reciprocal tariff on India to 18% from 25%. He also said Prime Minister Narendra Modi had agreed to move away from Russian oil and to purchase much more energy from the United States and, potentially, Venezuela. Mr Modi confirmed a new 18% tariff in a post on X but did not mention oil purchases. India had not publicly corroborated the president's oil claim. Some outlets described the move as cutting an effective 50% duty to 18%, a difference tied to a White House account that said a punitive 25% levy had been layered on top of a 25% reciprocal rate.

What Trump announced

Out of friendship and respect for Prime Minister Modi and, as per his request, effective immediately, we agreed to a Trade Deal between the United States and India, whereby the United States will charge a reduced Reciprocal Tariff, lowering it from 25% to 18%.
— Donald Trump

Mr. Trump framed the tariff move as part of a wider purchase compact, saying Mr. Modi had committed to buy more than $500 billion of U.S. energy, technology, agricultural and other products. Analysts cautioned that the headline figure was ambitious: Evan Feigenbaum warned that the details matter and described the $500bn target as a stretch. Ajay Srivastava of the Global Trade and Research Initiative said the Truth Social post left major questions unanswered and should be treated as a political signal until there is joint negotiated text. The Global Trade and Research Initiative noted that India's current annual imports from the United States are under $50bn, a scale point analysts say makes a $500bn pledge hard to reconcile with existing flows.

He agreed to stop buying Russian Oil, and to buy much more from the United States and, potentially, Venezuela.
— Donald Trump

Mr. Trump asserted that Mr. Modi had agreed to halt purchases of Russian oil when he announced the tariff change. Reuters reporting put India's Russian oil purchases at about 1.2m barrels per day in January, and projected them to fall to about 1m bpd in February and 800,000 bpd in March.

Modi's confirmation and omission

Wonderful to speak with my dear friend President Trump today. Delighted that Made in India products will now have a reduced tariff of 18%,
— Narendra Modi

Mr. Modi welcomed the reduction for Made in India goods, but his public note did not echo the president's statements about oil purchases; India had not publicly confirmed any agreement to stop buying Russian oil.

Why reports differed on the starting point

A White House official said Washington would rescind a punitive, 25% duty on all imports from India that had stacked on top of a 25% reciprocal tariff rate. That helps explain why some outlets described the headline change as from 50% to 18%, while Mr. Trump and other U.S. statements emphasised lowering the reciprocal 25% rate to 18%.

Markets, farms and factory floors

India's benchmark Nifty 50 closed 2.5% higher on Tuesday after the announcement, and the rupee strengthened 1% and was last trading at 90.26 per dollar. U.S. Agriculture Secretary Brooke Rollins said the deal would export more American farm products to India's market, lifting prices and helping reduce a roughly $1.3bn agricultural trade deficit. UBS economist Paul Donavon said the change should have limited impact on U.S. household affordability because Indian imports account for a small share of total U.S. imports.

Energy and oil figures

Shipping and trade trackers cited by Reuters show India's Russian oil imports were about 1.2m barrels per day in January, projected to decline to about 1.0m bpd in February and 800,000 bpd in March, a pace that makes any immediate shift in sourcing both costly and operationally complex.

Reactions: analysts and industry

Several commentators urged restraint in reading a single social post as a concluded treaty. Evan Feigenbaum cautioned that the details matter and described the $500bn target as a stretch. Ajay Srivastava of the Global Trade and Research Initiative warned that the Truth Social post left major gaps and should be treated as a political signal until negotiators produce a joint text. The Global Trade and Research Initiative also reiterated that India's current annual imports from the United States are under $50bn.

By contrast, market participants and industry groups highlighted the immediate economic signal. Nilesh Shah said the tariff cut removed a hanging sword over the rupee, equity and rates markets and called the outcome potentially win-win. Shilan Shah of Capital Economics said the move greatly boosts India's appeal as an alternative to China for supply-chain reconfiguration. The Confederation of Indian Textile Industry said the cut would help exporters compete more effectively in the U.S. market.

Consequences and forecasts

Forecasters and banking analysts offered a range of potential outcomes. The US-India Strategic Partnership Forum said both governments signalled this was only the beginning, with additional phases and negotiations anticipated. Trideep Bhattacharya of Edelweiss suggested that, together with the EU agreement, the moves could represent one of the strongest external growth stimuli for India in 2026. Other economists emphasised that the tariff cut may have only limited direct impact on U.S. consumer prices or household affordability.

Legal questions and enforceability

Legal experts and some Democratic lawmakers have questioned whether a president's public post can generate binding trade commitments without congressional approval, leaving uncertainty about whether the announcement can be turned into an enforceable treaty or statutory change without further action.

State of play

Taken together, the public statements converge on a newly stated 18% tariff rate. President Trump's post specifically points to an 18% reciprocal tariff. Prime Minister Modi's confirmation also pointed to 18%. A White House clarification about rescinding a punitive 25% duty helps explain why some outlets tallied a higher starting point. Market participants said the tariff cut removed a hanging sword over the rupee, equity and rates markets. Legal experts and some lawmakers have said those public announcements do not by themselves settle whether the deal is legally binding without congressional sign-off. Analysts are split: some treat the headline $500bn number skeptically, while others welcomed the immediate market relief and potential supply-chain gains. Both governments signalled that this is only the first phase, and further negotiations and text-level work are expected in the months ahead.
