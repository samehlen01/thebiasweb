Trump raises US tariffs on South Korean imports to 25% is the central development in a sudden trade standoff announced by President Donald Trump. He said he would increase U.S. tariffs on goods from South Korea from 15% to 25% and named vehicles, timber and pharmaceuticals among affected categories. Seoul said it had not received formal notice and sought urgent talks with Washington, while the White House had not yet issued a formal action to put any change into effect.

Because the Korean Legislature hasn't enacted our Historic Trade Agreement, which is their prerogative, I am hereby increasing South Korean TARIFFS on Autos, Lumber, Pharma, and all other Reciprocal TARIFFS, from 15% to 25%,
— Donald Trump, U.S. President

Despite the public post, no executive action had been posted by the White House as of Tuesday; officials noted that any new levies would require formal steps before taking effect.

Background and the memorandum

The announcement collided with a deal reached last October under which Seoul pledged about $350bn in investment to the United States and the two governments agreed to cut tariffs on many items to 15% as part of a memorandum of understanding. That agreement was submitted to South Korea's National Assembly on 26 November and, according to local media, was under review and expected to pass in February. South Korea's presidential office has insisted the arrangement is a memorandum rather than a treaty and therefore does not require parliamentary ratification.

By one count, total trade between the two countries was about $162bn as of late 2025, and tariffs are paid by importers, meaning U.S. firms would face a 25% tax on affected South Korean goods.

Stock markets reacted with volatility: Korean automakers' shares fell sharply at the open before paring losses, while the Kospi index reversed early losses and finished higher.

With a delegation on its way from Seoul to Washington, markets are viewing this latest twist as more carrot than stick,
— Derren Nathan, head of equity research, Hargreaves Lansdown

Many analysts echoed that view and said investors were sceptical the tariff threat would be enforced, treating the announcement as negotiating leverage rather than an immediate policy change.

Analysts and political readings

J. James Kim of the Stimson Center warned the move introduces fresh uncertainty for South Korean companies and will complicate investment decisions and logistics. Choi Seok-young, a former trade negotiator, said the White House message could be a political pressure tactic meant to extract concessions on non-tariff barriers. Deborah Elms and other trade experts argued that Mr. Trump has a history of making sweeping tariff threats and sometimes backing away, reducing the likelihood of full implementation. The ruling Democratic Party in Seoul said it would accelerate passage of a planned investment bill and aim to complete it by February, while opposition figures and some market analysts cautioned that a sudden outward shift of investments to the United States could further weaken the won; observers called the episode a setback to ties between the two allies.

Legal and procedural questions

Legal observers noted that many of the country-specific tariffs were imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act, and the U.S. Supreme Court was expected to rule soon on the law's use. White House officials said, speculatively, that if the court curtails that route they could turn to other legal authorities to impose levies. Analysts also speculated that Mr. Trump may not carry out every tariff threat he announces, while a source familiar with internal discussions suggested the step could have been prompted by South Korean regulatory action over a mass data leak at Coupang.

State of play

Across outlets, reporting agrees that the headline event is 'Trump raises US tariffs on South Korean imports to 25%'; President Trump publicly announced a tariff increase while Seoul says it had not received formal notice and no White House executive action had been published. The October investment memorandum and its roughly $350bn pledge remain on the record but are being reviewed by South Korea's legislature and are viewed differently in Seoul and Washington over whether parliamentary approval is required. Legal uncertainty remains because the Supreme Court may rule on the IEEPA authority and White House officials have said they could consider other legal paths; both capitals say talks are taking place and the next concrete steps will be either a formal implementing action from Washington or a court decision.

South Korea
Sources
Yonhap (State)

Yonhap reports Seoul will send Industry Minister Kim Jung-kwan and Trade Minister Yeo Han-koo to Washington to press for implementation of the trade agreement and investment pledge; Kim is due to meet Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick and Yeo will meet USTR Jamieson Greer. Yonhap said Trump's remark "We will work something out with South Korea" raised hopes for talks.

While the president lowered tariffs on Korea, the Koreans have made no progress on fulfilling their end of the bargain.
— White House official

Yonhap also highlighted the Coupang data‑breach probe: Seoul's investigators believe about 33.7 million customers were affected while Coupang says roughly 3,000 accounts were accessed; Republicans on the House Judiciary Committee posted on X that "This is what happens when you unfairly target American companies like Coupang."

Yonhap's market snapshot showed the KOSPI jumped to a record close on heavy volume, with foreigners net buying while retail investors net sold and bond yields easing.

The agency warned the tariff threat could imperil the $350 billion investment package.

South Asia
Sources
Republic World - World, The Hindu, News18 India

Indian outlets emphasised rollout risks and sector exposure. Republic World quoted Finance Minister Koo Yun-cheol saying it is 'unlikely' the $350 billion investment could begin in the first half because of administrative reasons and currency market volatility, and reported Bank of Korea Governor Rhee Chang-yong said he 'would not agree to any investment outflows to the U.S. if currency markets became unstable.'

  • Republic World — Koo Yun-cheol: 'it is unlikely' the $350bn rollout starts in H1 (administrative and FX concerns).
  • Republic World — Bank of Korea Governor Rhee Chang-yong: will not agree to investment outflows if currency markets destabilise.
  • The Hindu — autos account for 27% of South Korea's exports to the U.S.; the U.S. buys nearly half of Korea's car exports.
  • News18 India — places Korea among America's top‑10 trading partners and says the U.S. imports nearly $150 billion of Korean goods annually.

Together these items stress that central‑bank limits and administrative hurdles could slow or block the pledged U.S. investment and highlight Korea's heavy auto exposure to the U.S.; News18's ~US$150bn figure is higher than some other counts.
