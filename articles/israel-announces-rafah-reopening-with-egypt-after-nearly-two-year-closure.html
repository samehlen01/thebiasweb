Israel announces Rafah crossing reopening with Egypt after nearly two-year closure. COGAT, the Israeli defence ministry liaison, said the crossing will open for a limited flow of pedestrians in both directions starting on Sunday as part of an initial pilot.

COGAT said exit and entry via Rafah would be coordinated with Egypt and would require prior Israeli security clearance, with the movement monitored by a European Union mission and an additional screening and identification process taking place at a corridor under Israeli army control.

COGAT also clarified that returns from Egypt to Gaza would be limited to residents who left Gaza during the course of the war and that those returns would only take place after prior Israeli security clearance.

The actual passage of residents in both directions will begin upon completion of these preparations.
— COGAT (Israeli defence ministry)

COGAT described this initial phase as a pilot intended to test procedures and increase readiness ahead of any full operation, emphasising that further steps would depend on completing preparatory work.

Numbers and limits

Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu said he would allow 50 patients a day to leave Gaza. Sky News reported that other sources expect the number of people allowed to return could be about 150 per day. An Israeli defence official told reporters the crossing could physically hold between 150 and 200 people altogether going in both directions. An Israeli official speaking anonymously to the Associated Press said dozens of Palestinians would initially be allowed through each way, prioritising medical evacuees and those who fled during the war.

At 50 people per day it would take more than two years for 42,000 people who fled to return; at that same rate 6,000 people requiring urgent treatment would wait months. Palestinian medical sources say some patients will die before they can cross because of the limited throughput.

Humanitarian scale

The Palestinian health ministry has said there are about 20,000 patients waiting to leave Gaza for medical treatment. The Gaza health ministry and local sources report more than 71,000 people killed in Gaza since October 7, 2023. Articles report different tallies for deaths since the ceasefire: one report cites "more than 490" killed since the ceasefire, another cites "more than 500". USAID staff in early 2024 warned that northern Gaza had become an "apocalyptic wasteland" with dire shortages of food and medical aid. The opening is described as intended in part to help roughly two million or so displaced people who lack food, shelter and medicines.

Politics, control and reconstruction

The Rafah crossing lies in territory still held by Israeli forces after they pulled back behind the so-called 'yellow line', and Israeli troops still control more than half of Gaza. Prime Minister Netanyahu has said Israel's focus is on disarming Hamas and destroying its remaining tunnels and that without these steps there would be no reconstruction in Gaza. The opening has been tied to the second phase of a United States-brokered ceasefire plan, which reporting links to elements of President Trump's plan and to Israeli statements about the return of captive remains.

Several reports say the reopening is expected to allow a 15-member Palestinian technocratic body, the National Committee for the Administration of Gaza, to enter and begin overseeing day-to-day affairs for residents.

Reactions and opinions

Hamas called for the Rafah Crossing to be opened in both directions without restrictions and urged the guarantors of the ceasefire to pressure Netanyahu. Dr Mohammed Tahir, cited in reporting, said many people in Gaza fear that travelling abroad for medical care could become effectively permanent and that they may never be able to return.

In analysis, Ariel Cohen of Forbes argued that the 'New Gaza' reconstruction vision is unrealistic without first rebuilding core utilities and securing full disarmament, and he put likely costs at roughly $50–70 billion. By contrast, the special envoy Steve Witkoff has said he believes Hamas will have no choice but to demilitarize. Cohen warned that unless Hamas is fully disarmed private investors will likely avoid Gaza and that the U.S.-centred rebuilding architecture risks a stalemate; he also speculated that without full disarmament and removal of Hamas leadership reconstruction and private-sector investment are unlikely and future conflict is probable.

Operational mechanics and monitoring

Officials said the pilot will be monitored by the European Union mission under arrangements described as similar to a mechanism used in January 2025. Some officials speculated the numbers allowed through may be raised over time if the pilot is judged successful.

Discrepancies in reporting

Reports diverge on the expected scale. Sky News reported that the number allowed to return could be limited to about 150 per day, while Prime Minister Netanyahu has said 50 patients a day would be permitted; an Israeli defence official said the crossing could hold between 150 and 200 people in both directions, and an anonymous Israeli official told the Associated Press that dozens would cross initially. There is also a mismatch on how long Rafah has been effectively closed: some reports describe it as nearly two years of closure, while other accounts count the interruption as 842 days. Reporting after the ceasefire has likewise given slightly different short-term death tallies.

Those numeric differences matter: analysts point to long waits for returnees and medical evacuees if throughput is low, and commentators warn that uncertainty over disarmament and governance could deter the private investment needed for large-scale reconstruction.

State of play

Across reporting there is confirmation that COGAT says exit and entry via Rafah will be permitted in coordination with Egypt and under prior Israeli security clearance with EU supervision. Israel has announced a limited, pedestrian-only reopening scheduled to begin on Sunday. Hamas has called for the Rafah crossing to be opened in both directions without restrictions. The Palestinian health ministry has said about 20,000 patients are waiting to leave Gaza for treatment and local medical sources warn that some patients will die before they can cross if throughput remains constrained. Analysts disagree about reconstruction: some, writing in Forbes, see total costs of roughly $50–70 billion and say full disarmament is a precondition for viable private investment, while others, including a U.S. special envoy, express confidence that Hamas will be compelled to demilitarize. Officials have speculated the pilot may be expanded if judged successful, and the immediate next steps reported include EU monitoring, security screening at an Israeli-controlled corridor, and the planned entry of a 15-member Palestinian technocratic body to administer daily affairs.
