Former Conservative home secretary Suella Braverman defects to Reform UK, announcing she had resigned the Conservative whip and her party membership and saying she will represent Fareham and Waterlooville as a Reform MP with immediate effect.

Conservative response and retraction

The Conservatives' initial response included the sentence 'The Conservatives did all we could to look after Suella's mental health, but she was clearly very unhappy.' Hours later that sentence was removed and the party said the earlier version had been 'sent out in error' or was a 'draft' before issuing a corrected statement that read 'It was always a matter of when, not if, Suella would defect.'

The initial reference to Ms Braverman's mental health provoked criticism from politicians and mental-health charities and forced the Conservative Party to retract the line. Nigel Farage condemned the comment as 'pretty abusive' and said Reform would not 'lower ourselves' to that level. Brian Dow of Rethink Mental Illness said employers should never disclose details about the mental health of their employees or former staff and that using someone's mental health in political messaging 'trivialises' people's experiences.

Today I am announcing that I resign the Conservative whip and my party membership of thirty years, and because I believe with my heart and soul that a better future is possible for us, I am joining Reform UK.
— Suella Braverman

Her remarks framed the move as a principled decision after thirty years in the Conservatives, and media reports said her husband Rael Braverman had rejoined Reform alongside her.

Political reactions: parties and local officials

Manuel Martins, chairman of the Fareham and Waterlooville Conservative Association, said the defection 'came as both a surprise and a huge disappointment'. Liberal Democrat councillors have tabled a motion urging the council leader to write to Ms Braverman and ask her to resign, and Lib Dem leader David Hamilton said 'A by-election would give people in Fareham and Waterlooville a clear choice and that's how democracy should work'. Kemi Badenoch said she thought defections could be 'part of the ship being steadied' and that losing those who were not 'team players' might help steady the party. The BBC's Chris Mason argued that both Labour and the Conservatives feel 'discombobulated' and dubbed Nigel Farage the 'discombobulator-in-chief', while The Guardian argued Braverman's arrival was Farage's biggest political gamble and that she brings baggage which could limit Reform's appeal. Former Conservative minister Justine Greening wrote that the party could be helped electorally by shedding 'toxic' figures. Anna Turley said the move confirmed that Mr Farage was 'stuffing his party full of failed Tories', even as at least one Portsmouth News reader called it 'Excellent news. A great MP who's values and principles are in the right place'.

The centre right is finally uniting.
— Nigel Farage

Mr Farage also condemned the Conservatives' initial mental-health line as 'pretty abusive' and said Reform would not 'lower ourselves' to their level.

Implications and speculation

Media analysis said Braverman's defection adds to Reform's momentum and intensifies questions about whether the party can build a viable alternative government. Locally, politicians warned that trust in politics may be damaged when someone changes party and keeps their seat, prompting calls for a by-election. The Daily Mail reported speculation that Tory sources were monitoring ex-Cabinet figures including Gavin Williamson for possible defections. Other reporting has flagged Nigel Farage's May 7 deadline for defections, with some suggesting it is intended to consolidate switches before local elections. Ms Braverman previously served as attorney general under Boris Johnson and as home secretary during Liz Truss's brief premiership; she resigned in October 2022 after sharing a government document by personal email and was later sacked.

Disagreements and discrepancies

Accounts diverge on whether the move was a surprise: Manuel Martins said it 'came as both a surprise and a huge disappointment', while a corrected Conservative statement insisted 'It was always a matter of when, not if, Suella would defect.' The party's initial mental-health line, which it later said had been 'sent out in error' or was a 'draft', has been a focal point for criticism and retraction.

State of play

Reporting agrees that Suella Braverman has left the Conservatives and joined Reform UK and that the party later reissued a corrected statement after an initial line about her mental health drew criticism. Politicians, mental-health charities and local groups have made competing claims about the propriety and political impact of the messaging, Nigel Farage has framed the switch as part of a centre-right consolidation and some Conservatives argue defections can help steady the party. Speculation about further defections and about Mr Farage's May 7 deadline continues, and the next practical developments to watch are local motions seeking a by-election and whether other MPs move to Reform.
