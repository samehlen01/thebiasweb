U.S. Central Command said it launched another round of strikes on Iran on consecutive nights. The attacks were meant to further degrade Tehran's ability to attack ships in the Strait of Hormuz.

Iranian outlets Fars and Tasnim reported explosions and air-defence activity in several places.

Casualties and competing tolls

The U.S. military said the conflict had killed 18 U.S. service members. A Daily Mail report put the number at 17.

By contrast, Iran's Ministry of Health said U.S. strikes since late last month had killed 53 people in Iran, including three children.

Iran's Ministry of Health said U.S. attacks since late last month had injured 590 others.

A Daily Mail report said Iranian authorities had counted at least 50 dead and 517 wounded in the latest rounds of U.S. strikes.

Washington's stated rationale

CENTCOM said it targeted Iranian military command centres, maritime capabilities, missile and drone launch sites and air-defence systems. Secretary of State Marco Rubio said the U.S. was striking only assets used to attack global commercial shipping.

Trump said the United States would "bomb and destroy one bridge or power plant" for each Iranian attack on a ship in the Strait of Hormuz. He said Iran is badly damaged and wants desperately to meet, but the U.S. has no interest until negotiations are meaningful. He also said he expects Iran to be ready very soon for a deal. He said the United States does not need the Strait of Hormuz and tied the campaign to stopping Iran from getting a nuclear weapon.

A U.S. intelligence assessment said Tehran is unlikely to soften or make concessions quickly.

Tehran and regional escalation

Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf said no infrastructure would be safe and declared that the strait belongs to Iran.

Abbas Araghchi warned that any aggression would bring a powerful response and described the fight in eye-for-eye terms.

U.K. Maritime Trade Operations said a tanker was struck in the Strait of Hormuz and the crew abandoned ship.

The Joint Maritime Information Center in Bahrain said commercial traffic through the Strait of Hormuz fell to a three-week low.

Shipping through the Strait of Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb is slowing and vessels are avoiding the routes.

BBC and Daily Mail reports said Iran had targeted Bahrain, Kuwait and Jordan with drones or missiles.

Yemen's Houthis said they had hit Saudi oil tankers and declared a maritime embargo on Saudi Arabia.

Taken together, the reports suggest fighting is expanding through attacks on infrastructure and shipping across Bahrain, Kuwait, Jordan and the Red Sea.

Diplomatic fallout and mediation

The U.K. Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office said British staff had been temporarily withdrawn from Iran and that the embassy continued to operate remotely. It also warned British and dual British-Iranian residents about a significant risk of arrest, questioning or detention. That move leaves emergency consular help unavailable and travel disruption elevated.

Bloomberg Politics reported that Andy Burnham had approved the use of U.K. bases for the strikes and that bombers had left Britain.

UN Secretary-General António Guterres said attacks on civilian infrastructure were unacceptable and violated international law.

Pakistan and other mediators were trying to revive talks after the ceasefire and memorandum of understanding collapsed. One Pakistani official said the diplomatic apparatus had been active in recent days.

The U.S. and Saudi Arabia also signed a commercial nuclear cooperation agreement.

Markets and economic stakes

CNBC-linked analysis said oil, gasoline and diesel prices have all risen, increasing pressure on consumers.

Mark Zandi said there is nothing but downside for the U.S. and global economies. Higher fuel costs and war spending squeeze Americans' standard of living and savings at the same time.

By contrast, Mislav Matejka said investors should use geopolitical dips to add exposure because market risk is transitory.

Art Hogan warned that if oil stays above $85 (£63.56) to $90 (£67.29) into year-end, earnings estimates may be trimmed and the S&P 500 could correct. Jay Hatfield said a closed Red Sea could push oil above $100 (£74.77) a barrel, while B. Riley Wealth and Moody's Analytics said fuel-dependent sectors could lag if prices stay elevated.

Strategic debate and possible next moves

Richard Goldberg said regime collapse is not the current military objective. He said economic pressure or covert action would be the more plausible path if Washington wanted that result.

Fox News also reported, citing Financial Times columnist Gideon Rachman, that some in the Trump administration are debating whether to support Iran's downfall and an internal uprising.

Marko Papic said he is watching two things above all: whether Iran's hardliners gain power and whether the U.S. sends in more troops.
