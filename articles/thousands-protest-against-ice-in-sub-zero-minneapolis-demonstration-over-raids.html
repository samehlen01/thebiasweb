Thousands protest against ICE in sub-zero Minneapolis demonstration as demonstrators marched through Minneapolis in bitter cold to demand an end to President Donald Trump's immigration crackdown. Organisers said as many as 50,000 people took to the streets, though organisers acknowledged that figure could not be independently verified.

What happened

Organisers billed the day as an 'ICE OUT!' general strike. Many demonstrators later gathered indoors at the Target Center, a sports arena organisers noted has a capacity of 20,000.

Organisers said the marches followed weeks of sometimes violent confrontations between ICE agents and protesters. Thousands of federal officers have been deployed to Minnesota as part of 'Operation Metro Surge'. The ICE operation ordered by the Trump administration in Minnesota has been going on for more than six weeks, organisers said.

50,000
Organisers' crowd estimate
Organisers said the figure could not be independently verified.

Organisers said they expected around 50,000 people, but news outlets reported that it was not known on Friday night how many had actually attended.

Business closures: dozens or hundreds?

Some reporting described hundreds of businesses closing across Minnesota on the day, including an estimate of about 300 closures. Organisers and participants, by contrast, said dozens of businesses closed as part of the 'ICE OUT!' action.

Several major Minnesota-based Fortune 500 companies refrained from issuing public statements about the immigration raids.

Weather reports diverged

Al Jazeera reported the march began with temperatures as low as minus 29 Celsius (minus 20 Fahrenheit). The National Weather Service and reporting compiled by Forbes said afternoon temperatures reached around minus 9 degrees Fahrenheit and issued a cold weather advisory warning of wind chill values between 25 and 35 degrees below zero. The BBC and other outlets described conditions closer to roughly minus 23 Celsius (minus 10 Fahrenheit) during the marches.

Arrests at the airport

In one dramatic protest, local police arrested dozens of clergy who knelt, sang hymns and prayed on a road at Minneapolis–Saint Paul International Airport. Organisers, however, said about 100 clergy members were arrested at the airport. A spokesman for the airports commission told the Minnesota Reformer that the arrests took place after the permitted activity 'went beyond agreed upon terms' and were done to protect public safety and airport access.

Detentions and disputed targets

The Department of Homeland Security confirmed Border Patrol arrested Elvis Tipan Echeverria of Ecuador and that a two-year-old child was reunited with her father at a federal detention facility. The five-year-old boy taken into ICE custody has an active and pending immigration court case and cannot be legally deported yet, CBS News reported. ICE and DHS said they were seeking two men accused of sex crimes and had told the public that one target, Lue Moua, was at large. Minnesota Department of Corrections records indicate Moua is in prison and is scheduled for release in January 2027 with an active ICE detainer.

Renee Good shooting and investigations

Earlier this month, Renee Good, a 37-year-old U.S. citizen and mother of three, was shot multiple times by an ICE agent while behind the wheel of her car. An independent autopsy showed Good was shot three times — in the forearm, breast and head — and the Hennepin County Medical Examiner determined the manner of death was a homicide caused by multiple gunshot wounds. Reporting said Tracee Mergen, a supervisor in the FBI’s Minneapolis field office who attempted a civil rights inquiry into the officer, resigned amid pressure from leadership in Washington, D.C., to drop the case. The Independent reported the Department of Justice has issued subpoenas to at least five top Democratic Minnesota officials, including Governor Tim Walz and Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey. BBC reporting said at least six prosecutors in the Minneapolis US Attorney's office resigned over the administration's handling of the federal investigation.

Political reactions and contested narratives

Minnesota Attorney General Keith Ellison called 'Operation Metro Surge' a political tactic meant to punish Minnesotan leaders and described it as a 'federal invasion' that must stop. Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey said the federal actions are not about safety but 'political retribution' and accused the federal government of weaponizing its power against local leaders.

Republican strategist Tim Murtaugh said that if Democrats would honor ICE detainers, ICE would pick up people already in custody instead of searching the community. Kentucky Attorney General Russell Coleman said 'law enforcement works best when it works together' and urged collaboration with federal partners. Vice-President J.D. Vance visited Minneapolis to show support for ICE officers and called for local law enforcement to coordinate with federal officers.

President Donald Trump defended the ICE officer involved in Renee Good's shooting, saying Good 'viciously ran over the ICE Officer' and that the agent 'seems to have shot her in self defense'. Critics have alleged the administration's ICE operation is detaining migrants with no criminal record and even U.S. citizens. Hennepin County Attorney Mary Moriarty said there is 'no absolute immunity' for ICE agents who violate Minnesota law. The independent autopsy's finding that Good's death was a homicide stands in contrast to public defenses of the agent.

Speculation and forecasts

Former Virginia Attorney General Jason Miyares warned as a forecast that reversing cooperation with ICE would make the state less safe and predicted there would be violent crimes as a result. Virginia House Minority Leader Terry Kilgore said he could 'absolutely' envision scenes of unrest in his state after policy changes that limit cooperation with ICE.

Economic and human consequences

Restaurant owner Kim Bartmann said sales at her restaurants have dropped more than 30% over the past three weeks as a result of the ICE operation. A high school teacher at the protests described children of color as terrified, saying they are scared to wait at the bus, scared for their friends, and scared for their families.

Make no mistake, we are facing a full federal occupation by the United States government through the arm of ICE on unceded Dakota land.
— Rachel Dionne-Thunder, vice president of the Indigenous Protector Movement

State of play

Reporting confirms that large demonstrations took place in bitter cold as protesters demanded an end to the federal ICE operation. Organisers said they expected around 50,000 attendees and called the day an 'ICE OUT!' general strike, but organisers acknowledged that the headcount could not be independently verified. Several substantive disputes remain: Al Jazeera reported minus 29 Celsius (minus 20 Fahrenheit), the National Weather Service and Forbes recorded around minus 9 degrees Fahrenheit with wind chill warnings, and the BBC reported minus 23C (-10F); accounts also conflicted over whether an estimated 300 businesses closed or merely dozens of businesses closed, and federal statements that one target, Lue Moua, was at large contrast with Department of Corrections records showing the person scheduled for release in January 2027; reported subpoenas to at least five top Minnesota officials and the resignation of an FBI supervisor suggest continuing legal scrutiny.

Western Europe
Sources
ZDF Heute, Tagesschau (ARD), To Vima (Greece), The New Arab

European coverage emphasised new allegations about detention conditions and the operation's scale: reporting described an El Paso site presented as a roughly 5,000‑capacity facility on a military base where former detainees say they were allowed outside only once a week and were given long‑frozen food.

They gave us food that had been in the freezer for six months. As if we were wild animals.
— Former detainee

Reports also carried allegations that private contractors profit from large detention sites and warned of little local oversight; broadcast accounts relayed a U.S. government figure of roughly 3,000 arrests. Separate coverage said footage showed ICE officers threatening an Italian RAI crew (prompting political reaction in Italy) and described an apparently separate fatal shooting of a U.S. citizen, which has intensified scrutiny.

East Asia
Sources
Japan Times, Kyunghyang Shinmun (KR), The News Lens (Taiwan)

East Asian reporting added policy and legal frames not emphasised in some UK summaries: a Taiwan‑based analysis said a 2025 measure it labelled the 'One Big Beautiful Bill Act' had dramatically expanded ICE's resources, reporting the agency's 2025–26 budget rose to about $287 billion, a $100 billion 'unrestricted' fund was created, and staffing increased to roughly 22,000 — a change presented as central to the operation's scale.

That coverage summarised critics' legal concerns — administrative arrest warrants generally do not permit forced entry into private homes absent consent or emergency — and noted proposed measures (the 'No Secret Police Act' and the 'VISIBLE Act') intended to restrict masked federal agents.

Regional accounts also repeated organisers' larger statewide estimate that more than 700 businesses closed in solidarity and flagged an explicit presidential warning about invoking the Insurrection Act.

If Minnesota's corrupt politicians do not comply with the law and stop the agitators and rebels who attack the patriots of Immigration and Customs Enforcement carrying out their duties, I will, as many presidents have done in the past, invoke the Insurrection Act to swiftly end the tragedy unfolding in what was once a great state.
— Donald Trump, US President

The presidential quote underlines why some outlets foregrounded the risk of extraordinary federal measures; separate human‑interest reporting from the region quoted local volunteers — including a naturalised mother — saying they were scared but felt compelled to protect their community.

North America
Sources
CBC Canada, Globe and Mail

Canadian coverage highlighted sports‑industry and legal ripples: the players' union declared that players 'can no longer remain silent' and the NBA postponed and rescheduled a Timberwolves game after the reported killing of Alex Pretti. Court filings published domestically also say a judge barred transferring a detained toddler out of state, yet the child and father were placed on a commercial flight to Texas about 20 minutes later and flown back the next day.

NBA players can no longer remain silent.
— NBPA (players' union)

A national newspaper published fuller independent‑autopsy detail in one case, reporting that one bullet pierced the left side of the victim's head and exited on the right while other bullets struck the arm and breast — forensic specifics that add detail beyond earlier summaries.

Southeast Asia
Sources
Straits Times Singapore, Rappler Philippines

Southeast Asian reporting focused on community defence and electoral context: coverage said more than 100 volunteers now patrol south Minneapolis, handing out 'Know Your Rights' guides and escorting elders in a roughly 80,000‑strong Somali community, and quoted leaders warning the raids could intimidate Somali voters ahead of the 2026 midterms.

The power we have is to vote.
— Abdullahi Kahiye, community leader

A regional outlet also repeated organisers' claim that over 700 businesses statewide closed in solidarity — a higher statewide figure that widens the gap with other accounts reporting dozens or a couple of hundred closures.

South Asia
Sources
Hindustan Times, The Hindu

Indian reporting highlighted local small‑business solidarity: a Northeast Minneapolis Indian restaurant temporarily closed but distributed free samosas and meals to protesters and launched a GoFundMe that had raised more than $25,000 toward a $26,000 target to support staff during the shutdown.
