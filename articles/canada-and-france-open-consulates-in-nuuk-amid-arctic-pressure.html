Canada opened a consulate in Nuuk, with Foreign Minister Anita Anand traveling to the capital accompanied by Governor General Mary Simon to inaugurate the mission. France opened a consulate in Nuuk, where Jean-Noël Poirier has taken up duties as consul general, making France the first European Union country to establish a consulate general in Greenland. The steps come as former U.S. president Donald Trump has publicly insisted that Washington needs control of Greenland for security reasons, adding pressure to Arctic diplomacy.

Diplomatic missions and aims

Paris says the new mission will be tasked with deepening cooperation projects with Greenland in cultural, scientific and economic fields while strengthening political ties with local authorities.

The first item on the agenda will be to listen to Greenlanders, to hear them, to let them explain in detail their position,
— Jean-Noël Poirier, French consul general

Poirier framed the opening as a priority to listen to Greenlanders, saying the consulate would begin by hearing their position in detail.

Canada’s mission and timing

Ottawa had pledged to open a consulate in Greenland in 2024, and officials said the formal inauguration had been delayed from a planned November date because of bad weather.

Washington and the Arctic: history and pressure

The United States previously had a consulate in Nuuk from 1940 to 1953 and reopened a mission there in 2020; Iceland opened a consulate in Nuuk in 2013.

Officials said a US-Denmark-Greenland working group has been established to discuss Washington’s security concerns in the Arctic, though participants have not published its terms.

Since returning to office, Donald Trump has repeatedly argued that the United States needs Greenland for national security. In January he threatened to impose 10 percent tariffs that would rise to 25 percent on several European countries unless they allowed the U.S. to acquire Greenland. Last month he said he had stepped back from threats after claiming to have struck a 'framework' with NATO chief Mark Rutte.

Local reaction and polling

Polls show opposition to U.S. control: one survey found 58 percent of Americans oppose a U.S. takeover of Greenland, and a poll commissioned by the Danish newspaper Berlingske reported 85 percent of Greenlanders do not want incorporation into the United States.

Chagos, Diego Garcia and alliance strains

Under the Chagos agreement, the UK said it would transfer sovereignty of the British Indian Ocean Territory to Mauritius while leasing back the facility on Diego Garcia; the plan has been presented with a projected cost of about £35 billion over the next century.

Diego Garcia is the largest of the Chagos islands and hosts around 2,500 U.S. military and civilian personnel, serving as a hub for long-range bombers, logistics, intelligence collection and military communications across several regions.

However, if the lease deal, sometime in the future, ever falls apart, or anyone threatens or endangers US operations and forces at our base, I retain the right to militarily secure and reinforce the American presence in Diego Garcia,
— Donald Trump, former U.S. president

Downing Street said the UK and US would continue to work closely to guarantee the future operation of the base and to implement the agreement, while observers warned that public threats and heated rhetoric risked complicating implementation and creating diplomatic friction.

Opinions and analysis

Multiple outlets described the openings in Nuuk as a show of support and a signal of stronger diplomatic ties. Analysts said the new missions recognise Greenland’s growing autonomy and could give the territory opportunities to practice independence by diversifying ties beyond Denmark. U.S. administration figures argued that the United States needs Greenland for national security and suggested Denmark cannot adequately defend the island. Donald Trump publicly described the Chagos deal as profoundly misguided before later signalling support for the agreement. Warren Stephens, the U.S. ambassador to the UK, likewise condemned the UK's decision on the Chagos islands and used that position to argue for stronger U.S. strategic interests in Greenland. Conservative politicians characterised the Chagos arrangement as a strategic blunder and pledged to resist it.

Consequences and legal fights

Canadian officials said the new consulate could enhance cooperation with Greenland on climate change and Inuit rights. Analysts forecast that the arrival of more diplomatic missions could help Greenland reduce dependence on Denmark and give it practical experience in steps toward independence. Environmental groups said reopening previously protected marine areas to commercial fishing would likely prompt legal challenges.

Speculation and warnings

Natan Obed and some Inuit leaders warned speculatively that the United States might return to more serious territorial overtures and that Inuit Nunangat could be pivotal in such considerations. Observers speculated that Mr. Trump's Greenland push and tactics could threaten NATO unity and broader transatlantic cooperation. Analysts also suggested that economic pressure, including tariffs, could be used again to pursue U.S. strategic aims in the Arctic.

Differences of perspective

Denmark and Greenland have insisted that sovereignty and territorial integrity are a 'red line' and that Greenland is not for sale. The U.S. administration, by contrast, has argued that Greenland is strategically important and ought to be defended by Washington. Conservative politicians in the UK have described recent Chagos concessions as a security mistake and pledged to fight the deal.

What happens next

Officially, the working group is expected to continue talks but has not released details, and Downing Street said the two governments plan to keep coordinating to guarantee Diego Garcia's operation and implement the deal as leaders consult again.

State of play

Canada
Sources
Globe and Mail

Globe and Mail reported that more than 70 Inuit leaders from across Canada travelled to Nuuk to show solidarity at the opening of the Canadian consulate; the delegation chartered an Air Inuit plane, with 72 people signing up and covering their own costs. The paper said Greenland plans to open a consulate in Ottawa in 2028 and described Aviaq Brandt, an activist who stands each morning in front of the small U.S. consulate holding a large Greenland flag.

It was very hurtful. When you know that people are very proud of their identity and their culture and their homeland is so important, for it to be called a piece of ice, even for me, not even living here, was very, very, painful for people.
— Governor‑General Mary Simon

Globe and Mail said she added that families "were scared and people don't feel that it's over yet"; the paper reported the Inuit leaders travelled to Nuuk to show solidarity with Greenlanders in their battle with U.S. President Donald Trump.

France
Sources
Le Monde

Le Monde reported that Jean‑Noël Poirier arrived in Nuuk on Friday, took up his duties the same day and met Greenland's prime minister; the outlet said the consul currently has no office in Nuuk and that only nine French citizens are registered on the French abroad register there.

The political dimension of the opening of this post is real. It is even quite evident, given recent events.
— Jean‑Noël Poirier, Consul‑General

Le Monde noted that the small number of registered French and the lack of premises underline the consulate's symbolic political purpose rather than a large consular footprint.

Western Europe
Sources
Neue Zürcher Zeitung (NZZ), RTP Portugal (State)
We have collectively invested too little in the Arctic and the security of the Arctic. Therefore it is now high time to shift into a higher gear.
— Ursula von der Leyen, European Commission President

NZZ reported concrete European responses: 15 members of the Bundeswehr travelled to Nuuk (16–18 January) on a reconnaissance mission to explore frameworks for possible contributions to support Denmark, and TV2 reported Denmark planned a "substantial" troop deployment to Kangerlussuaq accompanied by Army chief Peter Boysen. NZZ also recorded domestic pushback — the boycott app "UdenUSA" topped Denmark's free-download charts — and noted that US Senators Lisa Murkowski and Jeanne Shaheen introduced a bill to bar a US president from annexing the territory of a NATO ally without that ally's consent.

RTP cited a YouGov poll finding public unfavourability toward the United States at decade lows (62% in France to 84% in Denmark) and that 41–55% across surveyed countries now favour prioritising European autonomy over preserving the transatlantic alliance.

European Union
Sources
Euronews

Euronews reported only eight French citizens are registered in Nuuk, though possibly around thirty live elsewhere in Greenland, and that until now French nationals relied on an honorary consul, Kristine Winberg, for basic administrative and consular assistance on behalf of the French embassy in Denmark. Euronews's count of eight is slightly lower than other reporting that put the number at nine.

Belarus
Sources
Nasha Niva (Belarus)

Nasha Niva published the new consul's comments that France would organise to be present if a crisis arises, that it aims to "understand the position of Greenlanders" and would be ready to support local people "if they and the Danish side so wish"; the outlet also reported the French consulate does not yet have a permanent address on the island.

If a crisis arises, we will organise to be on the scene.
— Jean‑Noël Poirier, French consul

Nasha Niva also quoted France's foreign minister describing the opening as a "political signal" linked to a desire to strengthen France's presence in Greenland.

Southeast Asia
Sources
Straits Times Singapore, The Manila Times

Straits Times reported about 50 people gathered for the Canadian consulate opening and applauded as the flag was hoisted, and noted Jean‑Noël Poirier had previously served as ambassador to Vietnam. The Manila Times described Canada's maple-leaf flag going up and dozens singing "O Canada," and reported that technical-level talks had begun between the U.S., Denmark and Greenland — saying the foreign ministers of Denmark and Greenland agreed to create a working group during a meeting with U.S. Vice‑President J.D. Vance and Secretary of State Marco Rubio before Trump's tariff threats.

The significance of raising this flag today and formally opening the consulate is that we will stand together with the people of Greenland and Denmark on many issues.
— Anita Anand, Canadian Foreign Minister

Those accounts portray the openings as both a ceremonial show of allied solidarity and a moment when technical contacts between Washington, Copenhagen and Nuuk were being advanced.

Latin America
Sources
G1 Brazil (Globo)

G1 recounted Jean‑Noël Poirier saying he was not afraid of the cold and contrasted Nuuk with his Libya posting — where his team had been hit by mortar shells — observing he would not need a bulletproof vest or helmet in Nuuk. The outlet also reported Canada announced in December plans to open consulates in Greenland and in Anchorage, Alaska to strengthen its Arctic presence.

South Asia
Sources
The Hindu

The Hindu reported that Anita Anand met Danish foreign minister Lars Løkke Rasmussen and posted on social media that, as Arctic nations, Canada and the Kingdom of Denmark are working together to strengthen stability, security and cooperation across the region.
