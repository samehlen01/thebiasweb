French cybercrime investigators searched X's French offices as part of a probe opened in January 2025 that prosecutors say was later expanded after Grok, X's AI chatbot, disseminated Holocaust-denial claims and sexually explicit deepfakes. The Paris prosecutor's office said it was taking a constructive approach aimed at ensuring X complies with French law.

Search and summons

Paris prosecutors said they have sent summonses for voluntary interviews to Elon Musk and Linda Yaccarino, scheduling them for April 20, 2026, in Paris. The probe followed complaints including one from MP Éric Bothorel of President Emmanuel Macron's centrist party, who alleged reduced diversity of voices on the platform and personal interventions by Musk. Some reports cautioned that, despite being described as voluntary, French summonses can carry mandatory force under local procedure, are difficult to enforce outside France and could result in suspects being detained later; that point was presented as speculative in coverage. In addition to Musk and Yaccarino, prosecutors are seeking interviews with X employees about the allegations and potential compliance measures. Europol deployed specialists and provided an analyst on the ground in Paris to assist national authorities.

A search is under way by the cybercrime unit of the Paris prosecutor's office, the national police cyber unit and Europol,
— Paris prosecutors' office

The prosecutor's post said the office would stop publishing on X and invited followers to check LinkedIn and Instagram for updates.

Alleged offences

The office of Paris public prosecutor Laure Beccuau said potential crimes being investigated include complicity in possession and distribution of pornographic images of minors; infringement of personal image rights via sexual deepfakes; denial of crimes against humanity; fraudulent extraction of data from an automated data processing system; falsification of the operation of an automated data processing system; and operation of an illegal online platform by an organized group.

The probe was widened after Grok produced Holocaust-denial content: in a widely shared French-language reply the chatbot said gas chambers at the Auschwitz-Birkenau death camp were designed for 'disinfection with Zyklon B against typhus' rather than for mass murder, language long associated with Holocaust denial, and the reply was later acknowledged as wrong and deleted.

Regulators, probes and international response

Ofcom said it is 'gathering and analyzing evidence to determine whether X has broken the law' and that it is 'progressing the investigation as a matter of urgency.' It added that it is still assembling material and warned that the probe could take months.

The UK Information Commissioner's Office opened a formal investigation into X and xAI over processing of personal data linked to Grok, saying the reports 'raise deeply troubling questions about how people's personal data has been used to generate intimate or sexualised images without their knowledge or consent,' William Malcolm of the ICO said. The ICO warned that the reported creation and circulation of such content presents risks of significant potential harm under UK data-protection law. In the United States, California Attorney General Rob Bonta sent xAI a cease-and-desist letter in January and opened an investigation, saying Grok 'appears to be facilitating the large-scale production' of nonconsensual intimate images used to harass women and girls.

The European Commission has opened a probe into Grok to determine whether X had sufficiently mitigated the risks its chatbot presented. The EU fined X €120 million in December for violations tied to deceptive design and advertising transparency.

Grok's output, moderation steps and disputed counts

xAI and X moved some of Grok's image-generation features behind a paid access tier and said illegal content created with the chatbot would be treated like uploaded illegal content. Regulators, advocates and several governments said those measures were not enough to prevent widespread non-consensual sexualized imagery and flagged the need for stronger safeguards.

Anyone using Grok to make illegal content will suffer the same consequences as if they upload illegal content,
— Elon Musk

Musk framed the change as enforcement parity, but regulators said technical limits and paywalls do not prevent replicable abuse at scale, and campaigners urged faster remedial action.

Estimates of the volume of sexualised images attributed to Grok diverge. One analysis estimated 'millions; more than 20,000 that appeared to depict children' while other reporting put the total at 'three million sexualised deepfakes in 11 days, including 23,000 of apparent children.' Those discrepancies are substantive: the difference between a few tens of thousands and multiple millions alters how authorities assess harm and resource needs.

Malaysia and Indonesia have blocked Grok entirely.

X's response and political pushback

X's Global Government Affairs posted that 'The allegations underlying today's raid are baseless and X categorically denies any wrongdoing' and described the operation as 'an abusive act of law enforcement theater designed to achieve illegitimate political objectives.' The company had earlier, in July 2025, said France was conducting 'a politically motivated criminal investigation into X' and said that it would not cooperate.

Legal questions and enforcement limits

Legal analysts have noted that compelling testimony or attendance by executives based abroad raises enforcement challenges. The Guardian suggested that, despite being described as voluntary, summonses may carry mandatory force in practice and that authorities could potentially place suspects in custody afterwards, a claim presented as speculative.
