This article covers the episode Trump warns UK it is 'very dangerous' to do business with China after Starmer's visit and the diplomatic, trade and political reverberations that followed. It centres on President Donald Trump's public warning after Prime Minister Keir Starmer's trip to Beijing and Shanghai. Mr Starmer, the first British prime minister to travel to Beijing in eight years, led a delegation of nearly 60 business executives. Downing Street said the visit secured visa-free travel for British citizens visiting China for fewer than 30 days and halved tariffs on Scotch whisky to 5%. Starmer described the results as modest wins, saying the trip yielded some £2.2 billion of export deals and had laid the groundwork for further business.

Well, it's very dangerous for them to do that,
— Donald Trump

The comment provoked immediate responses in Britain. Labour MP Chris Bryant said Mr Trump was wrong and pointed out the president had previously described Xi Jinping as a friend and, as Mr Bryant said, was expected to visit China himself. Keir Starmer sought to play down the row, arguing Mr Trump was probably talking more about Canada than the United Kingdom. Howard Lutnick said the Chinese market is very difficult for exporters and suggested it was unlikely British firms would quickly win new business; some outlets and commentators warned that the president's unpredictability could create nerves inside Downing Street.

Trade and investment

Downing Street framed the outcomes as a cautious reset, saying ministers had agreed to continue enhancing co-operation on areas of mutual interest while maintaining frank and open dialogue on areas of disagreement. The trip produced specific measures: officials said the UK and China would share intelligence aimed at identifying the supply routes used by people smugglers, and ministers noted that inflatable dinghy components used in some small-boat crossings can be traced to suppliers in China. Business groups in Mr Starmer's delegation welcomed improved market access even as some opposition politicians warned the trip did not resolve national-security and human-rights concerns.

AstraZeneca and investment claims

AstraZeneca's announced expansion became a focal point for business coverage, yet outlets gave different totals. CNBC and Fox News reported the company would invest $15 billion in China through 2030 to expand medicines manufacturing and research and development, while the Evening Standard described the pledge as 100 billion yuan (£10.4 billion) by 2030 for the same purposes. Both accounts identify AstraZeneca as the actor but attribute materially different sums.

Sanctions, legislatures and security

China's foreign ministry said the two sides had agreed in principle to resume normal exchanges between the legislatures of the two countries. That statement sits uneasily with a forceful joint reply from British MPs and peers who remain under Chinese sanction: they published a statement saying they would rather remain under sanction than have their status used as a bargaining chip to secure diplomatic or economic concessions.

Opposition MPs renewed criticism of the trip on human-rights and security grounds, arguing the government had not sufficiently pressured Beijing over abuses in Xinjiang. Officials travelling with the prime minister were reported to use temporary 'burner' phones and laptops to reduce the risk of their devices being compromised, and Mr Starmer said the UK should seize economic opportunities while remaining mindful of national security.

Analysis and wider perspective

Analysts and consultants framed the visit as a calibrated approach rather than a wholesale reorientation of UK foreign policy. Yue Su of the Economist Intelligence Unit described these engagements as managed, selective resets prompted by uncertainty about US policy, and Gabriel Wildau of consultancy Teoneo said Starmer is wary of alienating Washington and that the UK's squeezed position between the US and China is structural.

Where reporting stands

Across reporting there is agreement that Starmer's trip secured visa-free travel and a halving of tariffs on Scotch whisky to 5%, and Downing Street said the talks would continue to enhance co-operation while maintaining frank dialogue. Sanctioned MPs insisted they would rather remain under sanction than be used as a bargaining chip, and opposition MPs criticised the visit over national security and Xi's human-rights record; outlets also published differing figures for an AstraZeneca pledge, one reporting $15 billion and another 100 billion yuan. Analysts said the trip looked like a managed, selective reset rather than a strategic pivot, Starmer said he had laid the groundwork for billions of pounds of business, and some reporting warned the president's unpredictability could create nerves inside Downing Street.
