The event, Hegseth tells Senate Iran war has cost $37.5bn (£28 billion), unfolded as Pete Hegseth told senators that the Iran war had already cost $37.5bn (£28 billion) and that the Pentagon needed emergency money to keep the campaign going. The hearing was interrupted by protesters, and lawmakers pressed him hard over both the price tag and the strategy behind it. Hegseth said the figure was higher than the Trump administration's $29bn (£21.7 billion) estimate in May, a reminder of how fast the bill had risen as the conflict dragged on. What should have been a routine budget appearance instead became a public test of how much support the war still had, and how much patience Congress had left for the numbers attached to it.

Reports described the emergency request as about $87bn (£65 billion) overall, with some rounding it closer to $90bn (£67.2 billion), and about $67bn (£50.1 billion) of it was tied to Middle East operations. Hegseth said the extra money would keep training from being curtailed, expand production lines and speed the delivery of the munitions U.S. forces need most. The Pentagon also warned that without the funding it could face critical shortfalls, struggle to pay service members, replenish equipment and munitions, and sustain operations without disruption. The scale of the ask showed that the war was no longer just consuming ammunition; it was also shaping the industrial and personnel systems needed to keep it going.

Pentagon says it needs the money

Hegseth and Joint Chiefs chair Dan Caine said the request was meant to avoid critical shortfalls in an active war. Hegseth also argued that the spending was needed to deter adversaries, rebuild readiness and keep U.S. forces ahead of threats. They said the money would help pay service members, replace worn equipment and munitions, and keep operations moving without disruption. Hegseth's warning about training cuts made the same point from another angle. Together, the administration's case was that readiness and sustainment were already under strain and that delay would only deepen the problem.

Democratic senators challenge the package

Democratic senators rejected that framing. Patty Murray said the package did not make much sense because, in her view, it bundled items that had little to do with Iran and let the administration dodge the normal appropriations process. Kirsten Gillibrand said the White House wanted unlimited money for bombs and argued that Americans could not keep paying for the war while families struggled to feed themselves. Gary Peters was even harsher, calling Hegseth the failure because he believed the Pentagon lacked both a winning strategy and a long-term plan. Their objections combined process criticism with a broader argument that the package was being pushed before lawmakers had been given a convincing strategic case.

Chris Murphy widened the criticism into a challenge to the war itself. He called it illegal and unpopular and said Congress could end it simply by refusing to fund it. Murphy also argued that the war aims had been reduced to reopening the strait, with little hope of meaningfully degrading missile and drone capacity. Other senators on the panel said the war risked turning into another forever conflict, one that could cost American lives and billions of taxpayer dollars without a clear way out. In their telling, the problem was not just whether the war could be won, but whether it should continue at all.

Murphy said the conflict had already stripped U.S. missile and interceptor stocks enough to leave the country unable to fight another front if a new confrontation broke out today. He warned that rebuilding those inventories could take the better part of a decade, and he said casualties could increase sharply in the next few days and weeks if damaged air defences made Iranian fire easier to get through. He also said the war was already showing up in the broader economy, with gas, diesel and fertilizer prices up and farm bankruptcies rising. Those warnings tied battlefield depletion to the sort of costs people feel far from the front line, from military readiness to farm finances.

Hegseth defends the war powers

Hegseth answered that he was asking for the right weapons at the right time and said the best way to create peace was to prepare for war. He accused Democrats of refusing to fund the troops and turning the issue into partisan politics, while the administration said it already had the Article II authority needed to continue the operation without explicit congressional approval. The response tried to recast the debate as deterrence rather than escalation, and it made clear that the White House sees the fight as one over power as much as policy.

Both chambers of Congress, however, passed a war-powers resolution urging authorization. That vote underlined how far the legislature was willing to push back on the White House's claim that it could keep operating on its own. The clash now reaches beyond one request and into the deeper question of who gets to decide whether the war continues under executive control or legislative supervision.

Regional toll and wider stakes

Outside Washington, reports said hostilities resumed after a ceasefire collapsed and that strikes then continued for 10 consecutive days. Those same reports said at least three service members had died recently and at least 18 had died since the war began. The numbers point to a conflict that is still active and still costly, even as lawmakers in Washington argue over how to respond. In that sense, the hearing was taking place against a live war, not a distant one.

The Los Angeles Times said a June U.S.-Iran shipping deal collapsed, after which Iran fired on commercial vessels and pursued a toll system. It also said the war had raised concern about water and power infrastructure and warned it could inflame the global economy and harden into another forever conflict. That reporting linked military escalation to trade and energy concerns, showing why the dispute has spread beyond the battlefield. The fallout is no longer just a matter of missiles and drones; it is also about shipping, public services and the price of instability.

Donald Trump added a separate layer of uncertainty, saying that if the U.S. left now Iran would need 20 to 25 years to rebuild. NPR also reported him saying Iranian leaders were desperately trying to reach a deal, although he said he would not meet them until talks were meaningful. His comments suggested that negotiations, not just airstrikes, are now part of the pressure campaign. They also hinted at a long timetable, not a quick fix, which is why the conflict is being discussed in such strategic terms.

State of play

Across the reporting, the clearest points are that Hegseth told senators the war had already cost $37.5bn (£28 billion), the Pentagon is seeking emergency money, and the administration says it has Article II authority even as Congress has passed a war-powers resolution urging authorization. Democrats dispute the package's logic and legality, Hegseth says the spending is necessary, and lawmakers remain divided over who should decide the war's next phase. What remains uncertain is how long the war will continue and how much farther its costs, casualties and regional fallout will spread. The next step is a congressional test of the request, with the legal fight still hanging over it.
