Storm Chandra hit parts of the UK on January 26, bringing heavy rain and strong winds. The Met Office warned the wet weather could lead to surface-water flooding and travel delays. Forecasters said most places would see 10 to 20mm of rain, with a few spots exceeding 30mm, and an amber warning for parts of the south-west predicted 30 to 50mm. Dorset Council said it had logged about 100 flooding incidents and more than 40 road closures after rivers burst their banks.

What the forecasts said

Provisional Met Office figures show January rainfall was well above average in some regions: Northern Ireland recorded 195.6mm and southern England 136.8mm. Dr Amy Doherty at the Met Office said the month's wetness followed a persistent Atlantic pattern and a strong jet stream that repeatedly steered low-pressure systems toward the UK. The Met Office warned the wet conditions were likely to cause surface-water flooding and delays on roads and rail.

Those totals left southern England much wetter than normal for January, and England overall well above typical monthly totals.

Rain arriving from the south overnight Wednesday into Thursday is likely to turn heavy and persistent in places for a time during Thursday morning.
— Met Office

That forecast underpinned amber and yellow rain warnings for parts of the south-west and other exposed coasts. The warnings covered areas of Dorset and much of the south-west of England, where forecasters flagged the heaviest bands of rain.

Local impacts: Dorset and beyond

Dorset Council said crews responded to about 100 flooding incidents and put more than 40 roads out of use as rising water overtopped riverbanks. Local teams worked around the clock to make routes safe and to protect properties where they could.

There's nothing you can do; you can't stop the forces of nature.
— Melissa Foot, Cut Loose Unisex Hair Studio owner

Barbara Smith, the Woolpack pub owner, said the damage to her business had been devastating and that community support had been vital. Other local businesses reported being forced to close for days while cleanup continued.

Offshore operations and supplies

Ithaca said it would reduce personnel offshore to essential staff only until conditions improved and supply routes could operate reliably. TotalEnergies said it had rearranged activity and reduced the number of personnel on its Elgin platform to manage water stocks for cleaning and cooking. Graham Skinner of Offshore Energies UK said the prolonged run of stormy weather had been felt onshore and in the North Sea. Operators and trade bodies reported supply transport problems that had begun to cause shortages of fresh food and fresh water on some installations.

things like fresh food and fresh water had begun to run short.
— Graham Skinner, health and safety manager, Offshore Energies UK (OEUK)

OEUK said waves reached up to 6m and that the prolonged period at that level had affected supply boats and logistics for North Sea assets.

Transport and community disruption

Great Western Railways apologised for disruption and suspended services between Exeter St Davids, Barnstable and Crediton because of floodwater. The operator warned those services were not expected to resume until the following Wednesday while lines and drainage were made safe.

Ferry operator CalMac issued yellow and amber warnings and warned of disruption and cancellations to many of its services. The company advised travellers to expect delays and to check before setting out.

Organisers cancelled the Didcot and Grove Fields parkruns after inspections found the courses very waterlogged, slippery and muddy. Community event planners said they were prioritising safety while grounds recover.

Alerts in Wales, Surrey and Ireland

Natural Resources Wales issued flood alerts for Swansea Bay, the Pembrokeshire coast and the Gower peninsula, warning that flooding of low-lying land and roads was expected around the high tides from February 3 to February 5. Local teams were preparing temporary measures and advising residents in low-lying areas to be ready to act.

The Environment Agency issued 13 flood alerts across Surrey as prolonged rain kept river levels high, and people were advised to take the usual flood precautions. Local highways teams were monitoring vulnerable stretches of road.

Local authorities in Ireland said they were on 'full alert', with Dublin and Wicklow highlighted as areas of particular concern and Thursday night described as a pinch point. Officials said the combination of rainfall, high tides and onshore winds was likely to be the main short-term risk.

Aquadams were moved to Enniscorthy in Co Wexford and a programme to construct temporary flood-defence banks in Bridgetown was due to begin as a precautionary measure by local authorities.

Safety warnings and local response

We urge people not to drive though flood water. It is often deeper than it looks and just 30cm of flowing water is enough to float your car.
— Chris Wilding, Environment Agency

Councillor Joy Dennis, the cabinet member for highways and transport, said crews were doing all they could to keep roads open and would inspect and repair damage once conditions allowed. She added that while teams operate in difficult conditions there would be times when longer-term fixes would have to wait for safer working conditions.

Regional outlook

The Met Office forecast rain and sleet for Bolton, with the chance of precipitation rising to 70 to 80 per cent by 8 to 10pm and sleet and snow showers most frequent between 9pm and 1am. In Edinburgh, the forecast called for light rain changing to sleet, a top temperature near 4°C and gusts up to 32mph.

Forecasts and outlook

Paul Moore, a Met Éireann climatologist, said model runs show a split in the polar vortex around mid-February and that there is a possibility of cold air being pulled west over Ireland. A Met Office spokesperson said a subtle southward shift of low-pressure areas in the second week of February may allow colder air to spread across larger parts of the UK and raise the risk of wintry hazards. The Met Office also said cyclonic patterns in mid-February could produce more showers or longer spells of heavy rain.

Agency and business actions

Aquadams were brought to Enniscorthy and temporary banks were scheduled for construction in Bridgetown. Ithaca said it would keep only essential staff offshore until supply routes were reliable. TotalEnergies said it had reduced numbers on the Elgin platform and adjusted its activity schedule to manage supplies.
