The United States and Iran agreed to hold indirect nuclear talks in Muscat, Oman, on Friday to reduce the immediate risk of military confrontation. Diplomats from Washington and Tehran will meet in Muscat on Friday, U.S. and Iranian officials said. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi confirmed the talks were scheduled in Muscat at about 10am Friday and thanked Oman.

How the talks were salvaged

U.S. sources said the arrangements nearly unraveled after Washington initially resisted Iran's request to move a planned meeting from Turkey to Oman and to narrow the list of participants. Iranian negotiators had insisted on holding the exchange in Oman and keeping the conversation strictly to the nuclear file in a bilateral format. The decision to proceed was reportedly reached only after several Arab capitals intervened, with at least nine regional governments urging the White House not to cancel.

The diplomatic push comes as Iran continues to confront unrest at home: the Human Rights Activists News Agency said it had confirmed at least 6,883 deaths and more than 50,000 arrests in the country's recent crackdown on nationwide protests.

Diplomats and envoys on the move

White House spokespeople said special envoy Steve Witkoff remained engaged in preparatory diplomacy, and multiple reports indicated other U.S. envoys were expected to travel regionally ahead of the Muscat meetings.

A dangerous backdrop: military deployments and incidents

U.S. forces have routed additional assets into the region, deploying an aircraft carrier strike group centered on the USS Abraham Lincoln plus destroyers and aircraft across the eastern Mediterranean, Red Sea and Arabian Sea. Open-source imagery showed U.S. logistical platforms positioned throughout the area. In recent days those buildups coincided with risky encounters: the U.S. military shot down an unmanned Iranian drone that approached an American aircraft carrier, and Iranian gunboats were reported to have attempted to halt a U.S.-flagged oil tanker in the Strait of Hormuz.

The International Atomic Energy Agency's most recent assessment put Iran's total enriched uranium at roughly 9,870 kilograms and reported that a portion of it had been enriched to around 60% purity.

Agenda clash: what each side wants

Marco Rubio said U.S. officials would press Iran on ballistic missiles, sponsorship of militant groups and the country's treatment of its own people. Iranian officials, for their part, insisted the talks be held in Oman and limited strictly to the nuclear file and treated demands to limit missiles and proxy activity as breaches of sovereignty. Analysts noted that a bilateral, nuclear-only format would mean other U.S. priorities, including ballistic missiles, proxy activity and human rights, may not be negotiated at this stage.

Voices in Washington and beyond

Analysts said the talks could de-escalate the threat of military confrontation if they produce credible commitments. Some analysts warned the meetings could be a tactical distraction ahead of potential military action rather than a genuine de-escalation. U.S. officials expressed skepticism that a meaningful deal is possible and said they would not waste time on an insincere negotiation. J.D. Vance said the diplomacy was very complicated because Iran's supreme leader cannot be negotiated with directly. Retired Gen. Jack Keane advocated for a joint U.S.-Israel military strike to dismantle the Iranian regime and its program. Several Arab states lobbied the White House to proceed with the meeting to avoid potential wider conflict. Analysts said oil markets will remain volatile and continue to embed a risk premium even if talks reduce immediate tensions. A U.S. official said the administration agreed to the meeting in part to be respectful to allies and to continue pursuing diplomacy. President Trump has repeatedly threatened military action and said Iran's Supreme Leader should be very worried.

Markets, strategy and consequences

Market strategists warned that if talks fail or fighting spreads, especially attacks on tankers or other shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, oil prices could spike sharply. Even if immediate danger eases, analysts said markets would likely keep a risk premium and remain choppy. Some observers added that a genuine diplomatic breakthrough could offer an off-ramp that averts U.S. military strikes, while Iran's threats and preparations may be designed to raise the cost of war even while signalling willingness to talk. Several Arab leaders' pleas to Washington helped push U.S. officials to keep the meeting on the calendar because they feared a wider regional conflagration.

Speculations and scenarios

Tehran presented the Muscat sessions as the opening stage of a longer diplomatic process rather than a single, definitive settlement. Market commentators cautioned that investors may be over-interpreting early diplomatic signals and that a strike remains possible despite the talks. Reports said Iranian authorities vowed that, if attacked, Iran could retaliate by striking U.S. military assets and Israel or by disrupting regional shipping. Observers warned that because Iran's supreme leader does not sit at the negotiating table, any agreement may need his later approval and therefore could be fragile. Some Iranian officials and analysts speculated Tehran would insist on retaining its right to enrich uranium domestically and would refuse to ship its enriched stockpile to a third country.

I'm grateful to our Omani brothers for making all necessary arrangements.
— Abbas Araghchi, Iranian foreign minister

Araghchi's message underscored Tehran's public posture, thanking a neutral host while signalling that the talks should remain narrowly framed and bilateral. That posture helps explain the gap between what U.S. officials say they want to address and the limits Tehran has placed on the agenda.

State of play

Most outlets report that the United States and Iran have set a meeting in Muscat this Friday to try to de-escalate the immediate risk of military confrontation. Tehran has publicly insisted the discussions be limited to the nuclear dossier, which would mean U.S. priorities on ballistic missiles, proxy activity and human rights may not be negotiated at this stage. U.S. envoys were reported to be travelling in the region ahead of the session. Analysts warned markets and governments will watch closely to see whether the talks produce a substantive off-ramp or merely delay confrontation.
