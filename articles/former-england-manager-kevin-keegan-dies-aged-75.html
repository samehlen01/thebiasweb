Kevin Keegan, the former Liverpool and Hamburg star who won 63 England caps and later managed England, Fulham, Manchester City and Newcastle, died aged 75 after a battle with cancer.

Kevin had been battling cancer and was surrounded by his wife and daughters in his final moments.
— Keegan family

Earlier in January 2026, Keegan said he had stage four cancer and would soon begin treatment.

Early life and family

Born on 14 February 1951 in Armthorpe, near Doncaster, Keegan was spotted at 16 playing amateur football for Pegler, his employer at the time, before moving on to Scunthorpe and, later, Liverpool.

He met Jean Woodhouse at Doncaster fair, and their marriage lasted 51 years.

Playing career

At Liverpool, he won three First Division titles, two UEFA Cups, the FA Cup and the club's first European Cup in 1977. He also scored 100 goals in 323 appearances for the club. His transfer to Hamburger SV was a then-record British switch.

At Hamburg, he won the Ballon d'Or in 1978 and 1979, becoming one of the first English players to achieve such international recognition.

Keegan won 63 England caps and scored 21 goals. He captained his country 31 times, and in 1982 he was named PFA Players' Player of the Year after scoring 30 goals in all competitions.

Management

He later moved into management, taking charge of England, Fulham, Manchester City and Newcastle. At Newcastle, he guided the club to promotion as First Division champions in 1993 and built an attacking side that finished runners-up in 1995 to 96.

Tributes and memories

Sir Alex Ferguson said he had deep respect for Keegan and thought the Newcastle work was fantastic. Alan Shearer called him his hero, manager and friend. Eddie Howe said Keegan had inspired him and had shown warmth and generosity.

Kenny Dalglish said Keegan was one of the best players of his generation and also a wonderful person. Phil Thompson said he was the best person he had known in football or sport and that humility defined him. Jamie Carragher described Keegan as one of the greatest players this country has ever produced.

John Anderson called him a humble legend who put people at ease. Lee Clark said he became a hero and father figure to him. Newcastle United said Keegan had been the beating heart of the club and had changed how supporters saw it. Manchester City said he had transformed its mood by bringing optimism and vibrancy. Big Star Events described him as humble, helpful, generous with his time and down to earth.

At St James' Park, supporters described Keegan as a local legend whose influence reached far beyond the pitch. They left flowers, shirts and scarves outside the ground. Among Doncaster voices, civic leaders called him Armthorpe's own and one of South Yorkshire's greatest sporting sons. John Ryan said he had known Keegan for years, remembered him as a Rovers fan and regretted that he never played for the club. Jeremy Clarkson commented on where he fit among Doncaster's famous names.

In The Guardian's opinion pages, Rev Henry Corbett said Keegan avoided publicity and used his fame to encourage young people. Jennifer Basannavar said he learned German to communicate better and left a positive legacy in Germany.

Aftermath and memorials

  • Newcastle City Council said it would open condolence books and fly the flag at half mast.
  • Newcastle United said it would open a memory book and asked fans to keep laying floral tributes.
  • Big Star Events cancelled Keegan's Norwich meet-and-greet.
  • The Cresset Theatre said it would contact ticket holders about refunds and alternatives.
  • Stephen Hay said the ride he had planned would now also remember Keegan and others lost to cancer.
  • Henry Gallagher said he hoped Newcastle's match against Liverpool could become a celebration of Keegan's life.
  • Supporters and campaigners began calling for a statue, a stand or a plaque in Newcastle and Doncaster.

Newcastle United said it would consider a lasting tribute.
