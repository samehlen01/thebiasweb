US judge bars death penalty for Luigi Mangione in CEO killing case. U.S. District Judge Margaret Garnett on Friday dismissed the federal counts that exposed Luigi Mangione to a potential death sentence, removing capital punishment as an option in the federal prosecution; Mangione still faces federal stalking counts that carry a maximum penalty of life in prison without parole. Garnett concluded the federal stalking charges did not meet the statutory definition of a crime of violence.

Judge's reasoning

In an opinion explained at length, Garnett said Counts Three and Four could be capital-eligible only if the stalking counts themselves met the federal statutory 'crime of violence' test as a matter of law, and she concluded that requirement was not met. The judge also gave the government a 30-day window to decide whether to challenge her exclusion of the death-eligible counts.

The analysis contained in the balance of this opinion may strike the average person — and indeed many lawyers and judges — as tortured and strange, and the result may seem contrary to our intuitions about the criminal law,
— Judge Margaret Garnett

Garnett framed the ruling as a matter of statutory interpretation rather than an assessment of whether the conduct felt violent to an ordinary observer, saying the statute's text controls the legal outcome.

She also stressed that acknowledging the brutality of the underlying killing did not resolve the legal question: the court can recognize violent criminal conduct yet still conclude the statutory predicate for a capital charge is not satisfied.

Evidence and the backpack

Garnett refused to suppress evidence seized from a backpack recovered from Mangione at the time of his arrest, writing that the search was reasonable under the facts of this case and allowing prosecutors to introduce a 3D-printed handgun, a loaded magazine, writings and other items at trial. Defense attorneys argued the search and the chain of custody were improper, and they said officers initially reported finding only a magazine before a later search at a police station uncovered the gun and additional material.

That tension — a judicial finding that the search met legal standards and the defense's account of inconsistent discovery details — is a substantive factual discrepancy the defense says undermines the prosecution's handling of the evidence.

Prosecutors have told the court the notebook and writings taken from the bag could show intent and, in their view, amount to admissions tying Mangione to the killing; the judge's decision to let those materials in means jurors will see the documents prosecutors say are central to their case.

Timeline and related incidents

Brian Thompson was shot and killed on Dec. 4, 2024, in midtown Manhattan as he walked to an investor conference; surveillance showed a masked gunman shooting him from behind, and police said ammunition recovered at the scene had the words 'delay,' 'deny' and 'depose' written on it. Mangione was arrested days later at a McDonald's in Altoona, Pennsylvania. He has pleaded not guilty to state and federal charges. Separately, a Minnesota man identified as Mark Anderson was charged with impersonating an FBI agent after an alleged attempt to free Mangione from the Metropolitan Detention Center in Brooklyn.

Prosecution, defense and reactions

Attorney General Pam Bondi formally directed Manhattan federal prosecutors to seek the death penalty and described Thompson's killing as a premeditated, cold-blooded assassination and a form of political violence, a characterization the Justice Department said supported a death-penalty referral.

Defense attorneys responded that the referral was political and an improper bypass of ordinary federal protocols.

We’re all very relieved,
— Karen Agnifilo, defense attorney

Agnifilo's comment underscored the defense's relief at the removal of capital exposure and came as her team renewed complaints about how evidence was handled.

Richard Schoenstein, a legal expert, said the government’s theory that stalking qualifies as a 'crime of violence' is legally weak: stalking is a crime, he said, but not necessarily the sort of violent predicate that underlies a capital charge.

Supporters in court were heard cheering for Mangione, whom some described as a folk hero taking on the for-profit insurance industry. Outside commentators said Garnett’s ruling thwarted the Trump administration’s bid to see Mangione executed, while prosecutors emphasized that allowing the backpack evidence gives them items they say tie Mangione to the killing, including a handgun and writings.

Possible appeals and scheduling

Garnett scheduled federal jury selection to begin on Sept. 8, with opening statements set to start on Oct. 13, and she gave prosecutors a deadline to decide whether to appeal. Prosecutors did not immediately say whether they would challenge the ruling, and court observers warned an appeal could alter or delay the federal timetable.

State case and next steps

The Manhattan district attorney's office has urged a state judge to set a July 1 trial date so the state prosecution could proceed before the federal case, but it is unclear whether the state court will actually set that schedule.

State of play

Across reporting there is agreement on several points: Garnett removed the federal death-penalty option by dismissing the capital-eligible counts, she permitted backpack evidence to be used at the federal trial, and Mangione remains charged and has pleaded not guilty. The Justice Department and Attorney General Pam Bondi framed the killing as political violence and sought a death-penalty referral, while defense lawyers called that move political and alleged the search and chain of custody were improper; legal commentators dispute whether the stalking counts satisfy the statutory 'crime of violence' test the judge applied. Prosecutors have until Feb. 27 to decide whether to appeal, and that decision could alter the federal timetable, and the Manhattan district attorney has urged a July 1 state trial date that remains uncertain.
