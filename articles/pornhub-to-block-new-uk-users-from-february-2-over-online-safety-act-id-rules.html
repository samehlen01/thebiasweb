Pornhub to block new UK users from February 2 in protest over Online Safety Act ID rules. Aylo, the company that operates Pornhub, said that from 2 February it will stop allowing new account registrations from the United Kingdom, meaning only people who have previously created accounts will be able to access the site's free content. In an official statement the company framed the decision as a response to updates under the UK Online Safety Act that require age verification, saying those rules "have not achieved their goal of protecting minors" and that they have "diverted traffic to darker, unregulated corners of the internet."

What Aylo says

Aylo said the ban will extend to its other free platforms, including YouPorn and Redtube, while paid subscription services will remain accessible because credit-card payments are treated as a form of age assurance.

The company said it had sent letters to Apple, Google and Microsoft urging device-based age verification and said the tech firms had not responded. Solomon Friedman of the ECP, who has been public on the issue, speculated that device-based checks could in theory be used to filter explicit tweets, posts or AI chatbot output.

Aylo has pointed to traffic declines as part of its rationale: the company told outlets in October that its UK traffic had fallen about 77% since July.

Regulator response and enforcement

Ofcom told reporters that porn services have a choice between using age checks to protect users as required under the Act, or to block access to their sites in the UK, and added: "We've put in place age assurance rules that are flexible and proportionate, and we have seen widespread adoption."

The regulator said it has launched investigations into more than 80 porn sites and has fined a porn provider £1m, and it reported that 68 of the top 100 pornography services have deployed age checks as of this week.

Those figures do not align. Aylo gave a 77% decline, while Ofcom said the regulator recorded 9.8 million visits to Pornhub in August, a decline of 1.5 million compared with the same period in 2024.

anyone who has not gone through that process prior to February 2 will no longer be able to access [the sites] and they're going to be met with a wall,
— Alexzandra Kekesi, VP of Brand and Community at Aylo

Kekesi called the move a 'difficult decision' and said, "Our sites, which host legal and regulated porn, will no longer be available in the UK to new users, but thousands of irresponsible porn sites will still be easy to access."

Disagreement and analysis

Emma Drake of Bird & Bird argued: "The determined will find alternative routes, like the VPNs or the new entrants Aylo mentioned, but adding barriers to the most well-known sites can still protect a very large number of children who won't make that effort."

Solomon Friedman of the ECP said: "You have a dedicated regulator working in good faith, but unfortunately, the law they are operating under cannot possibly succeed." Dr Chelsea Jarvie, a cyber-security expert, cautioned that controls implemented at device level "are not a silver bullet". Aylo has alleged that some users are instead turning to sites with no uploader verification and weaker moderation, increasing the risk of exposure to illegal or dangerous content.

VPN downloads in the UK more than doubled after age verification took effect, rising from about 650,000 users to a peak above 1.4 million in mid-August and easing back to roughly 900,000 by December.

That surge has been cited by analysts as a factor that can undermine the Online Safety Act's age-verification by allowing users to mask their location and access sites that do not comply.

State of play

Aylo will restrict new registrations from 2 February and the same restrictions will apply across its other free sites; Ofcom says age-assurance rules are in force, 68 of the top 100 pornography services have adopted checks and the regulator has launched investigations and issued at least one £1m fine. Aylo alleges the Online Safety Act has failed and has driven users to unregulated corners while pointing to a steep fall in its UK traffic; Ofcom disputes that assessment and the scale of the traffic decline is contested. Next steps named in reporting include continued Ofcom enforcement, Aylo's push to get device-makers to adopt device-based age verification, and speculative suggestions that the government may consider restricting children's use of VPNs.
