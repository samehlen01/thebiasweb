Morgan McSweeney resigns as Keir Starmer’s chief of staff over Mandelson donor scandal. The decision ended a brief but politically explosive episode that followed the appointment of Lord Peter Mandelson as the United Kingdom’s ambassador to the United States and his rapid removal after reporting about links to Jeffrey Epstein, and it came after Metropolitan Police officers searched properties connected to Mandelson. In his resignation statement Mr McSweeney said he took full responsibility for advising the prime minister to make that appointment and called for a fundamental overhaul of vetting and due-diligence procedures to prevent similar failures.

What happened

Within days of the posting, coverage turned sharply negative. Critics said the decision to send Mandelson to Washington unravelled almost as soon as it began: outlets reported that Mandelson lasted seven months in the ambassadorial role before he was removed, and some commentary described the outcome as catastrophic for a senior Labour figure. That sudden fallout compounded the political embarrassment for Number 10 and intensified calls for answers about who had recommended and approved the posting.

Metropolitan Police officers searched Mandelson's London home and another property linked to him, according to reporting, as part of an inquiry the force described as complex. The government has said it will publish emails and other documentation connected to the appointment and that the Intelligence and Security Committee will examine the files, a step ministers framed as transparency but which risks producing additional political consequences.

a significant amount of further evidence gathering and analysis.
— Metropolitan Police

The force warned investigators they would need to gather and analyse material methodically before any conclusions were drawn, underlining that the probe will take time and substantial evidence-gathering.

Who is held responsible?

Mr McSweeney's public explanation mixed contrition with qualification: he said he accepted responsibility for advising the prime minister to go ahead with the Mandelson appointment, but he also asserted he had not overseen the day-to-day vetting and due-diligence work that preceded the posting. He urged a thorough overhaul of vetting systems so that comparable errors cannot recur.

Downing Street released an official statement saying the prime minister retained confidence in Mr McSweeney and defended the appointment process. By contrast, several Labour backbench MPs publicly called for Mr McSweeney to be sacked, with some describing his operation as 'rotten' and urging immediate change. Kemi Badenoch said Sir Keir should take responsibility for his own decisions rather than shifting blame to advisers.

Political fallout

Political commentators said Mr McSweeney's exit will be read as a significant blow to the prime minister and may increase pressure on his leadership. James Lyon wrote that the departure would make it harder for Sir Keir to remain as prime minister. Analysts also forecast that the government's vetting and due-diligence procedures will come under review or be overhauled in response. Some commentators and MPs speculated that Sir Keir might not survive politically because of the Mandelson affair, and a number of observers suggested that Mr McSweeney's resignation alone may not be sufficient to halt the wider damage.

Record and role within the party

Within Labour, Mr McSweeney had been widely credited with remaking the party's electoral prospects: reporting and analysis linked him to the strategy behind the 2024 general election victory and to Sir Keir's leadership campaign in 2020. He became chief of staff in 2024 after an internal power struggle and the departure of Sue Gray, stepping into a post that placed him at the centre of the prime minister's operations.

It’s been an honour working with Morgan McSweeney for many years. He turned our party around after one of its worst ever defeats and played a central role running our election campaign.
— Keir Starmer

Sir Keir's public words highlighted why Mr McSweeney's departure matters inside Labour even as it fuels political controversy outside the party.

Investigations and documents

Investigators have signalled the inquiry will be methodical: the Metropolitan Police said the investigation is complex and will require a significant amount of further evidence gathering and analysis. Ministers say that releasing emails and related files will allow parliamentary and independent scrutiny of how the appointment was handled.

That seven-month tenure has been used repeatedly by critics to illustrate how quickly the appointment went wrong and how rapidly political damage can accrue.

Reactions across the political spectrum

Responses ranged from institutional defence to partisan attack: Downing Street defended the prime minister's team, several Labour backbench MPs demanded Mr McSweeney's removal and labelled the operation 'rotten', Kemi Badenoch urged Sir Keir to accept responsibility, and commentators such as James Lyon warned of tougher political headwinds for the prime minister.

What happens next

The immediate items to watch are the promised publication of emails and documents, the Intelligence and Security Committee's review of related files, and the Metropolitan Police's continuing evidence-gathering. The pace, content and timing of any disclosures and of police work will be critical in determining whether the episode becomes a sustained leadership crisis or is contained.

State of play

Across reporting there is agreement that Morgan McSweeney has resigned as Keir Starmer’s chief of staff and that police officers searched properties linked to Lord Mandelson; Mr McSweeney says he took responsibility for advising the appointment while also saying he did not run the day-to-day vetting processes. The government has pledged to publish relevant emails and documents and the Intelligence and Security Committee will review files, and the Metropolitan Police said the probe will take time and extensive evidence-gathering. Commentators and some MPs speculate the fallout could threaten Sir Keir’s hold on power, and others have warned that the resignation alone may not be sufficient to defuse the political crisis; Downing Street, however, continues to defend the prime minister's team and investigators and parliamentary reviewers are expected to pursue the matter in the coming weeks.
