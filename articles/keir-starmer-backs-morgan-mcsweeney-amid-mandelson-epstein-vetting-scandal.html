Timeline and documents

Lord Mandelson was appointed as the UK's ambassador to the United States in December 2024. He was sacked in September after emails emerged showing supportive messages he had sent to Jeffrey Epstein following Epstein's guilty plea. Subsequent reporting and documents allege Epstein gave Mandelson $75,000 and that Mandelson shared sensitive inside information with Epstein. Police investigators have opened a probe into whether market-sensitive information was passed to Epstein and have asked the government not to publish documents that would undermine their inquiry.

The figure of $75,000 appears in documents cited by reporters and has become a focal point in media coverage and police enquiries. Police sources have asked the government to withhold publication of material that might prejudice that investigation while MPs press for transparency.

Vetting process and security checks

The Foreign Office says all ambassadors are required to undergo developed vetting, the most intrusive process required to access highly classified material, and that security vetting is carried out by UK Security Vetting, or UKSV. Starmer has acknowledged that the vetting of Mandelson included discussion of his relationship with Jeffrey Epstein and that No 10 sent three follow-up questions via Morgan McSweeney.

Files, police concerns and publication dispute

The government says documents relating to Mandelson's appointment are due to be released following pressure from MPs. The Metropolitan Police has reportedly asked the government not to publish material that would undermine its probe. That request could delay the release of the files.

APCO, Labour Together and the press

Reporting suggests Labour Together engaged APCO Worldwide to investigate journalists and to identify possible sources of damaging stories. Sources close to Morgan McSweeney say he did not make the decision to hire APCO and that the contract was a matter for Labour Together. The Electoral Commission fined Labour Together £14,250 in September 2021 after finding failures in donation reporting and related breaches. John McDonnell has called for the party to open an inquiry into Labour Together and anyone in the party connected to it. He warned the allegations could cause reputational damage and that a formal investigation may be warranted. APCO briefing materials also suggested, as an uncertain possibility, that one possible source for a Sunday Times story could have come from material illegally obtained in a 2023 hack.

The 2021 penalty followed an internal referral and covered late reporting and inaccuracies in donation returns, a regulatory finding that remains part of Labour Together's record. That prior enforcement, coupled with the new APCO allegations, has hardened calls for transparency within the party.

Views across the party and beyond

Several Labour MPs have said Morgan McSweeney should take responsibility and resign. Harriet Harman said Sir Keir Starmer looked 'weak, naive and gullible'. Nigel Farage described Sir Keir Starmer's apology as weak and 'not quite believable'. Anas Sarwar said Starmer should keep his job but should never have considered Mandelson for the ambassador role. Some Labour MPs warned that the revelations have intensified leadership speculation and that the prime minister's days may be numbered. Karl Turner warned that if Morgan McSweeney is not sacked, Keir Starmer's position would become 'untenable'.

Electoral implications

Political commentators and some MPs have speculated Labour could perform poorly in the upcoming Gorton and Denton by-election on 26 February, a contest being watched as a barometer of public reaction. That contest is being treated by some as an early test of whether the controversy damages Labour's local prospects.

Disputed facts and police caution

Reporting and released files present materially different accounts of what occurred. Keir Starmer has publicly accused Mandelson of lying during the vetting process, a charge the former minister rejects.

Why perspectives diverge

Actors' institutional roles help explain differing narratives: Downing Street has defended its senior staff and framed the files as potentially exculpatory. Labour Together, a pro-Labour organisation that was fined for reporting failures in 2021, faces scrutiny over its use of external advisers and how that was authorised. Opposition MPs and some former ministers have pushed for accountability and public answers. Reports that a PR firm was engaged to research journalists have intensified scrutiny over how the party and its affiliates operate.

Potential outcomes

Publication of the Mandelson appointment files could renew pressure on Sir Keir Starmer and prompt fresh calls for his resignation, Labour MPs warn. If Morgan McSweeney is not sacked, some MPs say the prime minister's position could become untenable. John McDonnell has demanded an inquiry into Labour Together and those connected to it. He and others argue the allegations about APCO's work could cause reputational damage and lead to formal investigations.

Next steps

Documents relating to Mandelson's appointment are due to be released following pressure from MPs. The immediate choices for the government are whether to publish the appointment files or to pause publication in deference to the Metropolitan Police's request. MPs and former ministers are likely to press for formal investigations into Labour Together and for clear answers about who authorised external research on journalists. Political actors will also watch the Gorton and Denton by-election for early signs of voter reaction.
