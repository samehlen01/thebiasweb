U.S. Signs Nuclear Power Agreement With Saudi Arabia

The Energy Department, under Chris Wright, said the United States and Saudi Arabia have signed a civilian nuclear cooperation agreement. The agreement includes a bilateral safeguards agreement and will go to Congress for review as a 123 agreement. Congress can review it next, and if lawmakers oppose it, they can vote to reject it, but stopping the deal would take a veto-proof majority, enough votes to override a presidential veto.

U.S. officials described the agreement as a decades-long, multi-billion-dollar partnership expected to last 30 years.

Supporters' case

The Trump administration framed the agreement as historic and consistent with nonproliferation. It said the agreement should create jobs, boost exports and help U.S. supply chains. It also said the agreement would deepen U.S.-Saudi ties.

Saudi officials said it extends existing energy cooperation and comes as part of Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman’s Washington visit. Westinghouse chief Dan Sumner called it a landmark step that strengthens energy security and opens opportunities.

Jonathan Hinze said U.S. companies need a nuclear trade agreement if they want to stay in the Saudi market. Without one, he argued, Riyadh would likely buy technology and supplies from France, Russia or China instead.

Bob McNally took a more relaxed view, saying the agreement is a win and that helping Saudi Arabia is not a major risk. He said the world still needs reliable power that runs all the time and does not produce emissions.

Critics and warnings

Critics say the agreement could fuel proliferation, trigger a regional arms race, and weaken long-term stability.

Rep. Brad Sherman said Congress should approve the agreement only if it carries gold-standard safeguards, and he accused Washington of helping normalize proliferation for profit. Sen. Edward Markey called the agreement reckless and said it makes the United States less safe.

Rosemary Kelanic said giving Saudi Arabia enrichment technology is a terrible idea. She warned that it could give Riyadh leverage over Washington in future bargaining. Alexander Bollfrass described the arrangement as revolutionary because, in his view, the United States would be transferring highly sensitive technology without insisting on the strongest internationally monitored protections first. Matthew Bunn said the agreement may make a reasonable U.S.-Iran nuclear deal harder to reach.

What remains unresolved

Earlier U.S. planning had tied Saudi nuclear access to normalization with Israel, but Trump appeared to drop that condition. Two U.S. officials and people familiar with the matter say the framework could still allow Saudi Arabia to enrich uranium and potentially build an enrichment facility after a joint study. Nonproliferation experts and Israeli officials warn that Saudi Arabia could eventually develop a nuclear weapon if the enrichment path stays open. The crown prince has said Saudi Arabia would move quickly if Iran got a bomb.

Analysts also say it may take years or even decades before the agreement reshapes regional power dynamics or creates the infrastructure needed to support it.
