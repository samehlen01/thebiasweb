Tropical Storm Bertha formed Monday evening and was about 140 miles south of Panama City, Florida. Bertha was the Atlantic hurricane season's second named storm, following Arthur.

Forecasts before landfall

Forecasters said Bertha may strengthen before it weakens as it approaches Louisiana. They also said it could linger near the coast long enough to make landfall in the New Orleans area by Wednesday. A later outlook had the storm dropping to depression strength and dissipating near Texas by the end of the week.

Forecasters warned of heavy rain, flooding and storm surge across the Gulf Coast.

Mississippi coastal areas still recovering from Arthur could face more heavy rain and flooding.

Warnings, watches and impacts

Tropical storm warnings were in effect in parts of Florida, Alabama, Louisiana and Mississippi, including metropolitan New Orleans and Lake Pontchartrain. A tropical storm warning means those conditions are expected within 36 hours. A storm surge watch stretched from the Alabama-Florida border to the mouth of the Mississippi River. It signaled rising, potentially life-threatening water moving inland from the coast within 48 hours.

The warning and watch zones overlapped only partly in Louisiana. Tropical storm conditions were possible within the watch area in Louisiana by Wednesday.

Residents and officials sandbagged against flooding, and beaches, parks, piers and some roads were closed.

Forecasters also warned of dangerous surf and rip currents. Isolated tornadoes could form in Florida's Big Bend.

Landfall update

Bertha later made landfall in St. Bernard Parish, Louisiana, about 40 miles east of New Orleans, just after 3 p.m. ET.

That update followed earlier forecasts centered on the New Orleans area.

Heat adds a second hazard

A dangerous heatwave was expanding across the region, with heat advisories and triple-digit feels-like temperatures.

Daytime readings could stay in the triple digits for several days with little relief overnight.

Reactions and analysis

Gov. Jeff Landry said the forecast was extremely encouraging and that Bertha was unlikely to produce the rainfall or wind event it could have, while Louisiana State Climatologist Jay Grymes said the storm should not leave widespread damage and should stay manageable for coastal communities.
