This article examines the event French and British navies seize suspected Russian ‘shadow fleet’ oil tanker in Mediterranean raid, in which French forces intercepted, boarded and diverted the tanker Grinch in the western Mediterranean and escorted it to port for investigation. British forces provided tracking and monitoring support as the ship transited the Straits of Gibraltar.

What happened

Video footage and reporting show soldiers descending from a helicopter onto the Grinch, and authorities say the boarding involved a navy boat and two navy helicopters.

French authorities said the tanker was subject to international sanctions imposed by the UK, EU and US. It was suspected of operating under a false flag while flying a Comoros flag.

Prosecutors in Marseille say a report has been filed and that they are investigating the ship's failure to confirm its nationality. Tracking records indicate the Grinch left the Russian Arctic port of Murmansk in early January. The Economist reported the Grinch was carrying about 730,000 barrels of Russian oil.

Russia's embassy in Paris said it had not been informed of the seizure.

Voices and reactions

Alongside our allies, we are stepping up our response to shadow vessels to choke off the funds that fuel Putin's illegal invasion of Ukraine.
— John Healey, UK Defence Secretary

The UK defence secretary framed the interception as part of a stepped-up allied effort to choke off funds for Russia's invasion. Emmanuel Macron described the operation as carried out on the high seas in strict compliance with the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea and said France was determined to enforce sanctions. Ukraine's president Volodymyr Zelensky said such vessels must be apprehended and suggested confiscating and selling the oil carried by these tankers would be fair. The UK Ministry of Defence also said British forces provided tracking and monitoring support, including HMS Dagger following the ship through the Straits of Gibraltar. The BBC framed the episode as part of a growing use of 'shadow fleets' by states such as Venezuela, Iran and Russia to evade sanctions.

Numbers and disagreements

Different outlets and actors report different measures and counts; the following points cite the distinct numbers offered by governments, outlets and analysts.

  • UK sanctions tally: 544 vessels, per UK government reporting.
  • Experts' estimate: more than 400 suspected false-flag ships.
  • Ministerial account: allied actions have pressured around 200 vessels at sea.
  • The Economist's recent count: at least five vessels seized in Western nets this month.
  • S&P Global's sector estimate: about one in five tankers are used to smuggle oil from sanctioned countries.

Those differences reflect distinct scopes — sanctions lists, suspected false flags, ministerial measures of pressure, episodic seizures and broader industry estimates — which helps explain why the headline numbers vary.

Legal and diplomatic questions

Some experts estimate there could be more than 400 such ships flying under false flags. The government has reportedly found a legal basis to allow British forces to board and detain shadow-fleet vessels under the 2018 Sanctions and Money Laundering Act, but that account is currently an uncertain report. Officials said the Grinch's crew is Indian.

Wider context

In October France detained the sanctioned tanker Boracay off its west coast and released it after a few days, and the Boracay's Chinese captain is due to stand trial in France in February. Earlier in January British armed forces supported a US operation to seize a Russian-flagged tanker in the Atlantic.

State of play
