Storm Goretti: Overview

Storm Goretti swept across northern Europe, cutting power, grounding services and forcing widespread closures and cancellations. The Met Office described the system as a 'multi-hazard' event and put amber and yellow warnings in place for large parts of the UK. Parts of the south-west faced a rare red wind warning and authorities urged people to stay indoors. French coastal stations recorded exceptional gusts — national reports noted a 213km/h reading in Barfleur. The Isles of Scilly logged gusts close to 99mph.

Power cuts, restoration and conflicting totals

Local and national operators reported large outages, but totals varied between outlets. Cornwall Live said more than 40,000 customers were affected in the South West and warned some households might not be reconnected until Sunday. A later Cornwall Live update put about 30,000 properties still without supply across the South West. Stoke Sentinel reported that more than 2,100 homes lost power in parts of Staffordshire. RTE said roughly 380,000 households had their electricity cut in France. National Grid told Cornwall Live it had restored 147,983 properties across the South West, Midlands and South Wales. By contrast, Daily Mail live coverage put the UK toll at about 65,000 homes without power.

Clifftop evacuations and coastal erosion

On the east coast, Great Yarmouth Borough Council told 14 households on The Marrams and Fakes Road that their clifftop homes were at imminent risk and residents were ordered to evacuate. Reporters and locals documented more than 30ft of recent land loss in parts of Hemsby, with about 13ft lost on a single day and several properties already marked for demolition. Campaigners from Save Hemsby Coastline described the pace of erosion as 'truly frightening' and accused the council of a lack of leadership. The council said it had been liaising with affected owners, offering support, and warning the public to keep clear of the dune face.

Health services and casualties

Coverage across outlets recorded storm-related deaths and rising pressure on health services. One Daily Mail report said at least eight people had died in weather-related incidents across Europe this week, while RTE cited specific fatalities such as a body recovered in Albania. In North Wales the Betsi Cadwaladr University Health Board said it was treating the situation as an internal critical incident and urged patients to plan ahead. The Welsh Ambulance Service warned cold weather brings more falls, breathing problems and road collisions and that response times may take longer.

Transport and travel disruption

Rail and air services were widely disrupted by the storm. State-owned Deutsche Bahn halted long‑distance services in northern Germany, citing severe winter conditions. Several airlines reported large numbers of cancellations, with KLM among those axing dozens of flights to and from Amsterdam. Eurostar and other operators suspended or cancelled multiple continental services.

Local impacts and household stories

“I’ve been left without heating since Saturday (January 3) and I still don’t know when it’s going to be fixed,” she said.
— Aliye Galloway, tenant

Aliye told the Northampton Chronicle that the air‑source heating installed in 2022 has repeatedly broken down and that many engineers sent were not trained to repair the system. She said temporary electric heaters provided were unsafe for her autistic son and did not heat the house adequately. In a different community, a Powys resident praised neighbours who collected her and a companion by tractor, calling Annie and Ryan 'the most amazing people'.

Gritting, road safety and council responses

Commuters and social‑media users complained of ungritted pavements, platforms and minor roads that they said were behaving like 'ice rinks', and at least one person required emergency treatment after a fall. The Local Government Association urged people to check on older and vulnerable neighbours and said councils cannot grit every road because doing so would cost hundreds of millions. Police forces reported multiple collisions and warned against unnecessary travel. The RAC advised drivers to allow more time, carry the right equipment and not use hot water to clear a frozen windscreen.

Water, National Grid and compensation

South West Water opened bottled‑water distribution points after a treatment works was knocked offline and warned repairs would be complex. National Grid said it had restored large numbers of properties and had brought in extra engineers to speed repairs in the worst-hit regions. Cornwall Live explained that National Grid's Guaranteed Standards of Performance set out compensation for long outages, with household payments possible if a cut lasts beyond the threshold. Operators said they were prioritising vulnerable customers while work continued.

Reactions and official comment

Local leaders called for more government help on coastal protection and compensation, while council directors said they were supporting residents through an acute emergency. The Norfolk councillor responsible for environment work described the pace of erosion as alarming and warned of the emotional toll on communities. A council director called on the government to review compensation policy for people affected by coastal erosion. German meteorologists placed the episode in the context of generally milder winters and said extremes like this are still possible even as the climate warms. Separately, the Met Office published a list of 73 UK areas it considered at risk of power cuts before Saturday.

State of play

Officials have confirmed that the storm brought high winds, heavy snow and significant service disruption, and forecasters and operators are continuing to update warnings and repair timetables. Residents and campaigners have alleged failings in local responses — from gritting to coastal protection — while councils and emergency services say they are prioritising safety and liaising with affected people. It remains unclear exactly how many households will face prolonged outages because national and local tallies differ; next, National Grid and local distributors will continue restoration work while the Met Office keeps issuing updates.

France
Sources
BFM TV

BFM TV's live coverage focused on France-specific responses and granular local impacts that UK audiences may not have seen: an interministerial crisis cell chaired by Interior Minister Laurent Nuñez, Transport Minister Philippe Tabarot's ban on heavy goods and the suspension of school transport across 38 departments, and detailed accident reporting including a major A63 motorway pile‑up involving a tanker. BFM provided Enedis outage counts by area (about 6,100 households in Pays‑de‑la‑Loire and 5,000 in Charente‑Maritime; earlier live tallies cited ~13,000 nationwide) and reported at least five deaths in France linked to snow and ice.

BFM also described operational measures — airport closures and large flight reductions in the west and north, restrictions on lorries, and an estimated €400–600m economic hit for a day of snow in Île‑de‑France — but its outage and casualty figures differ from other international tallies (for example Tagesschau cited a broader Enedis figure of ~380,000 outages and an earlier ministry line of six lightly injured). The reporting underlines how quickly regional counts and attributions were evolving.

Germany
Sources
Tagesschau (ARD)

Tagesschau framed the event as Storm 'Goretti' and highlighted cross‑Channel system impacts often missing from brief UK bulletins: EDF's precautionary shutdown of the Flamanville nuclear plant, National Grid's figure of roughly 55,000 UK households without power and Enedis' ~380,000 French outages, plus operational disruption (69 cancelled movements at Heathrow affecting about 9,000 passengers) and dramatic coastal conditions (10–13m waves in Brittany). Its early casualty snapshot — a ministry line of six people lightly injured in France — sits at odds with later French live reporting of multiple deaths, illustrating the fluidity of early tallies.

Spain
Sources
La Vanguardia

La Vanguardia emphasised the humanitarian response and social consequences in Catalonia: Creu Roja activated eight emergency shelters and Badalona opened a temporary pavilion; Barcelona's emergency night centre (CANE) has 100 places and the paper reported about 40 people on a waiting list. The article said roughly 85 people were assisted across Catalonia on the first night and charities such as Arrels ran day centres (about 75 people seen) to provide food, warmth and information.

That Spanish focus—numbers of people helped, beds available and charities' warnings—adds context UK audiences may not have seen: La Vanguardia cited an Arrels count of about 2,000 people sleeping rough in Barcelona (a 43% rise versus two years) and medical/relief concerns such as increased respiratory and dermatological problems among the homeless during the cold snap.

Sweden
Sources
Aftonbladet Sweden

Aftonbladet reported local economic and infrastructure fallout: a co‑op shop in Åmot said it discarded perishables with a purchase value of about SEK 73,000 after a three‑day power cut, and Ellevio noted roughly 900 households still without electricity in parts of Gävleborg at lunchtime. The piece contrasts immediate business losses with longer‑term resilience work — utilities and ministers noting many lines have since been weather‑hardened or buried after the 2005 Gudrun storm — and quotes shop staff on the constant stress of temperature monitoring.

Denmark
Sources
DR Denmark (State)

DR Denmark's headlines framed the episode as a Europe‑wide emergency: its coverage led with 'six dead' in the snow chaos and described municipal snow‑clearing crews under sustained pressure (headlines referenced crews scraping and salting for around 70 continuous hours). The Danish reporting emphasises operational strain on public services and long shifts for road crews rather than national political measures.

South Asia
Sources
The Hindu, Dawn Pakistan

The Hindu and Dawn largely relayed agency copy but added operational details useful to international readers: The Hindu highlighted Deutsche Bahn mobilising more than 14,000 staff to clear snow and quoted a DWD meteorologist warning temperatures could fall to around −20°C; Dawn emphasised wide travel disruption and large cancellations at Paris airports (the transport ministry numbers quoted in agency dispatches).

Their casualty and disruption snapshots differ: The Hindu referenced 'at least eight' deaths linked to the wider European episode, while Dawn and several wires cited six; likewise, reported flight cancellations at Schiphol varied between roughly 400 and 600 across feeds. These are examples of fast‑updating agency figures rather than sustained editorial dispute.

Sub-Saharan Africa
Sources
Channels TV Nigeria

Channels TV in Nigeria ran an AFP‑style summary that stressed travel disruption and casualty figures: it reported six fatalities, substantial reductions at Paris airports (about 40% at CDG and 25% at Orly), large numbers of flights grounded at Schiphol and operational strains such as KLM warning it had nearly run out of de‑icing fluid. The coverage concentrates on transport knock‑on effects and humanitarian incidents in the Balkans and western Europe, mirroring non‑European wires' emphasis on travel and casualty totals.
