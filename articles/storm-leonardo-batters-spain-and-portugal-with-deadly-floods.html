Storm Leonardo batters Spain and Portugal with deadly floods

Storm Leonardo has battered Spain and Portugal with deadly floods and high winds, sweeping vehicles from low-lying roads and inundating towns across southern Iberia. It is the latest in a series of about six storms to hit the Iberian Peninsula this year. In Alcacer do Sal, the Sado River breached its banks and flooded parts of the town. Portuguese authorities said a 70-year-old man died after floodwaters swept his vehicle off a road near a dam in the Alentejo region, although some local outlets described him as being in his sixties. In Andalusia, Malaga fire services said a girl was swept away by the Turvilla River while trying to rescue her dog. Other reports said the body of a woman who had been carried away was recovered on Friday.

Authorities have evacuated thousands in Andalusia, with some outlets putting the number at roughly 3,500 and others at more than 7,000. Spain's meteorological agency AEMET warned Storm Marta is expected to reach the region this weekend, raising the prospect of further heavy rain and landslides.

Scale and human impact

Andalusia’s emergency services said they had attended to more than a million incidents by midnight on Wednesday as crews dealt with floods, fallen trees and blocked roads. Antonio Sanz, head of the regional government's interior department, said 14 rivers and 10 dams were at "extreme" risk of overflowing. In Alcacer do Sal, residents reported waist-deep water after the Sado River breached its banks. Portugal’s National Civil Protection said it had registered at least 70 incidents by early Thursday, and Mario Silvestre, commander of ANEPC civil protection, warned that six rivers, including the Tagus, were at risk of significant flooding.

Local resident Maria Cadacha captured the mood on the ground when she said I've never seen anything like this; it's surreal.

We spent the whole afternoon and night yesterday searching in the river from the place where the girl fell in until the very end of the river. We found the dog, but not her.
— Manuel Marmolejo, Malaga fire chief

The fire chief's description, given on Spanish television, underlined that search teams were searching in the river and downstream reaches in the hours after the incident.

Rainfall extremes and measurements

672 mm
Rainfall recorded in Grazalema
BBC reported 672mm in a day-and-a-half; The Guardian said more than 700mm since Wednesday; AEMET cited over 40cm in 24 hours.

Reports from Grazalema differ depending on the time window and outlet: the BBC gave a day-and-a-half figure, The Guardian reported totals since Wednesday, and AEMET emphasised a separate 24-hour measurement.

Spain's state meteorological agency AEMET issued its highest red alert for heavy rainfall in Cádiz and parts of Málaga, where around 150mm was recorded in 12 hours, and it urged residents to heed local instructions.

Infrastructure, agriculture and costs

The storm has disrupted transport and energy. Flooding forced the closure of the M14 motorway near Madrid-Barajas airport and submerged the airport's metro station, and utilities reported tens of thousands without power after earlier storms. Nearly 134,000 households were still without electricity this week after Storm Kristin, according to utility and civil protection reports.

Portugal's economy minister, Manuel Castro Almeida, warned that reconstruction after last week's Storm Kristin alone may exceed €4 billion, while farmers' representatives put initial olive-harvest losses at about €200m.

The Portuguese government extended a state of calamity in 69 municipalities until the middle of February to coordinate relief and resources.

Accounts of last week's toll from Storm Kristin vary: some reports said five people died while other outlets said six people died, reflecting how early tallies can differ between sources.

Politics and the election-day debate

As the weather emergency unfolded, Portugal's election calendar became a contested issue. André Ventura, leader of the Chega party, urged the second-round presidential vote be delayed by a week, saying holding it now would not be equal for all Portuguese and describing the postponement as a matter of equality.

A state of emergency, weather alerts or overall unfavourable situations are not in themselves a sufficient reason to postpone voting in a town or region.
— National electoral authority

The national electoral authority said the vote would go ahead as scheduled and that weather alerts alone were not sufficient grounds to suspend voting in towns or regions.

Separately, Mario Silvestre of Portugal's ANEPC reiterated that six rivers, including the Tagus, were at risk of significant flooding as authorities balanced electoral timetables with emergency response.

Spain's prime minister Pedro Sánchez warned that complicated days lie ahead and appealed to the public to exercise caution and calm as damaged infrastructure and floodwaters are cleared.

Analysis, science and speculation

Scientists and climate analysts told outlets that human-driven climate breakdown is increasing the length, intensity and frequency of extreme weather events, a line of analysis many experts use when putting single storms into a broader context.

Meteorologists said Leonardo's intensity and persistence were driven by a southward-shifted jet stream combined with an atmospheric river carrying tropical moisture, a setup that can concentrate large volumes of rain over a small region.

Local officials and scientists also flagged secondary geophysical risks. The mayor of Gaucin said the tremors in his town appeared to be a hydroseismic event caused by underground water movement. The Andalusian president warned that full aquifers in the Grazalema mountains could provoke landslides and create large holes or sinkholes under houses or streets. A geologist cautioned that cavities in permeable rock may widen after heavy rain and cause the ground to sink, threatening buildings and roads.

Forecasters also warned of storm surges of up to two metres on exposed coasts as heavy rainfall warnings stay in force.

Regional knock-on effects

The disruption has extended beyond Iberia. More than 140,000 people in northern parts of Morocco have been evacuated as the army was deployed to assist with evacuations under a red alert for heavy rainfall.

State of play

Multiple authorities say Leonardo has dumped exceptional rainfall and caused destructive flooding. The storm has killed at least one person in Portugal. It has flooded towns such as Alcacer do Sal. Separately, the body of a woman who had been carried away by a river in Andalusia was recovered on Friday. It has forced large-scale evacuations in Andalusia, with some counts at roughly 3,500 and others exceeding 7,000, and it has also led to evacuations in northern Morocco, where more than 140,000 people have been evacuated. Ministers and regional officials have declared or extended emergency measures and given preliminary estimates that reconstruction and agricultural losses may run into the hundreds of millions or more. Portugal's economy minister warned that reconstruction after Storm Kristin alone may exceed €4 billion, and farmers' representatives put initial olive-harvest losses at about €200m. The national electoral authority has insisted voting should proceed despite calls to delay. Reporting diverges on several key figures, including evacuee totals, the exact rainfall recorded in Grazalema, and death tolls from last week's Storm Kristin, with some reports saying five people died and others saying six people died. Local search teams continue trying to resolve the case of the person taken by the Turvilla River. Meteorological services warn that Storm Marta may bring fresh heavy rain and strong winds this weekend.

Spain
Sources
ABC Spain, eldiario.es (Spain), El Mundo, La Vanguardia
The regional government (Junta) has raised to 11,000 the number of people who have been evacuated from their homes in Andalusia because of the storms.
— Antonio Sanz, Andalusian emergencies minister

Spanish outlets emphasised higher evacuee totals and a tightened emergency posture: they reported 11,000 people evacuated across Andalusia, kept the region at 'operational situation 2', and said emergency teams are operating across nine simultaneous scenarios while a technical committee of hydrogeology and landslide experts assesses riverbeds and subsurfaces.

Spanish meteorological and hydrological releases gave specific measurements: Grazalema recorded 577 mm on 3 February (described as the second‑highest 24‑hour total in Spain); the Cedillo reservoir was recorded releasing 6,348.55 m³/s and other reservoirs (El Vado, Pozo de los Ramos, Beleña) were listed as actively discharging; the Tagus at Trillo reached a red hydrological alert and the Guadalquivir ran at about 2,000 m³/s in Córdoba with another 40–60 l/m² forecast.

Officials and industry also flagged economic impacts: the Andalusian president estimated more than €500m in damage to regional roads, and carmakers such as Seat and Stellantis suspended shifts because parts could not reach plants after the Strait of Gibraltar was closed.

Portugal
Sources
Público Portugal, Observador (Portugal), RTP Portugal (State)
MOBI.E advised electric vehicle drivers to anticipate charging before the battery reaches low levels, to plan routes by confirming the availability of charging points, and to restrict travel to the minimum necessary.
— MOBI.E

Portuguese outlets reported a notably higher human toll and a larger relief package: officials said 13 people have died since last week following depressions Kristin and Leonardo, and the government extended a state of calamity to 68 municipalities until 15 February with support measures up to €2.5 billion.

Authorities announced emergency supports including a reactivated simplified lay‑off scheme (100% of normal net pay up to three times the minimum wage, €2,760; employer 20% / Social Security 80%; temporary exemptions of contributions), a three‑month extraordinary grant for the self‑employed up to €1,840, free replacement of storm‑damaged citizen cards until 31 March 2026, and mobilisation of 275 Citizen Service centres plus 12 mobile vans to assist residents.

Operational consequences included suspended rail services on multiple lines (Linha do Norte, Douro, Oeste, Beira Baixa, Cascais), local rehousing (Benavente 19; Cartaxo 26; Soure 10 evacuees) and warnings from Leiria business groups that power outages may cause an average 30‑day stoppage with recovery taking 3–6 months.
