A Taylor Farms lettuce sample that had tested positive for Cyclospora was rechecked and turned out to be a false positive, federal officials said. The voluntary recall tied to Central Mexico iceberg lettuce remains in force across 27 states.

The recalled product moved through both restaurant and grocery channels, which is why the warning has stayed broad rather than narrow.

False positive, investigation continues

FDA said the false positive does not change the basis for the outbreak investigation. Acting FDA Commissioner Kyle Diamantas said the inquiry is still looking at other potential sources and commodities for other clusters of illnesses. In FDA's view, the corrected result is only one piece of evidence, not the anchor for the case.

To clarify, this false positive lab sample does not change the basis for FDA's ongoing outbreak investigation or the overwhelming epidemiological data supporting the current voluntary recall by Taylor Farms.
— FDA

Taylor Farms said FDA apologized and made a mistake. It also said no positive product test has been identified, a distinction the company is using to argue that the agency has not proved the product itself was contaminated. Company executives went further, arguing that the FDA and CDC response had shortfalls and questioning the agencies' conclusions after meeting with White House and FDA officials.

Donald Prater said test results are difficult to obtain, so traceback and epidemiological information usually determine the source. In practical terms, that means investigators have to work from the broader pattern rather than waiting for one clean lab answer. That is one reason the lab correction has not been treated as the final word.

Recall distribution details

Taylor Farms said the recalled shredded iceberg product came from Central Mexico and was distributed between June 29 and July 16. The recall package also included certain lettuce sold to Taco Bell and Walmart, which is why the warnings reached more than one kind of customer. FDA says that recall still stands, so the withdrawn test has not changed the public-facing product list or the supply-chain question investigators are still trying to answer.

Case count discrepancies

1,644
confirmed cases
Since May 1, health officials reported 1,644 confirmed cases across Indiana, Kentucky, Ohio, West Virginia and Michigan, along with 94 hospitalizations and no deaths, and said other potential cases remain under investigation and total more than 5,100.

That official tally does not line up neatly with every other report. The Los Angeles Times said more than 6,000 people had been infected and 102 had been hospitalized, while Wired said Michigan authorities alone had counted 6,148 cases. Those figures are not interchangeable because they refer to different scopes, but together they show how unsettled the outbreak totals remain in public reporting.

The difference matters because readers are seeing three different kinds of accounting at once: a confirmed federal tally, a broader outbreak estimate and a state-only count. That can make the scale of the episode feel inconsistent even when each report is pointing to a real public-health problem. The safest takeaway is that the outbreak is large, still being counted and still generating different totals depending on who is doing the reporting.

Taco Bell and Walmart response

CDC warned against shredded iceberg lettuce at Taco Bell locations in five states, and health officials said all 1,644 confirmed cases in those states involved people who had eaten Taco Bell. Taco Bell said it was working to stop using any lettuce implicated by the investigation. The warning shows how quickly a produce case can become a restaurant problem as well.

Walmart also pulled four Marketside salad kit products and said there had been no confirmed illnesses associated with them. The company said it was working closely with its supplier and took immediate steps to remove the products from sale. CNBC reported that some diners have stayed away from salads altogether. Wired said foot traffic to leafy green chains is falling. The pattern suggests the scare has spread beyond the products named in notices.

Industry analysts on CNBC said the outbreak is likely to hurt sales only temporarily. They said the hit could last one to two quarters rather than becoming a long-term drag. Frank Yiannas said the latest development may add further confusion for consumers. FDA also says the inquiry could still identify other sources or affected restaurants, so the commercial hit and the investigative timeline are not moving at the same pace.

Traceability rule delay

The outbreak has also revived a separate argument about the Food Traceability Rule. The rule was set to take effect on January 20, 2026, but the Trump administration pushed the compliance date back to July 20, 2028, even though FDA says the regulation would allow faster identification and rapid removal of potentially contaminated food from the market, resulting in fewer foodborne illnesses and/or deaths. Supporters argue that better traceability would shorten the time between an outbreak and a recall like this one.

That delay led Matthew Cortland to speculate that Taylor Farms may have played a role in the rule's postponement, pointing to the company's $1 million (£0.75 million) donation to MAGA, Inc. five days after the deadline moved. The HHS account rejected that idea as fake news and said administration decisions are driven by science and safety. The exchange has kept a policy question attached to an outbreak story.

Why Cyclospora is hard to trace

Food-safety experts say Cyclospora is notoriously hard to pin on one farm or one shipment. Donald Schaffner said outbreaks often appear as separate, unrelated clusters, warned that this year's case load could break records and said contaminated water used on a batch of lettuce is one possible explanation. He added that the parasite's biology makes simple answers unlikely, especially with repeated summer outbreaks.

Confirmed, disputed, unknown

What is confirmed is that FDA has called the Taylor Farms sample a false positive and kept the recall in place. FDA says traceback investigations still converge on Taylor Farms lettuce from central Mexico. Officials have reported 1,644 confirmed cases, 94 hospitalizations, and no deaths.
