A coroner opened and immediately adjourned inquests into five babies whose deaths were linked to nurse Lucy Letby. The formal opening and adjournment took place at Cheshire Coroner's Court in Warrington, and Senior Coroner Jacqueline Devonish said the hearings were suspended pending the Thirlwall public inquiry.

Today we are opening five cases of babies who died at the Countess of Chester Hospital in 2015 and 2016 and Lucy Letby is serving a prison sentence for murder.
— Senior Coroner Jacqueline Devonish

Ms Devonish formally suspended the hearings until a review in three months' time, on May 5, and said full inquests had been provisionally scheduled for September 14 to 25, dependent on the outcome of the Thirlwall Inquiry.

Letby is serving 15 whole life prison terms after convictions for the murder of seven children and the attempted murder of seven others between June 2015 and June 2016.

The cases being opened

The infants named at the hearing were identified by cyphers as Baby C, Baby E, Baby I, Baby O and Baby P; the coroner reiterated that a previous High Court reporting direction remains effective as a worldwide order and asked that the infants be referred to by their anonymous cyphers.

Detective Inspector Darren Reid, the police coroner's officer, told the court each case had been referred because there was reason to suspect an unnatural death; he read short medical summaries and asked that the matters be opened. An inquest into Baby D that had been opened in January 2016 remained suspended and was further suspended at this hearing.

DI Reid said Baby C was born at 30 weeks and was suffering from respiratory distress syndrome due to prematurity and later collapsed and died aged four days.

DI Reid said Baby E developed necrotising enterocolitis with a sudden and steady decline for one to two hours and then suffered a cardiac arrest, was resuscitated but arrested again and resuscitation was stopped with family consent. Baby E was born on July 29 by c-section at 29 weeks old and died in August 2015.

DI Reid said Baby I had an underlying chronic lung disease of prematurity and an intermittent bowel obstruction that was under investigation; the baby had a prior cardiac arrest but was resuscitated and ventilated, then later had another cardiorespiratory arrest during which resuscitation attempts were unsuccessful. Baby I was born on August 7, 2015 at 27 weeks and died in October 2015.

DI Reid said Baby O was born at 33 weeks and two days with no complications but became quite poorly; his tummy became distended and he had a large vomit, his heart and O2 levels fell, and he was started on medication and ventilated but was not able to be resuscitated. Baby O was born at 33 weeks and two days on June 21, 2016 and died in June 2016.

DI Reid said Baby P was born in good condition at 33 weeks and two days but two days later his abdomen became distended, his oxygen levels dropped, and he required many episodes of resuscitation and drugs, none of which worked and he eventually passed away. Baby P was born on June 21, 2016 in good condition at 33 weeks and two days and died in June 2016.

Investigations and legal context

Letby was removed from clinical duties in July 2016 after consultant paediatricians raised concerns she may be deliberately harming babies, and an earlier inquest into Baby A in October 2016 recorded a narrative conclusion that it could not be determined what caused the youngster's collapse and subsequent death or whether it was due to a natural or unnatural event.

Cheshire Constabulary arrested Letby at her home on July 3, 2018 and arrested her twice more before charging her. The Netflix documentary includes police statements and bodycam footage and features Detective Superintendent Paul Hughes saying she emailed him from her NHS address naming the probe Operation Hummingbird; the film records that she wanted to know when we were going to speak to her, and the email was sent around April 2018.

Separately, Cheshire Constabulary said it was continuing an investigation into three former senior Countess of Chester bosses who were arrested last year on suspicion of corporate manslaughter or gross negligence manslaughter.

Prosecutor, police split on further charges

The Crown Prosecution Service said that following a thorough review of that evidence it had concluded the evidential test was not met and that no charges should be brought. Cheshire Police publicly disagreed, saying the CPS decision was not the outcome that we had anticipated and that they were confident that we held enough evidence to take to the CPS; the force said the material related to eight potential offences of attempted murder and one offence of murder.

Responses from defence, campaigners and families

Letby's legal team, led by her barrister Mark McDonald, issued a statement maintaining she is innocent and saying she had never hurt a child and never would. Campaigners and supporters have argued she has suffered a miscarriage of justice, blaming poor care and understaffing at the Countess of Chester. Counsel for bereaved families, Richard Baker KC, said the inquests must not be used as a collateral attack on the criminal convictions and argued the coroner was bound by those verdicts and could not act inconsistently with them. Letby's legal representatives questioned why the inquests were being opened at the time the CPS announced it would not bring further charges, and her parents, Susan and John Letby, criticised footage of her arrest as a complete invasion of privacy and said they would not be watching because it would likely kill us if we did.

Speculation and review

The Criminal Cases Review Commission confirmed it is considering evidence presented on her behalf from an international panel of medics who claim poor medical care and natural causes were the reasons for the babies collapsing. Letby's legal team lodged that file of new expert evidence in the hope her case will be referred back to the Court of Appeal a third time.

The coroner has set a review in three months' time, on May 5, and provisionally listed full inquests for September 14 to 25, dependent on the outcome of the Thirlwall Inquiry. The CCRC's consideration and any decision about referral to the Court of Appeal will be among the principal next steps.

Where things stand

Reporting from the hearing confirms the coroner opened and adjourned five inquests into deaths linked to Lucy Letby, who is serving 15 whole life prison terms after convictions for the murder of seven children and the attempted murder of seven others; the Crown Prosecution Service has said the evidential test was not met on other allegations while Cheshire Police say they were confident that we held enough evidence to take to the CPS.
