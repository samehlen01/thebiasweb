Spain beat Argentina 1-0 after extra time, with Ferran Torres scoring the only goal and giving Spain a second World Cup title. Lionel Messi left the field in tears after collecting the runners-up medal.

CNBC said the match had been expected to be his final World Cup appearance. It was the kind of finish that combined a title triumph with a deeply personal farewell. For Spain, the result completed a second climb to the top of world football, while Argentina were left with the kind of heartbreak that lingers long after the final whistle.

How Spain won

Spain's winner arrived only after the match had gone beyond regulation, which made the scoreline feel narrower than the occasion. Torres's strike was the moment that decided it and confirmed Spain as a two-time World Cup winner. For Argentina, Messi's tearful medal walk-off gave the loss a far more personal edge. The extra-time finish meant the final lingered in memory even before the off-field controversy took hold. It also meant the decisive moment came late enough to stretch the tension across the whole extra period, rather than letting either side settle into a rhythm.

The emotional weight around Messi mattered because the buildup had treated the final as potentially his last chance on the World Cup stage. CNBC's expectation was not a certainty, but it framed the match in farewell terms before a ball was kicked. By the time the medal ceremony arrived, his tearful exit made that framing feel even more plausible. In Argentina, that image was always likely to outlast the details of the scoreline.

Ceremony and scrutiny

The trophy ceremony provided a jarring shift in tone. Donald Trump lingered on stage during the medal and trophy presentation, and he and Gianni Infantino were booed. Instead of a clean celebratory ending, the final briefly turned into a scene of awkward crowd reaction and visible delay. That moment pulled attention away from the champions and toward the staging of the event itself. The boos also changed the emotional register in the stadium, replacing the expected medal-room polish with a much rougher public reaction.

FIFA later confirmed an investigation into the altercations with Spanish players. The Independent said the organisation had already begun potential disciplinary action after Argentina's banner supporting Falkland Islands claims. The same reporting said the brawl and the banner had pushed Argentina's conduct into controversy. Because the banner referenced the Falkland Islands, the off-field dispute carried a political edge that few finals have to absorb. In other words, the final ended not just with a trophy lift but with administrative questions still hanging over the aftermath.

The on-field flashpoint began when Leandro Paredes clashed with Eric Garcia and Gavi, setting off a broader brawl. Enzo Fernandez then picked up a second booking for a challenge on Pau Cubarsí, and his frustration spilled over when he kicked a pitch-side microphone. The Independent also said Argentina ended the tournament with 15 yellow cards, the most of any side. By then, the picture for critics was already set: a final that had demanded discipline from one side and composure from the other, but produced very little of either in the closing moments. Taken together, those details explain why the post-match debate focused so heavily on discipline and temperament.

Diverging reactions

British pundits were the loudest in their criticism. Micah Richards called the scenes embarrassing and not good. The report also said he told Paredes he was better than that. Joe Hart went further, describing the behavior as disgusting and saying Messi was the one man with class. Wayne Rooney said the reaction was sad and very poor because, in his view, it came from being outplayed. Alan Shearer said the better team won and that Spain's performance made the result good for football. The former players were not just reacting to a flashpoint; they were judging the whole temperature of Argentina's response after the loss. In their telling, sportsmanship mattered as much as the result, and the post-match scenes failed that test. The criticisms were different in tone, but they all pointed toward the same conclusion: Argentina had lost control at the end of the biggest game of the tournament.

The Daily Mail's own coverage sharpened the criticism further, describing Argentina as thuggish and leaning on match statistics to support that view. It pointed to a second booking, an atrocious foul, 24 fouls and only two shots. Its wording suggested that the line between intensity and ugliness had already been crossed, at least in the newspaper's view. That framing sat alongside the pundit reaction and gave the British debate a much harsher tone than the match score alone would suggest. It was a reminder that the same final could be read as either a sporting triumph or an ugly night of discipline issues.

Argentina's leading newspaper offered the clearest counterpoint. It said the team was polite, respectful and fought until the end, presenting the players as resilient rather than reckless. It also said they did not blame rivals. The paper's view was sympathetic without pretending the night had been easy. That was a very different reading of the same night, and it showed how national perspective can change the meaning of a final almost as much as the result itself. Where British coverage emphasized conflict, the Argentine view emphasized dignity and endurance.

The split in reaction also made the final feel unusually fragmented. One camp cared about sportsmanship, another about discipline, and another about what the title meant for Spain's wider mood. That divergence did not change the scoreline, but it did change what the scoreline was taken to mean. The same night could therefore look like a coronation, a disciplinary mess or both at once. That is why the reports keep circling back to the same match while telling rather different stories about it.

The New York Times took yet another angle, arguing that many fans saw the victory as part of a wider cultural, economic and sporting revival in Spain. In Madrid, thousands of supporters filled the streets. The team celebrated with a bus parade after winning the World Cup. That homecoming helped move the story away from the tension of the trophy stage and toward the scale of the celebration in Spain itself. The bus parade also gave Spain a public release valve after the tension of the final, turning private relief into a street celebration. For those fans, the night was not only about beating Argentina but about what that victory seemed to say about the country around the team.

Confirmed facts and open questions

Spain beat Argentina 1-0 after extra time, and Ferran Torres scored the winner as Spain took a second World Cup title. Messi left in tears after collecting the runners-up medal. FIFA said Argentina could face an investigation, and potential disciplinary action was also on the table. What remains disputed is the reading of Argentina's conduct after the brawl and banner. The Independent and the Daily Mail cast the night in a far harsher light. The Daily Mail described Argentina as thuggish and backed it with foul and shot statistics. Argentina's leading newspaper insisted the team was polite, respectful and fought until the end. CNBC's suggestion that the game might be Messi's last World Cup was only an expectation, not a confirmed farewell. The next step will be whatever FIFA decides to do with the inquiry. The final closes as both a title story and an unresolved controversy.
