French parliament passes social media ban for under-15s

French lawmakers passed the bill on Tuesday, with lawmakers in both chambers backing it, including a 279 to 81 vote in the National Assembly. The measure would bar under-15s from social media and make France the first European country to adopt such a restriction. For lawmakers, the vote also set up a bigger question about how far age-based regulation can reach online, especially when the target is a set of global platforms rather than a single domestic service. It also turned a child-safety proposal into a broader experiment in digital rulemaking.

The same law also bans mobile phones in schools and high schools. That phone restriction will take effect at the start of the school year. The social media ban was to be rolled out in two stages, with under-15s blocked from creating new accounts from Sept. 1. The ban would apply to existing accounts from January 2027, according to the legislation. The text exempts online encyclopaedias and educational platforms, and it sets no penalties for children or parents. In practice, the package tries to regulate both the classroom and the app store without treating them as the same problem, which helps explain why the government split the rollout rather than launching everything at once.

The ban will target Snapchat, TikTok, YouTube, Instagram and Facebook. X is also covered, while WhatsApp will not be affected. That narrower scope makes the law easier to describe, but not necessarily easier to enforce. It also suggests lawmakers are trying to separate the highly visible social networks that shape teen attention from the messaging tools many families treat as ordinary communication. By drawing that line, the bill avoids reading like a blanket ban on devices while still reaching the platforms most associated with youth screen time.

President Emmanuel Macron welcomed the approval as a major step and treated it as both a child-protection reform and a flagship measure of his second term. Campaigner Gaëlle Berbonde said supporters had no other option if they wanted to push back against tech giants. France's public health watchdog also said TikTok, Snapchat and Instagram were harmful to adolescents, particularly girls. The mix of support from the president, a campaigner and the watchdog shows the argument being built from both politics and public-health warnings, and that gives the bill a wider audience than a routine regulatory update. It is also why the bill has been framed less as a narrow tech fix and more as a question of whether the state should intervene earlier in children's digital lives.

France Unbowed lawmakers opposed the bill. They said its constitutionality was unclear. They also argued it would end online anonymity. In their view, it would be impossible to enforce. France Unbowed's criticism, by contrast, is aimed at the law's architecture rather than its goals, which is why its objections focus on rights, privacy and enforceability at the same time. That makes the party's response an important counterweight to the government's child-protection language, because it asks whether a well-intentioned rule can still be the wrong tool.

That privacy concern is also what makes the law feel broader than a simple age gate. If users have to prove how old they are, the debate quickly turns to how much identity has to be revealed. France Unbowed is pressing that point because it sees anonymity as part of the rights question, not a side issue. In that sense, the bill is forcing lawmakers to choose between a more intrusive safeguard and a lighter one that may not bite hard enough.

Enforcement questions

Digital Minister Anne Le Hénanff said the timeline was realistic because age-verification tools already exist, and she argued platforms must implement the rule. That stance tries to reassure lawmakers that the timetable is more ambitious than impossible. It also shifts the burden from parents and schools to the companies whose services the ban is meant to police, which is a key reason the government says the timetable can hold. In her telling, the law is not a theoretical promise but a practical deadline that platforms are expected to meet.

For four months, all of us in France will have to prove our age
— Anne Le Hénanff, digital minister

Regulators said ARCOM would enforce the ban, and users will have to prove their age. Anne Le Hénanff also said under-15 accounts will be closed. That is where the law turns from a parliamentary promise into a compliance test, with age checks and account removals carrying the policy into everyday platform operations. It is also the point at which the political argument becomes a technical one, because a rule that depends on proof of age only works if the verification step is accepted by platforms and users alike. Without that, the law risks becoming more symbolic than operational.

A constitutional review could delay implementation. Officials say the Constitutional Council review could delay implementation if it finds problems. Engadget also suggested France could run into the same age-verification and enforcement problems seen in Australia. Laure Miller said the major platforms are largely ready, while Catherine Morin-Desailly argued a blacklisting system would have taken more time and risked EU-law non-compliance.

Why Europe is watching

France is expected to be watched closely by other European states, and more countries are already considering similar bans. TechCrunch and Gizmodo went further, saying France is unlikely to be the last country to adopt a social media ban for kids. Their reporting suggests the French vote could become a reference point for lawmakers elsewhere, even before the implementation date arrives. That is one reason the French decision has been picked up so quickly beyond Paris, because it may help shape what counts as a workable youth-policy model in Europe.

TechCrunch and Gizmodo also said the ban could be one of Macron's last major domestic changes before he steps down next year. That gives the measure an additional political weight, because it may end up being remembered as part of the closing chapter of his domestic agenda. In that sense, the law is not only about children and platforms; it is also about the shape of Macron's final domestic record and how his government wants to be judged on digital policy.

By pairing the school-phone ban with the social-media age limit, lawmakers are effectively asking families to think about digital restraint in two different places at once. One part of the package changes what students can do during the day, and the other changes which accounts younger teenagers can hold outside school. That is why the debate has moved well beyond Parliament and into questions about daily routines at home and in class. It is a broad social rule, not just a web policy, and that helps explain why it has triggered so many different reactions.
