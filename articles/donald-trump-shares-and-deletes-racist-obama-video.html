Donald Trump Shares and Deletes Racist Obama Video

Donald Trump shared and then deleted a video on Truth Social that depicted Barack and Michelle Obama with their faces superimposed on apes, drawing widespread condemnation. The episode began when a post on Mr. Trump's Truth Social account included a short, roughly two-second clip that superimposed Barack and Michelle Obama's faces onto ape bodies. The clip appeared at the end of a roughly one-minute video that advanced claims of voter fraud in the 2020 presidential election. The post was later removed after a White House official said the item had been published in error by a staff member.

What was posted

Coverage by multiple outlets traced the quick Obama-as-ape fragment to the tail end of a 62-second video that had been shared on conservative feeds and advanced claims of voter fraud in the 2020 presidential election. Journalists said the Obama scene itself appears to have been clipped from an October post on X by the account @xerias_x; that original 55-second video appears to have been generated by AI and depicted several Democrats as animals.

Observers flagged that the Obama-as-ape passage is fleeting: outlets described the excerpt as a two-second clip set to the song 'The Lion Sleeps Tonight' tucked into the montage.

White House response

This is from an internet meme video depicting President Trump as the King of the Jungle and Democrats as characters from the Lion King. Please stop the fake outrage and report on something today that actually matters to the American public,
— Karoline Leavitt, White House press secretary

White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt framed the short animation as coming from an internet meme and pushed back against coverage, presenting that line as a reason the posting did not merit sustained outrage. Her statement was followed by a separate White House message saying the post had been taken down after an error, but the administration's characterization did not halt condemnation from lawmakers and civil-rights groups.

Media accounts differed on the timing and duration of the post's visibility: one report said the president removed the post late on Thursday night, while other outlets reported it was deleted late Friday morning after being live for about 12 hours.

A White House staffer erroneously made the post. It has been taken down,
— White House spokesperson

That 'staffer error' explanation was circulated by White House officials as a defence, but an outside Trump adviser and other reporting suggested the account could be a pretext and that staffers were reluctant to share fuller details.

Public and political reactions

Praying it was fake because it's the most racist thing I've seen out of this White House,
— Sen. Tim Scott

Senator Tim Scott, the only Black Republican in the Senate, posted on X that he was praying the clip was not real and described it as the most racist thing he'd seen coming from the White House, and he called for its removal.

Top congressional Democrats, including House Democratic Leader Hakeem Jeffries and Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer, condemned the post and demanded an immediate deletion and an apology to the Obamas.

Trump posting this video — especially during Black History Month— is a stark reminder of how Trump and his followers truly view people. And we'll remember that in November.
— NAACP

Civil-rights groups and many Democrats said the imagery evoked long-standing racist tropes and that the timing, during Black History Month according to some, made the post especially offensive.

Commentators and analysts framed the post as part of a pattern that stretches back through earlier, demeaning attacks on President Obama and his allies and argued it reflected an escalation in the use of AI-tinged memes and synthetic imagery in political messaging.

Political consequences and debate

Some strategists and analysts warned that opponents could seize on the episode to make it an issue in competitive races this year, while other commentators cautioned that such controversies often fail to change the views of the former president's core supporters and that the net political effect remained unclear.

The swift deletion and the 'staffer error' explanation prompted internal attention in the West Wing and were described in reporting as part of rapid damage-control efforts, though that same reporting also relayed speculation that the administration's explanation might not fully account for how the item was posted.

Origin and the AI angle

Researchers and reporters who traced the clip emphasized how quickly material from small online posts, including apparently AI-generated montages, can be clipped and republished. They linked the Obama segment back to an October X post from @xerias_x, whose 55-second original appears to have been produced with generative tools.

State of play

Across reporting there is agreement that Mr. Trump's Truth Social feed carried a short clip portraying Barack and Michelle Obama with their faces superimposed on apes. The post was removed after the White House said a staffer had made the post in error. The White House framed the material as an internet meme and described the deletion as corrective, while critics from civil-rights groups, Democratic leaders and some Republicans said the imagery invoked racist tropes and demanded apologies. Some outside advisers and other reporting speculated the 'staffer error' explanation could be a pretext, and analysts are divided over whether the episode will move voters ahead of the midterm elections.

Latin America
Sources
La Nación Argentina, El Financiero (Mexico)

La Nación traced the Obama‑as‑ape fragment to an X post and identified a watermark tying the clip to the account @xerias_x, reporting that account had about 45,900 followers. It dated the 55‑second original to 24 October 2025, said it was titled "Trump: Rey de la selva," and reported the item had more than 2,500 likes and over 1,100 reposts before deletion; La Nación added the clip was one of more than 60 posts Trump made overnight.

El Financiero framed the episode in the context of the video's election‑fraud claims by recalling recent high‑value Dominion Voting Systems settlements — $1.3 billion with Rudy Giuliani and $787.5 million with Fox News — used in reporting about the underlying allegations.
