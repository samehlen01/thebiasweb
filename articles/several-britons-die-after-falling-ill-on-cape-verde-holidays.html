Six Britons have died after holidaying in Cape Verde since January 2023, the law firm Irwin Mitchell said. The firm says it represents those bereaved families and more than 1,500 other holidaymakers who reported falling ill. The bereaved relatives have launched personal injury claims against tour operator Tui.

Lawyers for some families say four British holidaymakers died over a four-month period after developing severe gastric illnesses while on holiday in Cape Verde.

Public-health picture

Public-health agencies in Europe and Britain reported an increase in shigella infections tied to travel from Cape Verde. The UK Health Security Agency logged 137 cases between October and December, saying 80 per cent were linked to recent travel to the islands.

On December 15 the Foreign Office warned British travellers about shigella, saying it was the second outbreak on the islands in recent years and that medical facilities are 'very basic and limited'.

Families' experiences and legal claims

Relatives represented by Irwin Mitchell described local medical care as chaotic and criticised poor communication from clinics and holiday providers. They said hotel food was sometimes served lukewarm or undercooked, and that they were repeatedly left without updates as their relatives' conditions worsened.

The care she received felt chaotic, and we felt like we were in the dark around what was happening despite asking for updates.
— Family member

Jatinder Paul of Irwin Mitchell called the number of holidaymakers falling ill 'truly staggering' and urged 'meaningful and decisive action'. The firm says it represents the bereaved families and more than 1,500 other holidaymakers, and that it is seeking damages on their behalf.

Operator responses

Tui, which organises many package holidays to Cape Verde, said it is fully investigating the claims and that customers who fall ill while on holiday have access to appropriate care and assistance. The company added there are many possible causes of gastric illness beyond its control and that some guests do not report problems while away. RIU Hotels and Resorts said routine monthly testing of food and kitchen staff detected no shigella and that it adheres to high international hygiene standards.

At RIU Hotels and Resorts, the health and safety of our guests is always our main priority.
— RIU Hotels and Resorts

RIU said its Cape Verde operations comprise six hotels, totalling 4,650 rooms and employing 3,307 staff, and that in 2025 it welcomed over 400,000 guests.

One holidaymaker, Jess Richards, said she tested positive for shigella after a honeymoon in Cape Verde and later accepted a £4,000 settlement from Tui; she said she was never warned before travelling and told others to avoid the destination.

Discrepancies and open questions

The divergence between public-health case counts and what hoteliers report is central to the dispute. The UK Health Security Agency recorded 137 cases between October and December. It said 80 per cent were linked to recent travel from Cape Verde. RIU says its routine hotel testing detected no shigella. Tui has cautioned that gastric illness can have many causes beyond its control and that some guests do not report symptoms while on holiday. Some health experts have suggested Shigella sonnei, which can spread via contaminated food or water, may be involved. A law firm representing the bereaved families said investigations into the fatalities are ongoing.

Context
Shigella sonnei is a bacterial infection that causes severe diarrhoea, fever and stomach cramps and is commonly spread through contaminated food or water and person-to-person contact; experts have suggested it as a possible explanation for the outbreaks of gastric illness.

That link remains a hypothesis. Tui has argued there are many possible causes of gastric illness beyond its control and that some customers do not report illness while on holiday. Public-health investigators are continuing to assemble case histories and laboratory evidence.

Background

Visitor numbers to Cape Verde rose from about 115,000 in 2000 to almost one million in 2024, with British visitors accounting for more than a third of arrivals. Many who reported illness had been staying at all-inclusive resorts on Sal, particularly properties run by RIU and booked through Tui.

Next steps

Irwin Mitchell has called for 'meaningful and decisive action' to tackle reported hygiene problems and urged tour operators to work with authorities. The firm says it is seeking damages for the families and that investigations remain ongoing. The UKHSA and the Foreign Office have updated travel advice urging strict food and water hygiene. Tui says it is cooperating with inquiries and investigating the claims.

State of play

At this stage, statements and reporting converge on several points. Many British holidaymakers have reported gastrointestinal illness after visiting Cape Verde, and bereaved relatives are pursuing legal action, including personal injury claims against tour operator Tui. Irwin Mitchell says six Britons have died and that it represents the families and more than 1,500 other holidaymakers. Health agencies and the Foreign Office say shigella cases linked to travel rose last autumn and have advised stricter food and water hygiene. RIU says routine testing detected no shigella, and Tui warns gastric illnesses can have multiple causes. Investigators and lawyers are continuing inquiries and litigation, and official investigations and legal filings are under way.
