Overview

U.S. forces carried out a pre-dawn operation in Caracas on Jan. 3 that led to the capture of President Nicolás Maduro and his wife, Cilia Flores. Both were taken to New York to face federal drug and weapons charges. They have pleaded not guilty in Manhattan.

How the raid unfolded

Officials said law-enforcement teams and military personnel entered Maduro’s compound shortly after 1:01 a.m., and U.S. officials said he was out of Venezuelan airspace by about 3:29 a.m. ET. Gen. Dan Caine described a complex, multi-axis plan with aircraft launching from multiple points; military officials said at least one U.S. aircraft was struck but remained flyable.

Charges and legal framing

Prosecutors in New York unsealed an indictment accusing Maduro’s government of using state power to protect and promote illegal activity, including drug trafficking. Maduro appeared in Manhattan, pleaded not guilty and described himself as a 'prisoner of war.' The White House has framed the removal as a law-enforcement action, while some lawmakers and legal observers say the strikes resemble an act of war that should require congressional authorization. President Trump added that the United States would 'run the country' until a safe, proper and judicious transition could be arranged.

Congress and the war‑powers fight

Senators advanced a war‑powers resolution in a 52‑47 procedural vote intended to restrict further unilateral strikes in Venezuela. The effort, led by Sen. Tim Kaine with Sen. Rand Paul as a Republican co‑sponsor, was advanced after five Republicans crossed to support it. Lawmakers who attended classified briefings said they left dissatisfied and demanded more public detail. Some Democrats also publicly raised impeachment and 25th‑Amendment talk.

Political reactions — supporters

Administration allies and many Republicans hailed the raid as a carefully planned, strategic strike against narcotics networks. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth called the mission the 'exact opposite' of the Iraq invasion. Senator Tom Cotton and other Republicans emphasised Maduro's alleged role in drug trafficking. Secretary of State Marco Rubio said the U.S. had arrangements to sell 30 to 50 million barrels of Venezuelan oil. Speaker Mike Johnson and conservative commentators celebrated the outcome. At least one Democratic senator, John Fetterman, described the operation as 'surgical and very efficient.'

Political reactions — critics

Progressive critics and some conservatives accused the administration of ulterior motives or recklessness. Representative Alexandria Ocasio‑Cortez wrote that 'it's not about drugs; it's about oil and regime change.' Senator Chris Murphy called plans to seize Venezuelan oil 'insane.' Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene warned the move could presage more regime‑change campaigns. Senator Elissa Slotkin said the president's turn to foreign entanglements looked like a distraction from domestic problems. Anti‑war campaigners and local UK parties staged protests and called the intervention illegal or a war crime. Conservative MP Kemi Badenoch said the action was morally right even while acknowledging legal questions.

Regional and global response

International reaction split sharply. The U.N. secretary‑general said he was deeply concerned that the raid could increase instability. At CELAC, an effort by several leftist governments to condemn Maduro's detention failed after a bloc of countries blocked consensus. China said it was 'deeply shocked' and strongly condemned the U.S. use of force. Mexico's president rejected intervention in another country's internal affairs. Colombia's president and the U.S. president described a constructive phone exchange. The UK indicated billions in Venezuelan gold would remain frozen. Cuba acknowledged 32 of its citizens were killed and declared two days of national mourning. Moscow framed the episode as a sign of shifting great‑power behaviour.

Maritime enforcement and sanctions

Intelligence trackers reported that the tanker Bella 1 repainted its hull, changed its name to Marinera and reflagged as Russian in a bid to evade interception; U.S. officials later said they had seized the vessel for sanctions violations and that Navy SEALs boarded it.

Discrepancies and contested facts

Counts of the dead and other operational details vary between outlets and officials. One Irish report put the toll at more than 115 people killed; an official told The New York Times the strikes killed 'around 40' civilians and military personnel; Cuban state media acknowledged 32 Cuban personnel killed; Venezuelan military postings listed 24 dead, which some outlets combined with Cuba's figure to report at least 56 casualties. U.S. officials emphasised that no U.S. fatalities have been reported, though some U.S. troops were injured. Reporting also conflicted about whether the 'Gang of Eight' was notified before the operation; some accounts say they were informed only after it began.

Disinformation and media

The capture set off a torrent of misleading material online: repurposed footage and AI‑generated images circulated rapidly, and some chatbots initially gave contradictory answers about whether the operation had occurred. U.S. government broadcasters said they significantly expanded coverage and reported millions of audience impressions in the region.

Markets and the economy

Markets reacted quickly: shares in major U.S. oil companies rose, gold and silver ticked higher as safe havens, and some investors began to re‑price Venezuelan debt on the prospect of political change.

Analysis and wider implications

Commentators warned the precedent could have broad consequences: some labelled the move a return of 'gunboat diplomacy', others argued it weakens U.S. appeals to international norms and that rivals might exploit the episode, and analysts cautioned that attempts to control Venezuelan oil could provoke violent resistance.

Mr. Trump also told The New York Times he had watched the operation in real time at Mar‑a‑Lago and declined to say whether he had consulted Congress beforehand, saying only 'We'll discuss that.'

State of play

Officials have confirmed Nicolás Maduro was captured and brought to U.S. custody to face federal narcotics charges; the White House characterises the action as law‑enforcement while critics — including U.N. officials and many lawmakers — say it likely breached international law. Casualty totals and some operational details remain disputed across outlets. Next, Congress is set to continue votes and briefings, and a small U.S. diplomatic team has begun exploratory work about reestablishing missions even as sanctions enforcement proceeds.

Venezuela
Sources
Correo del Orinoco Venezuela (State), Runrunes Venezuela, El Nacional Venezuela

Venezuelan state and independent outlets are highlighting sovereignty, press restrictions and immediate information gaps. Correo del Orinoco republishes Beijing’s public pledge of continued support and frames external comments as interference; El Nacional flags the domestic political reverberations, including President Trump’s public attack on five Republican senators who backed a war‑powers restraint resolution. Runrunes, quoting Reporters Without Borders, warns of an information vacuum: it reports roughly 200 foreign journalists stranded in Cúcuta, multiple temporary detentions during Delcy Rodríguez’s inauguration, and the circulation of AI‑generated deepfakes — operational and press‑access details often absent from brief international summaries.

Russia
Sources
Rossiyskaya Gazeta (State), Kommersant, Izvestia (State)

Russian state and business media frame the US action as an illegal act of aggression and foreground diplomatic and energy angles. Rossiyskaya Gazeta emphasised UN criticism and the blunt language of Russia’s UN envoy; Izvestia relays White House briefings saying Washington claims strong leverage over Venezuela’s temporary authorities; Kommersant ran a substantive piece outlining Marco Rubio’s reported three‑step US plan (stabilisation, reconstruction, transition) and also amplified reporting that the White House lacked a clear post‑capture blueprint. Russian outlets therefore stress alleged US motives (control over Caracas’s oil and influence) and point to an absence of a transparent long‑term plan.

Hong Kong
Sources
South China Morning Post

South China Morning Post’s coverage combines analysis and diplomacy: Beijing has repeatedly condemned the US raid but, SCMP argues, will avoid direct military confrontation while exploiting the episode diplomatically. Analysts quoted by the paper say the operation undermines any 'G2' co‑governance framing and gives China room to court disaffected Global‑South partners with economic and political outreach rather than an immediate tit‑for‑tat response.

Brazil
Sources
Folha Brazil

Folha reports President Lula has moved quickly to coordinate regionally: he phoned Colombia’s Gustavo Petro, Mexico’s Claudia Sheinbaum and Canada’s Mark Carney to press defence of international law and peaceful resolution. The paper also says Brazil is dispatching 40 tonnes of medical and dialysis supplies (from a larger 300‑tonne haul) to Venezuela — a concrete humanitarian step and diplomatic push that frames Brazil’s response as regional containment rather than endorsement of the US operation.

Western Europe
Sources
La Vanguardia, El Mundo, France 24, Tagesschau (ARD), Irish Times, NRK Norway (State)

Across Western European outlets the emphasis is on legality and alliance strain. Tagesschau’s poll (ARD‑DeutschlandTrend) found a large majority of Germans view the US action as unjustified; Irish papers quote ministers stressing that international law and the UN Charter must be upheld; La Vanguardia reports Spain’s foreign minister spoke directly with US Secretary Marco Rubio and noted Spain’s public stance and consular developments.

Spanish daily El Mundo is publishing reporting London readers may not have seen prominently: US senators have opened a formal probe into major oil companies (letters to Chevron, Exxon, BP, Shell and others requesting documents and communications), linking the military action to energy‑industry lobbying and post‑operation investment plans. France 24’s media analysis also flags questions about how mainstream coverage shaped consent for the raid — a framing some European editors say UK roundups have underplayed.

Southeast Asia
Sources
Straits Times Singapore, VnExpress

Singapore’s Straits Times focuses on US domestic politics and operational follow‑up: several pieces report White House aides are privately frustrated that President Trump’s foreign focus risks midterm messaging, and they note US preparations to re‑open its Caracas embassy quickly. Vietnam’s VnExpress emphasises Hanoi’s calls for restraint and multilateralism, underlining a diplomatic nuance — Southeast Asian governments stressing the UN Charter and stability rather than binary support or condemnation.

South Asia
Sources
The Hindu, News18 India, Express Tribune Pakistan

Indian and Pakistani outlets stress two strands: congressional oversight and geopolitical fallout. The Hindu outlines the imminent Senate vote to curb the president’s authority and the constitutional war‑powers debate; News18 highlights hardline Russian rhetoric after US seizures of tankers and records China’s sharp diplomatic pushback when asked about Venezuela’s defences; Pakistan’s Express Tribune republishes US late‑night satire and commentary, signalling how domestic US media and comedians are shaping public scepticism at home.

North America
Sources
Globe and Mail, National Post Canada

Canadian coverage pairs the US constitutional angle with hemispheric strategy. The Globe and Mail details the Senate war‑powers motion, its narrow arithmetic and the long road to becoming law; the National Post frames the Maduro raid as the practical application of a new US hemispheric doctrine (a 'Trump Corollary' to the Monroe Doctrine) and urges Ottawa to assess economic and Arctic security implications — a perspective linking Venezuela to direct Canadian policy calculations.

Latin America
Sources
La Nación Argentina

Argentina’s La Nación foregrounds regional human impacts that are less visible in high‑level coverage: it reports Cubans’ mix of hope and fear, noting Venezuela supplied roughly 35,000 barrels per day to Cuba in late 2025 and quoting citizens anxious about fuel for hospitals, transport and everyday services — local social and humanitarian consequences of the Caracas operation.

Middle East
Sources
Mehr News Iran, Daily Sabah Turkey

Iranian state press and Turkish outlets pick out different knock‑on effects. Mehr News cites analysts who expect China’s independent refiners to shift toward more Iranian heavy crude as Venezuelan flows to China fall, a market‑adjustment detail not always noted in Western political briefings. Turkey’s Daily Sabah highlights a geopolitical precedent: Ukraine’s president publicly urged Washington to use similar means against Ramzan Kadyrov — an illustration of how other leaders view the Maduro operation as a potential template.

Sub-Saharan Africa
Sources
News24 South Africa

South African analysis frames the operation through BRICS and resource politics: News24 argues the US narcotics narrative is a red herring, cites official US data pointing to Mexico as the principal source of many illicit drugs, and says months of strikes on vessels preceded the raid. The column reads the intervention as primarily about oil access and urges coordinated political and economic pushback from BRICS — a strategic lens not prominent in many Anglo‑Western summaries.

Eastern Europe
Sources
Czech TV (State)

Czech TV relays Reuters and ABC‑sourced details that add transactional elements to the story: it reports the White House pressed Caracas’s interim government to curb ties with China, Russia, Iran and Cuba before easing oil export restrictions, and that US energy officials signalled sanctions relief tied to US control over proceeds. It also carried reporting that Washington warned Diosdado Cabello he could be targeted if he did not cooperate — concrete diplomatic and coercive details that deepen the picture of US‑Venezuelan negotiations after the raid.
