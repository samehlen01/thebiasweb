On Tuesday, Volodymyr Zelenskyy removed Oleksandr Syrskyi and put Mykhailo Drapatyi in his place. Syrskyi had been commander-in-chief since 2024 and was already one of the capital's most experienced wartime commanders, having led the defence of Kyiv in 2022. That history made the reshuffle especially sensitive because it touched a commander linked to one of the war's earliest defensive successes. The change came after protests had already pushed the issue into the open.

The change landed after last week's ouster of Fedorov. That ouster sparked six days of demonstrations. Zelenskyy later told journalists in Kyiv that he wanted unity. He did not absolve himself of responsibility when the sides failed to come together.

I wanted unity very much. The sides did not achieve it. And that isn't just their problem, but mine as well. I do not absolve myself of responsibility. And in such a situation, the way out is either one side or the other, because without me they don't sit down together.
— Volodymyr Zelenskyy

Those comments, made in Kyiv, framed the problem as a failure inside the ruling wartime camp rather than a dispute that could be solved by one quick dismissal. They also left the door open to further changes, because Zelenskyy was admitting that the tension had not been fully fixed. AP reporting said it was still unclear whether the latest move would calm the protesters or simply shift the argument to a new target. That was a careful line, because he was acknowledging a problem without fully defining its scale.

Why the dispute hardened

Fedorov, whom one report described as a tech-savvy 35-year-old, said his push to modernise the army had collided with the existing leadership. He accused Syrskyi of blocking his ideas and leaning on Zelenskyy to push him out. Syrskyi rejected the suggestion that the split was beyond repair and said their working relationship remained professional. Syrskyi also argued that hard work, not public drama, is what wins the war, warning that personal confrontation only helps Russia. In that telling, the dispute was less about personalities than about how much public pressure should shape wartime decisions.

On the street, demonstrators cast Fedorov as the moderniser and Syrskyi as a figure from an older, Soviet-style command culture. That made Fedorov's praise for Drapatyi easy to understand: he described the new appointee as a fresh start and a voice of change. Their reaction suggested that protesters were not just reacting to one personnel move, but to what they saw as a larger struggle over whether the military was serious about renewal. They treated Drapatyi not as a neutral administrator, but as the sort of officer who might carry the reform argument into the military itself.

Multiple outlets described Drapatyi as popular, battle-hardened and more reform-minded. Some identified him as Fedorov's ally. He currently commands the Joint Forces in Kharkiv Oblast. Zelenskyy said the new assignment would put him in charge of reorganising the command structure and carrying out reform-related changes, which is why his arrival was presented not just as a promotion but as part of a wider reset. For supporters of change, that detail mattered almost as much as the title itself, because it suggested the president wanted a commander who could combine battlefield credibility with reform credentials.

a respected position in government that would unify the country's technology sector
— Volodymyr Zelenskyy

The offer showed that Zelenskyy was not simply clearing space around the army command; he was also trying to keep a technocratic figure inside government. AP reporting said it was still unclear whether Fedorov accepted. If he stayed put, the reshuffle could look like containment; if he moved on, it would underline how far the shake-up reached. Either way, the offer showed that the president was trying to control both the military and the reform agenda at the same time. It also mattered because Fedorov had become a visible part of the government's modernising image.

Protest pressure and competing readings

The Guardian forecast that Zelenskyy would hope the reshuffle calms protesters and brings peace to the streets. AP said it remained uncertain whether the latest moves would satisfy them. Protesters demanded Fedorov be reinstated and Syrskyi removed. They said they would keep going indefinitely if Fedorov was not restored. Those forecasts pointed in different directions, with one source expecting pressure to ease and the other warning the protests could continue. One side saw a possible cooling effect; the other was preparing for a prolonged standoff.

The backlash also reached institutions beyond the street. United24 said it would pause its publications to join the protests, and some senior defence officials, including the deputy commander of the Air Force, resigned. That showed the argument was no longer confined to slogans or a single square. Once resignations and pauses enter the picture, the dispute looks less like temporary frustration and more like a governance problem.

Analysts who spoke to AFP and whose comments were carried by RTE and TheJournal.ie said Syrskyi was not a political threat to Zelenskyy. In their reading, the row was about stalled reform, battlefield losses and military management, not a bid to turn the commander-in-chief into a rival power centre. That perspective shifts the emphasis from palace politics to the practical strain of running a war while trying to modernise the armed forces. It also explains why some coverage treated the dispute as institutional rather than personal, even when the rhetoric around it was emotionally charged.

What remains unresolved

Syrskyi had held the post since 2024. He had also helped defend Kyiv in 2022. Zelenskyy removed Syrskyi and promoted Drapatyi. He then tried to present the move as part of a push for unity.

What is contested is the cause of the break. Fedorov says Syrskyi obstructed reform and pressured the president. Syrskyi rejects the idea that the split was irreparable and says his relationship with Fedorov remained professional. Analysts frame the problem as military management rather than political rivalry.

What remains unknown is whether Fedorov accepted the new offer, and whether the protests will cool or continue. The next test now rests on Drapatyi's ability to deliver the promised reorganisation. It is still not clear whether the latest moves would satisfy protesters and bring peace to the streets. For now, the reshuffle has changed the cast, but not yet settled the argument.
