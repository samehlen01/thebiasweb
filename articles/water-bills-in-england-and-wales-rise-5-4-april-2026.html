Water bills in England and Wales rise 5.4% April 2026

Water bills in England and Wales to rise 5.4% from April 2026, the announcement confirmed, increasing the average household bill by £33 to about £639 a year. The industry and the regulator say the money will pay for upgrades to pipes, meters and sewage treatment, but campaigners and some MPs argue customers are being asked to shoulder the cost of past failings.

Wide variation by company

But the 5.4% headline conceals sharp supplier and regional differences: Affinity Water's central region will see a 13% rise, Bristol Water 12%, Severn Trent 10%, United Utilities 9% and Thames Water just 0.4%.

Some suppliers are pushing much larger increases to finance local projects, while others will record almost negligible rises.

  • South East Water is raising bills by an average of 7 per cent to £324 a year, despite recent supply disruptions that left about 30,000 properties without water.
  • Ofwat has launched a probe into whether South East Water breached its licence conditions on customer service standards.
  • Thames Water will raise bills by around 0.4% (about £3), taking the average to about £658 a year.
  • Yorkshire Water customers will see roughly a 5.6% rise as part of an £8.3bn programme that includes a £38m leakage reduction scheme, replacement of 353km of mains and 350,000 smart meter exchanges.
  • Southern Water plans to increase average household bills by about 8% to fund an £8.5bn investment programme, saying 'every penny' will be reinvested and 'nothing going to shareholders'.

Yorkshire Water has also said that the money collected from customer bills, alongside shareholder investment and borrowing, will be spent on regional improvements from storm overflows to mains replacements and new water resources.

Several companies, including Southern Water, say the proceeds will be reinvested in the network and that shareholders will not be paid from the extra cash; campaigners and watchdogs counter that nearly a third of bills go on servicing debt and dividends and that 'record investment' claims obscure long-running failures.

We understand increasing bills is never welcome, but the money is needed to fund vital upgrades to secure our water supplies, support economic growth and end sewage entering our rivers and seas.
— David Henderson, chief executive, Water UK

Water UK said companies were preparing to invest £20 billion over 2026–27 to secure supplies and tackle sewage discharges; it added that around 2.5 million households would be supported through discounted social tariffs with average discounts of around 40%, and that more than two million households currently access support with an expected extra 300,000 households to be added over the coming year. The industry also pointed to a money-back guarantee under which customer bills would be refunded if promised improvements are not delivered.

We've seen complaints brought to CCW about the affordability of water bills almost triple in the past year and further bill rises will compound people's worries.
— Mike Keil, chief executive, Consumer Council for Water

The Consumer Council for Water said it had recorded a 51 per cent increase in complaints about water companies in 2025 and warned a stronger safety net is needed for people who cannot afford higher bills.

Regulator: standards and expectations

Ofwat has authorised an aggregate 36% increase in bills between 2025 and 2030, and said about 20 percentage points — roughly £86 on the average bill — were front‑loaded into last April's rise.

Ofwat's interim chief executive set out concrete deliverables the regulator wants to see: companies are expected to have installed more than eight million water meters, replaced almost 3,000km of piping and reduced storm overflow sewage spills by 30% from 2024 levels by April 2027.

Pushback and legal challenges

Campaigners and MPs registered strong opposition, arguing too many customers are being asked to cover failures in services and river quality.

  • Glyn Preston (Welsh Liberal Democrats) said 'Asking families in Montgomeryshire and across north-east Wales to swallow a 9% rise in water bills is completely unacceptable' and called for a complete overhaul of how water is run and regulated in Wales.
  • Liz Jarvis (MP) described the rise as 'an absolute insult' and said households should not have to 'foot the bill' for private water companies' failures.
  • Rob Abrams of Surfers Against Sewage said water bills were being 'milked for profit', adding that nearly a third of bills go on servicing debt and dividends while sewage continues to pollute waterways.
  • James Wallace (River Action) said 'when the water sector brags about "record investment", what it really means is that bill payers, not water companies, are being forced to pick up the tab for decades of failure'.

Environmental campaigners remain sceptical that higher bills will deliver cleaner rivers and seas, and River Action has said it is taking the government to court arguing the hikes were not granted properly.

If Ofwat concludes South East Water breached licence conditions, the supplier could be stripped of its licence and put into a special administration regime until a new buyer is found.

Where things stand

Across reports there is agreement that Water bills in England and Wales to rise 5.4% from April 2026 and that the average household bill will rise by £33 to about £639; industry figures say the money will fund a £20 billion investment programme over 2026–27 and support around 2.5 million households through discounted tariffs. Campaigners and River Action say 'bill payers, not water companies, are being forced to pick up the tab for decades of failure' and River Action is taking the government to court, while Ofwat has authorised larger rises, set delivery targets that include more than eight million new meters and a 30% reduction in storm overflow spills by April 2027, and is investigating breaches that could lead to licence stripping and special administration.
