Lord Peter Mandelson resigned his Labour Party membership after files released by the US Department of Justice appeared to show three money transfers of $25,000 each to accounts linked to him in 2003 and 2004.

$75,000
Total reported in the released records
Three transfers of $25,000 each in 2003 and 2004, according to the released files.

The material comes from a large disclosure tied to the Epstein case: reporting said the batch contained more than 2,000 videos, over three million pages and in the order of 180,000 associated images, a volume that has prompted rapid scrutiny and a rush of follow-up checks.

What the files show

Journalists working through the documents focused on a cluster of items: apparent bank statements that list payments, emails linking Epstein to public figures, and a large set of photographs and videos. Coverage singled out a photograph described as showing Mandelson wearing only his underwear, and one email thread in the files concerns bankers' bonuses and tax policy.

Broadcasters also reported a specific banking entry: a $25,000 payment dated 14 May 2003 that was paid into a Barclays account in the name of Reinaldo Avila da Silva, who was identified in coverage as Mr Mandelson's then partner.

Beyond transactional records, the files reportedly include private messages. One item published in reporting is a message said to be from Mr Mandelson to Epstein after Epstein's 2008 conviction in which the lines read: I think the world of you and I feel hopeless and furious about what has happened. I can still barely understand it.

any real chance of making the tax only on the cash portion of the bankers bonus
— Jeffrey Epstein (email to Mandelson)
Trying hard to amend as I explained to Jes last night. Treasury digging in but I am on case.
— Lord Peter Mandelson (reply in released files)

If the exchange is genuine, it would be evidence of direct contact between Epstein and Mandelson on the detail of tax policy. But the provenance of the material has been contested: an unnamed source flagged formatting errors and technical implausibilities in parts of the release, and the Department of Justice cautioned the files may include fake or falsely submitted images, documents or videos.

It is also unclear whether the sums shown on the statements ever cleared into the named accounts, and some outlets emphasised the need for bank confirmation before treating the entries as settled payments.

Mandelson's response and party exit

Mr Mandelson said he had written on Sunday evening to Hollie Ridley, general secretary of the Labour Party, to say he was stepping down from his party membership, and he said he wanted to repeat his apology to the women and girls whose voices should have been heard long before now.

I have no record and no recollection of receiving these sums and do not know if the documents are authentic,
— Lord Peter Mandelson

He remains a life peer and is on a leave of absence from Parliament; reporting also said he was sacked as UK ambassador to the United States last year amid revelations about Epstein.

Political fallout

Across Westminster the allegations prompted swift and sharp responses. Several Labour MPs branded Prime Minister Keir Starmer weak for appointing Mandelson and failing to act on warnings, and the Labour backbencher Andy McDonald said: He should be immediately expelled from the Party.

The Conservative Party said Lord Mandelson was completely disgraced and insisted that there must now be a full and thorough independent investigation into his appointment and Downing Street's role. The party's intervention specifically urged an independent probe of how and why he was given public office.

The Labour Party issued a separate statement that The Labour Party takes all complaints seriously and they are investigated in line with our rules and procedures. SNP Westminster leader Stephen Flynn wrote to the prime minister's ethics adviser calling for an immediate and swift investigation into Mandelson's conduct in public life and said the SNP wanted him stripped of his life peerage.

Ministers in the government offered qualified statements rather than immediate punitive steps. Education minister Olivia Bailey said the resignation was the right decision but warned the mechanics of removing a peerage are complicated, noting you have to have been sent to jail for over 12 months or an Act of Parliament needs to be passed to strip a peerage. Communities Secretary Steve Reed said any action should await clarity about whether money had been declared and that Mr Mandelson should explain himself.

Authenticity disputes and US scrutiny

Questions about the provenance of the material have become an immediate focus. An unidentified source told reporters the financial statements could not be taken at face value, pointing to formatting errors and technical implausibilities, for example that dollar cheques could not be paid directly into UK accounts, and urged banks to confirm whether the entries were genuine. The Department of Justice attached a caveat to the publication that the release may include fake or falsely submitted images, documents or videos.

There are reports the US House Oversight Committee may summon Lord Mandelson to testify on his links to Epstein, and pressure is growing for him to appear before US congressional investigators to answer questions about what the records show and how any payments, if genuine, were held or declared.

Peers, Parliament and practical questions

Practical issues remain prominent in Westminster: Mr Mandelson remains a life peer and is on leave from the Lords, and ministers emphasised the limited routes for removing a title under current law. Commentators also flagged that a return to the upper chamber, even on leave, raises questions about public cost, with some outlets suggesting taxpayers could face costs related to any return to the House of Lords.

What happens next

For now the narrow points on which reporting converges are that Lord Peter Mandelson has resigned his Labour Party membership and that files released by the US Department of Justice appear to include three transfers of $25,000 each. Beyond that, the provenance of the material is disputed: the DOJ itself warned the release may include fake or falsely submitted images, documents or videos, and an unidentified source has pointed to formatting errors. Mr Mandelson says he has no record or recollection of receiving these sums. Political pressure for a full and thorough independent investigation has grown, and there are reports US congressional investigators may seek his testimony next.
