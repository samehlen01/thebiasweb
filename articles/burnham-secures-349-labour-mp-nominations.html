Labour MP nomination total
The MPs' contest is complete, but the affiliate stage still has to be cleared.

Burnham’s 349 nominations mean no rival can now challenge him. He still needs backing from three affiliated organisations, including at least two trade unions.

If Press Association’s timetable holds, Friday would mark the handover point, with the move into No 10 still set for 20 July.

Burnham’s policy pitch

stick by Labour's 2024 general election manifesto
— Andy Burnham

Burnham says he will stick to Labour’s 2024 manifesto and does not plan an early election. He argues that Britain needs a new style of politics and a redistribution of power away from Westminster.

His main policy priorities include:

  • He wants Labour to consider proportional representation in a future manifesto.
  • He also wants greater public control over water, energy and transport, and a Manchester-based devolution unit.
  • He would keep the state pension triple lock and preserve the current formula for annual pension rises.

He said during his Makerfield campaign that net migration needs to fall further. Reviving HS2 could add north-south capacity and support more balanced growth, while his devolution plan would push more power away from Whitehall.

some room for movement on tax
— Andy Burnham

Burnham has also said there is some room for movement on tax, and there is a case for higher business rates on warehouses. Burnham says public control and business-rate changes could lower bills and help high streets.

By contrast, Miatta Fahnbulleh says he should ease cost-of-living pressure and put more money back into people’s pockets. Near-term cost-of-living support could give households some respite.

Internal criticism and mandate questions

Rachel Reeves says Burnham needs a worked-through plan and must stay laser-focused.

Jesse Norman is more critical, saying Burnham has offered only a few press releases, has barely taken questions and still leaves basic issues unanswered. He says Burnham should face MPs before recess.

The Daily Mail argues that he lacks both a public mandate and clear plans, saying he did not stand on Labour’s 2024 mandate and that his by-election backing was tiny compared with the wider electorate.

Burnham’s response is that support is coming from across the party and that politics needs to be rewired away from Westminster and toward ordinary people.

Immediate pressure points

On immigration, the sharpest row inside Labour is over Shabana Mahmood’s immigration bill. Burnham is expected to vote for it and to back the associated indefinite leave to remain changes.

The proposal would stretch the qualifying period from 5 to 10 years, and i News and The Guardian say that could fuel a Labour rebellion. Nearly 80 Labour MPs say the changes are unfair, retrospective and against Labour values. Burnham’s spokesperson calls the bill compassionate, credible and fair.

The Guardian reported that transitional arrangements are being considered. A possible five-year route for some current migrants would narrow the impact even if the overall direction stays the same.

BBC correspondents say foreign policy will be judged first by continuity and by whether Burnham can win over major partners abroad. They name the United States, China, Russia, Europe and India as the audiences watching most closely. They say room for a dramatic break is limited.

Burnham may try a charm offensive with Donald Trump, although policy differences could still dominate the effort. His foreign policy may stay broadly continuous with Keir Starmer’s line rather than turning sharply in another direction.

Sir Mel Stride says Burnham will lean further into tax and warns that markets could get worse.

The Daily Mail and other reports suggest a larger autumn Budget or spending review. They say it could help fund housing and utilities through new taxes. Other options, such as higher council tax revaluations or a mansion tax, are still speculative.
