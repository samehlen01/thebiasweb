Doomsday Clock 2026 updated by scientists amid nuclear and AI concerns: the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists moved the Doomsday Clock to 85 seconds to midnight, the closest setting in its history. The Bulletin first unveiled the clock in 1947 in the wake of the U.S. using atomic bombs against Japan in World War II.

What scientists cited

The Bulletin said increasing nuclear risks — including modernization of delivery systems, rising arsenals, and the impending expiry of the New START treaty — drove the clock movement. Daniel Holz, chair of the Bulletin's Science and Security Board, described AI as a significant and accelerating disruptive technology that is supercharging mis- and disinformation and complicating responses to other existential risks.

The Bulletin warned that unregulated 'mirror life' — chemically synthesized mirror-image organisms — could displace natural microbes and evade immune systems, creating unprecedented biological risks. It added that droughts, fires, floods and storms are intensifying, and that last year's global average surface temperature ranked among the hottest on record.

Humanity has not made sufficient progress on the existential risks that endanger us all,
— Alexandra Bell, president and CEO, Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists

Bell urged collaboration and said the issues influencing the clock are solvable if people act. Daniel Holz framed the exercise as optimistic and said the whole point is that 'there are ways to turn back the clock.'

Reactions and critics

Maria Ressa warned: "We are living through an information Armageddon — the crisis beneath all crises," and she said the crisis is driven by technology that spreads lies faster than facts.

Some social-media commenters wrote: "I really hate the doomsday clock. It feels like just a fear tactic for the public." Another commenter wrote: "It's total bulls**t, it's not based on anything measurable and the start at 7min before midnight was just a random guess." A Gizmodo author argued the Doomsday Clock is, in many ways, a silly relic of the Cold War era but added it "is also not wrong."

Consequences highlighted by the Bulletin

The Bulletin warned that the expiry of major arms-control constraints could lead to a runaway nuclear arms race. It cautioned that an AI arms race and AI-driven mis- and disinformation could have dire consequences and make it harder to address other threats. The group also wrote that the rise of nationalistic autocracies undermines cooperation and accelerates existential threats, and that unregulated mirror life could enable organisms that evade immune systems and cause deadly pandemics.

Expert speculation

  • Alicia Sanders–Zakre (International Campaign to Abolish Nuclear Weapons) said the Clock could be moved forward by at least one second.
  • Hamza Chaudhry (Future of Life Institute) said the clock could be moved "five to 10 seconds" forward.
  • Dr SJ Beard (Centre for the Study of Existential Risk) said they thought the clock should be moved nine seconds forward.
  • The Bulletin said the US administration may be considering resuming explosive nuclear testing.
  • Chaudhry also speculated that China is on a trajectory to match US and Russian ICBM numbers by the end of the decade.
  • Dario Amodei (Anthropic) warned that AI could hand humanity 'almost unimaginable power' and create risks such as autonomy, misuse and economic disruption.

Those predictions were presented as speculation by the sources and remain unsettled.

Why accounts diverge

The divergence in interpretation stems partly from who speaks and why: Bulletin scientists offered a policy-focused assessment tied to nuclear, technological and climate trends, while social-media critics questioned the Clock's methodology and motives.

State of play

Reporting agrees that the Bulletin moved the Doomsday Clock to 85 seconds to midnight and that the organization identified nuclear escalation, AI-driven disinformation, biological risks and intensifying climate impacts as key drivers. The Bulletin and board members argued that the problems are solvable if people work together, while social-media critics called the device a fear tactic or headline-grabbing symbol and experts offered speculative numeric adjustments that remain unsettled. The looming expiry of New START and questions about whether the U.S. might resume explosive testing were singled out as immediate items to watch.
