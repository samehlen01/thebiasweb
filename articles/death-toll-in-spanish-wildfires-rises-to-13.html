The wildfire death toll in Andalusia's Almería province reached 13 after a 93-year-old British woman died. Spanish authorities warned that 23 people were still missing, so the death toll could rise. About 7,000 hectares had burned. About 1,400 to 1,500 people were evacuated.

13
Deaths confirmed
Andalusian authorities said 12 of the 13 victims were foreign nationals.

Separately, experts coordinating the identification work said they had identified six of the 12 bodies found at the fire's epicentre. Many bodies were so badly burned that DNA samples had to be taken to identify them. Spanish authorities also believed some victims were foreign tourists who had abandoned their vehicles and fled on foot.

Meanwhile, more than 500 firefighters were still tackling the blaze. Andalusian authorities said the fire had been contained enough for some residents to go home.

Families caught in the fire

Pete and Fran Gillam were last heard from on Thursday, July 9, around 7 pm. Their daughter, Danielle Gillam-Kirton, said police had confirmed the couple did not survive after they texted that they were evacuating.

We are heartbroken to share that we have received confirmation from the police that Mum and Dad did not survive the fire.
— Danielle Gillam-Kirton, their daughter

On Saturday, Spanish police rescued two British tourists who had suffered burns to 40 per cent of their bodies after they became trapped while hiking. Simon Batchelor and Lisa Fryett were pulled off a mountainside and remain in critical condition. Their family said they were fighting for their lives, and a fundraising page said they had begged rescuers to save them so they could see their children again. The rescue took place in Almería province.

Another survivor, Malcolm Timbrell, said he believed he and Annette Kilgore would have lived if he had not gone back for the cats in Bédar, in Almería province. He said four of the six cars instantly combusted, and as each one started to go, he moved back one car; the last two cars survived, though badly singed and burnt.

If we'd have done the sensible thing and gone the other way and let our cats die, we both would be alive. But when you've got animals, you don't think like that.
— Malcolm Timbrell, survivor

Warnings and cause

Thomas-Wolf Verdonckt said no officials warned his group or gave them information before they ran from the flames. The Andalusian government said sheltering in place was the safest choice. Antonio Sanz, a regional official, said an alternative evacuation route had become a mortal trap.

The cause remained uncertain, but Spanish authorities said the fire may have been caused by a fallen power line or electrical cable.

Juanma Moreno said dry vegetation and wind had turned it into a ticking time bomb and linked the disaster to climate chaos. Félix Bolaños said a climate emergency had driven its ferocity and speed.
