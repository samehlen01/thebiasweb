Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky told reporters the United States wants Russia and Ukraine to sign a peace deal that would end the war by June. Zelensky said Washington was pressing for a peace deal to be signed by June. He said Washington had proposed that the two negotiating teams meet in the United States, probably in Miami next week, and that Ukraine had confirmed participation.

What Zelensky outlined

Zelensky said U.S. officials told negotiators that Washington would be actively involved in monitoring any future ceasefire. He said the U.S. proposed resuming an agreement to suspend strikes on energy infrastructure and noted that a similar initiative two weeks earlier produced four days without Russian strikes on power plants. He added that when Moscow previously agreed to a U.S.-suggested one-week pause, it was violated after only four days.

Last night's attack involved more than 400 drones and around 40 missiles of various types.
— Volodymyr Zelensky, President of Ukraine

Zelensky described the strike as part of a renewed campaign against Ukraine's power system. Ukrenergo said it was the second mass strike on energy infrastructure this year and forced nuclear power plants to reduce output. Ukrenergo and government officials warned that the damage caused blackouts and disrupted heating and water supplies for civilians.

Officials said the strikes targeted generation facilities and distribution substations and worsened damage along transmission lines. Ukrenergo reported that nuclear plants had reduced output in response to the damage. Zelensky said depriving Moscow of the ability to use the cold as leverage would reduce Russia's capacity to pressure Ukraine in winter.

Diplomacy and sticking points

Senior-level delegations held a second round of negotiations in Abu Dhabi, which reporting says were mediated by envoys linked to former President Trump, Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner. Zelensky said Russia had demanded that Ukraine withdraw forces from parts of Donbas still under Kyiv control, a demand Kyiv rejects, insisting territorial matters be resolved along current lines.

Reporting described the mediators as envoys associated with former President Trump, naming Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner as coordinating figures.

Zelensky said the parties had discussed, for the first time, the prospect of a trilateral leaders' summit to address the most difficult remaining issues. He cautioned that preparatory steps would be needed before such a leaders' meeting could take place.

Security guarantees and economic offers

Zelensky said the Trump administration was refusing to sign a U.S.-Ukraine security guarantees agreement even though Kyiv considers it nearly ready, and that Washington prefers to sign security guarantees roughly at the same time as other peace agreements. He argued that signing the security guarantees first would increase public trust in Ukraine that any deal was real. Zelensky also said Russia had presented the United States with a $12 trillion (£8.83 trillion) economic proposal that he called the 'Dmitriev package.' He said Moscow must be deprived of the ability to use the cold as leverage against Ukraine.

Kyiv says signing the security guarantees first would demonstrate to Ukrainian citizens that any package is credible, while U.S. officials prefer to coordinate the timing with other parts of a deal.

Accusations, denials and outside views

Russian Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov accused Ukraine of being behind an assassination attempt on Lt. Gen. Vladimir Alekseyev. He framed the allegation as an attempt to undermine negotiations and said the act was intended to sabotage the peace talks. Ukraine denied any involvement.

Zelensky said the American push toward a June deadline was being driven by U.S. domestic politics, including the midterm elections, and that Washington wanted to get as much accomplished before then as possible. Experts, including Mark Galeotti, said it is hard to see a credible path to a rapid peace by June and warned that Ukraine could face pressure to accept disproportionate compromises. Zelensky speculated that if the June deadline is not met, the Trump administration would likely put pressure on both sides to meet it.

Something could be happening.
— Donald Trump

Trump's brief comment has been cited by U.S. envoys as a sign of possible progress, but analysts have urged caution, saying it does not show that core differences have been resolved.

Mark Galeotti said it is hard to see much ground for a rapid peace by June and cautioned that Kyiv could face disproportionate pressure to concede.

State of play

President Zelensky says the United States is pushing for a peace settlement by June. He said Washington had proposed hosting the next round of talks in the United States, likely in Miami next week, and that Ukraine had confirmed participation. Senior-level delegations have already met in Abu Dhabi under mediation linked to envoys associated with former President Trump. Russian Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov accused Ukraine of being behind an assassination attempt on Lt. Gen. Vladimir Alekseyev and said it was intended to sabotage the peace talks. Ukraine denied any involvement. Russia has demanded that Ukraine withdraw forces from parts of Donbas it still holds, which clashes with Kyiv's insistence on resolving territorial issues along current lines. Zelensky said Russia presented the U.S. with a $12 trillion (£8.83 trillion) economic proposal that he called the 'Dmitriev package.' Experts say a credible path to a June settlement is hard to see. Zelensky said the U.S. side confirmed it will be actively involved in monitoring a potential future ceasefire. He said the U.S. proposed resuming an agreement to suspend attacks on energy facilities, noting a similar initiative two weeks earlier produced four days without Russian strikes on power plants. Zelensky said that if the June deadline is not met, the Trump administration would likely put pressure on both sides to meet it.

TASS (State)
The Russian negotiating group was headed by Igor Kostyukov, head of the Main Intelligence Directorate of the General Staff.
— TASS (state news agency)

Russia's state news agency dated the first round of trilateral security consultations to Jan 23–24 and named Igor Kostyukov as head of the Russian negotiating group; the same dispatch quoted a U.S. envoy as saying Moscow and Kyiv had agreed a 314‑person prisoner exchange and that consultations would continue in the coming weeks. This account comes via state media, which limits independent verification.

Western Europe
Sources
France 24, ZDF Heute, taz (Germany), Die Welt (Germany), Il Giornale (Italy), To Vima (Greece)
A reactor shut down automatically. That is a level of attacks that no terrorist in the world would allow.
— Volodymyr Zelensky, President of Ukraine

European reporting added battlefield and infrastructure details not emphasised in the UK synthesis: Kyiv's general staff said its forces struck the Balashovo oil depot in Russia's Saratov region and damaged several drone‑control centres in occupied areas; Ukraine's energy minister was cited saying two western thermal power plants and key distribution substations were hit. Italian accounts described hypersonic Zircon missiles and drones knocking out the Burshtyn thermal plant — leaving electricity guaranteed for "at most two hours a day" — and relayed an ISW warning of a possible summer offensive involving roughly 165,000 troops. Continental pieces also noted two Polish airports briefly suspended flights and that Washington had notified Congress of a $185m Class‑IX spare‑parts sale; one Greek report quoted energy firm DTEK calling the strike the "tenth mass" attack since October 2025. Several outlets repeated a Reuters‑sourced timetable pushing for a March draft and a May referendum, a schedule some participants described as unrealistic.

Southeast Asia
Sources
Straits Times Singapore
Only Trump can stop the war.
— Andrii Sybiha, Ukrainian foreign minister

The foreign minister said Ukrainian and Russian leaders need to meet in person to settle the hardest issues and added delegations in Abu Dhabi concluded an exchange of 314 prisoners (157 each). The report says U.S. interlocutors told Kyiv they would seek congressional ratification of U.S.–Ukraine security guarantees as a backstop without deploying U.S. ground forces, and a Paris communiqué envisaged allied participation in a U.S.‑led ceasefire monitoring mechanism using drones, sensors and satellites rather than U.S. troops. Sybiha also said some unnamed countries beyond Britain and France privately offered to send deterrent troops, warned any recognition of Russian sovereignty over Crimea or the Donbas would be "legally void," and cited analysts who estimate Russia has gained only about 1.3% of Ukrainian territory since early 2023.

The Ukrainian Air Force spokesperson Yurii Ihnat was quoted as saying only 38 missiles were shot down, noting many were ballistic and therefore harder to intercept. The same reporting quoted an SBU official who said Ukrainian drones struck a plant in Russia's Tver region that allegedly makes missile‑fuel components, causing a large fire and temporarily complicating Russia's rocket‑fuel production.
