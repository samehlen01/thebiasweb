John Virgo, the BBC snooker commentator and Big Break star, has died aged 79. The World Snooker Tour announced his death and said he is survived by his wife Rosie and their children, Gary and Brooke-Leah.

Announcement and location

Jason Francis of Snooker Legends posted that Virgo 'passed away in Spain', called the news 'heartbreaking' and said Virgo 'was doing what he loved to do right up until the end'.

I was borrowing £200,000 against the mortgage just to keep up the pretence that I was successful.
— John Virgo

That admission is part of a long-standing public account in which Virgo described gambling problems: he said he borrowed £200,000 against his mortgage and that his Surrey home was repossessed.

A career on the baize and the box

He spent 18 years as a professional, won the 1979 UK Championship and reached the World Championship semi-finals that year, and rose to a career-high world ranking of number 10.

On television, Virgo co-presented Big Break for the BBC from 1991 to 2002 and became a household name; at its peak the show attracted over 13 million viewers.

After retiring in 1994, Virgo moved into full-time commentary with the BBC and remained a regular presence on its tournament coverage; his voice and the catchphrase 'Where's the cue ball going?' were widely recognised.

Reports noted Virgo's final BBC commentary came on 18 January when Kyren Wilson defeated John Higgins 10-6 in the Masters final at Alexandra Palace.

He was later inducted into the World Snooker Tour hall of fame in recognition of his playing and broadcasting, and he was also known for exhibition performances, trick shots and impressions.

There was an outcry in 2022 when media reports said the BBC planned to drop Virgo and Dennis Taylor from its team; the corporation reversed its decision and he remained on the commentary roster.

Tributes and memories

Top players paid tribute. Ronnie O'Sullivan said he was 'absolutely gutted'. Neil Robertson described Virgo as 'a great character and an amazing pundit and commentator'. Mark Selby posted he was 'so so sad' and that Virgo would be 'greatly missed'. Shaun Murphy said he learned much from sharing a commentary box and called Virgo 'the voice of snooker'. Neal Foulds described him as 'incredibly generous, kind and funny'.

Comedian Jim Davidson called Virgo 'a great mate' and wrote 'what a character, what a talent'. Frank Bruno recalled his humour and called him 'a really nice man', saying it was 'a sad loss' for those who knew him.

On the circuit

A moment of silence was observed before the second session of World Grand Prix matches in Hong Kong involving all eight players, including Mark Allen and Shaun Murphy. Some commentators said 'snooker commentaries won't be the same'.

Legacy and style

Away from competitive play, Virgo cultivated a light-hearted persona and regularly appeared in exhibitions, performing trick shots and impressions that became a popular part of his public life.

Where reporting stands
