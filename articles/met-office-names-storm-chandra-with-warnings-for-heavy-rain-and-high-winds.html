Met Office names Storm Chandra and issues warnings for heavy rain and high winds is the event this report covers. The Met Office named the low-pressure system Storm Chandra and said it was forecast to bring strong winds and heavy rain to the UK on Tuesday. The agency also issued multiple yellow and amber warnings across several regions.

Storm Chandra has been named and is forecast to bring strong winds and heavy rain to the UK on Tuesday.
— Met Office

National headlines summarised the situation, reporting ten yellow or amber warnings and quantifying impacts with figures such as up to 80mm of rain, 20cm of snow and 80mph gusts in some places.

Warnings and timing

An amber wind warning covers eastern parts of Northern Ireland from 05:00 on Tuesday until 21:00, with damaging gusts of up to 75mph (120km/h). An amber warning for heavy rain in south-west England runs from 17:00 Monday until 09:00 Tuesday, with 30-50mm widely and 60-80mm across some higher ground, especially south Dartmoor.

A yellow warning for persistent rain in Northern Ireland is valid from 12:00 Monday until 18:00, with 10 to 20mm widely and up to 40mm over the hills. Forecasters said a spell of heavy rain would move north-eastwards across the region during Monday afternoon, with 10-20mm widely, rising to 20-30mm in a few areas and up to 40mm on high ground.

Strong east to south-easterly winds were forecast for Tuesday, with gusts of 40-50mph inland and 60-70mph possible along some exposed coasts. Forecasters said the strongest winds are likely in the South West and Wales where gusts could reach 80mph in locations such as Pembrokeshire and the Isles of Scilly.

Regional agencies and alerts

Met Éireann issued Status Yellow rain warnings for counties including Carlow, Kilkenny and Waterford from midnight until 23:00 on Tuesday, and Status Yellow wind warnings for several southern and eastern counties including Cork, Kerry and Dublin from 05:00 until 23:00; it forecast winds would be very strong and gusty at coasts and exposed areas. The Environment Agency in England issued 97 flood alerts and 19 warnings, Natural Resources Wales issued two alerts and the Scottish Environment Protection Agency had four warnings.

Impacts, risks and speculation

Forecasts and advisories warned of a high chance of significant impacts including air, sea and rail delays or cancellations, spray and flooding on roads, interruption to power supplies, and some homes and businesses flooding; they also cautioned that a few communities might be cut off by flooded roads and that trees could be damaged or blown down. Coastal gales may produce large waves likely to overtop sea walls and promenades. The BBC noted there is a risk of flooding as more rain falls on ground that is currently saturated.

Reactions and guidance

Paul Gundersen of the Met Office (official_statement) said the storm would bring a range of hazards, including strong winds, heavy rain on saturated ground and snow on higher ground. Marco Petagna of the Met Office (forecast) said most parts of the UK would certainly see wet and windy weather over the next couple of days and that the far South West could be particularly windy with heavy rain in parts. Steve Willington of the Met Office (official_statement) said unsettled conditions would continue with rain, brisk winds and some further hill snow, with an enhanced potential for impactful weather from Monday night into Tuesday. Nick Mullender of the RAC (analysis) warned motorists that another named storm was set to heap more misery on motorists and advised against driving through standing water.

Differences in reporting

Reporting and agency briefings emphasised similar dangers but differed on some headline numbers. Some national headlines and tabloid summaries presented top-end figures such as up to 80mm of rain and 80mph gusts, while Met Office and BBC briefings highlighted 75mph gusts for parts of Northern Ireland and 40-70mph more widely, and gave area-specific rainfall ranges such as 30-50mm widely in the south-west. Snow totals were another contrast: one reported figure put snow at around 20cm in places, whereas Met Office briefings referred instead to hill snow without a large numeric estimate.

What happens next

At this stage, agencies agree that the Met Office has named Storm Chandra and that region-specific warnings for heavy rain and strong winds are in force while environment agencies have issued multiple flood alerts. Forecasters and the BBC warn of a high chance of travel disruption, flooding and power interruptions, and Met Office forecasters said unsettled conditions could continue into Monday night and Tuesday. The UK Health Security Agency has activated cold health alerts for northern England from 6pm tonight until Friday, while some outlets continue to report larger top-end figures for gusts and snow as forecasters refine area-by-area assessments.
