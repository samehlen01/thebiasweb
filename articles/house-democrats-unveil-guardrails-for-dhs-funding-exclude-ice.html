House Democrats unveil guardrails for DHS funding, exclude ICE

House Democrats unveiled guardrails for Department of Homeland Security funding and floated legislation that would exclude ICE as a shutdown deadline neared. Democratic leaders released a list of DHS guardrails meant to set limits on immigration enforcement and other agency operations as talks continued in the Capitol. Meanwhile, the House passed an appropriations package that funds Defense, Health and Human Services, Labor, Housing and Urban Development, State and Transportation through September and keeps the Department of Homeland Security funded at 2025 levels until Feb. 13.

What Democrats proposed

Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer and House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries circulated the guardrails as an opening framework for negotiations. The list targets how agents operate, including restricting masks, requiring IDs and body cameras. Schumer and Jeffries also said the administration could take immediate steps to show good faith, including removing DHS Secretary Kristi Noem and fully ramping down the immigration operation in Minneapolis.

Timeline and Republican response

Republicans pushed back. Senate Majority Leader John Thune said Republicans were not close to a funding agreement and called a deal by Feb. 13 an 'impossibility,' and he also said 'There's some room there' for limited items. House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries warned, 'Absent bold and meaningful change, there is no credible path forward with respect to the Department of Homeland Security funding bill next week,' and Sen. Chuck Schumer said, 'I'm just going to say we're sending them a proposal and we await their response.'

As of right now, we aren't anywhere close to having any sort of an agreement that would enable us to fund the Department of Homeland Security,
— Sen. John Thune (R-S.D.)

Thune's floor remarks underscored Republican skepticism that the two-week DHS stopgap gives limited time to negotiate and that a binding settlement is likely before Feb. 13.

Court ruling and ICE practice

In a separate legal development, U.S. District Judge Mustafa Kasubhai granted a preliminary injunction in Oregon barring warrantless immigration arrests unless officers perform individualized assessments of flight risk. The court concluded it had evidence that agents routinely arrested people for alleged immigration violations without warrants or individualized determinations of likelihood to flee. ICE acting head Todd Lyons, by contrast, issued a memo that emphasized agents should not make an arrest without an administrative arrest warrant issued by a supervisor unless they develop probable cause and believe the person is likely to escape before a warrant can be obtained. The plaintiff in the case, Victor Cruz Gamez, said he was taken into custody during a traffic stop last fall and held in immigration detention for three weeks despite having legal authorization to work and a pending visa application.

An NPR tally found 32 people died in ICE custody in 2025, including two victims of a shooting in Texas, making the year among the deadliest for detainees in decades. In the past year, nearly 400 U.S. Public Health Service officers did monthlong tours helping to provide basic medical care to detainees at ICE facilities nationwide. Last year, about 340 USPHS officers left the service.

We have been tasked with protecting and promoting health, and instead, we are being asked to facilitate inhumane operations,
— Rebekah Stewart, former USPHS nurse practitioner

Stewart and others described moral distress from deployments. Six of 11 USPHS officers who spoke with NPR said they planned to leave or had already tendered their resignations largely because of recent or impending deployment to ICE facilities. Admiral Brian Christine said the mission of the USPHS Commissioned Corps is to care for people where the need is greatest and warned against abandoning those individuals, and public health experts warned that losing these officers weakens the nation's ability to respond to public health crises in the long term.

Political stakes and divergent views

Inside the Democratic conference, pressure on Senate leaders surfaced. Some progressives said they felt Schumer gave up the party's best leverage by reopening government, and one House Democrat speaking on background said the main feeling among members is a lack of trust in his strength and ability to strike a hard bargain.

Republicans labeled the guardrails a publicity exercise. Sen. Katie Britt called the Democratic proposal a ridiculous Christmas list of demands for the press and added, "This is NOT negotiating in good faith." Rep. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez warned that failing to secure reform could be a huge failure for Senate Democrats, and Rep. Chuy García said Democrats should lean on public opinion, arguing that Republicans ought to be worried how their policies are faring with the American people.

Schumer needs to get the hell out over and over and over until he does. He continues to demonstrate to us that he can't meet the moment.
— Rep. Delia Ramirez

Rep. Delia Ramirez said Schumer should step down from leadership. Some progressives said they felt Schumer gave up the party's best leverage by reopening government, and Ramirez's remarks reflect a factional split: some members want tougher bargaining and publicly expressed impatience, while others hope the guardrails can be translated into enforceable constraints at DHS through bargaining in the Senate.

State of play

House lawmakers approved a broad appropriations package that covers many agencies through September while placing DHS on short-term footing through Feb. 13. Senate Democrats circulated guardrails and urged immediate steps such as removing DHS Secretary Kristi Noem, and Sen. Schumer says he is sending a proposal and awaiting a response. What is contested is whether negotiators can convert those guardrails into enforceable reforms before the stopgap expires. Senate Majority Leader John Thune called a Feb. 13 deal 'an impossibility' even as he said some modest items 'could' be done. A federal judge's injunction and ICE's internal guidance present differing accounts of arrest practice that will shape further legal and political bargaining.
