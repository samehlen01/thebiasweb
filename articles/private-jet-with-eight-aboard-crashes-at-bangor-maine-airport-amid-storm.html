This report covers the event 'Private jet with eight aboard crashes at Bangor, Maine airport'. According to federal aviation officials, a private jet carrying eight people crashed while attempting to take off from Bangor International Airport at around 7:45 p.m. local time on Sunday.

What happened

The Federal Aviation Administration said the aircraft was a Bombardier Challenger 600, while an unnamed source who spoke to CNN described the plane as a Bombardier Challenger 650.

Photographs and video from the scene showed the aircraft flipped and smoking, and a government official briefed on the incident said the jet was engulfed in flames after the crash.

All traffic is stopped on the field! All traffic is stopped on the field!
— Air traffic controller (LiveATC audio)

Live air-traffic recordings captured controllers and pilots discussing poor visibility minutes before the accident; those recordings also include discussion of de-icing on busy channels, and some outlets cautioned that it is unclear which aircraft those transmissions involved.

Aircraft upside down. We have a passenger aircraft upside down.
— Air traffic controller (LiveATC audio)

CNN published the audio and reported that the initial alarm came roughly two minutes after the jet had been cleared for takeoff.

First responders are on scene and assessing the situation
— Bangor International Airport

Airport officials said the field was closed, first responders remained on scene and were expected to be working for several more hours, and members of the public were urged to avoid the area.

Weather and operations

The National Weather Service had issued a winter storm warning for the Bangor area, forecasting between 10 and 16 inches of snow in parts of Maine and reporting temperatures at or below freezing around the time of the accident.

More than 11,000 flights were canceled and nearly 5,500 delayed
U.S. flights affected Sunday (FlightAware)
Tracker FlightAware (reported by outlets)

Those disruptions came as airlines and airports adjusted operations for heavy snow and subfreezing wind chills.

The FAA and the National Transportation Safety Board said they are investigating the crash, while FlightRadar24's publicly available data suggested the jet had been preparing to take off after arriving from Houston and federal registration records show the plane is registered to a Houston-based law firm.

Officials have not yet released the identities or the conditions of the eight people who were on board.

Opinions and speculation

Several officials and forecasters framed the weather as a major factor: Bangor Police Sgt. Jeremy Brock described conditions as challenging, and some forecasters have called the storm catastrophic. Reporters and analysts have speculated that pilots were struggling with visibility before takeoff and that de-icing or other winter procedures were discussed on air-traffic channels, though those points remain unconfirmed.

Consequences

Airport managers closed the field and canceled other flights at Bangor, first responders were expected to work for hours at the scene, and the accident occurred during a storm that multiple reports say has led to deaths and widespread power outages.

State of play

Federal officials and multiple outlets agree that a private jet carrying eight people crashed while attempting to depart Bangor International Airport; investigators from the FAA and the NTSB have opened an inquiry but have not released names or medical conditions for those aboard. Early reporting conflicts over the aircraft model — the FAA named a Challenger 600 while an unnamed source told CNN it was a Challenger 650 — and recordings that captured controllers discussing visibility and other exchanges have not been definitively linked to this flight, which remains disputed and speculative. Imagery and audio show an overturned, burning aircraft and emergency crews are working the scene; investigators said they will gather evidence and provide updates as the inquiry continues.

South Asia
Sources
Hindustan Times, Indian Express, Times of India

Indian outlets published registration and ownership details not emphasised elsewhere: they identified the jet as a Bombardier Challenger 650, gave its federal registration as N10KJ and named the registered owner KTKJ Challenger LLC, noting that company shares a Houston address with the law firm Arnold & Itkin; those reports cite FAA records showing the aircraft entered service in April 2020.

three crew and possibly five passengers
— Air traffic controller

Those outlets cite LiveATC/CNN audio in which a controller cleared the pilot for takeoff on Runway 33 and later relayed that passenger/crew count; they also publish eyewitness descriptions that the aircraft "lifted off, started shuttering, rolled onto the roof and burst into flames." Reports said a preliminary accident report may be released on Monday, January 26, and Times of India noted emergency declarations were approved for multiple states as conditions worsened.

Latin America
Sources
CiberCuba, El Nacional Venezuela

CiberCuba presented the passenger/crew composition as a presumption — three crew and five passengers — and reported a controller authorised the aircraft's takeoff on Runway 33; it listed the items the FAA and NTSB would review, including pre‑takeoff communications, meteorological data, runway conditions, the aircraft's technical condition and de‑icing procedures.

Cuban reporting here is framed as a presumption and may lack independent verification given constraints on media in Cuba.

there is no capacity in the airlines to rebook people two and a half days out on planes
— U.S. Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy

El Nacional gave wider storm figures — six dead and power outages affecting more than one million homes — and cited Duffy while reporting more than 11,000 cancellations and 17,000 delays with roughly 2,600 cancellations expected the next day; the 17,000 delays figure is notably higher than the roughly 5,500 flight delays reported by FlightAware in UK coverage.

North America
Sources
CBC Canada

CBC supplied precise local geography: Bangor International is about 320 kilometres north of Boston and roughly 180 kilometres east of the New Brunswick border, and the outlet noted the airport's direct connections (Orlando, Washington, D.C., Charlotte) as context for regional travel disruption.
