Nigel Farage unveiled Dan Thomas as Reform UK's leader in Wales at a rally in Newport as the party ramped up campaigning ahead of the Senedd election. The announcement followed the defection of Senedd member James Evans to Reform UK, giving the party two MSs in the Senedd.

Farage's pitch

Farage said he had chosen Thomas because he was 'decent', 'respectable' and 'battle hardened'. He insisted Thomas would have 'full autonomy' on Welsh policy and said he was 'not a hard line dictator' about devolved governance. The appointment is part of a wider campaign drive: Reform said it will name Senedd list candidates and publish a Wales manifesto in early March.

Some of the banks won't like it,
— Nigel Farage, Reform UK leader

Farage used the rally to outline an unconventional economic idea. He proposed stopping interest payments on reserves held at the Bank of England. He had previously floated the suggestion publicly at Davos. When asked about it, he was unapologetic. The Economist described the policy as dangerous but noted it had some unexpected allies. Analysts warned the policy could have wider economic consequences and said banks would oppose it.

The appointment did double duty as a policy signal: Reform has already said it would scrap the Welsh Government's default 20mph limit in urban areas. Who is Dan Thomas?

Dan Thomas was born and grew up in the South Wales Valleys and, according to reporting, led Barnet Council from 2019 to 2022 before returning to Wales and resigning his council seat. He became opposition leader after 2022 and served in that role until May 2024. He defected to Reform from the Conservatives in June last year. Reform UK has said it would abolish the Welsh Government's default 20mph speed limit in urban areas; Thomas objected to the 'blanket' approach and said reversing the limit had not been costed. At the rally, Thomas said houses in multiple occupation were 'changing the character of our community forever' and described 'uncontrolled immigration' as one of the major causes of the housing crisis.

Plaid Cymru leader Rhun ap Iorwerth called Thomas 'Farage's Welsh deputy'. Welsh Labour said Reform was 'absolutely packed with Tories jumping ship'. Jane Dodds said the same people who 'trashed our economy and hollowed out public services' should not be trusted to reinvent themselves and lecture Wales about change. Darren Millar said Thomas 'appears to have been parachuted back to Wales'.

Opponents warned 'They will just be Nigel Farage's puppet, doing his bidding from Westminster and silencing the voices of the people of Wales'. Conservative frontbencher Kemi Badenoch said the unveiling followed 'the last one was sent to prison' and warned a Reform government would mean 'more incompetence' and 'more dodgy appointments'. Nathan Gill was sentenced to 10-and-a-half years in November after admitting taking bribes for pro-Russia interviews and speeches, a fact critics highlighted when attacking the new leadership. Some critics said that despite Thomas's Welsh origins he is essentially a political product of London.

Electoral context and momentum

Reform UK said it would announce Senedd list candidates in the first half of March and planned to field nearly 100 candidates across the 16 new constituencies, with a full Wales manifesto due in the first two weeks of March. James Evans said 'Britain is broken. Wales is broken' and that was his reason for joining Reform. Media analysis suggested Farage will be hoping the new Welsh leader will give Reform extra potency as it seeks to pull off a far bigger surprise on 7 May.

The by-election in Ynys Gybi gave Reform its first representative on Isle of Anglesey County Council. Celfyn Furlong said 'I am absolutely humbled and honoured to have been elected to represent Ynys Gybi'. Local commentary suggested the result sent a message that people want change.

Supporters described the Ynys Gybi outcome as evidence of local appetite for change and a sign of momentum. Plaid's Rhun ap Iorwerth called Thomas 'Farage's Welsh deputy'. Welsh Labour said Reform was 'absolutely packed with Tories jumping ship'.

Electoral math

Polling in Scotland by More in Common put Reform at around 20% on the regional vote and the SNP at roughly 25%. Polling analysis noted the anti-independence vote is split and Reform is strong among unionists while the SNP still leads on constituency votes. According to analysis by Professor Sir John Curtice, that distribution could produce notably different results: he estimated a scenario that would give the SNP 59 seats and put Reform and Labour on 19 each. The model allocated the remaining seats among the Liberal Democrats, Conservatives and Greens. Such analysis prompted projections that under some scenarios Reform could increase its representation substantially. Some analysts said the rise of Reform is altering party dynamics and may be pivotal to election outcomes.

State of play

Reform and Nigel Farage say Thomas will run Welsh policy with full autonomy, while opponents accuse him of being 'Nigel Farage's puppet' and point to his recent London-based record. Analysts and local commentators disagree about whether by-election wins and polls indicate real momentum, and Reform has signalled it will name Senedd list candidates and publish a Wales manifesto in early March as it targets the 7 May vote.
