U.S. Supreme Court allowed California to use a voter-approved, Democratic-leaning congressional map in this year's midterm election. Analysts say the plan favors Democrats and could help them win up to five additional U.S. House seats. California Republicans and lawyers for President Trump urged the justices to block the map, alleging it was an unlawful racial gerrymander, but the court declined that emergency request.

What the court did

A three-judge federal panel in Los Angeles heard the challenge and, in a 2-1 decision, upheld the new congressional map. Voters had approved the plan through Proposition 50. The Supreme Court issued an emergency order. That order allows California to use the voter-approved map this fall. In December, the court had similarly allowed Texas Republicans to use their new congressional map. Analysts say that ruling could offset partisan gains from California's change.

The Supreme Court on Wednesday allowed California to use a new voter-approved congressional map that is favorable to Democrats in this year's elections, rejecting a last-ditch plea from state Republicans and the Trump administration.
— Associated Press

Filing for California's congressional primaries starts Monday. Analysts say the new lines favor Democrats and could help them gain up to five additional U.S. House seats.

Competing legal narratives

At trial, U.S. District Judges Josephine Staton and Wesley Hsu found that evidence of racial motivations was exceptionally weak, while evidence of partisan motivations was overwhelming.

State attorneys told the court that before Proposition 50 there were 16 Latino-majority districts and that after Proposition 50 there remained 16 such districts, and that the average Latino share of the voting-age population declined in those districts.

By contrast, California Republicans and lawyers for President Trump alleged the map was an unconstitutional racial gerrymander intended to benefit Latino voters, citing the 13th Congressional District in Merced County as an example.

Legal context and the Court's doctrine

The Supreme Court previously held that partisan-gerrymandering claims are not reviewable by federal courts, limiting the avenues available to litigants seeking federal relief for politically motivated line-drawing.

Justice Samuel Alito wrote that both states had adopted new maps for political advantage, a characterization that frames those disputes as partisan fights. The Trump administration's Justice Department supported the Texas redistricting while opposing California's plan, calling it "tainted by an unconstitutional racial gerrymander." The Los Angeles Times reported that it was unusual for the Justice Department and the U.S. solicitor general to intervene in a state election dispute after taking a different view in Texas.

Political stakes and projections

Analysts project that the California map could help Democrats gain as many as five additional U.S. House seats, a shift that would matter for control of the chamber. The Los Angeles Times reported that passage of Proposition 50 could put six Republican incumbents at risk, a somewhat more aggressive estimate than other projections. Some analysts say rulings upholding maps in both Texas and California may largely cancel each other out in their national partisan effect.

Beyond immediate seat counts, analysts warn that decisions like this could undercut provisions of the Voting Rights Act. They say this could lead to declines in representation by Black members of Congress, a longer-term consequence that civil-rights groups and lawyers are following closely.

Reactions and political interpretations

Gov. Gavin Newsom accused former President Donald Trump of having 'started this redistricting war' and suggested Mr. Trump acted as if he were entitled to extra congressional seats. Attorney General Rob Bonta called the Supreme Court's decision good news for Californians and for democracy. Tianna Mays of Democracy Defenders Action said Proposition 50 reflected a political choice rather than an unlawful racial gerrymander. Marina Jenkins of the National Democratic Redistricting Committee said the GOP drive to redraw maps has backfired and empowered Democratic states to fight back. Jon Fleishman noted that the immediate practical effect is that this year's elections will be held on the new lines and that outcome will shrink California's already small Republican delegation. The Los Angeles Times added that the Justice Department's split posture across the two cases was unusual.

Immediate next steps

Filing for congressional primaries in California begins on Monday.

State of play

Reporting agrees that the Supreme Court allowed California to use a voter-approved congressional map in the upcoming midterm election. District judges and state attorneys found evidence of racial motivation to be weak. They said Proposition 50 did not increase the number of Latino-majority districts, while California Republicans and lawyers for President Trump allege the map is an unconstitutional racial gerrymander. Filing for congressional primaries begins on Monday. Analysts continue to say the new lines are expected to help Democrats gain up to five additional U.S. House seats, even as some observers note that rulings in states like Texas could blunt the national net effect.
