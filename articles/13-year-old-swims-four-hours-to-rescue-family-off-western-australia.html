Austin Appelbee, 13, swam about 4 km (2.5 miles) over roughly four hours to reach shore and raise the alarm after his family were swept out to sea off Western Australia.

How it happened

Multiple sources say the family were holidaying in Quindalup in Geographe Bay and were using hired kayaks and inflatable paddleboards when strong winds dragged them offshore. Austin said he initially tried to paddle their inflatable kayak back but it was taking on water; he abandoned it and later removed his life jacket because it impeded his swimming.

WA Water Police and rescuers said Austin reached shore at about 18:00 local time, found his mother's bag and called emergency services, and authorities said his call triggered a multi-agency search and rescue operation.

The waves are massive, and I have no life jacket on.… I just kept thinking, ‘Just keep swimming, just keep swimming,’
— Austin Appelbee, 13

After calling emergency services, Austin passed out from exhaustion and was taken to hospital; multiple reports say he later woke and phoned his father, distressed.

Police said that by about 8:30 p.m. a search helicopter had located Joanne Appelbee, 47, and her children Beau, 12, and Grace, 8, clinging to a paddleboard roughly 14 km (nine miles) offshore, and that they had spent up to 10 hours in the water.

Rescue crews directed a volunteer marine rescue vessel to the family's position and St John WA paramedics assessed them before they were taken to Busselton Health Campus. The family were later discharged and returned to thank the marine rescue volunteers.

Medical findings

Doctors later told Austin that the strain on his body from the swim was comparable to running two marathons and gave him crutches to help his legs recover.

Metro reported that Austin had failed his swimming test earlier that morning; the outlet labelled that detail uncertain and it has not been independently verified.

Reaction and analysis

Police Inspector James Bradley said the boy's actions deserved the highest praise and that the teenager's determination helped save his mother and siblings. Naturaliste Volunteer Marine Rescue commander Paul Bresland described the effort as superhuman. Bresland said Austin's accurate descriptions of the kayak and paddleboards helped rescuers locate the family's craft. Western Australia premier Roger Cook praised Austin as a true West Aussie hero and called the feat extraordinary. Survival expert Mike Tipton said the swim was a remarkable achievement driven by the motivation to save family. Joanne Appelbee said sending Austin to shore was one of the hardest decisions she has ever had to make. Grandmother Doreen Cunningham said she was relieved and hoped the episode would be a lesson for others about small inflatables.

Police Inspector James Bradley said that all three people found on the paddleboard were wearing lifejackets and that the jackets contributed to their survival. Officials have used the incident to warn that ocean conditions can change rapidly.

Differences in accounts

Many sources describe Austin's swim as roughly 4 km over about four hours. Medical staff characterised the physical toll as comparable to running two marathons.

Police statements emphasise that the three people found on the paddleboard were wearing lifejackets. Austin and some rescuers say he removed his life jacket because it impeded his swimming.

An unverified Metro report that Austin failed a swimming test earlier that morning remains uncorroborated.

Some coverage used more sensational wording: a tabloid source described the waters as 'shark-infested', language that is speculative and not presented as an official finding.

State of play

Across reporting and official statements there is agreement that Austin Appelbee reached shore and raised the alarm. A multi-agency search later located Joanne Appelbee and the two younger children clinging to a paddleboard. Rescue crews and St John WA paramedics assessed and took the family to Busselton Health Campus.

Doctors have told outlets the swim placed such strain on Austin that clinicians compared it to running two marathons. Family members described their survival as nothing short of a miracle. Metro's claim that Austin failed a swim test earlier that morning remains unverified.

Disputed points include whether Austin wore a life jacket for the entire swim; he says he removed it because it impeded his stroke.
