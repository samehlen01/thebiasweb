The Foreign Office is reviewing the payoff given to Lord Mandelson after his sacking as ambassador to the US. Government sources confirmed the Foreign Office reached a financial settlement with Mandelson but have not disclosed the amount. Mandelson was appointed the UK's ambassador to Washington in December 2024 and was sacked in September.

What triggered the review

The review was prompted by material among documents tied to Jeffrey Epstein that were released by US authorities. Released Epstein files include an email indicating Mandelson gave Epstein advance notice of a €500bn ($590 billion / £434 billion) EU bailout in 2010. Journalists have examined those files, and Whitehall ministers and police say they require scrutiny.

Among the released material are financial records that press coverage has highlighted. The Epstein files include bank statements suggesting Mandelson and his husband received payments from Epstein totalling tens of thousands of pounds. The combination of paperwork and press reconstructions prompted both political questions and a criminal inquiry.

Those revelations have already triggered criminal and administrative responses. The Metropolitan Police searched properties linked to Mandelson and is investigating allegations of misconduct in public office. Lord Mandelson has disputed the documentation and the inference drawn from it.

Peter Mandelson has said he has no record of receiving the cited payments, questioned the documents' authenticity, and says he did not act criminally or for financial gain.

This will be a complex investigation requiring a significant amount of further evidence gathering and analysis. It will take some time to do this work comprehensively and we will not be providing a running commentary.
— Deputy Assistant Commissioner Hayley Sewart

The Metropolitan Police emphasised that the inquiry will be methodical and that it would not provide a running commentary on ongoing enquiries.

How much was paid?

Estimates of the severance vary by outlet and by how analysts applied salary bands. Mandelson secured an exit payment equivalent to three months' salary when he left the ambassador role. Some reports said the exit payment was up to £40,000 ($54,376). Other reports placed the implied payout between about £38,750 ($52,677) and £55,000 ($74,767) (three months at the top of the diplomatic pay band).

Legal, parliamentary and administrative responses

Government sources confirmed the Foreign Office reached a financial settlement with Mandelson but have not disclosed the amount. A Foreign Office spokesperson said Mandelson's civil service employment was terminated in accordance with legal advice and the terms and conditions of his employment.

Parliament decided ministers should release government files and electronic communications about Mandelson's appointment. Parliament will see disclosure of the exact payment and reasons after MPs voted to release related files.

Political fallout

A No10 source told the Press Association: 'Given what we know now, Mandelson should either pay the money back or give it to a charity to support victims.' Multiple ministers and allies echoed that stance and argued Mandelson should return or donate the payout.

Dame Priti Patel said 'A five-figure taxpayer-funded payout for Mandelson is a betrayal of Epstein's victims and should be recovered.' Kirsty Blackman said 'Scottish taxpayers will be disgusted that public money was used for the pay-off.'

Inside Labour, voices are divided. Pat McFadden said 'The party should stick with the Prime Minister and not change leadership; doing so would create chaos and uncertainty.' By contrast, trade union figures such as Steve Wright (FBU) said 'The prime minister has lost authority and should consider his position.'

Rosena Allin-Khan said 'Someone needs to take responsibility for the appointment and the advisers who recommended it should be held to account.' Many MPs are agitating for the Prime Minister to sack his chief of staff Morgan McSweeney, who is blamed by some for pushing Mandelson's appointment.

Some departmental sources told reporters that Officials believed at the time they had no legal option but to pay the severance, a claim that ministers and officials have used to explain the decision-making that led to the settlement.

Public reaction has also registered in polling. An Opinium poll showed 55% of British voters thought Sir Keir should quit as Labour leader.

Speculation and likely consequences

Commentators and opposition figures warned of broader political damage. The scandal could damage the prime minister's position and prompt a no-confidence vote or leadership change. There will be political pressure to claw back or recover the taxpayer-funded pay-off.

Some commentators and senior figures went further. The Mandelson affair may trigger a leadership contest or force Sir Keir Starmer to resign. Former prime minister Gordon Brown said The released messages amount to a betrayal and may amount to financial wrongdoing if investigated. If the police investigation proceeds, the apparent payments and exchanges could be regarded as financial wrongdoing.
