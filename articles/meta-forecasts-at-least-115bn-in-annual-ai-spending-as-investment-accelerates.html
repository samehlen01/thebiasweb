This article examines the announcement Meta forecasts at least $115bn in annual spending as AI investment accelerates and unpacks what Meta, investors and multiple outlets reported about the plan. Meta told analysts on an earnings call that it expects 2026 capital expenditures of $115 billion to $135 billion. By contrast, The Guardian reported a substantially larger estimate, saying Meta expects to spend between $162bn and $169bn in 2026. The company spent about $72 billion in capital expenditures in 2025.

Earnings and market reaction

Meta reported $59.89 billion in revenue for the fourth quarter of 2025 and earnings per share of $8.88. The company showed 24% year-over-year revenue growth, driven by online ads.

Meta's shares jumped nearly 10% in after-hours trading after the earnings release.

Why Meta says it's spending

Mark Zuckerberg said 2026 will be the year AI starts to dramatically change the way that we work. He said projects that used to require big teams can now be accomplished by a single, very talented person with AI tools.

Meta finance chief Susan Li said output per engineer has risen 30% since the start of 2025 and that power users have increased output 80% year over year.

Investor and industry reactions

Wall Street seemed broadly comfortable with Meta's increased AI spending. Chuck Robbins warned the current AI market is probably a bubble and said some companies 'won't make it' even as he suggested AI could be bigger than the internet. Jamie Dimon has voiced similar concerns. Sundar Pichai said there was some 'irrationality' to the AI boom. Sam Altman said he believes investors overall are overexcited about AI.

Katelyn Chedraoui of CNET argued that Meta's troves of personal data give it an advantage for personal AI but warned that those same data sets raise privacy and backlash risks. TechCrunch wrote that as the VR business continues to lose money and Meta pivots toward AI, it is unclear what will quickly turn the struggling VR business around.

Reality Labs and staffing

Reality Labs lost some $19.1 billion in 2025 and posted a $6.2 billion loss in the fourth quarter.

I expect Reality Labs losses this year to be similar to last year, and this will likely be the peak, as we start to gradually reduce our losses going forward.
— Mark Zuckerberg

Engadget and TechCrunch reported the company cut more than 1,000 employees from Reality Labs earlier this month, while the BBC reported it had laid off several hundred workers mainly in its Reality Labs division, a material discrepancy in accounts of the cuts. BBC reporting also suggested Zuckerberg's comments seemed to hint at further layoffs at the company.

Infrastructure and partnerships

Meta invested $14.3 billion in Scale AI, reportedly bringing founder Alexandr Wang and some engineers and researchers to Meta.

The company announced a deal worth up to $6bn with Corning to supply fiber optic cables for its datacenters.

Meta said its datacenters have supported 30,000 skilled-trade jobs during construction and 5,000 operational jobs.

The company spent $6.4m to run ads in November and December in state capitals and Washington.

Political scrutiny and spending priorities

Georgia is leading a legislative effort to ban building new datacenters until March, and Democrats in Congress are investigating reports that big tech companies are passing on soaring utility costs to ordinary Americans.

Meta expects much of its 2026 spending to be directed to infrastructure costs and employee compensation, including new hires to support AI expansion.

For Reality Labs, the company said it will direct most investment toward glasses and wearables while focusing on making Horizon a massive success on mobile and making VR a profitable ecosystem over the coming years.

Unresolved questions and speculation

BBC reported that Zuckerberg's comments seemed to hint at further layoffs at the company. CNBC said it remains unclear whether Meta will have new AI products that can generate meaningful revenue in the near term. TechCrunch speculated that as Meta pivots away from VR to AI, it is unclear what will turn the ailing VR business around.

Disputed numbers and reporting gaps

The standout factual discrepancy across coverage concerns how much Meta plans to spend in 2026: Meta's guidance to analysts cited $115 billion to $135 billion in capital expenditures, while The Guardian reported a $162bn to $169bn estimate.

What happens next

Meta said it expects to start shipping new models and products over the coming months and to steadily push the frontier over the course of the year. The company is also forecasting higher operating income for 2026.

State of play

Across reporting there is agreement that Meta is planning a large step-up in AI infrastructure spending, and Meta told analysts it expects 2026 capital expenditures of $115 billion to $135 billion after spending about $72 billion in 2025. Meta and its executives say much of that money will go to infrastructure, employee compensation and hiring to support AI, and Zuckerberg said the company expects to start shipping new models and products over the coming months. What remains unresolved are matters like The Guardian's higher estimate that Meta expects to spend between $162bn and $169bn in 2026, the diverging accounts that Engadget and TechCrunch said the company cut more than 1,000 employees from Reality Labs earlier this month while the BBC reported it had laid off several hundred workers mainly in its Reality Labs division, and whether new product rollouts will generate meaningful revenue; the BBC also reported that Zuckerberg's comments seemed to hint at further layoffs and lawmakers have increased scrutiny of datacenter buildouts.
