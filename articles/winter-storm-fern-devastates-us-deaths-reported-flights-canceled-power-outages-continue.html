The Associated Press, in an uncertain report, said Winter Storm Fern has so far claimed 25 lives. NBC News, also reporting with uncertainty, said 13 people have died as a result of this weekend's severe weather. RTE reported that the system has killed at least 30 people and left more than half a million Americans without power. The Daily Mail said the inclement weather struck 34 states and had claimed at least 28 lives.

Deaths and local tragedies

Frisco police reported that a sledding accident in Frisco, Texas, killed a 16-year-old girl and left another child critically injured. The Federal Aviation Administration said a small plane crashed while taking off in Bangor, Maine, killing seven people. New York City Mayor Zohran Mamdani confirmed that seven people died in the city during the storm. Mayor Mamdani's office separately said at least eight people were found dead outside as temperatures plunged and that the causes of death were under investigation.

If your car is stuck, stay put. An LMPD officer will be along shortly to push, pull, lift, drag, or simply judge you,
— Louisville Metropolitan Police Department (social media)

The department later posted an apology for the tone of its tweets. Some social media users praised the LMPD feed as entertaining. Other commentators said the posts were 'cringe' and embarrassingly unfunny.

Travel chaos and timelines

FlightAware, citing real-time tracking, said as an official finding that on Sunday more than 11,000 flights were canceled, the largest single-day total since the pandemic. By 2:45 pm ET on Monday FlightAware reported over 4,816 cancellations and 5,124 delays. A later FlightAware tally listed at least 6,095 delays and 5,183 cancellations as of Monday evening. RTE described the broader disruption as more than 9,000 flights affected across the country. Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy said air travel is expected to return to normal only by Wednesday.

Outages and grid strain

Multiple outlets reported that as many as 800,000 people across 10 southeastern states were still without power on Monday morning. PowerOutage.com showed more than 690,000 outages nationwide by Monday afternoon. Other PowerOutage.com updates indicated that more than 540,000 customers remained without electricity in earlier morning counts. PowerOutage.us recorded more than 525,000 people without power as day broke on Tuesday, including more than 100,000 customers out in each of Tennessee, Mississippi and Louisiana. Utility companies including Duke Energy, Cleco, and CEMC said customers could be without power for several days. Duke Energy reported over 18,000 outages across the Carolinas on Sunday afternoon. Cleco said it had restored power to 60% of roughly 17,000 affected customers across Louisiana and Mississippi. CEMC warned that tree damage and broken electrical equipment will likely make this a 'multi-day restoration event'. PJM said it is bracing for record-breaking energy demand and warned the grid will rely on its generation fleet. Outages could persist for several days and restorations will be multi-day events in some areas.

Snow totals, closures and local measures

The National Weather Service said at least 19 states received more than a foot of snow. Parts of Connecticut saw over 56cm of snow, and Boston recorded more than 40.6cm. In Canada, some areas of Toronto were buried under nearly 60cm of snow and more than 500 flights were canceled on Sunday after the city's main airport was snowed in. Pearson International Airport has logged more than 88.2cm of snow this month, making it the snowiest January on record at that facility. The University of Mississippi closed its Oxford campus until February 1 because of continued extreme winter weather and recovery efforts. Municipalities including Nashville opened emergency warming shelters for residents. Pittsburgh declared a state of emergency after 37 of its snowplows broke overnight. New York City schools closed and students logged on from home for remote learning.

Public comments and warnings

Social media users mocked Mayor Zohran Mamdani's shoveling form, with one comment saying 'You can tell Mamdani has never touched a shovel in his life'. Truck driver and snow-plow operator 'Princess Cat' told a local outlet: 'Please do not come out here. If you can prevent it, do not come out here.' Meteorologist Dylan Federico warned that the coming cold is extreme and that model runs show below-zero wind chills down to the Gulf Coast.

Speculation and analysis

The National Weather Service cautioned that the potential is increasing for another significant winter storm to impact the eastern United States this coming weekend. The NWS also warned that dangerous wind chills as low as -50°F may persist and that much-below-normal temperatures could continue into early February. An estimate in The Independent suggested the storm could cost the country more than $100 billion, though that figure remains speculative. Scientists told reporters that an increasing frequency of polar-vortex disruptions may be linked to climate change, but they emphasized the debate is not settled and natural variability also plays a role.

State of play and next steps

Across reporting there is agreement that Winter Storm Fern dumped heavy snow across many states. The storm also upset travel and energy: FlightAware reported thousands of canceled flights, and power trackers and utilities said hundreds of thousands of customers were left without electricity. What remains disputed is the final death toll — NBC News, the Associated Press, the Daily Mail and RTE have reported, respectively, 13, 25, 28 and 30 deaths — and estimates of economic damage and any climate link remain speculative; forecasters warned another storm may be possible with dangerously cold wind chills, and Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy said air travel may not normalize until Wednesday.

International Outlook

Canada
Sources
Globe and Mail

Globe and Mail reported officials in affected US states had recorded at least 45 deaths and that the US aviation system saw more than 17,000 commercial flights cancelled over the weekend — figures noticeably higher than several other tallies circulating internationally.

The paper also flagged local restoration and health impacts: Entergy warned some of its roughly 6,000 customers in Grenada, Mississippi might not have power until Sunday, and it cited a Louisiana health department report of at least one carbon-monoxide death.

Latin America
Sources
Folha Brazil, El Financiero (Mexico)

Folha placed the death toll at 42 and reported extreme lows near -30°C in parts of the US; it additionally said at least 46 children in Nashville received treatment for carbon-monoxide poisoning during outage conditions.

El Financiero focused on aviation and commercial damage: FlightAware showed more than 1,500 cancellations as of 9:15 a.m. ET Tuesday, American Airlines warned of a $150–$200 million revenue hit, and reporting detailed a near-shutdown of American’s Dallas hub that stranded thousands of crew, produced delays up to 27 hours and rebooking lines of seven to eight hours.

Singapore
Sources
Straits Times Singapore

Straits Times put the toll at 38 across 14 states (as of Jan 27) and said ten fatalities occurred in New York City, which recorded its coldest temperature in eight years (about -13°C).

Its reporting emphasised local humanitarian strain: New York postponed its HUD homeless count, placed roughly 500 people in shelters and had outreach teams checking 350 high‑risk individuals every two hours; Nashville’s shelters were full at about 1,400 and the Nashville Rescue Mission's nightly clients swelled from roughly 400 to about 7,000. The paper also reported three young boys died after falling through ice on a pond near Bonham, Texas.

Spain
Sources
La Vanguardia

La Vanguardia supplied state‑level outage detail — Tennessee more than 172,000 homes without power, Mississippi about 140,000, Louisiana about 99,200 — and gave a higher flight‑cancellation tally, reporting roughly 20,000 cancelled since the storm began.

India
Sources
Hindustan Times, Republic World - World

Hindustan Times relayed an AccuWeather outlook that ice accumulation was possible across at least 13 states between Jan 30 and Feb 1 (listing Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, the Florida Panhandle, Georgia, Tennessee, South Carolina, North Carolina, Kentucky, West Virginia, Virginia, Maryland and Delaware) and noted two possible weekend storm tracks, including a 'more intense' scenario that could push snow into parts of the Southeast.

Republic World reported an ownership detail about the Bangor crash aircraft: the Bombardier Challenger 600 was registered to a corporation sharing a Houston address with the law firm Arnold & Itkin, and one of the firm’s founding partners is listed as that company’s registered agent.

Russia
Sources
Ria Novosti (State)

Ria Novosti, a state outlet, republished an Associated Press figure putting the death toll at at least 42 — a higher count than many other reports. Its coverage largely echoed AP copy rather than providing independent confirmation.

Other
Sources
Czech TV (State), El Nacional Venezuela

Czech TV, citing AP, reported at least 35 deaths and identified three brothers aged 6, 8 and 9 who fell through ice on a private Texas pond; it cited PowerOutage.us figures showing more than 560,000 customers without electricity nationwide, including over 170,000 in Tennessee, and noted NWS warnings of extreme cold with forecasts down to -29°C in some areas.

El Nacional ran a NOAA historical note that the term 'polar vortex' first appeared in print in 1853 and quoted climate researchers who said stretched polar‑vortex events can pull Arctic air south and are associated with extreme winter outbreaks, while emphasising that attributing any single event to climate change remains uncertain.
