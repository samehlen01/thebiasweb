US Federal Reserve holds interest rates steady at January meeting. The Federal Reserve voted to keep its key lending rate between 3.5% and 3.75%. The central bank opted for its first pause after three rate cuts last year.

Inflation remains somewhat elevated.
— Federal Reserve

The Fed's post-meeting statement said available indicators suggest economic activity has been expanding at a solid pace, and it added that job gains have remained low and the unemployment rate has shown some signs of stabilization.

The Fed's assessment that the unemployment rate has shown some signs of stabilization sits alongside reporting that the unemployment rate has inched up to 4.4%.

Dissent and legal questions

This meeting saw two governors dissent: Governors Stephen Miran and Christopher Waller voted against the hold and advocated another quarter-point cut.

Chair Jerome Powell warned that losing the Fed's independence would make it hard to restore the institution's credibility and said he is strongly committed to retaining that independence.

The Justice Department has subpoenaed the Fed as part of a criminal investigation into testimony Powell gave last June about a $2.5 billion building renovation, and Powell has alleged the subpoenas were a pretext to punish the Fed for not cutting rates as sharply as President Trump wanted.

Markets and forecasts

Traders are still pricing in about a 60% chance of two additional, quarter percentage point rate cuts this year, and futures markets are pricing at most two rate reductions in 2026. Economists say that if inflation slowly declines this year, the Fed may cut again in the spring or summer. Some commentators have speculated that if there is a single most likely window, it could be the January FOMC, particularly if President Trump is looking to redirect attention away from a Fed that did not cut.

Analysts were divided: Ellen Zentner (analysis) said lower interest rates may be coming but investors will have to remain patient; Charlie Ripley (analysis) said policy rates are much closer to neutral and it's time for a long pause; Heather Long (analysis) said it's détente at the Fed for now but warned a shakeup could come with a new Fed chair in May.

State of play

Across reporting there is agreement that the US Federal Reserve held its target range at 3.5% to 3.75% and that the post-meeting statement described inflation as somewhat elevated while saying available indicators suggest economic activity has been expanding at a solid pace. But some data show the unemployment rate has inched up to 4.4%, two governors dissented in favour of another quarter-point cut, and the Justice Department has subpoenaed the Fed — an action Powell has alleged was a pretext to punish the Fed for not cutting rates as sharply as President Trump wanted. Markets continue to price the possibility of cuts later this year, economists say a slow decline in inflation would make cuts more likely in the spring or summer, and a new Fed chair is expected in May which could alter policy direction.
