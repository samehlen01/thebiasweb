Revolution Bars group set to enter administration, putting about 2,200 jobs at risk. Revel Collective (official statement) said it had filed a notice to appoint administrators to protect creditors and that, unless circumstances change, the board intends to appoint administrators within 10 business days. The company added that its pubs and bars will remain open and that trading will continue while advisers pursue a potential sale of all or parts of the business.

What the company said

Since the transactions being contemplated are not expected to deliver any return to shareholders, the board has resolved to take action to protect creditors.
— Revel Collective board

That wording made clear the board believed any of the deals under discussion were unlikely to deliver a return to shareholders and that investors could be left with nothing. The company (official statement) said it was working with advisers to preserve as much value as possible while advancing a potential sale of parts or all of the business, and that trading would continue during that process.

Buyer talks and conflicting accounts

Revel Collective (official statement) has said talks with a potential buyer were well advanced. In December the group also said it had identified a significant number of potential buyers. By contrast, national reporting cited by the Reading Chronicle (uncertain report) said the business had been unsuccessful in attracting buyers. The company said an announcement on a sale was expected in the coming days.

Scale, finances and recent changes

Revel Collective operates around 62 sites and employed just over 3,000 people as of the end of June 2024. The firm has been suspended from trading on London's AIM market. It reported revenues fell 7.4% to £26.3 million over the three months to September, driven by a 10.5% like-for-like decline in its bars business, while borrowings rose to £25.3 million from £22.1 million. A turnaround plan earlier in 2024 saw it close 15 loss-making bars.

Local impact and venues at risk

Local reporting named specific Revolution venues at risk, including Durham's North Road site, Newcastle's Collingwood Street and York's Coney Street bar.

Why leaders and analysts blame policy and costs

Revel Collective (allegation) blamed challenging economic conditions and measures in Chancellor Rachel Reeves's first Budget for much of the pressure on the business. The company said higher National Insurance, the minimum wage rise and an increase in spirits duty would together cost it more than £4 million a year. Company bosses (analysis) also said younger customers were reining in their spending and a warm summer had weakened trade. Karl Chessell of NIQ (analysis) said relentless increases in operating costs were taking a severe toll on the hospitality sector. Commentators writing in the Bolton News (analysis) suggested Britain’s nightlife could be among the next casualties of policy choices.

Policy, business rates and potential support

Industry groups (forecast) warned that the crisis facing pubs and hospitality will deepen when planned changes to how business rates are calculated take effect in April. UKHospitality and the British Beer and Pub Association (estimate) said pub business-rates bills will rise by about 15% (roughly £1,400) in April and could increase by 76% (about £7,000) by 2028/29. Bolton News (speculation) has reported that the chancellor may be preparing a roughly £300 million support package for pubs. The BBC (speculation) has suggested ministers may water down the planned changes for pubs, but those ideas remain unconfirmed. Bolton News (forecast) also reported that analysts warned targeted support for pubs could draw ire from other parts of the hospitality industry that miss out.

Immediate next steps

The board said it intends to appoint administrators within 10 business days unless circumstances change. It added that venues would remain open and trading would continue while advisers advanced potential sale options. Revel Collective (official statement) said it expected to announce a sale in the coming days. However, some press reports (uncertain report) have suggested no buyer was ultimately found.

State of play

Reporting converges on several confirmed points: Revel Collective has filed notice to appoint administrators, the group's AIM listing has been suspended, and the company operates around 62 sites and employed just over 3,000 staff at the end of June 2024. The company alleges challenging economic conditions and Budget measures introduced by Chancellor Rachel Reeves, while its managers have also cited weaker spending by younger customers and an unusually warm summer. A central dispute remains over whether viable buyers exist — Revel Collective says talks were well advanced and expected a sale announcement, while some reporting says no buyer emerged. Administrators are likely to be appointed within about 10 business days and advisers will continue to pursue sale options, while industry groups warn that planned business-rates reforms in April could deepen the sector's problems and there is speculative reporting that ministers may offer a support package for pubs.
