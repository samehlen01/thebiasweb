Government unveils NHS 2035 cancer plan, publishes warnings

The government today unveiled the NHS 2035 cancer survival plan and published 21 warning signs, aiming to reshape how cancer is detected, treated and supported over the next decade. Under the plan, the NHS commits that three in four people diagnosed from 2035 onward will be cancer-free or living well five years after diagnosis. Ministers say the investment will aim to deliver faster diagnoses, quicker treatment and more support to help people live well with cancer.

What the plan promises

The government has pledged to meet the 62-day cancer waiting-time standard by 2029. It said the package includes a £2.3 billion investment to deliver 9.5 million additional tests by 2029, funding scanners, digital systems and automated testing capacity. The Department of Health and Social Care estimates that the plan could translate into roughly 320,000 more lives saved over the ten-year period.

Context
The central performance metric referenced by ministers is that 85% of patients should start treatment within 62 days of referral.

That standard is the yardstick clinicians and policymakers use to assess cancer-service performance, and it underpins the government's timetable for improvement.

The NHS plans to scale up robot-assisted procedures from about 70,000 a year today to roughly half a million by 2035. NHS officials say that increase is expected to reduce complications and free up hospital beds.

The package is focused on scanners, lab automation and digital tools intended to speed diagnosis, while the plan also sets out clinical, genomic and surgical ambitions.

Screening, testing and the NHS App

The plan says early detection tests that could detect multiple cancers, including liquid biopsies and breath, saliva or urine screens, might be added to NHS screening within a decade. The government qualified that ambition and said any adoption would depend on review by the UK National Screening Committee and evidence of efficacy, safety and value. The NHS is already running a major trial called Galleri to test whether a multi-cancer blood screen works in people without symptoms. Ministers also said they will lower the threshold that triggers further bowel-screening checks to identify more cancers. Campaigners and clinicians note the urgency because currently just over half of cancers are diagnosed at stages one and two and that proportion has improved little in the past decade. The plan promises to offer genomic tests to patients who could benefit so doctors can choose the treatments most likely to work for them. Officials set a timetable for digital change: the NHS App should be the front door for cancer care by 2028 and by 2035 bring together genomic and lifestyle data with the single patient record.

This work, alongside our wider efforts to speed up clinical trials in the UK, will make a real difference in helping more cancer patients access trials and accelerate getting new life-changing treatments to those who need them.
— Lord Vallance, science minister

Patients will be able to sign up for clinical trials through the NHS App and 'Be Part of Research' will be integrated to help match patients to studies.

Capacity, workforce and realism

Workforce surveys suggest there is a 30% shortfall in radiologists and a 15% shortfall in clinical oncologists.

Dr Stephen Harden, president of the Royal College of Radiologists, said plans to diagnose and treat cancer faster are welcome but warned that, without sustained investment in the specialist workforce, there will not be enough doctors to deliver timely and effective care. Health experts said meeting the 62-day target by 2029 will require significant extra investment, particularly in staff. Sarah Woolnough of The King's Fund argued the system will not meet cancer treatment standards by 2029 without a big step change.

Discrepancies and contested timelines

Digital Health reported that the NHS has not met its central cancer performance target (that 85% of patients should start treatment within 62 days of referral) since 2014. By contrast, The Guardian reported that some cancer performance targets in England have not been met since 2015. Those different dates are materially important because they change the baseline used to judge recent progress.

Children and campaigners

It has been an enormous privilege to learn, work and battle alongside Charlotte over the last four years.
— Dame Caroline Dinenage MP

Dame Caroline Dinenage and campaigners said their work helped shape the plan and that the children and young people chapter must deliver real change. The taskforce's recommendations, now included in the National Cancer Plan, include a travel fund providing up to £10 million annually, which will help cover travel costs for families. The Department of Health said it will consider during design whether to set a per-family cap on that fund, leaving the final detail unclear.

Reactions: charities, alliances and media

Michelle Mitchell, chief executive of Cancer Research UK, said the promises represented a significant commitment that will require a wide range of measures. Julia Jessop of the South Yorkshire & Bassetlaw Cancer Alliance welcomed the plan as 'a plan that puts the needs of people first' and said it aligns with local partnership strategies. Thalie Martini of Breast Cancer UK said she was disappointed there was not enough emphasis on prevention through bold and proactive policies on alcohol, diet, exercise and chemical exposure. A Sky News editorial described the National Cancer Plan as bold and ambitious and said the government deserves credit for bringing it forward.

What happens next: rollouts, reviews and targets

Ministers said breast pain and post-menopausal bleeding clinics will be rolled out nationally by the end of 2026. They have set a public target to meet cancer waiting-time standards by March 2029. Officials expect the NHS App to be the front door for cancer care by 2028 and to integrate genomic and lifestyle information with the single patient record by 2035. Pilots such as the Galleri study will continue while evidence is gathered. Whether any multi-cancer screening tests are rolled out nationally will depend on the UK National Screening Committee's review and clear evidence of clinical benefit and value.

State of play

Across reporting there is agreement that a new national plan has been published. It sets out ambitions such as the NHS commitment that three in four people diagnosed from 2035 onward will be cancer-free or living well five years after diagnosis. The government has publicly pledged to meet the 62-day waiting-time standard by 2029.
