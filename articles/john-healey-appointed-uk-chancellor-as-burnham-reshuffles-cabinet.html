Burnham entered Downing Street as prime minister and promised to restore hope in Britain.

He chose Healey to run the Treasury as chancellor. Healey had just quit as defence secretary after arguments over defence spending.

Burnham also handed Ed Miliband foreign secretary, made Shabana Mahmood home secretary and gave Louise Haigh Cabinet Office roles, underlining that his first day was as much about a wider reshuffle as about one Treasury appointment.

First moves in office

Burnham said he would make announcements this week and explain how he would pay for them. He said they would give people breathing space now and help with the cost of living.

give people some breathing space now, some help with the cost of living
— Andy Burnham

He then widened the pitch beyond short-term relief, setting out a 10-year plan for Britain and saying he wanted to bring forward the biggest changes in the last 40 years to British politics. On tax, he said he was looking at the tax-free personal allowance before his first autumn budget, but he stressed that altering it would be difficult in current economic conditions and called it premature to talk about restoring the 50p top rate. Taken together, the remarks suggested that he and Burnham would work in lockstep to meet fiscal rules with a buffer against uncertainty.

He also said the government would help more young people into work, build more council homes and honour the UK's defence commitments.

Burnham is talking about a 10-year plan and major changes in politics while promising to keep the books under control. The choice of a former defence secretary for the Treasury sends a signal that he wants movement without pretending the arithmetic has disappeared. He also quit as defence secretary over defence spending disagreements.

That promise matters because Burnham is trying to front-load relief before the wider budget debate has even settled. By speaking in the language of breathing space and cost-of-living help, he is signalling that he wants his first week in office to be felt in household budgets, not just in Westminster headlines. It is also a way of drawing a line between immediate comfort and the harder question of how to pay for it.

Fiscal rules and defence

work in lockstep to meet the fiscal rules with a buffer against uncertainty
— John Healey

Healey used the same interview to argue that Treasury orthodoxy had too often acted as a dead hand, and that defence should be seen as a growth driver rather than a drain. That line fits a politician who has just quit as defence secretary over funding disputes.

That is why the move from the defence department to the Treasury matters so much. Healey is not arriving as a blank slate; he is arriving with a record of quitting over spending plans and a public argument that Treasury orthodoxy had too often acted as a dead hand, and that defence should be seen as a growth driver rather than a drain. The new job asks him to reconcile those instincts with a chancellor's duty to keep a lid on the books.

How the appointment was read

BBC Business called Healey a safe pair of hands. Daily Mail sources inside Burnham's new government described him as experienced and trusted, and said he shared Burnham's outlook. The Independent said he was chosen for national security credibility and appeal among Labour's right.

The different descriptions also reflect different audiences. Business coverage tends to ask whether a minister is market-friendly, insiders stress reliability, and politics reporters focus on party balance. In Healey's case, none of those outlets presented him as a wildcard; they presented him as a steadying choice with a clear task.

Market reaction

7%
rise in Babcock shares
Early FTSE 100 trading after the announcement

BAE Systems also rose 3.23 per cent and Rolls-Royce gained nearly 2 per cent, while The Independent said BAE alone added about £1.85bn ($2.48 billion) to its market value. The share moves mattered because they showed investors treating the appointment as a signal about future public spending, not just a personnel change in Westminster. In a market that had been looking for clues, defence stocks were the first to move.

£4bn
added to the defence sector's value
($5.35 billion) Tuesday trading after Healey's appointment

Taken together, The Independent said the gains left the sector almost £4bn ($5.35 billion) more valuable. It argued that the market reaction suggested the defence industry could be among the early beneficiaries of Burnham's government.

Chris Beauchamp of The Guardian said Healey would face many competing spending demands and would not simply be the MoD's man in No 11. He argued that substantial extra defence money would be hard to find. Investors, meanwhile, speculated that Healey may push more military spending. They also speculated he could revisit defence bonds, which would earmark borrowing for military spending and which he has previously backed. Andrew Wishart of The Independent Business said Healey's exit from defence pointed to higher military expenditure, although he said the funding source was unclear.

The better reading of the share rally is that investors think Healey may push more defence spending. That is why the defence industry could be among the early beneficiaries of Burnham's government. Even so, that enthusiasm is still conditional, because no one has yet seen the size, timing or source of any extra spending.

Cost-of-living push

Today’s energy tax cut will give families some breathing room on bills, and provide some reassurance this winter.
— John Healey

The VAT cut on electricity bills takes effect in October, and the government said it will be funded by cancelling the Digital ID scheme this year. The move was framed as giving families breathing room and reassurance this winter.

The Guardian also forecast that Healey is expected to outline more long-term action to bring down living costs later this year. It said Burnham could use flexibility in the fiscal rules to bolster public investment. It also noted speculation about a lower bus fare cap and a freeze on private rents.

The flurry of talk about bus fares and rents also shows how wide the cost-of-living debate has become. For now, those ideas remain only speculation, but they point to the direction of travel analysts are trying to map.

Burnham's rhetoric about a 10-year plan and major change still leaves a gap between what voters hear and what the Treasury can deliver.
