Jannik Sinner beat Alexander Zverev to retain the Wimbledon men's singles title on Centre Court on Sunday, winning 6-7(7-9), 7-6(7-2), 6-3, 6-4. After dropping the opening-set tie-break, Sinner recovered to win the next three sets and close out the final.

The win made Sinner only the 10th man in the Open era to retain the Wimbledon men's singles title. Sinner also became the first player in 48 years to win the title after being taken to five sets in his opening match. Zverev arrived in the final as the recent French Open winner.

Reaction

The Princess of Wales called the achievement fantastic and said it would be inspiring for children to see tennis played at that level.

Sinner said there is no better place to play tennis at Wimbledon, pointing to the atmosphere, the organisation and the people around the event. Sinner also said Zverev can be a Wimbledon champion soon and promised to be 'very, very careful.'

Darren Cahill, his coach, said Sinner comes back bigger, stronger and faster after tough moments.

Zverev said Sinner showed why he is the best player in the world, while the Wimbledon crowd played a big part in his journey to the final.

Event-day scene

The Princess of Wales presented the men's trophy to Sinner on Centre Court, as Prince William, George and Charlotte watched from the royal box. Wimbledon does not let winners keep the actual cup, so champions take home a smaller replica of the iconic 18-inch cup engraved with the names of all previous champions.

Temperatures peaked at 29C on the final day of the grand slam, and the Princess of Wales, George and Charlotte used hand-held fans to keep cool. Officials said these Championships were expected to be the hottest on record, and rain did not interrupt play at Church Road for only the ninth time in history.

Analysis

In The Guardian, Jonathan Liew called Sinner a brilliant player and already an all-time great, with power tennis that is hard to stop once it clicks. Liew argued Centre Court will never adore Sinner in the same way it once embraced Roger Federer or Andy Murray, and described his game as mechanical.

Liew also argued that Sinner's victory capped a Wimbledon that drew more public attention than expected in a World Cup year.

Liew forecast that Sinner's five Grand Slam titles could easily double over the next couple of years, linking that projection to Carlos Alcaraz's wrist injury and Novak Djokovic turning 40 next year.

Prize money and tax

Sinner's £3.6m ($4.82 million) winner's cheque also prompted a tax calculation. Craig Hughes estimated that the final UK income-tax bill could exceed £1.6m ($2.14 million) and leave around £2m ($2.68 million) after UK tax.
