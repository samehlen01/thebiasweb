The New START nuclear arms control treaty between the United States and Russia expired on Feb. 5, 2026. Some reports placed the expiry on Feb. 4, 2026.

What New START limited

1,550
Deployed strategic warheads per side
Limit under New START (deployed warheads)

Under New START, each side was limited to 1,550 deployed strategic warheads and to 700 deployed delivery vehicles, a cap that covered missiles, submarines and heavy bombers.

Verification and inspection history

New START included verification measures such as on-site inspections, data exchanges and movement notifications, but on-site inspections were suspended during the COVID-19 pandemic and have not resumed. Before Moscow suspended participation in early 2023, both parties had carried out 328 on-site inspections and exchanged more than 25,000 notifications, according to the U.S. State Department. Russia suspended participation in early 2023 while saying it would still observe agreed restrictions, and in September President Vladimir Putin offered to voluntarily continue to abide by the treaty's limits for another year.

Disputed end date

Coverage diverged on the specific day: outlets including Axios, NBC News and NPR reported Feb. 5, 2026 as the expiry, while The Independent's coverage placed the end on Feb. 4, 2026.

Feb. 5 is going to mark the end of an era,
— Kingston Reif, former deputy assistant secretary of defense for threat reduction and arms control

Kingston Reif, a former deputy assistant secretary of defense for threat reduction and arms control, told Axios that the expiry date underscored the end of an era in bilateral nuclear limits.

Reactions and policy divides

Arms control experts warned the treaty's expiry increases nuclear risk and could spark a new arms race.

Frank Miller argued that abandoning the treaty could make the United States stronger by freeing it to build deterrent forces adequate against both Russia and China.

Rose Gottemoeller urged the United States to continue to observe New START's limits as an insurance policy while preparing for future talks with Russia and China.

The Kremlin said Russia will remain a responsible nuclear power and will be guided by its national interests in maintaining strategic stability.

UN Secretary-General António Guterres warned the expiry 'marks a grave moment' and urged the United States and Russia to return to negotiations without delay.

Consequences

Analysts warned that the treaty's end could prompt increased nuclear-weapons spending and a buildup of delivery systems and warheads.

Experts said the loss of treaty verification and transparency could increase the risk of misunderstandings and inadvertent escalation.

Lt. Col. Johannes Kibsgaard warned that allied confidence in the U.S. nuclear guarantee may erode, prompting partners to bolster conventional and possibly nuclear planning.

Nikolai Sokov cautioned that non-nuclear states might react with diplomatic pressure or even a 'mutiny' at perceived failures to pursue disarmament.

Speculations and scenarios

Some analysts, including Rose Gottemoeller, speculated that Russia could rapidly upload additional warheads onto existing delivery systems after the treaty's expiry.

Nikolai Sokov forecast that an unconstrained arms race might take five to seven years to emerge and would likely prioritize more accurate, sophisticated weapons rather than merely increasing warhead numbers.

Several sources suggested U.S. and Russian negotiators could opt for a non-legal 'handshake' or informal arrangement to observe New START terms for a limited period despite the treaty's formal expiry.

Politics, China and the limits to extension

President Donald Trump has said he wants China included in any future arms-control deal and has been noncommittal about a formal extension.

The treaty was extended once for five years in 2021 and, under its terms, cannot be extended again.

China is estimated to have about 600 warheads and, according to SIPRI, its arsenal has been growing by roughly 100 warheads per year since 2023.

State of play

Reporting agrees that the New START nuclear arms control treaty between the United States and Russia has now expired and that the treaty previously capped each side at 1,550 deployed strategic warheads; the text allows only a single five-year extension, which was used in 2021 so the treaty cannot be extended again. Russia has publicly suspended participation while saying it would continue to observe restrictions, and analysts warn the loss of formal verification raises the risk of misunderstandings and could spur higher spending even as others argue abandoning the treaty would free the United States to bolster deterrence against both Russia and China. It remains disputed whether Moscow and Washington will strike an informal 'handshake' to preserve limits in practice, and the next steps could range from renewed negotiations and informal confidence measures to a longer-term shift toward qualitative arms competition.

Russia
Sources
Kommersant

Russian coverage reported that U.S. and Russian envoys met in Abu Dhabi and reached a verbal understanding to observe New START's numerical limits — preserving the 1,550 deployed‑warhead cap — for roughly six months while negotiating a replacement; reporting emphasised the arrangement was informal and would require presidential sign‑off, and some accounts named U.S. participants Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner.

The Kremlin framed the treaty's end as regrettable while signalling a mixed posture: spokesman Dmitry Peskov said Moscow regretted the expiry; Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov said a 'vacuum' had arisen but that Russia preferred dialogue and would wait to see the U.S. stance; the foreign ministry warned it was prepared to take 'decisive military‑technical countermeasures', and the Kremlin said President Vladimir Putin discussed consequences with Xi Jinping.

We view it negatively. We express our regret in this regard.
— Dmitry Peskov, Kremlin spokesman

Other Russian voices sent contrasting signals: Deputy Security Council chair Dmitry Medvedev posted a 'Winter is coming' image on X, while Federation Council vice‑speaker Konstantin Kosachev publicly said he did not expect immediate catastrophic consequences.

South Asia
Sources
News18 India, The Hindu, Republic World - World

Indian reporting highlighted U.S. criticism at the UN Conference on Disarmament: Under Secretary Thomas DiNanno described New START as having 'fundamental flaws', blamed repeated Russian violations and design weaknesses, and urged broader three‑way talks that include China, which he characterised as having "no limits, no transparency, no declarations, [and] no controls."

At the same Geneva session Russia's ambassador Gennady Gatilov said Moscow would only participate in new negotiations if other nuclear powers such as the UK and France were included, underscoring Moscow's demand for a wider negotiating table.

Indian outlets also recorded a U.S. government denial of a post‑expiry deal — White House spokeswoman Karoline Leavitt said, "Not to my knowledge" when asked about any U.S.–Russia agreement to continue observing New START limits — and flagged President Trump publicly rejecting Putin's one‑year voluntary extension while calling for a 'new, improved, and modernized' treaty.

East Asia
Sources
Newtalk (Taiwan)

Taiwanese reporting said the United States and Russia resumed high‑level military dialogue in Abu Dhabi — the first such exchange since autumn 2021 — and that U.S., Russian and Ukrainian representatives held a three‑way meeting; some accounts reported both sides orally agreed to observe treaty limits for six months as a 'verbal agreement' pending formal leader approval.

North America
Sources
CBC Canada

Canadian coverage highlighted a Pentagon assessment that China's arsenal could grow from roughly 600 warheads toward more than 1,000 by 2030, and quoted former U.S. diplomat Thomas Countryman saying "the risk that a nuclear weapon could be used ... is now the highest it's been since the Cuban Missile Crisis of 1962."

Western Europe
Sources
EUobserver

EUobserver emphasised European defence implications: it reported France and Germany are exploring joint development of tactical capabilities in response to the lapse, and noted NATO still stores fewer than 200 U.S. B61 tactical warheads in Europe — warheads that are being upgraded for carriage by F‑35 aircraft.

Oceania
Sources
SBS Australia

Australian reporting quoted ANU emeritus professor Paul Dibb warning that an all‑out nuclear arms race would be "very dangerous, sick and hopeless."
