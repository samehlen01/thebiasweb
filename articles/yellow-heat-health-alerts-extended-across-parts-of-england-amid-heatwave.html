UKHSA has extended yellow heat-health alerts across parts of England as the heatwave continues. Amber and yellow heat-health alerts remain in place until 9pm on Wednesday, July 15.

The alerts are meant to tell health and care services that the heat can put pressure on them. Pressure can rise quickly when temperatures stay high.

Hot conditions can also overwhelm the body's cooling system and lead to heat exhaustion or heatstroke. UKHSA says the heat can also mean more deaths, higher demand, overheating and water-related incidents.

Temperature records

Reading University's count tracks days at least 30C, while the Met Office tracks more extreme temperature records.

15
days this summer at least 30C
Reading University's count, compared with 14 in 1976

Reading University said five of those days were among the university's 20 highest temperatures ever recorded.

Separately, the Met Office says 2026 has set record heat milestones.

8
days above 34C so far this year
previous record of seven, set in 1976 and matched in 2020

Temperatures of 35C or higher were recorded in May, June and July for the first time in the UK's weather records.

A reading of 34.7C in Coton in the Elms, Derbyshire, was one of the temperatures behind that record.

Immediate impacts

The hot, dry spell has already fed fire risk.

The National Fire Chiefs Council says severe or extreme wildfire risk is high in many areas.

  • Wildfires are burning across England and Wales.
  • Water companies have imposed hosepipe bans and restrictions.
  • Dry weather and high demand are squeezing supplies.
  • Some schools in the UK have closed early or adjusted schedules because of the heat.

Emergency services are dealing with 19 active incidents across England and Wales.

The National Fire Chiefs Council says seven English regions have been issued with yellow wildfire alerts between Tuesday and Friday.

What can begin as a small fire can spread incredibly quickly
— National Fire Chiefs Council

Dry vegetation, warm air and gusts can turn a local blaze into a wider incident.

Under these conditions, a spark can become a serious blaze before responders can get ahead of it.

Dr Stephen Burt and Professor Hannah Cloke of the University of Reading have also warned that if dry conditions continue, wildfire risk could stay above normal and sparks could trigger fast-moving fires.

Climate researchers say extreme heat can disrupt schools, hospitals, transport, agriculture and infrastructure.

Researchers from Imperial College London, the Met Office and the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine said their analysis estimated that more than 2,700 people died from heat-related causes in England and Wales during the May and June heatwaves.

They said about 550 deaths came in the nine days of the May event and about 2,200 more in the 11 days around the June spell.

The same analysis said 42% of those deaths were linked to extra heat caused by human activity, mostly burning fossil fuels.

Dr Clair Barnes of Imperial College London said the numbers are large.

In her view, they show climate-change-fuelled heat is already claiming lives, disrupting services and forcing adaptation.

Political reaction

Zack Polanski of the Green Party of England and Wales said the deaths are on the hands of politicians who have failed to act over the years. He warned that more deaths will follow without stronger action.

Researchers also project that heat-related deaths in parts of northern Europe could rival cold-related deaths within a couple of decades.

That outcome depends on emissions cuts and adaptation.

Forecasts and what comes next

Forecasters expect the heatwave to continue through much of the week, with temperatures around 30C to 33C and isolated peaks higher. Southern-central England could see 33C, and another 34C-plus reading is anticipated on Friday.

What remains uncertain is how long the heat will last. BBC Weather and Met Office deputy chief forecaster Tom Crabtree said a cooler spell may arrive from Saturday, but the outlook is uncertain because high pressure could come back and keep the warm pattern going.
