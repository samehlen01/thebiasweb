Overview

Iranian authorities largely shut down internet access as protests spread nationwide, and there were widespread reports that telephone service was also cut. Web monitors including NetBlocks, Kentik and Cloudflare recorded abrupt falls in traffic and described the country as almost completely offline. The unrest began as protests over a plunging rial and rising prices, and quickly broadened into anti-government demonstrations. Rights groups' tallies differ sharply: one rights group reported many hundreds killed and more than 10,000 arrests, while other monitors recorded figures in the dozens of deaths and a few thousand detentions.

How the blackout was measured

Traffic-monitoring firms and independent researchers registered what they described as a near-total disconnection. Kentik's Doug Madory and an Iranian cybersecurity researcher told reporters the shutdown appeared to begin on Thursday evening local time. NetBlocks' live metrics showed national connectivity collapsing and, in one update, national connectivity was recorded at about 1% of ordinary levels. Cloudflare analysts said only a small amount of residual traffic remained.

Scale and spread of protests

Multiple outlets reported demonstrations across all 31 provinces and in hundreds of towns. The US-based Human Rights Activists News Agency (HRANA) told some outlets that protests had formed in 585 locations across 186 cities by the movement's 15th day. Social-media footage, which could not be fully verified, showed people tearing the national flag and in some videos chanting 'Long live the king.' The unrest began with shopkeepers and traders in Tehran's bazaar and then widened.

Discrepancies in casualty and arrest tallies

Counts of deaths and arrests vary widely between sources and rights groups. For example, The Independent quoted rights groups putting deaths in the hundreds and arrests above 10,000. CBS and other outlets cited HRANA figures of roughly 42 deaths and about 2,260 detentions. Sky News reported HRANA as saying 34 protesters and four security personnel had been killed with about 2,200 arrests. The Guardian noted Iran Human Rights (IHR) reporting 45 protesters killed while an AFP tally put total deaths at about 21. Fox News pieces cited different tallies — NCRI's 44 and IHRNGO's 51 — and another Fox report gave HRANA's tally of 65. TheJournal.ie published CHRI warnings that "hundreds of protesters have been killed", and the Daily Mail described 'hundreds' feared dead and cited overwhelmed hospitals.

Government statements and security response

Iran's leadership and state media have presented a coordinated narrative: state outlets accused US and Israeli 'terrorist agents' of inciting violence and said public and private property had been set alight. State television listed "people's private cars, motorcycles, public places such as the metro, fire trucks and buses set on fire" and spoke of casualties. Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei urged forceful action, saying the republic would not 'back down' and accusing 'vandals' of 'ruining their own streets to make the president of another country happy'. Interior and police officials described 'vandalism' and said 'significant' arrests were made, while the Revolutionary Guard accused 'terrorists' of attacking security bases and reported casualties among security personnel.

International reactions and risks

Abroad, leaders and commentators reacted quickly. President Donald Trump warned Iran he would "hit them very hard" if authorities killed protesters and said the United States stood ready to help. US media outlets reported that officials had briefed the president on military options, though those outlets said no final decision had been made. European governments — France, Britain and Germany — issued a joint statement urging restraint, while Iranian politicians warned that American bases and Israeli territory would be legitimate targets if Iran were attacked.

Voices condemning the blackout

Artists in the diaspora and rights campaigners have spoken out. Filmmakers Jafar Panahi and Mohammad Rasoulof called the shutdown a blatant tool of repression and urged the international community to facilitate communication. Actors and visual artists such as Golshifteh Farahani and Soheila Sokhanvari voiced solidarity and said Iranians must not be left isolated. Amnesty International and other rights groups accused security forces of unlawful force as hospitals reported being overwhelmed and blood supplies running low.

Technical assessment of the shutdown

Digital-rights analysts said the shutdown was unusually surgical. Experts described the action as more precise than past blackouts and argued Tehran had developed tools to whitelist some services while cutting off others. One researcher described the domestic effect bluntly: 'There is no reception on the phones. There is no antenna. It's like you are living in the middle of nowhere, with no BTS towers.' Another analyst warned, 'This might be for the long haul.' NetBlocks and other monitors said connectivity had plunged to a tiny fraction of normal levels.

"In recent days, following the presence of millions of Iranians in the streets protesting against the Islamic Republic, the government has once again resorted to its most blatant tools of repression."
— Jafar Panahi and Mohammad Rasoulof

Khamenei framed the unrest as foreign-directed and repeatedly urged security forces to act; state broadcasts echoed warnings that rioters were saboteurs. Opponents and rights groups say the communications shutdown is being used to conceal or hamper reporting of security-force violence, and they are calling for measures to restore access and for independent monitoring.

State of play

Multiple independent monitors and news outlets agree the country's networks were deliberately and extensively disrupted while demonstrations spread across many cities. Rights organisations allege security forces have used lethal force against demonstrators, while Iranian officials and state media accuse foreign 'terrorist agents' and say they will act forcefully. Exact death and detention totals remain disputed and unverifiable while communications are cut, and international leaders have urged restraint as the United States warns of possible punitive steps.
