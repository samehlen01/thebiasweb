The Russian frigate Neustrashimy conducted a live-fire artillery drill in international waters roughly 40 nautical miles south of Plymouth, while shadowed by a Royal Navy vessel. Some reports translated that distance as about 45 miles, while another account rounded it to about 46 miles.

The frigate gave HMS Tyne advance notice before starting the drill, and the patrol vessel moved to a safer distance. The firing lasted around 30 minutes and took place in international waters, with several outlets saying it began around 9am Monday morning. BBC reporting also said a French military aircraft followed the vessel, while UK and French aircraft were scrambled nearby as the activity unfolded.

Because the ship gave advance notice, the encounter did not have the feel of a sudden breach of protocol. Even so, the episode had a political charge that went beyond routine training.

Timing and intent

Several reports said the exercise happened the same day Andy Burnham entered government as prime minister. Dmitry Peskov said Russian warship actions comply with law, there is no need to read signals into the exercise, and Burnham had signalled support for Ukraine. In that telling, the political backdrop should not change how the drill is read.

Wes Streeting said the exercise was performative and irresponsible and warned Russia not to doubt the government's resolve.

Lord Dannatt was even more suspicious about the timing. He said the exercise did not look accidental on Burnham's first day, and he framed the move as a Kremlin challenge. That view fed speculation that the firing was deliberately staged as a show of strength.

Tom Sharpe added a more technical layer to the scepticism. He said the drill was very unusual, looked like a signal, and lacked extra safety measures, pointing to the frigate's close approach to the south coast and the risk created by a 100mm cannon. The close range, in his view, made the exercise stand out from ordinary naval training because it raised the possibility of ricochet near busy waters. The ship informed HMS Tyne ahead of time and HMS Tyne moved to a safer distance. Sharpe also floated a separate speculation that Moscow may have been answering the UK's boarding of Smyrtos.

Emma Salisbury offered a different kind of warning. She argued that the drill could be defended under the rules even while still giving Moscow the effect it wanted, and she pointed to the missing NAVAREA warning as part of a broader Russian habit of calibrated pressure and visible reassertion. Salisbury said the pattern extracts a propaganda dividend at minimal cost. She expects more Russian activity off the UK coast.

Luke Pollard tried to steady local nerves after the drill. He said residents should be reassured and that the Armed Forces remained vigilant, while acknowledging that activity this close to the coastline could make people in Plymouth and elsewhere uneasy.

Once the ship was placed in roughly the same area south of Plymouth, the debate turned back to whether Moscow intended a routine weapons exercise or a deliberate demonstration of reach.

Russian activity near UK waters

The Plymouth incident also sits inside a wider pattern the Ministry of Defence has been counting. The MoD said Royal Navy vessels were activated 116 times in 2025-26 to monitor 61 Russian warships and 28 merchant vessels in the UK's exclusive economic zone, and it said that represented a 30% increase over the previous two years. BBC analysis said the Neustrashimy episode will serve as another reminder of that rise in Russian naval activity near UK waters.

James Cartlidge called the exercise very concerning and argued for more surface ships and frigates. More surface ships and frigates would send a powerful signal to Putin, and Cartlidge said they were needed urgently. Cartlidge's urgency reflects the view that deterrence depends on visible presence, not just statements.

The reactions also map onto different institutional vantage points. The Royal Navy continues to track the vessel’s activity closely, and stands ready to protect UK national security.
