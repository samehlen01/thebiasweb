Overview

A week of high‑stakes moves began when US forces carried out an operation in Venezuela that, the White House said, resulted in the capture of President Nicolás Maduro and his wife. Within days Mr Trump refocused attention on Greenland, arguing the island matters to US defence and that Washington should play a larger role there. White House spokespeople said the president and his national security team were "actively" discussing options and that, although diplomacy was described as the administration's preferred route, "all options are always on the table."

"We do need Greenland, absolutely," Trump said. "We need it for defense."
— President Donald J. Trump

What the White House and Trump said

Mr Trump also told reporters the island was "covered with Russian and Chinese ships," an assertion that allies have disputed. On US television, White House deputy chief of staff Stephen Miller argued there would be no military contest over Greenland and said "Nobody's going to fight the United States militarily over the future of Greenland." Separately, reporting from Capitol Hill said Secretary of State Marco Rubio told lawmakers the administration's stated preference was to pursue a purchase, not an invasion.

Responses in Copenhagen and Nuuk

Allied capitals reacted with swift condemnation. Denmark's prime minister warned that an attack on a NATO ally would undermine the alliance, saying that "if the United States decides to attack another NATO country, then everything would stop". Greenland's elected leaders and unions insisted the island was not for sale; one Greenlandic politician declared "This is enough. No more pressure. No more hints. No more fantasies about annexation." Several European governments issued a joint statement that 'Greenland belongs to its people' and urged collective approaches to Arctic security.

Voices on the ground

Reporting from Nuuk and other towns found a mixture of fear, indignation and cautious pragmatism. Some residents said they were scared to speak and described sleepless nights; one told RTÉ News, "I don't sleep very well because I'm afraid." Another local told reporters, "They have no respect for us, for my country, for our country." Greenland politicians were split: Aaja Chemnitz called Washington's comments "a clear threat," while Pele Broberg of the pro‑independence Naleraq party said Greenland should explore direct talks with the United States.

UK and US political reaction

In Westminster and on Capitol Hill the episode prompted calls for restraint. Scottish MPs urged Prime Minister Keir Starmer to rebuke the president, warning that "staying silent" would allow pressure to escalate. In Washington, Senator Ruben Gallego said he would introduce a resolution to block any attempted invasion of Greenland, and other lawmakers issued statements stressing that 'Greenland is not for sale' and treaty obligations must be honoured.

Military feasibility and legal view

Military analysts and legal experts reached very different conclusions. Some defence commentators told reporters a short, well‑resourced US operation could be executed quickly and might face little armed resistance; one analyst said it would be "completely unopposed." By contrast, former officials warned such a seizure would violate international law and senior European leaders said it would strip Article 5 of its meaning and effectively destroy NATO's cohesion.

Policy options on the table

Officials and reporting identified a menu of non‑military and coercive options: a negotiated purchase, a compact or free‑association arrangement, commercial deals and targeted incentives for Greenlanders. News reports quoted Reuters saying US officials had considered direct payments to residents in the range of $10,000–$100,000. Company executives also confirmed Washington was discussing commercial investment and support for mine projects as part of those economic levers.

Economic stakes and mining interest

Part of Washington's interest is economic: geologists and government surveys point to extensive critical minerals and hydrocarbon potential beneath Greenland's ice. The US Geological Survey has been cited as estimating 'around 31 billion barrels of oil‑equivalent in hydrocarbons' in onshore northeast basins. Analysts' valuations for the island vary wildly — one American think‑tank and senior economist said 'something in the trillions looks about right,' while other analysts produced far lower estimates.

Conflicting accounts and numerical differences

Even basic numbers and details differ between outlets. Several pieces cited Greenland's population at about 57,000; others used roughly 56,000. Reporting also diverged over how the Maduro operation unfolded and how many casualties it caused, and outlets gave sharply different price estimates for Greenland's worth — from tens of billions in some calculations to the multi‑trillion figures cited by others. Journalists have flagged that accounts of whether Rubio categorically ruled out an invasion differ between diplomatic briefings and other reporting.

Diplomatic moves and next meetings

Copenhagen and Nuuk have sent envoys to Washington; Denmark's ambassador and Greenland's chief representative met White House National Security Council officials this week to press their case. Secretary Rubio is due to hold talks with Danish officials next week, and European capitals are discussing coordinated responses — limited political measures, economic pressure, and possible deterrent deployments have all been floated in private talks.

How outlets and commentators framed the story

Coverage mixed alarm, legal analysis and business angles. Public‑facing outlets and columnists posed questions about the legality and the consequences for NATO; opinion writers warned that the rhetoric signalled a dangerous shift in US behaviour. Business and mining coverage emphasised commercial opportunity and feasibility questions, while human‑interest and local reporting in Greenland focused on fear, social divisions and the long history of Danish governance.

State of play

Confirmed across multiple outlets: the White House says the issue is under active consideration and Mr Trump has publicly argued Greenland is important for US security. Claimed or alleged (by administration figures): that Russian and Chinese ships now surround Greenland and that the island should be under a stronger US umbrella. Disputed or unclear: whether the administration has formally ruled out military force (some diplomats reported Rubio 'ruled out' invasion; other reporting emphasised military options were not taken off the table) and the island's precise monetary valuation. What comes next: Secretary Rubio's meetings with Danish officials and repeated diplomatic engagement in Washington and Brussels are the next steps reported by outlets.

Western Europe
Sources
Le Figaro, France 24, BFM TV, RTBF Belgium, ZDF Heute, Tagesschau (ARD), Der Standard Austria, RTE Ireland, La Repubblica, RAI News, El País, La Vanguardia, El Mundo, NOS Netherlands, Público Portugal, RTP Portugal (State), Euronews, Politico EU, Czech TV (State)

Several continental outlets added legal and economic detail UK roundups often omitted: RTBF and Le Figaro ran valuation pieces citing studies that Greenland’s mineral potential could dwarf its GDP; Le Figaro published polls (≈28% favour purchase, ≈8% favour military action). Many papers repeated Reuters reporting that US advisers discussed one‑off payments (reported $10,000–$100,000 per person) and that US officials have met Danish and Greenlandic envoys; several said Rubio is preparing talks with Danish leaders.

Coverage emphasised diplomatic pushback and contingency planning: RTE, La Repubblica and others noted a joint statement by France, the UK, Germany, Italy, Poland, Spain and Denmark that 'Greenland belongs to its people'. Tagesschau and ZDF ran legal analyses stressing that an attack on a NATO member would trigger an unprecedented alliance crisis and recalled the 1951 US–Denmark defence arrangements; Politico EU and some capitals report Paris/Berlin sketching deterrent options (troops/exercises, economic measures), while Czech TV warned a 'fait accompli' seizure could be executed in hours.

Northern Europe
Sources
Yle Finland (State), Helsingin Sanomat, NRK Norway (State), VG Norway, Aftonbladet Sweden
If the United States attacks another NATO country, everything stops.
— Mette Frederiksen, Danish prime minister

NRK, VG, Yle and Helsingin Sanomat carried granular local reporting: on‑the‑ground interviews show fear in Greenland and NRK cited polling that about 85% of Greenlanders oppose becoming US citizens. Helsingin Sanomat reported Greenland’s reliance on Danish subsidies at roughly a quarter of its budget, while other coverage gives higher estimates — a concrete disagreement that affects how attractive direct payments would be. Papers also noted Copenhagen’s pledge to increase Arctic defence spending and highlighted pro‑independence figures open to 'free association' options.

Eastern Europe
Sources
RT Russia (State), TASS (State), TVN24 Poland, Ukrainska Pravda, Ria Novosti (State), Rossiyskaya Gazeta (State), Kommersant, The Bell Russia

Russian state media framed the episode as transatlantic disarray and amplified specifics some UK summaries did not: Ria Novosti and Rossiyskaya Gazeta highlighted Reuters reporting that US officials discussed one‑off payments of roughly $10,000–$100,000 per Greenlander, cited Polymarket pricing (~16% chance of Greenland joining the US) and pointed to YouGov polling showing US public opposition. RT outlined four possible European responses (negotiated concessions, increased funding, economic retaliation or military deterrence) and TASS amplified a poll showing ~85% Greenlander opposition. Polish and Ukrainian outlets (TVN24, Ukrainska Pravda) instead stressed legal norms and the joint European statement warning that intra‑NATO coercion would undermine the alliance.

South Asia

All options are always on the table for President Trump.

South Asian outlets added diplomatic and tracking details: The Hindu reports Denmark’s ambassador and Greenland’s Washington representative met National Security Council officials and US lawmakers; The Hindu and Hindustan Times say Rubio will meet Danish officials next week and that US advisers favour purchase over invasion. The Hindu published MarineTraffic/LSEG vessel‑tracking analysis it says contradicts claims of Chinese or Russian ships near Greenland.

Economic and legal context ran throughout coverage: Hindustan Times and India Today noted Greenland’s GDP is about $3.6bn (2023) and reporting diverges on Danish subsidies (figures reported from roughly a quarter to about half of Greenland’s budget). Commentators recalled the 1946 US $100m offer (≈$1.6bn today), flagged that 25 of 34 EU 'critical' minerals have been identified on the island, highlighted congressional/constitutional hurdles to force (Indian Express) and noted COFA as a possible intermediate arrangement; Pakistani outlets said Greenland’s government requested to join planned Rubio–Danish talks.

North America
Sources
Globe and Mail, CBC Canada, National Post Canada

Canadian outlets combined on‑the‑ground voices with diplomatic detail: CBC captured Greenlanders' anger and insult at being talked about as an object of purchase while noting some local politicians urged calm and negotiation; the Globe and Mail relayed reporting that US officials discussed payments and kept military options on the table and noted Canada’s planned consulate in Nuuk and public backing for Denmark. National Post opinion pieces emphasised Greenland’s control of the GIUK gap and warned US control would reshape NATO dynamics, arguing expanded access under existing defence arrangements is more plausible than outright annexation but would carry reputational cost.

Latin America
Sources
La Nación Argentina, Clarín Argentina, Folha Brazil, G1 Brazil (Globo), CubaDebate (State), 14ymedio Cuba, OnCuba News

Argentine, Brazilian and Cuban outlets tied the Greenland episode to broader US policy in the region: La Nación and Clarín stressed Greenland’s strategic position (the GIUK gap) and ran explainers on what the 1951 US–Denmark defence pact allows and forbids; Folha and G1 relayed Reuters’ reporting that US advisers discussed direct payments and that Rubio told lawmakers the administration prefers purchase to invasion. Cuban titles framed the episode as imperialism — CubaDebate and 14ymedio emphasised US rhetoric and claims about military options, while OnCuba ran the Greenland prime minister’s call for calm.

Oceania
Sources
ABC Australia, Nine News Australia, SBS Australia, 7News Australia

Australian outlets set out the policy menu and warned of legal and alliance consequences: ABC outlined invasion, purchase or a Compact of Free Association (COFA), with experts telling ABC a forcible seizure would breach international law and carry heavy diplomatic costs — analysts largely treat military rhetoric as bargaining posture. Nine and SBS reported Rubio’s planned talks with Denmark and recent US movements into UK bases; 7News ran former‑intelligence commentary describing the push as strategic positioning rather than an imminent large‑scale invasion.

East Asia
Sources
South China Morning Post, Japan Times

SCMP and Japan Times questioned the security claims and purchase precedents: SCMP reported vessel‑tracking that it says does not support the White House’s description of Chinese activity near Greenland and noted Beijing’s public criticism of US rhetoric while flagging China’s broader Arctic ambitions. Japan Times ran commentary warning there is no market mechanism to 'price' a country, described historical purchase precedents as poor analogues and reported officials drafting templates ranging from purchase to COFA, urging European partners to take the rhetoric seriously.

Southeast Asia

Singapore outlets emphasised tactical politics: the Straits Times and Channel NewsAsia reported Greenland politicians (notably Naleraq leader Pele Broberg) urging direct talks with Washington that could bypass Copenhagen, explained COFA as a midway option and noted Rubio’s planned meeting with Danish officials while NATO envoys discussed stepped‑up Arctic deterrence — framing the episode as active diplomacy rather than mere presidential spin.

Middle East
Sources
Tehran Times, Al Jazeera (Qatar), Times of Israel, Daily Sabah Turkey, Anadolu Agency Turkey (State), Mehr News Iran, SANA Syria (State)

Regional outlets split between polemic and procedural accounts: Tehran Times linked the Greenland push to US actions in Venezuela and repeated claims that Caracas might hand over up to 50 million barrels (≈$2.8bn) to Washington, casting the moves as imperialism. Al Jazeera and the Times of Israel ran straighter accounts (Rubio’s planned meetings, 'all options' rhetoric and Greenland’s strategic assets such as the Pituffik base, the GIUK gap and rare‑earth potential). Turkish outlets and SANA urged Europe to take US rhetoric seriously and revisited Denmark’s colonial record; Mehr News amplified an assertion attributed to J.D. Vance that Greenland is 'critical' to missile‑defence infrastructure.

Sub-Saharan Africa
Sources
Punch Nigeria, News24 South Africa, Daily Maverick SA

African outlets largely echoed European calls to respect Greenland’s sovereignty and international law: Punch and News24 relayed the joint European statement and Denmark’s diplomatic protests, while Daily Maverick critiqued the Venezuela/Greenland moves as 'gunship diplomacy', warning that such precedents threaten smaller states and revive imperial‑style interventions.
